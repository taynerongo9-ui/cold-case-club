# Case #2: The Belmont Hotel Fire — Story Bible

## Classification
- **Case Number:** CCC-2024-002
- **Case Name:** The Belmont Hotel Fire
- **Setting:** The Belmont Hotel, 412 King Street, Charleston, South Carolina
- **Date of Incident:** October 17, 1993
- **Official Ruling:** Accidental death — fell asleep with lit cigarette
- **True Cause:** Arson and murder

---

## NARRATIVE SUMMARY

On the night of October 17, 1993, a fire broke out on the fourth floor of the Belmont Hotel, a landmark Charleston establishment known for its antebellum architecture and old-money clientele. The body of Marcus Webb, 58, co-owner of the hotel, was recovered from Room 416 the following morning. The Charleston Fire Department ruled the blaze accidental — the result of a lit cigarette left unattended by a man who had fallen asleep. Case closed.

Except Marcus Webb did not smoke.

His business partner, Gerald Tate, had been embezzling from the hotel for years, laundering money through a network of shell accounts tied to offshore holdings. When Webb discovered the fraud and prepared documentation for the IRS with the help of his attorney Samuel Cross, Tate decided that Webb — and the evidence inside Room 416 — needed to disappear.

On the night of October 17, Tate attended the annual Charleston Yacht Club Autumn Gala, ensuring over 200 witnesses could place him across town. At 9:45 PM, he slipped out through the kitchen service entrance, drove twelve minutes to the Belmont, disabled the fourth-floor security system using his owner access codes at 9:38 PM (the system had been remotely triggered slightly earlier via the hotel's phone-dial interface), entered Room 416 where Marcus Webb lay unconscious from chloral hydrate Tate had slipped into his bourbon earlier that evening, set fires at two separate origin points using an accelerant, and drove back to the gala. He re-entered through the kitchen by 10:30 PM and was photographed with the mayor at 10:42 PM.

Detective Lorraine Voss of the Charleston PD suspected foul play from the beginning. She noted the two fire origin points, the absence of cigarettes in Webb's personal effects, and inconsistencies in Tate's timeline. But Tate's family had deep roots in Charleston society, and political pressure from above shut down her investigation. The case was sealed.

Until now.

## COMPLETE TIMELINE

### Before October 17, 1993

- **1971:** Gerald Tate and Marcus Webb meet at the University of South Carolina. Tate comes from old Charleston money (the Tate family owned rice plantations in the 18th century). Webb is from a working-class family in Columbia.
- **1978:** Webb and Tate purchase the Belmont Hotel, a faded grand dame on King Street. Webb handles operations; Tate handles finances and "society connections."
- **1982:** The Belmont is restored to its former glory. It becomes a Charleston landmark again.
- **1985:** Tate begins skimming from hotel accounts, initially small amounts.
- **1988:** Tate establishes offshore accounts in the Cayman Islands. Laundering operations escalate. He creates fictitious vendor accounts (linen services, food suppliers) that route payments to shell companies he controls.
- **1991:** Marcus marries Patricia Davenport, a Charleston gallery owner. Gerald's wife Claire expresses private concerns about Gerald's spending to friends.
- **March 1993:** Hotel accountant flags irregularities during annual review. Webb begins his own quiet investigation.
- **June 1993:** Webb contacts attorney Samuel Cross. Cross advises documenting everything before going to authorities.
- **July 1993:** Webb compiles financial records showing $1.4 million in fraudulent transactions over 8 years. He stores copies in Room 416 (his personal office/retreat in the hotel) and with Cross.
- **August 1993:** Webb confronts Tate privately. Tate denies everything, then begins planning.
- **September 1993:** Tate increases the hotel's insurance policy from $800K to $2.3M, citing "renovation value increases." Webb does not co-sign (Tate forges his signature).
- **October 1, 1993:** Eva Chen, hotel housekeeper, notices a strange chemical smell in Room 416 while cleaning. She reports it to night manager Ray Hicks, who logs it but does not investigate further.
- **October 10, 1993:** Tate purchases chloral hydrate through a contact (a disgraced veterinarian in Savannah). This is never directly proven but implied through pharmacy records from Savannah showing unusual purchases.
- **October 14, 1993:** Patricia Webb updates Marcus's will at his request, adding the Charleston Arts Foundation as a beneficiary. This will later make her appear suspicious.
- **October 15, 1993:** Webb tells Delilah Monroe, the hotel lounge singer and his trusted confidante, that he's "about to do something that will change everything" and that he's "scared of what Gerald might do."

### October 17, 1993 — The Night Of

- **6:00 PM:** Marcus Webb arrives at the Belmont Hotel. He goes to Room 416, his usual retreat/office.
- **6:30 PM:** Gerald Tate stops by Room 416. They share bourbon from Webb's personal decanter. Tate has pre-dosed the decanter with chloral hydrate during a visit earlier that day.
- **7:00 PM:** Tate leaves for the Charleston Yacht Club Autumn Gala. He is seen arriving at 7:25 PM.
- **7:30 PM - 9:00 PM:** Webb works in Room 416. The chloral hydrate takes effect gradually. By 9:00 PM, Webb is unconscious.
- **8:00 PM:** Ray Hicks begins his night shift. Notes all quiet on floors 3 and 4.
- **9:15 PM:** A guest checks into Room 418 under the name "David Crane." This is actually journalist Martin Hale, investigating a separate corruption story involving the hotel's role in county zoning deals. He has no connection to the fire.
- **9:38 PM:** The Belmont's fourth-floor security system is disabled remotely via the hotel's dial-in phone interface. Only Tate and Webb know the access code.
- **9:45 PM:** Tate excuses himself from the gala, telling a tablemate he needs air. He exits through the kitchen service entrance.
- **9:57 PM:** Tate arrives at the Belmont via the rear service alley. He uses his master key to enter through the ground-floor service door.
- **10:00 PM:** Tate enters Room 416. Webb is unconscious on the settee. Tate places a half-smoked cigarette (purchased from a gas station; Tate does not smoke either) near Webb's hand. He sets the first fire near the window curtains using a petroleum-based accelerant. He sets the second fire near the room's entry closet, ensuring the hallway will be impassable.
- **10:03 PM:** Tate exits via the service stairs. He drives back to the Yacht Club.
- **10:12 PM:** Smoke detected by a guest on the 4th floor. They pull the fire alarm.
- **10:14 PM:** Ray Hicks calls 911.
- **10:17 PM:** Charleston Fire Department dispatched.
- **10:22 PM:** First engine arrives at the Belmont.
- **10:30 PM:** Tate re-enters the Yacht Club through the kitchen. He orders a scotch at the bar.
- **10:42 PM:** Tate is photographed with Mayor Dalton at the gala.
- **11:15 PM:** Fire on the 4th floor is contained. Room 416 is destroyed. Rooms 414 and 418 sustain heavy smoke and water damage.
- **11:45 PM:** Firefighters recover a body from Room 416.

### After October 17, 1993

- **October 18:** Body identified as Marcus Webb. Charleston Fire Department begins investigation. Fire Chief Warren Briggs leads the inquiry.
- **October 19:** Chief Briggs notes two separate fire origin points in his preliminary report. He flags this as "inconsistent with accidental cause."
- **October 20:** Detective Lorraine Voss assigned to the case by Charleston PD.
- **October 21:** Gerald Tate gives his initial statement. Alibi: Charleston Yacht Club all evening.
- **October 22:** Patricia Webb is questioned. The will change from October 14 is discovered. Media begins speculating about the wife.
- **October 25:** Autopsy conducted by county medical examiner Dr. Franklin Hayes. Cause of death: smoke inhalation. Blood alcohol: 0.14. No toxicology screen for sedatives is ordered. A lit cigarette is listed as probable fire cause.
- **October 28:** Chief Briggs submits final report. Despite noting two origin points and accelerant traces, he is pressured to classify the fire as accidental. He privately tells Voss he disagrees with the ruling.
- **November 1:** Detective Voss files a supplementary report recommending the case remain open. Her captain, under political pressure, overrules her.
- **November 5:** Case officially classified as accidental death. Tate collects $2.3M in insurance.
- **November 1993 - February 1994:** Voss continues investigating off the books. She interviews hotel staff, gala attendees, and financial contacts.
- **December 1993:** Waiter Thomas Pruitt at the Yacht Club tells Voss that Tate was absent from the gala for approximately 45 minutes around 10 PM.
- **January 1994:** Voss obtains Webb's doctor records confirming he was a non-smoker.
- **February 1994:** Voss is reassigned to another division. Her supplementary files are boxed.
- **March 1994:** Attorney Samuel Cross attempts to submit Webb's IRS documents to federal authorities. Tate's lawyers file injunctions. The documents are sealed pending litigation.
- **1994-2024:** The case remains closed. Gerald Tate sells the Belmont in 1997, retires to Kiawah Island. Patricia Webb moves to Savannah. Voss retires in 2008 but keeps her personal case files.
- **2024:** Cold Case Club obtains Voss's files, Briggs's original report, and previously sealed documents through FOIA requests and Voss's personal cooperation.

## CHARACTER PROFILES

### Detective Lorraine Voss
- **Age in 1993:** 41
- **Background:** Born in Beaufort, SC. First woman detective in the Charleston PD Crimes Against Persons unit (promoted 1988). Known for meticulous case files and stubborn persistence.
- **Role in case:** Lead investigator. She saw the truth from the beginning — two origin points, no cigarettes, a partner with financial motive — but was blocked by political pressure. The Tate family's influence in Charleston made the case radioactive.
- **Personality:** Measured, precise, dry humor. Writes in clipped professional prose but her personal notes reveal fury at the system. She kept her own shadow file on the case for 30 years.
- **Voice:** Professional, Southern but not folksy. Uses law enforcement terminology precisely.
- **Current (2024):** Retired, age 72, living in Mount Pleasant, SC. Cooperated with Cold Case Club because "someone ought to finally say what happened in that hotel."

### Gerald Tate — THE KILLER
- **Age in 1993:** 52
- **Background:** Born into the Tate family of Charleston — old rice money, society connections, membership in every club that mattered. Educated at Citadel, then UofSC business school where he met Webb. Charming, well-dressed, comfortable in any room.
- **Motive:** Webb discovered Tate had laundered approximately $1.4M through the hotel's accounts over 8 years using fictitious vendor companies. Webb was preparing to report this to the IRS. Additionally, Tate had inflated the hotel's insurance to $2.3M. Killing Webb and burning Room 416 would destroy the evidence AND trigger a massive insurance payout.
- **Method:** Dosed Webb's bourbon decanter with chloral hydrate earlier on October 17. Attended the Yacht Club gala for his alibi. Left through the kitchen at 9:45 PM, drove to the hotel, set two fires with accelerant in Room 416, returned to the gala by 10:30 PM.
- **Personality:** Smooth, patrician, always the smartest man in the room (in his own estimation). Genuinely liked Webb in the early years but came to see him as a threat. Rationalizes everything.
- **Key evidence against him:** Waiter's testimony of his absence, security system disabled with owner code, smoke on his tuxedo (dry cleaning receipt), financial records linking hotel accounts to his offshore holdings.
- **Current (2024):** Age 83, living on Kiawah Island. Never charged.

### Patricia Webb
- **Age in 1993:** 54
- **Background:** Born Patricia Davenport, Charleston gallery owner. Married Marcus in 1991. Their marriage was genuine but complicated — Marcus was consumed by the hotel business, and Patricia suspected he was keeping secrets (he was, but about the fraud, not an affair).
- **Role in case:** Primary red herring. The will change three days before the fire looks damning, but she was adding the Charleston Arts Foundation as a beneficiary at Marcus's request. She inherited the estate but cooperated fully with investigators.
- **Personality:** Elegant, reserved, sharp. Not the grieving widow stereotype — she processes grief privately and intellectually.
- **Current (2024):** Living in Savannah, Georgia. Age 85. Still maintains Marcus was murdered.

### Raymond "Ray" Hicks
- **Age in 1993:** 34
- **Background:** Charleston native, worked at the Belmont since 1986. Fired in March 1993 by Tate (officially for "insubordination" — actually because Hicks questioned a suspicious after-hours delivery). Rehired in June 1993 by Webb, who overruled Tate.
- **Role in case:** Secondary red herring (grudge angle — fired and rehired) but also a key witness. He was on duty the night of the fire. He noticed the security system was offline at 9:38 PM but assumed it was a glitch. He called 911 at 10:14 PM.
- **Personality:** Working-class, straightforward, carries guilt about not investigating the security system issue sooner.
- **Current (2024):** Age 65, retired, living in North Charleston.

### Delilah Monroe
- **Age in 1993:** 29
- **Background:** Singer at the Belmont's Blue Room Lounge. Grew up in North Charleston, trained at Charleston Southern. Beautiful voice, sharp mind. Marcus Webb was her mentor and friend — he funded her first demo tape and treated her like family.
- **Role in case:** Confidante. Marcus told her he was afraid of what Gerald "might do." She was never properly interviewed in 1993 because investigators focused on Patricia and Hicks.
- **Personality:** Warm, perceptive, protective of Marcus's memory. Speaks in vivid, emotional language.
- **Current (2024):** Age 60, still performing in Charleston. Music teacher at Charleston County School of the Arts.

### Fire Chief Warren Briggs
- **Age in 1993:** 56
- **Background:** 30-year veteran of the Charleston Fire Department. Decorated. Known for thorough investigations. The Belmont fire was the case that haunted him.
- **Role in case:** Conducted the fire investigation. His report clearly notes two separate origin points and traces of petroleum-based accelerant — both inconsistent with an accidental cigarette fire. He was pressured by the fire commissioner (a friend of the Tate family) to classify it as accidental. He complied but never agreed.
- **Personality:** Gruff, honest, guilt-ridden about caving to pressure.
- **Current (2024):** Deceased (2019). His original unedited report was preserved in Voss's shadow file.

### Claire Tate
- **Age in 1993:** 49
- **Background:** Born Claire Beaumont, another old Charleston family. Married Gerald in 1972. Social butterfly, charity organizer, the perfect society wife. She loved Gerald but wasn't blind to his flaws.
- **Role in case:** Unwitting evidence holder. She took Gerald's tuxedo to the dry cleaner after the gala. The dry cleaning receipt notes "heavy smoke odor — customer states from fireplace at event." But the Yacht Club gala was held in the Grand Ballroom, which has no fireplace. Claire never thought twice about it.
- **Personality:** Gracious, social, not unintelligent but trained from birth to not ask questions.
- **Current (2024):** Age 80, still on Kiawah Island. Still married to Gerald.

### Samuel Cross
- **Age in 1993:** 61
- **Background:** Marcus Webb's personal attorney. Old-school Charleston lawyer with an office on Broad Street. Cautious, meticulous, principled.
- **Role in case:** Holds the copies of the financial documents Webb prepared for the IRS. After the fire, Tate's lawyers tied up the documents in litigation. Cross kept his copies in a sealed file, waiting for the right moment.
- **Personality:** Lawyerly, careful with words, but deeply angry about what happened to his client.
- **Current (2024):** Retired, age 92. Released documents to Cold Case Club under Voss's request.

### Eva Chen
- **Age in 1993:** 42
- **Background:** Hotel housekeeper at the Belmont since 1989. Immigrant from Taiwan, meticulous worker.
- **Role in case:** Noticed a "chemical smell, like medicine or cleaning fluid but stronger" in Room 416 on October 1, 1993 — sixteen days before the fire. She reported it to Ray Hicks. This smell was likely from Tate's early attempt to stage the scene or test the accelerant.
- **Personality:** Quiet, observant, precise memory. Frustrated that no one took her report seriously.
- **Current (2024):** Age 73, retired, living in Charleston with her daughter.

### Dr. Julian Hart
- **Age in 1993:** N/A (not involved until re-analysis)
- **Background:** Toxicologist at the Medical University of South Carolina. Specialist in post-mortem chemical analysis.
- **Role in case:** In 2024, at Voss's request (facilitated by Cold Case Club), re-examined preserved tissue samples from Webb's autopsy. Found clear evidence of chloral hydrate — a sedative that would have rendered Webb unconscious within 30-45 minutes of ingestion. The original autopsy never tested for it.
- **Personality:** Clinical, precise, quietly outraged that the original ME didn't run a full tox panel.
- **Current (2024):** Active faculty at MUSC, age 58.

### Supporting Characters

**Thomas Pruitt** — Waiter at the Charleston Yacht Club. Age 22 in 1993. Served Tate's table at the gala. Noticed Tate was gone from approximately 9:45 to 10:30 PM. Told Detective Voss in December 1993.

**Martin Hale / "David Crane"** — Journalist for the Charleston City Paper, age 37 in 1993. Checked into Room 418 under a fake name on October 17 to investigate zoning corruption involving the hotel. Pure coincidence. Red herring.

**Dr. Franklin Hayes** — County medical examiner who conducted the original autopsy. Did not order a sedative screen. Retired 1998.

**Fire Commissioner Lloyd Denham** — Friend of the Tate family. Pressured Chief Briggs to classify the fire as accidental.

**Captain Arthur Sykes** — Voss's superior at Charleston PD. Overruled her recommendation to keep the case open.

## SOLUTION SUMMARY

**Who:** Gerald Tate, Marcus Webb's business partner of 22 years.

**Why:** Webb discovered that Tate had been laundering approximately $1.4 million through the hotel's financial accounts over 8 years using fictitious vendor companies that routed payments to Tate's offshore accounts in the Cayman Islands. Webb had compiled documentation and was preparing to report Tate to the IRS. Tate also stood to collect $2.3 million in insurance money from the fire — a policy he had recently inflated and co-signed with a forged signature.

**How:** On October 17, 1993, Tate dosed Webb's personal bourbon decanter in Room 416 with chloral hydrate, a powerful sedative. He then attended the Charleston Yacht Club Autumn Gala to establish his alibi. At 9:45 PM, he left the gala through the kitchen service entrance, drove twelve minutes to the Belmont Hotel, entered through the rear service door using his master key, and found Webb unconscious in Room 416. He staged the scene with a lit cigarette, set fires at two separate origin points using a petroleum-based accelerant, and drove back to the gala, re-entering through the kitchen by 10:30 PM. The fire destroyed Room 416 and the financial evidence stored there.

**Cover-up:** Tate's family connections in Charleston society insulated him from investigation. The fire commissioner pressured Chief Briggs to rule the fire accidental. Detective Voss's recommendation to keep the case open was overruled by her captain. The financial documents held by attorney Samuel Cross were tied up in litigation by Tate's lawyers.

## PRODUCTION NOTES

- All documents should feel era-appropriate for 1993 (typewriter fonts for police reports, dot-matrix for computer printouts, handwritten notes in cursive)
- Charleston details: reference King Street, the Battery, Sullivan's Island, Broad Street, specific Charleston landmarks
- Weather on October 17, 1993: Clear, 68 degrees F, low humidity
- The Belmont Hotel is fictional but modeled on Charleston's grand hotels (Francis Marion, Mills House)
- Currency and prices should reflect 1993 values
- Phone numbers use 803 area code (Charleston was 803 until 1998)
- Technology level: no cell phones (pagers only for professionals), no email, no internet for civilians, fax machines, landlines
- Music at the Belmont lounge: jazz standards, Sinatra, Ella Fitzgerald

## TONE AND STYLE GUIDE

- **Police reports:** Clipped, formal, passive voice. "The body was recovered at approximately 2345 hours."
- **Newspaper articles:** Slightly breathless Southern journalism. Formal but dramatic.
- **Personal testimony:** Conversational, regional dialect hints (not caricature). "I told him something wasn't right about that room."
- **Financial documents:** Dry, technical, lots of numbers and account references.
- **Detective Voss's notes:** Sharp, frustrated, professional with personal asides. "This case stinks and everyone in this building knows it."
- **Gerald Tate's interview:** Smooth, cooperative on the surface, subtly deflecting. "Marcus was my dearest friend. I would have done anything for him."
- **Medical/scientific reports:** Clinical, precise, laden with terminology but accessible.

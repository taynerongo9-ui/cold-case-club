## Evidence Item 7.2 -- Witness Statement: Thomas Pruitt, Waiter

[PRINT FORMAT: Typed witness statement on Charleston PD form. Handwritten signature at bottom. This is the critical alibi-breaking document.]

---

CHARLESTON POLICE DEPARTMENT
WITNESS STATEMENT

**Case:** 93-HOM-0842
**Date:** December 14, 1993
**Witness:** Thomas James Pruitt
**DOB:** 04/08/1971
**Occupation:** Waiter, Charleston Yacht Club (part-time); Student, College of Charleston
**Address:** 88 Coming Street, Apt. 3, Charleston, SC 29403

**Statement taken by:** Det. Lorraine Voss, Badge #2847

*I, Thomas James Pruitt, provide the following statement voluntarily:*

---

I've been working at the Yacht Club as a part-time waiter since August 1992. I work mostly the big events -- galas, regattas, member dinners. I was assigned to serve Tables 3 and 4 at the Autumn Gala on October 17th.

Table 4 was the Mayor's table. Mr. Tate was there with his wife. I know Mr. Tate because he's a regular at the club and he's always polite to the staff -- tips well, remembers your name, asks about your classes. He's the kind of member who makes you feel like a person, not furniture. That's what made that night stick in my memory -- because his behavior was different.

Dinner service for Table 4 started around 8:15. Everything was normal through the main course -- roast duck with fig glaze, if I recall. I served Mrs. Tate the fish option. Mr. Tate had the duck. He ate about half of it, which was unusual -- he's typically a clean-plate guy.

Senator Pinckney gave his speech around 9:00. After that, dessert and coffee were served -- pecan tart with bourbon cream. I was clearing dessert plates from Table 4 at about 9:30 or 9:35.

At that point, Mr. Tate was at the table. His wife was talking to Mrs. Middleton about a garden club event. Mr. Tate seemed distracted -- he kept checking his watch. Not glancing at it casually the way you do when you're wondering about the time. Checking it. Deliberately. Every couple of minutes. Like he was counting down to something.

I also noticed he wasn't drinking much. Mr. Tate usually has two or three scotches at these events. That night, his glass had been sitting half-full for over an hour. He was staying sharp.

I went to the kitchen to drop off the plates and pick up a coffee refill. When I came back to the floor -- maybe five minutes later, so around 9:40 or 9:45 -- Mr. Tate's chair was empty. His napkin was on the seat, not on the table. Mrs. Tate was still there. I didn't think much of it -- people step out for the restroom, to smoke on the veranda, whatever.

But he was gone for a while.

I kept serving the floor. I passed Table 4 several times over the next forty-five minutes. Each time I noticed, his chair was still empty. Mrs. Tate didn't seem worried -- she was talking to the other ladies. But I remember thinking it was a long time to be gone from the Mayor's table. You don't leave the Mayor's table for forty-five minutes at the Autumn Gala. That's not how it's done.

The dancing started around 9:45 or 10:00. I was refilling water glasses and clearing the bar area. I didn't see Mr. Tate on the dance floor or at the bar or on the veranda. I looked, actually -- not because I was suspicious, but because I was curious. He's a visible guy. Big personality, loud laugh. The room felt different without him in it.

Then, sometime around 10:25 or 10:30, I saw him come through the kitchen service corridor. I was picking up a tray of champagne flutes from the kitchen pass-through window, and he came through the swinging door from the back hallway. The door that leads to the rear service area and the parking lot.

He was adjusting his bow tie and his hair was slightly mussed -- not dramatically, but noticeable if you knew how he usually looked. He was walking fast. Not running, but faster than a man who'd just been standing on the veranda for a few minutes. And he was perspiring. His forehead. October evening, 68 degrees, and the man is sweating.

He didn't see me, or if he did, he didn't acknowledge me. He went straight to the bar, ordered a scotch -- Macallan 18, neat, which is his usual -- and drained half of it in one swallow. Then he went back to Table 4. Mrs. Tate said something to him and he smiled and put his hand on her shoulder. Normal couple stuff.

A few minutes later, maybe 10:40, the Post and Courier photographer came around and took a picture of him with the Mayor. Mr. Tate suddenly had his big smile back on. Like a switch. One second he was the man who came through the kitchen door looking like he'd been somewhere he shouldn't have been. The next second he was Gerald Tate, society figure, grinning for the camera with the Mayor of Charleston.

Like a switch.

So he was gone from the table from about 9:40-9:45 until about 10:25-10:30. That's roughly 45 minutes. Maybe a little less, maybe a little more. But I'm confident in the range because I can anchor it to the event schedule -- dessert was at 9:30, dancing started at 9:45, and the photographer came around at about 10:40. Those are fixed points.

I didn't think anything of it at the time. I heard about the hotel fire the next day but didn't connect it to Mr. Tate. Why would I? A man steps out of a party -- so what?

Detective Voss came to the Yacht Club in December asking questions. She was polite, professional, and very specific. She asked about Mr. Tate's movements that night -- not generally, specifically. What time did he leave the table, what time did he return, which door did he use, what did he look like. I told her what I just told you. She asked if I was sure about the times, and I said I was as sure as I can be -- I don't carry a watch, but I know the event schedule and I can place things relative to the speeches and the dancing.

She asked one more thing. She asked if Mr. Tate smelled like anything when he came back. I thought about it and said yes -- I caught a whiff when he passed me at the kitchen door. He smelled like smoke. Not cigarette smoke. Something heavier. Like a campfire or a wood stove.

I have nothing against Mr. Tate. He's always been good to me. I'm just telling you what I saw.

---

*Signed: Thomas J. Pruitt*
*Date: December 14, 1993*

*Witnessed: Det. L. Voss, Badge #2847*

---

[Voss's annotation on a separate note:]
*"Pruitt is credible. No motive to lie. He's a 22-year-old college kid working a side job -- he doesn't move in Tate's circles and has no stake in this. His timeline matches the fire timeline perfectly. Tate left the gala at 9:45. Fire alarm at 10:12. Tate back at 10:30. And he smelled like smoke. Not cigarette smoke -- 'heavier.' Like a building fire. Everything fits. -- LV"*

*THE MATH:*

*Total absence: ~45 minutes*
*Driving time (round trip): 12 min x 2 = 24 minutes*
*Time at hotel: 45 - 24 = 21 minutes*
*Time needed to enter service door, climb stairs, enter 416, set two fires, exit: ~6-8 minutes*
*Buffer time: 13-15 minutes*

*He had MORE than enough time. He could have driven the speed limit and stopped at a red light and still had time to spare.*

*The security system was disabled BEFORE Tate left the gala. At 9:38 PM, the system went down via phone dial-in. Tate was still at the table at 9:35 (Pruitt confirmed). He could have called from the Yacht Club's lobby phone, from a payphone on the veranda, or from a phone in the men's lounge. Disabled the system, returned to the table, waited a few minutes, then left.*

*Everything was timed. Everything was planned. This man is methodical.*

*THIS. IS. THE. TIMELINE.*

## Evidence Item 7.5 -- Claire Tate's Gala Observations (Second-Hand Account)

[PRINT FORMAT: Typed investigative note, Voss's supplementary file. Based on an informal conversation, not a formal interview -- Claire Tate declined to give an official statement.]

---

CHARLESTON POLICE DEPARTMENT
INVESTIGATIVE NOTE (Unofficial)

**Case:** 93-HOM-0842
**Date:** January 20, 1994
**Subject:** Informal conversation with Claire Tate
**Officer:** Det. Lorraine Voss, Badge #2847

NOTE: This is not a formal witness statement. Mrs. Tate agreed to speak with me informally at a charity event at the Gibbes Museum but declined to sit for a recorded interview. Her attorney (Beauregard) was not present.

---

I approached Claire Tate at the museum during a cocktail reception. She was gracious but guarded. I asked about the evening of October 17.

She confirmed that she and Gerald arrived at the Yacht Club together at approximately 7:20 PM. She sat at Table 4 and was "having a lovely time" throughout the evening.

When I asked about Gerald's movements, she said: "Gerald stepped out for a bit during the dancing. He said the ballroom was too warm. He does that sometimes -- he's not much of a dancer."

I asked how long he was gone. She said: "I really couldn't say. Fifteen minutes? Twenty? I was talking to Betty Middleton about the spring garden tour. I wasn't watching the clock."

I noted that other witnesses placed his absence closer to forty-five minutes. She looked at me with an expression I can only describe as practiced blankness and said: "I'm sure you have better sources than my memory, Detective."

I asked if Gerald seemed different when he returned. She said: "He seemed fine. Perfectly normal. He ordered a drink and sat down."

I asked about the tuxedo -- specifically, the smoke smell. She said: "There was a fire in the fireplace at the club that evening. Gerald stood near it."

I told her the Grand Ballroom does not have a fireplace. She paused, then said: "Then I must be thinking of another evening. Gerald and I attend so many events. They blur together."

She excused herself shortly after.

---

*My assessment: Claire Tate is not stupid. She knows something is wrong. She may not know exactly what Gerald did, but she knows his absence was longer than fifteen minutes and she knows the smoke smell doesn't have an innocent explanation. She is protecting her husband -- instinctively, habitually, the way she was raised to do. I don't think she's a co-conspirator. I think she's a woman who has been trained her entire life not to ask questions, and the one time it mattered, she didn't.*

*-- LV*

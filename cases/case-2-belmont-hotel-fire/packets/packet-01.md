## Evidence Item 1.1 -- Cold Case Club Case Reopening Letter

[PRINT FORMAT: Typed on Cold Case Club letterhead. Modern paper stock, clean sans-serif font (Inter). This is the only modern document -- everything else in the case is period-appropriate. Gold (#c9a84c) accent line at top of page.]

---

COLD CASE CLUB
Case File #002
Classification: REOPENED

Dear Detective,

Welcome to your second case.

On the night of October 17, 1993, a fire tore through the fourth floor of the Belmont Hotel in Charleston, South Carolina. By morning, the hotel's co-owner, Marcus Webb, was dead. The Charleston Fire Department ruled it an accident -- a man who fell asleep with a lit cigarette.

The case was closed in five weeks.

For thirty years, that ruling has stood. But one detective never believed it. Lorraine Voss spent her career haunted by the Belmont fire -- the evidence that didn't fit, the questions no one wanted answered, the pressure from powerful people who wanted the case to go away.

Detective Voss has turned over her complete case files to us. We've also obtained the original fire investigation report, sealed court documents, financial records, and witness statements that were never made public.

Over the next six months, you will receive twelve evidence packets containing everything we've gathered. The documents are real. The witnesses are real. The truth has been buried for three decades.

Your task: examine the evidence, follow the clues, and determine what really happened in Room 416 of the Belmont Hotel.

Was it an accident? Or did someone want Marcus Webb dead?

The answer is in these pages. Find it.

Good luck,
Cold Case Club
Case Division

P.S. -- A note about the evidence you'll receive: these packets contain reproductions of original documents, including police reports, fire department records, newspaper clippings, personal testimony, and forensic analyses. Some documents show signs of age, redaction, or damage consistent with their history. This is intentional. Real cases don't come in neat packages.

A TRADITION OF ELEGANCE

Since 1889, the Belmont Hotel has stood as a jewel of Charleston hospitality. Originally built by cotton merchant Josiah Belmont as a grand lodging house for visiting dignitaries, the hotel has welcomed governors, senators, artists, and travelers from around the world to its storied halls.

The Belmont survived the great earthquake of 1886 -- or rather, its predecessor did. Josiah Belmont rebuilt on the same foundation, incorporating steel reinforcement into the brick and timber design, ensuring that the new Belmont would stand for centuries to come. The building that stands today is a testament to that vision: five stories of Charleston grandeur, with heart pine floors, eighteen-foot ceilings, and wraparound piazzas that overlook King Street and the rooftops of the Holy City.

After a period of decline in the mid-twentieth century, the Belmont was lovingly restored beginning in 1978 by partners Marcus Webb and Gerald Tate, who returned the hotel to its former glory while preserving its historic character. The restoration was featured in Southern Living and Historic Preservation Magazine, and the Belmont was listed on the National Register of Historic Places in 1984.

Today, the Belmont offers 72 elegantly appointed rooms and suites, each reflecting the grace and charm of the Lowcountry.

## Evidence Item 1.4 -- Detective Lorraine Voss's Introductory Note

[PRINT FORMAT: Handwritten on lined yellow legal pad paper. Neat but slightly hurried cursive. Blue ballpoint pen. Paper should look aged -- slightly wrinkled, edges softened. A few words are underlined for emphasis. Three pages.]

---

To whoever is reading this --

My name is Lorraine Voss. I was a detective with the Charleston Police Department for twenty-six years. I worked homicides, assaults, fraud -- the worst of what people do to each other. I've seen plenty of cases go sideways. That's the job.

But there is one case I have carried with me every single day since the fall of 1993.

Marcus Webb did not die by accident. I knew it then, and I know it now. The evidence was there from the start -- the fire, the cigarette that shouldn't have been there, the money, the partner, the pressure to close the case before anyone looked too hard. I wrote it all up. I filed the reports. I made my recommendations.

And they shut me down.

I was told the case was closed. I was told to move on. When I didn't, I was reassigned. They took the Belmont fire away from me, but they couldn't take what I knew. I kept my own files. Every interview, every note, every document I could copy before they boxed the official case. I kept it all in a filing cabinet in my home office for thirty years.

I'm seventy-two years old now. I've got arthritis in my hands and a bum knee, but my memory is sharp as it ever was. I remember the smell of that fourth floor -- not just smoke, something chemical underneath it, something wrong. I remember the look on Gerald Tate's face when I told him we had questions. Not surprise. Not grief. Calculation. I've interviewed enough suspects to know the difference between a man who has lost his friend and a man who is running numbers in his head, figuring out how much trouble he's in.

I remember how fast the phone calls started -- from the fire commissioner, from city hall, from lawyers with three last names. Within forty-eight hours of Marcus Webb's body being pulled from that room, I had people above me telling me to "be careful" and "consider the bigger picture" and "focus on the evidence as it is, not as you'd like it to be."

The evidence as it is. That's what galls me. The evidence as it is points to murder. It always has. Two separate fire origin points in the same room. Accelerant at both locations. A cigarette placed next to a man who never smoked a day in his life. A security system disabled with an owner-level code thirty-four minutes before the alarm went off. A business partner with $1.4 million in reasons to want Marcus Webb dead and a forty-five-minute window where nobody can account for his whereabouts.

The evidence as it is should have put Gerald Tate in a courtroom.

Instead, the fire commissioner ordered the fire chief to change his report. My captain stamped my supplementary findings "Not Approved for Distribution." The case was classified as accidental on November 5, 1993, eighteen days after a good man was murdered in his own hotel.

Someone didn't want this case investigated. And for thirty years, they got what they wanted.

I'm done with that.

Everything I have is in these files. I've organized it as best I can, but fair warning -- this case is a mess. There are leads that go nowhere and leads that go somewhere important. There are people who lied and people who told the truth and got ignored. There are red herrings and there are smoking guns, and sometimes they look the same until you've stared at them long enough.

You'll need to sort it out. Read everything twice. Trust the physical evidence over the testimony -- people lie, chemistry doesn't. Pay attention to the timeline. Follow the money. And when something doesn't make sense, don't assume it's a coincidence. In this case, the coincidences are the clues.

I believe the answer is here. I always have.

Don't let me down.

-- L. Voss
Detective (Ret.), Charleston PD
Badge #2847

P.S. -- One more thing. If you've already solved Case #1 for this outfit, you might notice a familiar name or two in the Belmont's records. Small world. Pay attention.

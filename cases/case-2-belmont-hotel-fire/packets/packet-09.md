### BACKGROUND

This analysis was requested as part of a re-examination of the circumstances surrounding the death of Marcus Webb in the Belmont Hotel fire of October 17, 1993. The original autopsy, conducted by Dr. Franklin Hayes, determined the cause of death to be smoke inhalation. A blood alcohol concentration of 0.14 g/dL was reported. **No comprehensive toxicological screening for drugs or sedatives was performed.**

This omission is notable. Standard autopsy protocol for fire deaths -- particularly when the victim is found in a position suggesting incapacitation (no evidence of attempted escape) -- calls for a comprehensive toxicological screen including sedatives, hypnotics, and central nervous system depressants. The decision to test only for alcohol was, at minimum, a significant departure from accepted forensic practice.

At the request of Detective Voss (retired) and with authorization from the Charleston County Coroner's Office, preserved tissue and fluid samples from the original autopsy -- maintained in the Coroner's evidence archive per standard retention protocols -- were released to this laboratory for re-analysis.

### METHODOLOGY

Samples were subjected to comprehensive toxicological screening using:
- Immunoassay screening (EMIT/FPIA)
- Gas chromatography-mass spectrometry (GC-MS) for confirmation and quantitation
- Liquid chromatography-tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS) for enhanced sensitivity
- Headspace gas chromatography (for volatiles, including alcohol confirmation)

Analyses targeted over 300 compounds including common drugs, medications, sedatives, hypnotics, opiates, benzodiazepines, barbiturates, amphetamines, and their metabolites.

### INTERPRETATION

**Chloral hydrate** (2,2,2-trichloroacetaldehyde hydrate) is a sedative-hypnotic drug first synthesized in 1832 and historically used as a sleeping agent and procedural sedative. It is rapidly absorbed after oral ingestion and metabolized in the liver and red blood cells to **trichloroethanol (TCE)**, its primary active metabolite, which produces sedation, hypnosis, and respiratory depression. TCE is further metabolized to **trichloroacetic acid (TCA)**, an inactive metabolite excreted renally.

The blood TCE concentration of 38 mcg/mL is **approximately 2.5 to 3 times the upper therapeutic range** (therapeutic: 5-15 mcg/mL; toxic: >20 mcg/mL; lethal: >100 mcg/mL in most reported cases). At this concentration, combined with a blood alcohol level of 0.12 g/dL, the subject would have experienced:

- **Within 15-20 minutes of ingestion:** Drowsiness, impaired coordination, slurred speech, confusion
- **Within 30-45 minutes:** Profound sedation progressing to unconsciousness
- **At peak effect (1-2 hours post-ingestion):** Deep pharmacological unconsciousness with significantly depressed respiratory drive, muscular relaxation rendering ambulation impossible, suppressed pain response and arousal reflexes, and inability to respond to environmental stimuli (including smoke, heat, and pain)

**At this dosage, combined with alcohol, Marcus Webb would have been completely unconscious and physically incapable of self-rescue at the time of the fire.** He could not have awakened, moved, cried out, or attempted to reach the door. He would not have felt the flames.

The simultaneous presence of alcohol and chloral hydrate at these concentrations produces a **synergistic sedative effect** -- the drugs potentiate each other, meaning their combined effect is greater than the sum of their individual effects. This combination has been documented in criminal poisonings since the late 19th century and is historically known as a "**Mickey Finn**" or "knockout drops."

### ROUTE OF ADMINISTRATION

The oral route is consistent with the pharmacokinetic profile observed. Chloral hydrate dissolves readily in ethanol (alcohol) and can be administered in an alcoholic beverage. The drug has a slightly bitter taste and a mildly pungent odor, but these characteristics would be partially masked by the flavor profile of bourbon whiskey, particularly a full-bodied variety such as Maker's Mark.

The metabolite distribution (blood TCE > vitreous TCE > liver TCE) is consistent with oral ingestion approximately 2-4 hours prior to death -- placing administration at approximately 6:00-8:00 PM on October 17, 1993. This timeframe is consistent with witness statements that the victim consumed bourbon in Room 416 during the evening.

### SIGNIFICANCE

The presence of chloral hydrate in the victim's system at supra-therapeutic concentrations, combined with alcohol, establishes that Marcus Webb was chemically incapacitated at the time of the fire. This finding is:

1. **Inconsistent with accidental death.** A man who has been drugged into unconsciousness cannot accidentally start a fire with a cigarette -- particularly a man who does not smoke.

2. **Consistent with deliberate incapacitation.** The dosage suggests intentional administration by a third party with the goal of rendering the victim unconscious and unable to escape.

3. **Consistent with the physical evidence at the scene.** The victim's body position (no evidence of attempted escape, found near the settee rather than near the door or windows) corroborates pharmacological incapacitation.

4. **A critical omission from the original investigation.** The failure of the original autopsy to screen for sedatives is a significant deficiency that fundamentally compromised the investigation. Had this analysis been performed in 1993, the case would almost certainly have been classified as homicide.

### CONCLUSION

Marcus Allen Webb was drugged with chloral hydrate prior to his death. The drug was most likely administered orally, dissolved in an alcoholic beverage. The combination of chloral hydrate and alcohol rendered Webb unconscious and unable to escape the fire.

**This finding, in my professional opinion, changes the manner of death from accidental to homicide.**

## Evidence Item 9.6 -- Chloral Hydrate: What You Need to Know

[PRINT FORMAT: Cold Case Club informational insert. Modern design, clean typography. Educational supplement for subscribers who may not be familiar with the drug.]

---

**COLD CASE CLUB -- EVIDENCE BRIEFING**
*Understanding Chloral Hydrate*

**What is it?** Chloral hydrate is one of the oldest synthetic sedative drugs, first produced in 1832 and widely used as a sleeping aid from the 1870s through the mid-20th century. It was the original "knockout drop" -- the drug slipped into drinks by criminals in the 19th and early 20th centuries.

**How does it work?** Taken orally, chloral hydrate is rapidly absorbed and converted in the body to trichloroethanol (TCE), which depresses the central nervous system, producing drowsiness, sedation, and eventually unconsciousness. When combined with alcohol, the effects are dramatically amplified.

**How fast does it act?** Effects begin within 15-30 minutes of ingestion. Full unconsciousness can occur within 30-60 minutes depending on dose and the presence of alcohol.

**Can the victim taste it?** Chloral hydrate has a slightly bitter taste and a mild chemical odor. In a strong-flavored drink like bourbon, particularly in a crystal decanter where the victim is not examining the drink closely, the taste would be difficult to detect.

**Was it available in 1993?** Yes. While primarily used in veterinary medicine by the 1990s, chloral hydrate was obtainable through veterinary supply channels. It was not a commonly monitored substance in the same way as opiates or amphetamines.

**Why wasn't it detected in 1993?** Because nobody looked for it. The original autopsy tested only for alcohol. A standard toxicology panel in 1993 would have included sedative screening -- but only if ordered by the medical examiner. Dr. Hayes chose not to order it.

**Case:** 93-HOM-0842
**Date:** October 23, 1993 (initial); January 8, 1994 (follow-up)

**DOB:** 11/12/1959
**Occupation:** Night Manager, Belmont Hotel
**Address:** 2214 Rivers Avenue, North Charleston, SC 29405

**Statements taken by:** Det. Lorraine Voss, Badge #2847

**INITIAL STATEMENT — October 23, 1993:**

I've been working at the Belmont since 1986. Started as a bellman, moved up to front desk, then night manager in 1990. I know that hotel inside and out. Every creaking floorboard, every sticky window, every guest who snores loud enough to hear from the hallway. It's my building after sundown.

October 17th was a Sunday. Sundays are usually quiet — maybe half occupancy, light bar traffic. I came on shift at 8:00 PM, took over from Nancy Whitfield on the day desk. She briefed me — normal stuff. Fourth floor had a few guests, Mr. Webb was in 416 as usual, a walk-in had checked into 418 paying cash, which was a little unusual but not unheard of.

I did my first walk-through at 8:15. Lobby, lounge, all five floors, back of house. Everything was normal. Miss Monroe was setting up for her set in the Blue Room. Kitchen was closing down for the night. I checked each floor — mostly just walking the corridors, listening, looking for anything out of place.

Fourth floor was quiet. I didn't knock on 416 — I never bothered Mr. Webb unless he called down. His light was on under the door. I figured he was working late as usual.

Here's the thing that's been eating at me.

Around 8:45 or 9:00 PM, I was at the front desk doing paperwork. I have a small monitor behind the desk that shows the security system status for each floor — green light means active, red means offline. All floors were green when I started my shift.

At 9:38 PM — I know the exact time because I looked at my watch — the fourth-floor indicator went red. Offline.

I should have gone up to check. That's the procedure. If a floor goes offline, you investigate, reset the system, and log the event. But here's what happened: the system on four had been glitchy for about two weeks. It had gone offline three times in October — once on the 3rd, once on the 9th, and once on the 14th. Each time it came back on its own within an hour. The maintenance guy, Randall, said it was probably a wiring issue and he'd look at it the next week.

So when it went red at 9:38, I figured it was the same glitch. I wrote it in the log book and planned to check on it during my next walk-through at 10:30.

I never got to make that walk-through.

At 10:12, the fire alarm went off. Mr. Delacroix from Room 412 was banging on the stairwell door by the time I got to the lobby phone. Smoke was already visible — this thin haze drifting down from the upper floors. I called 911 at 10:14.

I went up to three to start evacuating. The smoke was getting thicker. Got to the fourth-floor landing and I couldn't — it was like a wall. Black smoke, floor to ceiling. The heat was radiating through the stairwell door. I tried to get to 416 but I couldn't get more than ten feet into the corridor. The door to 416 was hot to the touch even from the hallway.

Mr. Webb was in there. I knew he was in there. And I couldn't get to him.

I got the other fourth-floor guests out. The man in 418, "Crane," he didn't want to leave — he was shoving papers into a bag. I practically dragged him down the stairs. Mrs. Sams in 422 was calm, thank God. The Beaumonts in 410 were already on three by the time I got there.

The fire department arrived and took over. I stood on King Street and watched the smoke pour out of the fourth-floor windows and I thought: I should have checked when the system went down. If I'd gone up at 9:38, maybe I would have smelled something, seen something, gotten Mr. Webb out before it was too late.

I will carry that for the rest of my life.

---

**FOLLOW-UP STATEMENT — January 8, 1994:**

Detective Voss came back to talk to me in January. She had more questions about the security system, and she asked about some things I hadn't thought about before.

She asked me about the "glitches" on October 3rd, 9th, and 14th. Whether the system really went offline on its own or whether someone could have been testing the remote access. I hadn't considered that. But she pointed out something that made the hair on the back of my neck stand up: the system only went offline on days when Mr. Tate had visited the hotel. I checked the front desk sign-in logs against the security system log, and she was right.

October 3rd — Tate signed in at 2:00 PM. System went offline at 2:18 PM.
October 9th — Tate signed in at 4:30 PM. System went offline at 5:02 PM.
October 14th — Tate signed in at 11:00 AM. System went offline at 11:34 AM.
October 17th — Tate signed in at 3:15 PM (left at 4:50 PM). System went offline at 9:38 PM.

The first three times, the system came back on within an hour. Like someone was testing it. Seeing if it would be noticed. Seeing how the staff would react.

The fourth time, it stayed off.

Detective Voss asked me who knew the remote dial-in access code for the security system. I told her: Mr. Webb and Mr. Tate. That's it. The system was installed in 1989 and the vendor set up a phone-dial interface so the owners could check on things remotely or disable the system for maintenance. The access code was a six-digit number. I didn't have it. Nancy didn't have it. Only the owners.

She asked me one more thing. She asked about the night I was fired.

I was fired in March of '93 by Mr. Tate. It was over a delivery. Late one night — maybe 1:00 AM — a van pulled up to the service entrance. Two men started unloading boxes into the ground-floor storage room. I went to check because we weren't expecting any deliveries. Mr. Tate was there supervising. He told me to go back to the desk. I said I needed to log the delivery per procedure. He said it was a "private arrangement" and to mind my own business.

I pushed back. I said I couldn't have unlogged deliveries coming into the building on my shift. He got angry. Real angry. Colder than I've ever seen him. The next day, I got a termination letter — "insubordination."

Mr. Webb brought me back in June. He didn't know why I'd been fired — Tate had told him I'd been let go for "performance issues." When I told Mr. Webb the real reason, he got quiet. Real quiet. He said he'd "look into it."

I think he did look into it. And I think that's what got him killed.

---

*Signed: Raymond E. Hicks*
*Date: January 8, 1994*

*Witnessed: Det. L. Voss, Badge #2847*

**NOTES FROM SENTRYGUARD TECHNICIAN (R. Coleman):**

The dial-in access feature allows authorized users to arm or disarm individual floor zones via touch-tone telephone. The caller dials the system's dedicated phone line, enters a six-digit authorization code, and uses menu prompts to select floor and action. The system does not record the originating phone number of dial-in calls — it only logs the authorization level (OWNER, MANAGER, MAINTENANCE) and the method (DIAL-IN, KEYPAD, SCHEDULE).

There are two OWNER-level codes in this system. They were set at installation in 1989 and, per our records, have never been changed. The codes are assigned to:

- Owner Code 1: Marcus A. Webb
- Owner Code 2: Gerald R. Tate

The system cannot distinguish which owner code was used — both register as "OWNER" in the log.

**KEY POINT:** When Floor Zone 4 is disarmed, the smoke detectors on that floor do NOT send alerts to the central panel. The standalone fire alarm system (manual pull stations) is a SEPARATE system and remains active regardless of security zone status. This is why the fire was detected by a guest pulling the manual alarm at 10:12 PM, NOT by the electronic smoke detectors.

If the security system had been active, the smoke detectors in Room 416 would have triggered an alert at the front desk minutes before the fire became visible — potentially early enough for intervention.

**INITIAL STATEMENT -- October 23, 1993:**

I've been working at the Belmont since 1986. Started as a bellman, moved up to front desk, then night manager in 1990. I know that hotel inside and out. Every creaking floorboard, every sticky window, every guest who snores loud enough to hear from the hallway. It's my building after sundown. That's how I think of it -- during the day it belongs to the guests and the managers and the owners. But after eight PM, it's mine. I'm the one who walks the halls, checks the locks, listens for trouble. I'm the one who makes sure everyone sleeps safe.

October 17th was a Sunday. Sundays are usually quiet -- maybe half occupancy, light bar traffic. I came on shift at 8:00 PM, took over from Nancy Whitfield on the day desk. She briefed me -- normal stuff. Fourth floor had a few guests, Mr. Webb was in 416 as usual, a walk-in had checked into 418 paying cash, which was a little unusual but not unheard of. Mrs. Sams in 422 was attending some conference. The Beaumonts in 410 were celebrating their anniversary.

I did my first walk-through at 8:15. Lobby, lounge, all five floors, back of house. Everything was normal. Miss Monroe was setting up for her set in the Blue Room -- I stopped and listened for a minute because she was warming up with "Autumn Leaves" and her voice just fills that room like nothing else. Kitchen was closing down for the night. Chef Ravenel's assistant was wrapping up.

I checked each floor -- mostly just walking the corridors, listening, looking for anything out of place. Floors two and three were quiet. Fifth floor -- the penthouse was empty, terrace was closed for the season.

Fourth floor was quiet. I didn't knock on 416 -- I never bothered Mr. Webb unless he called down. His light was on under the door. I could hear music, faintly -- jazz, as usual. I figured he was working late, same as always. I moved on.

Here's the thing that's been eating at me.

Around 8:45 or 9:00 PM, I was at the front desk doing paperwork -- occupancy reports, next day's checkout list, that kind of thing. I have a small monitor behind the desk that shows the security system status for each floor -- green light means active, red means offline. All floors were green when I started my shift.

At 9:38 PM -- I know the exact time because I looked at my watch when I noticed -- the fourth-floor indicator went red. Offline.

I should have gone up to check. That's the procedure. If a floor goes offline, you investigate, reset the system, and log the event. But here's what happened: the system on four had been glitchy for about two weeks. It had gone offline three times in October -- once on the 3rd, once on the 9th, and once on the 14th. Each time it came back on its own within an hour. The maintenance guy, Randall, said it was probably a wiring issue and he'd look at it the next week. He never did.

So when it went red at 9:38, I thought: another glitch. Same thing as the last three times. It'll come back on its own. I'll check it on my next walk-through at 10:30. I wrote it in the log book: "Floor 4 security offline at 9:38 PM. Probable equipment malfunction consistent with prior occurrences. Will monitor."

I went back to my paperwork.

That decision will haunt me until the day I die.

Thirty-four minutes later, at 10:12, the fire alarm went off. Not the electronic security system -- the manual pull station alarm, which is a separate system. Mr. Delacroix from Room 412 was banging on the stairwell door yelling "Fire!" by the time I got to the lobby phone.

Smoke was already visible -- this thin haze drifting down from the upper floors, hugging the ceiling like fog on the harbor. Within a minute, it was thicker. The smell hit me -- not just smoke. Something chemical. Sharp. Like when you light a charcoal grill with too much starter fluid.

I called 911 at 10:14. Told them about the fire, about Mr. Webb in 416, about the security system being down. The dispatcher told me to get out. I didn't want to get out. I wanted to go up those stairs and get Mr. Webb.

I went up to three. The smoke was banking down the stairwell, getting worse with each floor. Got to the fourth-floor landing and I couldn't -- it was like hitting a wall. Black smoke, floor to ceiling, so thick I couldn't see my hand in front of my face. The heat was radiating through the stairwell door. I put my hand on the door handle and jerked it back -- hot enough to blister.

I tried to push through. I got about ten feet into the corridor before the heat drove me back. My eyes were burning, I couldn't breathe, the smoke was like something alive trying to push me down. I could see an orange glow from the direction of 416. Not just smoke -- light. The fire was throwing light into the hallway.

Mr. Webb was in there. I knew he was in there. Sixty feet away from me. And I couldn't get to him.

I got the other fourth-floor guests out. The man in 418, "Crane," he didn't want to leave -- he was shoving papers into a bag like whatever he was working on was more important than his life. I grabbed his arm and physically pulled him. He fought me for a second, then the smoke hit him and he came. Mrs. Sams in 422 was calm -- she had her shoes on and her purse and she walked down those stairs like she was leaving a church service. God bless her. The Beaumonts in 410 were already on three by the time I got there.

The fire department arrived and took over. I stood on King Street and watched the smoke pour out of the fourth-floor windows. The flames were visible -- orange behind the glass, flickering, alive. I thought about Mr. Webb's watch collection on his desk. His jazz records. His reading glasses. The bourbon decanter that was always there, catching the light.

And I thought: if I had gone upstairs at 9:38, when that light went red, I might have smelled the accelerant before it was lit. I might have found the decanter tampered with. I might have knocked on 416 and found Mr. Webb already unconscious and dragged him out.

I didn't go upstairs. I wrote it in the logbook and went back to my paperwork.

I will carry that for the rest of my life.

---

**FOLLOW-UP STATEMENT -- January 8, 1994:**

Detective Voss came back to talk to me in January. She had more questions about the security system, and she asked about some things I hadn't thought about before.

She asked me about the "glitches" on October 3rd, 9th, and 14th. Whether the system really went offline on its own or whether someone could have been testing the remote access. I hadn't considered that. But she pointed out something that made the hair on the back of my neck stand up: the system only went offline on days when Mr. Tate had visited the hotel. I checked the front desk sign-in logs against the security system log, and she was right.

October 3rd -- Tate signed in at 2:00 PM. System went offline at 2:18 PM. Duration: 36 minutes.
October 9th -- Tate signed in at 4:30 PM. System went offline at 5:02 PM. Duration: 45 minutes.
October 14th -- Tate signed in at 11:00 AM. System went offline at 11:34 AM. Duration: 47 minutes.
October 17th -- Tate signed in at 3:15 PM (left at 4:50 PM). System went offline at 9:38 PM. Duration: never re-armed.

The first three times, the system came back on within an hour. Like someone was testing it. Seeing if it would be noticed. Seeing how the staff would react. And each test ran a little longer -- 36 minutes, then 45, then 47. Like someone was pushing the limits, finding out exactly how long they could keep it offline before anyone got worried enough to investigate.

And I didn't investigate. Not once. I logged it as a glitch and went back to my desk. I was the test subject, and I failed.

The fourth time, it stayed off. Because this time it wasn't a test. It was the real thing.

Detective Voss asked me who knew the remote dial-in access code for the security system. I told her: Mr. Webb and Mr. Tate. That's it. The system was installed in 1989 and the vendor, SentryGuard, set up a phone-dial interface so the owners could check on things remotely or disable the system for maintenance. The access code was a six-digit number. I didn't have it. Nancy didn't have it. Only the owners.

She asked me one more thing. She asked about the night I was fired.

I was fired in March of '93 by Mr. Tate. It was over a delivery. Late one night -- maybe 1:00 AM, February or March, I don't remember exactly -- a van pulled up to the service entrance. Two men started unloading boxes into the ground-floor storage room. Brown cardboard boxes, no labels, no shipping marks. Maybe twenty of them.

I went to check because we weren't expecting any deliveries. Mr. Tate was there supervising. He was in regular clothes, not a suit -- unusual for him to be at the hotel that late, and unusual for him to be dressed down. He told me to go back to the desk. I said I needed to log the delivery per procedure -- every delivery gets logged, time in, time out, contents, vendor name. Hotel policy. His hotel's policy.

He got angry. Real angry. Colder than I've ever seen him. His face didn't change -- he still had that polished look, that society face -- but his eyes went flat. Dead flat. He said it was a "private arrangement" and to mind my own business. I said I couldn't have unlogged deliveries coming into the building on my shift -- my name is on the shift log, I'm responsible for everything that happens in this building between 8 PM and 8 AM.

He looked at me for a long time. Then he said, very quietly, "Mr. Hicks, I suggest you remember who signs your paycheck."

The next day, I got a termination letter. "Insubordination."

Mr. Webb brought me back in June. He didn't know the real reason I'd been fired -- Tate had told him I'd been let go for "performance issues." When I told Mr. Webb what happened -- the van, the boxes, Tate's reaction -- he got quiet. Real quiet. He sat behind his desk in Room 416 and rubbed his face with both hands. Then he said, "I'm going to look into this, Ray. And I'm sorry."

I think he did look into it. I think what he found in those books started with what was in those boxes. And I think that's what got him killed.

---

*Signed: Raymond E. Hicks*
*Date: January 8, 1994*

*Witnessed: Det. L. Voss, Badge #2847*

**NOTES FROM SENTRYGUARD TECHNICIAN (R. Coleman):**

The dial-in access feature allows authorized users to arm or disarm individual floor zones via touch-tone telephone. The caller dials the system's dedicated phone line ((803) 555-0416), enters a six-digit authorization code, and uses menu prompts to select floor and action.

The system does not record the originating phone number of dial-in calls -- it only logs the authorization level (OWNER, MANAGER, MAINTENANCE) and the method (DIAL-IN, KEYPAD, SCHEDULE).

There are two OWNER-level codes in this system. They were set at installation in 1989 and, per our records, have never been changed. The codes are assigned to:

- Owner Code 1: Marcus A. Webb
- Owner Code 2: Gerald R. Tate

The system cannot distinguish which owner code was used -- both register as "OWNER" in the log.

The dial-in process takes approximately 45-60 seconds from initial call connection to completed action (arm/disarm). The caller hears automated prompts and responds with touch-tone inputs.

---

[PUZZLE NOTE: The security system's dial-in phone number is (803) 555-0416. The "0416" suffix matches Room 416 -- an intentional choice by the hotel when the system was installed. The offline durations (35:45, 44:37, 46:37) increase by approximately 9-11 minutes each time, suggesting the perpetrator was growing more confident that no one would respond. The final test (46:37) is almost exactly the duration between the 9:38 PM disable time and the 10:12 PM fire alarm -- as if the perpetrator was calibrating how long he needed the system offline to execute his plan and have the fire discovered "naturally" by a guest rather than by an electronic alarm.]

**KEY POINT:** When Floor Zone 4 is disarmed, the electronic smoke detectors on that floor do NOT send alerts to the central panel or the front desk. The standalone fire alarm system (manual pull stations + heat detectors) is a SEPARATE system and remains active regardless of security zone status.

This is why the fire was detected by a guest pulling the manual alarm at 10:12 PM, NOT by the electronic smoke detectors. If the security system had been active, the smoke detectors in Room 416 would have triggered an alert at the front desk within 2-3 minutes of fire initiation -- potentially 5-7 minutes before the fire became visible in the corridor.

Those 5-7 minutes might have been enough time to save Marcus Webb's life. If he had been conscious.

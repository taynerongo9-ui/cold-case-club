*Case #002 — THE BELMONT HOTEL FIRE*

# THE SOLUTION

## WHO KILLED MARCUS WEBB?

**Gerald Robert Tate** — Marcus Webb's business partner of twenty-two years, co-owner of the Belmont Hotel, and one of Charleston's most prominent citizens — murdered Marcus Webb on the night of October 17, 1993.

He was never charged. He was never arrested. He has lived freely for over thirty years.

This is the complete account of what happened and why.

---

## THE MOTIVE

Since 1985, Gerald Tate had been systematically embezzling from the Belmont Hotel. Using his exclusive control over the hotel's finances — a power granted to him by the partnership agreement and sustained by Marcus Webb's trust — Tate created fictitious vendor companies and billed the hotel for services that were never provided.

The primary shell companies were:
- **Coastal Textile Services** — billed monthly for "supplemental linens" and "specialty cleaning"
- **Lowcountry Consulting Group** — billed quarterly for "strategic advisory" and "market analysis"

The payments were funneled through a domestic pass-through account at Charleston First National Bank, then wired quarterly to an offshore account at First Cayman International Bank — account OJL-6681-PPK, held in the name of Coastal Expenses Service, Ltd., a Cayman Islands shell company controlled solely by Tate.

Over eight years, Tate laundered approximately **$1,412,000** through this scheme.

In March 1993, a routine accounting review flagged irregularities in the vendor payments. Marcus Webb, despite having trusted Tate with the finances for fifteen years, began his own investigation. By July, with the help of attorney Samuel Cross, Webb had compiled comprehensive documentation of the fraud and was preparing to report it to the Internal Revenue Service.

Webb confronted Tate in August 1993. Tate denied everything and became, in Webb's words, "cold and menacing."

At that point, Tate began to plan.

In September, he increased the hotel's property insurance from $800,000 to $2,300,000, forging Webb's signature on the consent form required by the partnership agreement. This would serve a dual purpose: destroying the evidence stored in Room 416 would protect Tate from criminal prosecution, and the fire would generate a massive insurance payout.

Marcus Webb had to die. And the fire had to destroy Room 416.

---

## THE METHOD

### Preparation (October 1-16, 1993)

**The sedative:** On October 10, Tate obtained 500 milliliters of chloral hydrate solution through Dr. Eugene Flagg, a disgraced veterinarian in Savannah, Georgia, who purchased it from Coastal Veterinary Supply under the pretense of large animal sedation. Flagg's attorney was later revealed to be from the same firm that represents Tate — Beauregard, Simms & Hayne.

**Testing the delivery method:** On or around October 1, Tate visited Room 416 and likely tested the chloral hydrate by adding a small amount to Webb's bourbon decanter. Hotel housekeeper Eva Chen noticed an unusual chemical smell near the decanter during her cleaning that day and reported it. The report was logged but not investigated.

**Testing the security system:** On October 3, 9, and 14, Tate remotely disabled the Belmont's fourth-floor security system using the dial-in phone interface and his owner access code. Each time, he re-enabled the system within an hour. These were dry runs — testing the process, testing whether staff would investigate (they didn't — night manager Ray Hicks logged the outages as "glitches"), and establishing a pattern of "malfunctions" that would make the October 17 outage seem routine.

**The alibi:** Tate ensured he and Claire had prominent seats at the Charleston Yacht Club Autumn Gala on October 17 — Table 4, with the Mayor, a judge, and other society figures. Two hundred and fourteen witnesses would place him across town.

### Execution (October 17, 1993)

**3:15 PM** — Tate arrives at the Belmont. He signs in at the front desk, citing "monthly accounts." He visits Room 416 and doses Webb's bourbon decanter with a concentrated amount of chloral hydrate. Housekeeper Eva Chen sees him near Room 416 at approximately 3:40 PM.

**4:50 PM** — Tate departs the hotel.

**6:00 PM** — Marcus Webb arrives at the Belmont and goes to Room 416 to work, as was his habit.

**6:30 PM** — Tate returns to the hotel "to drop something off." He goes to Room 416 and shares bourbon with Webb from the dosed decanter. This ensures Webb drinks the tainted bourbon and provides Tate a final confirmation of the dosing. Webb pours the drinks — Tate likely avoids drinking from the decanter or takes only a minimal sip.

**6:45 PM** — Tate leaves. The chloral hydrate begins to take effect. Combined with the bourbon, the sedative would render Webb profoundly unconscious within 30 to 45 minutes. By 7:30 PM at the latest, Webb is incapacitated. By 9:00 PM, he is in a deep pharmacological unconsciousness from which he cannot be roused.

**7:25 PM** — Tate arrives at the Yacht Club gala with Claire. They are seated at Table 4. Tate is social, visible, present. Building his alibi.

**9:38 PM** — Tate remotely disables the fourth-floor security system via the dial-in phone interface. He may have used a phone at the Yacht Club or stepped out briefly to use a nearby payphone. The security system goes offline. Night manager Hicks notes the red indicator light but assumes it's the recurring "glitch."

**9:45 PM** — Tate excuses himself from the gala table. Waiter Thomas Pruitt observes his departure. Tate exits through the kitchen service entrance — a back corridor used by staff, not visible from the ballroom.

**9:45-9:57 PM** — Tate drives from the Yacht Club to the Belmont Hotel. The route is 4.2 miles via Lockwood Boulevard and King Street. Drive time: approximately 12 minutes. He likely used a second vehicle staged near the Yacht Club — his own Mercedes remained in the valet lot all evening.

**9:57 PM** — Tate arrives at the Belmont via the rear service alley on the south side of the building. He enters through the ground-floor service door using his master key.

**10:00 PM** — Tate takes the service stairs to the fourth floor. The security system is offline — no door sensors, no motion detectors, no smoke alerts. He enters Room 416. Marcus Webb is unconscious on the settee, exactly as Tate planned.

Tate places a half-smoked Marlboro Red cigarette near Webb's right hand — a prop to support the "fell asleep smoking" narrative. He then sets two fires:

- **Origin Point A:** Base of the window curtains on the east wall. Accelerant (petroleum-based lighter fluid) is applied to the curtain fabric and the carpet below.
- **Origin Point B:** Entry closet on the west wall, near the hallway door. Accelerant applied to stored linens and the closet interior.

Two origin points serve two purposes: they ensure the room is thoroughly destroyed (protecting Tate's financial evidence from recovery) and they trap anyone who might attempt to enter from the hallway (the closet fire blocks the door).

**10:03 PM** — Tate exits Room 416 and descends the service stairs. He leaves through the service entrance and drives back to the Yacht Club.

**10:12 PM** — Guest Robert Delacroix in Room 412 sees smoke in the hallway and pulls the manual fire alarm. The electronic smoke detectors on Floor 4, which would have activated minutes earlier, are offline.

**10:14 PM** — Ray Hicks calls 911.

**10:22 PM** — Engine Company 7 arrives.

**10:25-10:30 PM** — Tate re-enters the Yacht Club through the kitchen service corridor. Waiter Thomas Pruitt sees him come through the swinging door, adjusting his bow tie. Tate goes to the bar, orders a scotch, and returns to Table 4.

**10:42 PM** — Tate is photographed with Mayor Dalton. Smiling.

**11:15 PM** — The fire is contained. Room 416 is destroyed.

**11:45 PM** — Marcus Webb's body is recovered. He never woke up. The chloral hydrate and alcohol ensured he felt nothing.

That is the one mercy in this story.

## THE COVER-UP

Gerald Tate did not act alone in the aftermath — not in the criminal sense, but in the systemic sense. Charleston's power structure closed ranks around one of its own.

- **Fire Commissioner Lloyd Denham**, a personal friend of the Tate family, ordered Chief Warren Briggs to reclassify the fire from "suspicious" to "accidental" on November 3, 1993 — despite Briggs's report clearly documenting two fire origin points, accelerant evidence, and a staged cigarette.

- **Captain Arthur Sykes** of the Charleston PD overruled Detective Lorraine Voss's recommendation to keep the case open, stamped her supplementary report "Not Approved for Distribution," and reassigned her in February 1994.

- **Tate's attorneys** at Beauregard, Simms & Hayne filed injunctions that prevented Samuel Cross from submitting Webb's financial evidence to the IRS or the U.S. Attorney. The litigation continued until 1999, when the statute of limitations on the tax fraud expired.

- **Dr. Franklin Hayes**, the county medical examiner, did not order a toxicology screen for sedatives — a significant departure from standard autopsy protocol for fire deaths. Whether this was incompetence or something worse has never been determined.

No one was bribed. No one was explicitly threatened. The system simply worked the way it was designed to work when a powerful family was involved. Phone calls were made. Expectations were communicated. Careers were quietly protected or quietly endangered. And a murderer walked free.

## Evidence Item 12.2 — Aftermath: What Happened to Everyone

### WHERE ARE THEY NOW?

**Gerald Tate** — Age 83. Lives on Kiawah Island, South Carolina, with his wife Claire. Retired in 1997 after selling the Belmont Hotel (which was repaired and reopened in 1994 under his sole ownership). Never charged with any crime. His offshore accounts were never seized — the statute of limitations protected him. He remains a member of the Charleston Yacht Club, the Gibbes Museum Advisory Board, and St. Michael's Episcopal Church. He has never publicly addressed the allegations.

**Patricia Webb** — Age 85. Lives in Savannah, Georgia. She received Marcus's estate, including the proceeds from the eventual sale of his 50% share of the Belmont (purchased by Tate at a below-market price through a protracted legal negotiation). She has always maintained that Marcus was murdered. She gave her consent for Samuel Cross to release the sealed financial documents in 2024. "I waited thirty years for someone to care," she said. "I'm glad it finally happened, even if it's too late for justice."

**Detective Lorraine Voss** — Age 72. Retired in 2008 after 26 years with the Charleston PD. Lives in Mount Pleasant, South Carolina. She kept her shadow case file on the Belmont fire for thirty years — every report, every interview, every note, stored in a filing cabinet in her home office. When Cold Case Club contacted her in 2023, she said: "I've been waiting for this call my whole life." She provided full access to her files and participated in the 2024 toxicology re-analysis request. "Marcus deserved better from this city. I hope he knows someone finally told the truth."

**Raymond "Ray" Hicks** — Age 65. Retired. Lives in North Charleston. Left the hotel industry in 1995, unable to return to work in a hotel after the fire. Became a maintenance supervisor for the Charleston County School District. Still carries guilt about not investigating the security system outage at 9:38 PM. "If I'd gone up those stairs when the light went red, maybe Marcus would still be alive. I think about that every day."

**Delilah Monroe** — Age 60. Still in Charleston. Music teacher at the Charleston County School of the Arts. Still performs Friday nights at a jazz club on Upper King Street. She sings "Someone to Watch Over Me" every set — it was Marcus's favorite. "He watched over me when I had nobody. I sing for him now."

**Fire Chief Warren Briggs** — Deceased, 2019. Age 82. Retired from the Charleston Fire Department in 1998. He never publicly recanted the official accidental ruling, but his original unedited report — preserved in Voss's shadow file — speaks for itself. His daughter, Margaret Briggs-Collins, told Cold Case Club: "The Belmont case ate at my father for the rest of his life. He knew what happened. He knew what he was forced to do. I think it took years off him."

**Claire Tate** — Age 80. Still on Kiawah Island. Still married to Gerald. She never learned the significance of the dry cleaning receipt. When shown the evidence in 2024 as part of this investigation, she declined to comment through her attorney.

**Samuel Cross** — Age 92. Retired attorney. Still lives in Charleston. Released the sealed financial documents in 2024 with Patricia Webb's consent. His cover letter to Detective Voss is included in Packet 11. He has stated publicly: "Marcus Webb trusted the system to protect him, and the system failed. I trusted the legal process to deliver justice, and it was used against us. This case is a testament to what happens when powerful people are allowed to operate above the law."

**Eva Chen** — Age 73. Retired. Lives in Charleston with her daughter, Linda. She remembers the chemical smell in Room 416 as clearly as if it were yesterday. "I told someone. I told Mr. Hicks. Nobody did anything. If they had listened to the housekeeper, maybe they would have found the poison before it was too late."

**Dr. Julian Hart** — Age 58. Active faculty at MUSC. His 2024 toxicology re-analysis is the single most important piece of new evidence in this case. "The original autopsy was deficient. There's no polite way to say it. A comprehensive toxicology screen is standard protocol for fire deaths. If it had been done in 1993, this case would have been classified as a homicide from day one."

**Thomas Pruitt** — Age 53. Former waiter at the Charleston Yacht Club. Now a high school history teacher in Mount Pleasant. He gave his statement to Voss in December 1993 and was never contacted again by law enforcement. "I just told the truth about what I saw. I didn't realize until years later how important it was."

**Martin Hale / "David Crane"** — Age 68. Retired journalist. His zoning corruption story was published in December 1993. He had no connection to the fire. "Wrong place, wrong time. I feel terrible that my being there might have distracted from the real investigation, even for a moment."

## Evidence Item 12.3 — Detective Voss's Final Letter

[PRINT FORMAT: Handwritten on yellow legal pad paper, consistent with her notes throughout the case. This is her farewell to the subscriber. Emotional but restrained — Voss doesn't cry on the page, but you feel the weight of thirty years.]

---

To the detective who solved this case —

You did it. You did what I couldn't do, what Briggs couldn't do, what Cross couldn't do, what the entire city of Charleston wouldn't do for thirty years.

You found the truth.

I don't know if Gerald Tate will ever face a courtroom. He's eighty-three years old. His lawyers are still the best money can buy. The statute of limitations on the financial crimes has expired. A murder charge has no statute of limitations in South Carolina, but the physical evidence is thirty years old and the political will to prosecute a Tate in Charleston is... well, you've seen how that works.

But the truth matters even when justice doesn't come. Marcus Webb was not a careless man who fell asleep with a cigarette. He was a good man who did the right thing and was killed for it. And now someone besides me knows that.

I want you to understand what this case taught me. It taught me that the system doesn't always work. That powerful people can make evidence disappear, can silence investigators, can run out the clock on justice. It taught me that sometimes the good guys lose.

But it also taught me that the truth is stubborn. You can bury it, box it, stamp it "Not Approved," lock it in a filing cabinet for thirty years — and it will still be there, waiting, when someone finally comes looking.

You came looking. Thank you.

If I could tell Marcus one thing, I'd tell him that he was right. About Gerald, about the money, about all of it. And I'd tell him that someone, finally, listened.

I hope that's enough. It has to be.

— Lorraine Voss
Detective (Ret.), Charleston PD

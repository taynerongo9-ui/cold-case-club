## WHO KILLED MARCUS WEBB?

**Gerald Robert Tate** -- Marcus Webb's business partner of twenty-two years, co-owner of the Belmont Hotel, and one of Charleston's most prominent citizens -- murdered Marcus Webb on the night of October 17, 1993.

He was never charged. He was never arrested. He has lived freely for over thirty years.

This is the complete account of what happened and why.

---

## THE MOTIVE

Since 1985, Gerald Tate had been systematically embezzling from the Belmont Hotel. Using his exclusive control over the hotel's finances -- a power granted by the partnership agreement and sustained by Marcus Webb's trust -- Tate created fictitious vendor companies and billed the hotel for services that were never provided.

The primary shell companies were:
- **Coastal Textile Services** -- billed monthly for "supplemental linens" and "specialty cleaning" (~$58,000/year)
- **Lowcountry Consulting Group** -- billed quarterly for "strategic advisory" and "market analysis" (~$14,000/year)

The payments were funneled through a domestic pass-through account at Charleston First National Bank, then wired quarterly to an offshore account at First Cayman International Bank -- account OJL-6681-PPK, held in the name of Coastal Expenses Service, Ltd., a Cayman Islands shell company controlled solely by Tate.

Over eight years, Tate laundered approximately **$1,412,000** through this scheme.

In March 1993, a routine accounting review flagged irregularities. Marcus Webb began investigating. By July, with help from attorney Samuel Cross, he had compiled comprehensive documentation of the fraud. He decoded Tate's personal ledger, tracing the money from fictitious vendors through the domestic pass-through to the Cayman account. He was preparing to report everything to the IRS.

Webb confronted Tate in August. Tate denied everything and became, in Webb's words, "cold and menacing." From that moment, Tate began planning his partner's murder.

In September, Tate tripled the hotel's insurance to $2.3 million, forging Webb's co-signature. Killing Webb and burning Room 416 would accomplish three objectives: eliminate the witness, destroy the physical evidence, and generate a massive insurance payout.

## THE METHOD

### Preparation (October 1-16, 1993)

**The sedative:** On October 10, Tate obtained 500 milliliters of chloral hydrate through Dr. Eugene Flagg, a disgraced veterinarian in Savannah whose attorney was a partner at Tate's own law firm.

**Testing the delivery:** On or around October 1, Tate added a test dose of chloral hydrate to Webb's bourbon decanter in Room 416. Housekeeper Eva Chen noticed the chemical smell and reported it. Nobody investigated.

**Testing the security system:** On October 3, 9, and 14, Tate remotely disabled the fourth-floor security system via the phone dial-in interface. Each test ran slightly longer (36, 45, 47 minutes). Each time, night manager Ray Hicks logged it as a "glitch." By the fourth occurrence, Hicks was conditioned to ignore it.

**Clearing the adjacent room:** Tate ordered maintenance work on Room 414 that kept it vacant for three weeks -- ensuring no witness adjacent to Room 416 on the night of the fire.

**The alibi:** Tate secured prominent seats at the Charleston Yacht Club Autumn Gala -- Table 4, with the Mayor, a judge, and 212 other witnesses.

### Execution (October 17, 1993)

**3:15-4:50 PM:** Tate visits the Belmont. He enters Room 416 and doses Webb's bourbon decanter with concentrated chloral hydrate. Housekeeper Eva Chen sees him near the room at 3:40 PM.

**6:30 PM:** Tate returns to "share a bourbon" with Webb, ensuring his partner drinks the tainted liquor.

**6:45 PM:** Tate leaves the hotel. He is whistling. Nancy Whitfield remembers.

**7:25 PM:** Tate arrives at the Yacht Club gala. He is visible, social, charming. Building his alibi.

**9:37 PM:** Someone at the Yacht Club calls the Belmont's main line. Duration: 58 seconds. Likely Tate, confirming Hicks is at the desk.

**9:38 PM:** Tate remotely disables the fourth-floor security system via phone dial-in.

**9:45 PM:** Tate leaves the gala through the kitchen service entrance. Waiter Thomas Pruitt notes his empty chair.

**9:57 PM:** Tate arrives at the Belmont via the rear service alley. Enters through the service door with his master key.

**10:00 PM:** Tate enters Room 416. Webb is unconscious on the settee -- the chloral hydrate and bourbon have rendered him completely incapacitated. Tate places a half-smoked Marlboro Red near Webb's right hand. He sets fires at two origin points using petroleum-based lighter fluid: Origin Point A at the window curtains (visible from the street, ensuring discovery) and Origin Point B at the entry closet (blocking escape and rescue).

**10:03 PM:** Tate exits through the service stairs and drives back to the Yacht Club.

**10:12 PM:** Guest Robert Delacroix pulls the fire alarm.

**10:25-10:30 PM:** Tate re-enters the Yacht Club through the kitchen. Pruitt sees him -- bow tie loose, perspiring, smelling of smoke. Tate orders a scotch, drains half in one swallow, and returns to Table 4.

**10:42 PM:** Tate is photographed with Mayor Dalton. Smiling.

**11:45 PM:** Marcus Webb's body is recovered from Room 416. He never woke up. The chloral hydrate ensured he felt nothing. That is the one mercy in this story.

## THE COVER-UP

Gerald Tate did not act alone in the aftermath -- not in the criminal sense, but in the systemic sense. Charleston's power structure closed ranks around one of its own.

- **Fire Commissioner Lloyd Denham** ordered Chief Briggs to reclassify the fire from "suspicious" to "accidental" -- despite the report documenting two origin points, accelerant evidence, and a staged cigarette.

- **Captain Arthur Sykes** overruled Detective Voss's recommendation to keep the case open and told her: "Sometimes the smart play is knowing when to stop."

- **Tate's attorneys** filed injunctions that prevented Samuel Cross from submitting Webb's financial evidence to the IRS. The litigation ran until 1999 -- the statute of limitations expired by design.

- **Dr. Franklin Hayes** did not order a toxicology screen for sedatives -- a departure from standard protocol that remains unexplained.

No one was bribed. No one was explicitly threatened. The system worked the way it was designed to work when a powerful family was involved. Phone calls were made. Expectations were communicated. Careers were quietly protected or quietly endangered. And a murderer walked free.

### WHERE ARE THEY NOW?

**Gerald Tate** -- Age 83. Lives on Kiawah Island with Claire. Sold the Belmont in 1997 for $3.2 million. Collected $2.3 million in insurance after the fire. His offshore accounts were never seized. He remains a member of the Yacht Club, the Gibbes Advisory Board, and St. Michael's Episcopal Church. Never charged with any crime.

**Patricia Webb** -- Age 85. Lives in Savannah. Received Marcus's estate, including the below-market proceeds from the sale of his hotel share. Gave consent for the sealed documents in 2024. "I waited thirty years for someone to care. I'm glad it finally happened, even if it's too late for justice."

**Detective Lorraine Voss** -- Age 72. Retired. Mount Pleasant, SC. Kept her shadow case file for thirty years. Facilitated the 2024 toxicology re-analysis. "Marcus deserved better from this city. I hope he knows someone finally told the truth."

**Raymond Hicks** -- Age 65. Retired. North Charleston. Left hotels in 1995. "If I'd gone up those stairs when the light went red, maybe Marcus would still be alive. I think about that every day."

**Delilah Monroe** -- Age 60. Music teacher, Charleston County School of the Arts. Still performs Friday nights. Sings "Someone to Watch Over Me" every set. "He watched over me when I had nobody. I sing for him now."

**Fire Chief Warren Briggs** -- Deceased, 2019, age 82. His original report speaks for itself. His daughter: "The Belmont case ate at my father for the rest of his life."

**Claire Tate** -- Age 80. Still on Kiawah. Declined to comment through her attorney.

**Samuel Cross** -- Age 92. Released the sealed documents in 2024. "Marcus trusted the system to protect him, and the system failed. I trusted the legal process to deliver justice, and it was used against us."

**Eva Chen** -- Age 73. Retired. Charleston. "I told someone. I told Mr. Hicks. Nobody did anything. If they had listened to the housekeeper, maybe they would have found the poison before it was too late."

**Dr. Julian Hart** -- Age 58. Active faculty, MUSC. "A comprehensive toxicology screen is standard protocol for fire deaths. If it had been done in 1993, this case would have been classified as a homicide from day one."

**Thomas Pruitt** -- Age 53. High school history teacher, Mount Pleasant. "I just told the truth about what I saw. I didn't realize until years later how important it was."

**Martin Hale / "David Crane"** -- Age 68. Retired journalist. "Wrong place, wrong time."

## Evidence Item 12.3 -- Detective Voss's Final Letter

[PRINT FORMAT: Handwritten on yellow legal pad paper. Blue ballpoint pen. Three pages.]

---

To the detective who solved this case --

You did it. You did what I couldn't do, what Briggs couldn't do, what Cross couldn't do, what the entire city of Charleston wouldn't do for thirty years.

You found the truth.

I don't know if Gerald Tate will ever face a courtroom. He's eighty-three years old. His lawyers are still the best money can buy. The statute of limitations on the financial crimes expired a quarter century ago. A murder charge has no statute of limitations in South Carolina, but the physical evidence is thirty years old and the political will to prosecute a Tate in Charleston is -- well, you've seen how that works.

But the truth matters even when justice doesn't come. Marcus Webb was not a careless man who fell asleep with a cigarette. He was a good man who discovered his best friend was a thief, who did the right thing, and who was murdered for it. And now someone besides me knows that.

I want you to understand what this case taught me. It taught me that the system doesn't always work. That powerful people can make evidence disappear, can silence investigators, can run out the clock on justice. It taught me that sometimes the good guys lose.

But it also taught me that the truth is stubborn. You can bury it, box it, stamp it "Not Approved," lock it in a filing cabinet for thirty years -- and it will still be there, waiting, when someone finally comes looking.

You came looking. Thank you.

If I could tell Marcus one thing, I'd tell him that he was right. About Gerald, about the money, about all of it. And I'd tell him that someone, finally, listened.

I'd also tell him about Delilah. How she still sings for him every Friday night. How she kept his memory alive when the rest of this city wanted to forget. Marcus would have liked knowing that. He would have been proud.

I hope that's enough. It has to be.

-- Lorraine Voss
Detective (Ret.), Charleston PD

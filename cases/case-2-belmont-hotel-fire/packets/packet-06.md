## Evidence Item 6.1 -- Gerald Tate Interview Transcript

[PRINT FORMAT: Typed transcript on Charleston PD letterhead. Standard police interview format with timestamps. Courier font. Pages numbered. Tate's responses should read as polished, cooperative, and subtly controlling.]

---

CHARLESTON POLICE DEPARTMENT
CRIMES AGAINST PERSONS UNIT
WITNESS INTERVIEW TRANSCRIPT

**Case:** 93-HOM-0842
**Date of Interview:** October 21, 1993
**Time:** 10:00 AM - 11:47 AM
**Location:** Charleston PD, Interview Room 2
**Interviewing Officer:** Det. Lorraine Voss, Badge #2847
**Witness:** Gerald Robert Tate, W/M, DOB 06/22/1941
**Witness Attorney:** Harrison T. Beauregard, Esq. (Beauregard, Simms & Hayne)

**NOTE:** Witness appeared voluntarily. Attorney present throughout.

---

**DET. VOSS:** Mr. Tate, thank you for coming in. I know this is a difficult time.

**TATE:** Of course, Detective. Anything I can do to help. Marcus was -- he was my closest friend. Twenty-two years as partners. Thirty-five years as friends. I still can't quite believe he's gone.

**DET. VOSS:** I appreciate that. Let me start with some background. How would you describe your partnership with Mr. Webb?

**TATE:** It was the best kind of partnership. Complementary, I suppose you'd say. Marcus handled the day-to-day -- he was brilliant at it, really. He knew every inch of that hotel. Every floorboard, every guest's preference, every staff member's birthday. I handled the financial side. The banking, the accounts, the investors when we needed them, the relationships with the business community. We divided the work the way we divided the ownership -- fifty-fifty, each man in his element.

**DET. VOSS:** Were there any disagreements between you? Any tension recently?

**TATE:** [Pause] Every partnership has its moments, Detective. Marcus could be stubborn. He was a perfectionist. He wanted to do a major renovation of the fourth floor -- new plumbing, electrical, the works. I thought the timing was wrong financially. We debated it. But it was never heated. We'd been through too much together for that. [Pause] We survived Hurricane Hugo. We survived the recession. We survived everything life threw at us for twenty-two years. A disagreement about plumbing wasn't going to undo that.

**DET. VOSS:** Mr. Tate, some of Mr. Webb's associates have suggested the tension between you and Mr. Webb went beyond plumbing.

**TATE:** [Glances at attorney] I don't know what associates you're referring to, Detective, but I can assure you that Marcus and I were on excellent terms. Were we as close as we were in our twenties? Perhaps not. Time changes all relationships. But there was no animosity between us.

**DET. VOSS:** When was the last time you saw Marcus?

**TATE:** The evening of October seventeenth. I stopped by the hotel around six-thirty. Marcus was in his room -- 416, his little retreat. I brought him some papers to sign -- vendor contracts for the coming quarter. We had a bourbon together. He seemed fine. Tired, maybe, but fine.

**DET. VOSS:** How long were you there?

**TATE:** Fifteen minutes, perhaps twenty. I had the gala at the Yacht Club that evening and needed to get home to dress.

**DET. VOSS:** What did you talk about?

**TATE:** Business, mostly. The hotel's October bookings were strong. We talked about Thanksgiving staffing -- November is always tricky. He mentioned wanting to take Patricia to Savannah for their anniversary in March. Nothing remarkable. [Pause] If I had known it would be the last time I'd see him, I would have -- [voice breaks] -- I'm sorry. I would have said something that mattered.

**BEAUREGARD:** Take your time, Gerald.

**TATE:** I'm fine. I'm fine. Please continue, Detective.

**DET. VOSS:** You mentioned bourbon. Was this from Mr. Webb's personal supply?

**TATE:** Yes. Marcus kept a decanter in the room. Maker's Mark. It was a ritual -- end of the day, one pour, neat. He offered, and I joined him. It was what we always did.

**DET. VOSS:** Did you pour the drinks?

**TATE:** [Pause] I believe Marcus poured. He always did -- it was his room, his bourbon. He was particular about it. [Pause] Why do you ask?

**DET. VOSS:** Just establishing the scene, Mr. Tate. You also visited the hotel earlier that day, at three-fifteen.

**TATE:** Did I? Yes, I suppose I did. I was reviewing the monthly accounts in the business office -- Room 420. Standard end-of-month review.

**DET. VOSS:** A housekeeper saw you near Room 416 at approximately three-forty.

**TATE:** I may have passed by Marcus's room. I often did when I was on the fourth floor. The business office is just down the hall. I didn't enter 416 that afternoon -- Marcus wasn't there yet.

**DET. VOSS:** You're certain you didn't enter Room 416 during your three-fifteen visit?

**BEAUREGARD:** Detective, my client has answered the question.

**TATE:** It's all right, Harrison. No, Detective, I did not enter 416 during my afternoon visit. I had no reason to. I was in 420, reviewing accounts. If the housekeeper saw me near 416, I was simply walking past.

**DET. VOSS:** Now, you left the Belmont at what time?

**TATE:** Quarter to seven, perhaps. I signed out at the front desk. You can check the log.

**DET. VOSS:** And you went to the Yacht Club gala?

**TATE:** I went home first -- I live on Legare Street, about ten minutes from the hotel. Changed into my tuxedo. Claire -- my wife -- and I drove to the Yacht Club together. We arrived around seven-twenty, seven-thirty.

**DET. VOSS:** And you were at the Yacht Club the entire evening?

**TATE:** The entire evening, yes. I was seated at Table Four with Mayor Dalton, Judge Harrington, Chip and Betty Middleton, and the Ravenels. We had dinner, listened to the speeches, danced a bit. Claire and I left around eleven-fifteen, I believe. We heard about the fire when we got home -- there was a message on our answering machine from the hotel's front desk.

**DET. VOSS:** Did you leave the gala at any point during the evening? Step outside, use the restroom, anything like that?

**TATE:** I may have stepped out briefly for some air at some point -- it was quite warm in the ballroom with two hundred people. But I didn't leave the Yacht Club, no.

**DET. VOSS:** How long were you outside?

**TATE:** A few minutes. Five, perhaps. I couldn't say exactly. It was not a significant event.

**DET. VOSS:** Do you know of anyone who would want to harm Marcus?

**TATE:** No. Absolutely not. Marcus was -- he was a good man. Honest to a fault. He didn't have enemies. He had competitors and occasionally a disagreeable guest, but enemies? No. Marcus was universally liked.

**DET. VOSS:** What about the business? Any financial difficulties? Debts, disputes?

**TATE:** The Belmont is in excellent financial health. Our occupancy rates are above market average. We carry normal business debt -- a mortgage on the property, a line of credit for operations -- but nothing unusual. No disputes with creditors or vendors. The books are clean.

**DET. VOSS:** Are you aware of any recent changes to the hotel's insurance policy?

**TATE:** I updated the policy in September. We increased coverage to reflect the current replacement value of the building and its contents. It's standard practice -- property values in downtown Charleston have increased significantly since our last appraisal. Our insurer recommended the adjustment.

**DET. VOSS:** The coverage went from eight hundred thousand to two point three million. That's nearly a threefold increase.

**BEAUREGARD:** Detective, is my client a suspect in this investigation?

**DET. VOSS:** Mr. Tate is a witness. I'm gathering information.

**BEAUREGARD:** Then let's keep the questions to what Mr. Tate witnessed, shall we? The insurance question sounds like it belongs in a different kind of interview.

**TATE:** It's all right, Harrison. The increase reflects the appraised value. I can provide the appraisal documentation. There's nothing unusual about it. Any responsible business owner reviews insurance coverage periodically.

**DET. VOSS:** Did Mr. Webb co-sign the insurance change?

**TATE:** [Pause] I believe the paperwork was completed with both our signatures, yes. Standard procedure.

**DET. VOSS:** I may follow up on that. Mr. Tate, do you know whether Marcus smoked?

**TATE:** [Pause] Marcus? No. No, I don't believe he did. I've never seen him smoke. Not once in thirty-five years. Why?

**DET. VOSS:** A cigarette was found near his body.

**TATE:** [Long pause] That's -- I don't know what to make of that. Perhaps he started recently. Stress can drive people to new habits. Or perhaps a guest left it earlier and it rolled -- I don't know, Detective. I don't know.

**DET. VOSS:** Is there anything else you think I should know?

**TATE:** Only that I have lost my oldest friend, and I want answers as much as anyone. If someone did this to Marcus, I want them found. I will cooperate fully, in every way, for as long as it takes. Whatever you need from me, you have it.

**DET. VOSS:** Thank you, Mr. Tate. We may need to speak again.

**TATE:** My door is always open, Detective. Always.

**[END OF INTERVIEW -- 11:47 AM]**

---

[Handwritten notes on back of last page, Voss's writing:]

*Notes to self:*
- *He said Marcus "always" poured the drinks. Then why did he hesitate when I asked who poured? Because Marcus didn't pour that night. Tate poured. And Tate knows I'll never be able to prove it.*
- *"Stepped out briefly for some air" -- "a few minutes, five perhaps." Try forty-five minutes, Gerald.*
- *Insurance increase from $800K to $2.3M one month before the fire. "Standard practice" my foot. The partnership agreement requires both signatures for changes over $100K. Where is Webb's signature? When I asked, Beauregard jumped in like a guard dog.*
- *"I didn't enter 416 during my afternoon visit." Eva Chen saw him near the room. Was he testing the decanter? Was he planting the chloral hydrate? I'll never prove he entered that room at 3:40, but I know he did.*
- *His lawyer jumped in fast when I mentioned the insurance. They came prepared for that question. They came prepared for all of them.*
- *"The books are clean." The books are not clean, Gerald. The books are a work of fiction.*
- *He's smooth. Too smooth. Every answer was perfect. Rehearsed. People in genuine grief aren't this polished. They stammer. They contradict themselves. They say "I don't know" when they mean it. Tate said "I don't know" exactly once -- about the cigarette -- and it was the only moment he was performing confusion rather than actually confused.*
- *"If someone did this to Marcus, I want them found." Yes, Gerald. I'm sure you do.*

**ARTICLE III -- OWNERSHIP AND CAPITAL CONTRIBUTIONS**

Section 3.1. The Partners shall hold equal fifty percent (50%) interests in the Company.

(b) Marcus A. Webb: $90,000.00 (cash, personal savings)

[...]

**ARTICLE V -- MANAGEMENT AND RESPONSIBILITIES**

Section 5.1. Marcus A. Webb is hereby designated as Managing Partner. The Managing Partner shall have primary authority over daily hotel operations, including staffing, guest services, maintenance, and vendor relations for operational supplies.

Section 5.2. Gerald R. Tate is hereby designated as Financial Partner. The Financial Partner shall have primary authority over all financial matters, including bookkeeping, banking relationships, accounts payable, accounts receivable, tax compliance, insurance, and investor communications.

Section 5.3. Both Partners shall have full access to all Company records, accounts, and financial documentation at any time upon reasonable request.

[...]

**ARTICLE VIII -- INSURANCE**

Section 8.1. The Company shall maintain comprehensive property and liability insurance in amounts sufficient to protect the full replacement value of the Property and its contents.

Section 8.2. Any changes to insurance coverage exceeding $100,000.00 in aggregate shall require the written consent of both Partners.

[...]

**ARTICLE XII -- DISSOLUTION AND DEATH**

Section 12.1. In the event of the death of either Partner, the surviving Partner shall have the right of first refusal to purchase the deceased Partner's interest at fair market value, as determined by independent appraisal.

Section 12.2. The estate of the deceased Partner shall receive the appraised value of the deceased Partner's interest within twelve (12) months of the date of death.

[Voss's handwritten notes on margin:]

*Things that bother me about these numbers:*

*1. "Linen & Laundry" -- $78K/yr for a 72-room hotel? I called the Francis Marion (250 rooms). Their linen budget is $95K. The Mills House (125 rooms) spends $62K. The Belmont is spending more on linens per room than any hotel in Charleston. Who is the linen vendor? (Answer: Palmetto Linen Supply at $72K/yr PLUS "Coastal Textile Services" at another $58K/yr. CTS doesn't exist.)*

*2. "Miscellaneous/Other" has grown every year -- $48K to $54K to $62K to $53K. That's a 29% increase from 1990 to 1992 with no explanation. What's in "Other"? Tate controls this line item. No oversight.*

*3. "Professional Services" -- $34K in 1992. What professionals? The hotel has no outside accountant (Tate does the books himself). Legal fees for the year were $8K per Cross. Where's the other $26K going? (Answer: "Lowcountry Consulting Group" -- which doesn't exist.)*

*4. These are the numbers Tate WANTS us to see. The real books are hidden. The phantom vendors are where the money disappears.*

*5. PUZZLE NOTE for whoever reads this someday: Look at the "Miscellaneous/Other" line across all four years. The numbers are 48, 54, 62, 53. Take the last two digits of each. But the numbers are there. -- LV*

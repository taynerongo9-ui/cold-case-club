## Evidence Item 6.1 — Gerald Tate Interview Transcript

[PRINT FORMAT: Typed transcript on Charleston PD letterhead. Standard police interview format with timestamps. Courier font. Pages numbered. Tate's responses should read as polished, cooperative, and subtly controlling.]

---

CHARLESTON POLICE DEPARTMENT
CRIMES AGAINST PERSONS UNIT
WITNESS INTERVIEW TRANSCRIPT

**Case:** 93-HOM-0842
**Date of Interview:** October 21, 1993
**Time:** 10:00 AM - 11:47 AM
**Location:** Charleston PD, Interview Room 2
**Interviewing Officer:** Det. Lorraine Voss, Badge #2847
**Witness:** Gerald Robert Tate, W/M, DOB 06/22/1941
**Witness Attorney:** Harrison T. Beauregard, Esq. (Beauregard, Simms & Hayne)

**NOTE:** Witness appeared voluntarily. Attorney present throughout.

---

**DET. VOSS:** Mr. Tate, thank you for coming in. I know this is a difficult time.

**TATE:** Of course, Detective. Anything I can do to help. Marcus was — he was my closest friend. Twenty-two years. I still can't quite believe it.

**DET. VOSS:** I appreciate that. Let me start with some background. How would you describe your partnership with Mr. Webb?

**TATE:** It was the best kind of partnership. Marcus handled the day-to-day — he was brilliant at it, really. He knew every inch of that hotel. Every floorboard, every guest's preference. I handled the financial side. The banking, the accounts, the investors when we needed them. We complemented each other perfectly.

**DET. VOSS:** Were there any disagreements between you? Any tension recently?

**TATE:** [Pause] Every partnership has its moments. Marcus could be stubborn. He wanted to do a major renovation of the fourth floor — new plumbing, electrical, the works. I thought the timing was wrong financially. We debated it. But it was never heated. We'd been through too much together for that.

**DET. VOSS:** When was the last time you saw Marcus?

**TATE:** The evening of October seventeenth. I stopped by the hotel around six-thirty. Marcus was in his room — 416, his little retreat. I brought him some papers to sign. We had a bourbon together. He seemed fine. Tired, maybe, but fine.

**DET. VOSS:** How long were you there?

**TATE:** Fifteen minutes, perhaps twenty. I had the gala at the Yacht Club that evening and needed to get home to dress.

**DET. VOSS:** What did you talk about?

**TATE:** Business, mostly. The hotel's October bookings were strong. We talked about Thanksgiving staffing. Nothing remarkable. [Pause] If I had known it would be the last time I'd see him, I would have — [voice breaks] — I'm sorry. I would have said more.

**BEAUREGARD:** Take your time, Gerald.

**TATE:** I'm fine. I'm fine. Please continue, Detective.

**DET. VOSS:** You mentioned bourbon. Was this from Mr. Webb's personal supply?

**TATE:** Yes. Marcus kept a decanter in the room. Maker's Mark. It was a ritual — end of the day, one pour, neat. He offered, and I joined him.

**DET. VOSS:** Did you pour the drinks?

**TATE:** [Pause] I believe Marcus poured. He always did — it was his room, his bourbon. Why do you ask?

**DET. VOSS:** Just establishing the scene, Mr. Tate. Now, you left the Belmont at what time?

**TATE:** Quarter to seven, perhaps. I signed out at the front desk. You can check the log.

**DET. VOSS:** And you went to the Yacht Club gala?

**TATE:** I went home first — I live on Legare Street, about ten minutes from the hotel. Changed into my tuxedo. Claire — my wife — and I drove to the Yacht Club together. We arrived around seven-twenty, seven-thirty.

**DET. VOSS:** And you were at the Yacht Club the entire evening?

**TATE:** The entire evening, yes. I was seated at Table Four with Mayor Dalton, Judge Harrington, Chip and Betty Middleton, and the Ravenels. We had dinner, listened to the speeches, danced a bit. Claire and I left around eleven-fifteen, I believe. We heard about the fire when we got home — there was a message on our answering machine from the hotel's front desk.

**DET. VOSS:** Did you leave the gala at any point during the evening? Step outside, use the restroom, anything like that?

**TATE:** I may have stepped out briefly for some air at some point — it was quite warm in the ballroom. But I didn't leave the Yacht Club, no.

**DET. VOSS:** How long were you outside?

**TATE:** A few minutes. Five, perhaps. I couldn't say exactly.

**DET. VOSS:** Do you know of anyone who would want to harm Marcus?

**TATE:** No. Absolutely not. Marcus was — he was a good man. Honest to a fault. He didn't have enemies. He had competitors and occasionally a disagreeable guest, but enemies? No.

**DET. VOSS:** What about the business? Any financial difficulties? Debts, disputes?

**TATE:** The Belmont is in excellent financial health. Our occupancy rates are above market average. We carry normal business debt — a mortgage on the property, a line of credit for operations — but nothing unusual. No disputes with creditors or vendors.

**DET. VOSS:** Are you aware of any recent changes to the hotel's insurance policy?

**TATE:** I updated the policy in September. We increased coverage to reflect the current replacement value of the building and its contents. It's standard practice — property values in downtown Charleston have increased significantly. Our insurer recommended the adjustment.

**DET. VOSS:** The coverage went from eight hundred thousand to two point three million. That's nearly a threefold increase.

**BEAUREGARD:** Detective, is my client a suspect in this investigation?

**DET. VOSS:** Mr. Tate is a witness. I'm gathering information.

**BEAUREGARD:** Then let's keep the questions to what Mr. Tate witnessed, shall we?

**TATE:** It's all right, Harrison. The increase reflects the appraised value. I can provide the appraisal documentation. There's nothing unusual about it.

**DET. VOSS:** I may follow up on that. Mr. Tate, do you know whether Marcus smoked?

**TATE:** [Pause] Marcus? No. No, I don't believe he did. I've never seen him smoke. Why?

**DET. VOSS:** A cigarette was found near his body.

**TATE:** [Long pause] That's — I don't know what to make of that. Perhaps he started recently. Stress can drive people to new habits. Or perhaps a guest left it earlier and it rolled — I don't know, Detective. I don't know.

**DET. VOSS:** Is there anything else you think I should know?

**TATE:** Only that I have lost my oldest friend, and I want answers as much as anyone. If someone did this to Marcus, I want them found. Whatever you need from me, you have it.

**DET. VOSS:** Thank you, Mr. Tate. We may need to speak again.

**TATE:** My door is always open.

**[END OF INTERVIEW — 11:47 AM]**

PARTNERSHIP AGREEMENT
WEBB-TATE HOSPITALITY GROUP, LLC

State of South Carolina

**[EXCERPTED SECTIONS]**

**ARTICLE III — OWNERSHIP AND CAPITAL CONTRIBUTIONS**

Section 3.1. The Partners shall hold equal fifty percent (50%) interests in the Company.

Section 3.2. Initial Capital Contributions:
(a) Gerald R. Tate: $250,000.00 (cash, from Tate Family Trust)
(b) Marcus A. Webb: $90,000.00 (cash, personal savings)

Section 3.3. Additional financing in the amount of $200,000.00 has been secured from First Charleston Bank, personally guaranteed by both Partners.

[...]

**ARTICLE V — MANAGEMENT AND RESPONSIBILITIES**

Section 5.1. Marcus A. Webb is hereby designated as Managing Partner. The Managing Partner shall have primary authority over daily hotel operations, including staffing, guest services, maintenance, and vendor relations for operational supplies.

Section 5.2. Gerald R. Tate is hereby designated as Financial Partner. The Financial Partner shall have primary authority over all financial matters, including bookkeeping, banking relationships, accounts payable, accounts receivable, tax compliance, insurance, and investor communications.

Section 5.3. Both Partners shall have full access to all Company records, accounts, and financial documentation at any time upon reasonable request.

[...]

**ARTICLE VIII — INSURANCE**

Section 8.1. The Company shall maintain comprehensive property and liability insurance in amounts sufficient to protect the full replacement value of the Property and its contents.

Section 8.2. Any changes to insurance coverage exceeding $100,000.00 in aggregate shall require the written consent of both Partners.

[...]

**ARTICLE XII — DISSOLUTION AND DEATH**

Section 12.1. In the event of the death of either Partner, the surviving Partner shall have the right of first refusal to purchase the deceased Partner's interest at fair market value, as determined by independent appraisal.

Section 12.2. The estate of the deceased Partner shall receive the appraised value of the deceased Partner's interest within twelve (12) months of the date of death.

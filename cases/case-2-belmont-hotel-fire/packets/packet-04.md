# BLAZE CLAIMS LIFE AT HISTORIC BELMONT HOTEL

## Hotel co-owner Marcus Webb, 58, found dead after fourth-floor fire; cause under investigation

**By Margaret Calloway, Staff Writer**

CHARLESTON -- A fire on the fourth floor of the historic Belmont Hotel on King Street claimed the life of the hotel's co-owner, Marcus Allen Webb, 58, late Sunday night.

Charleston firefighters responded to the five-story hotel at 10:17 p.m. after a fire alarm was activated on the fourth floor. Upon arrival, Engine Company 7 found heavy smoke and flames concentrated in Room 416, a suite maintained by Webb as a personal office and retreat.

Webb's body was recovered from the room at approximately 11:45 p.m. He was pronounced dead at the scene. The Charleston County Coroner's Office identified Webb through dental records Monday morning.

Three hotel guests were treated for minor smoke inhalation at Roper Hospital and released. All other hotel guests and staff were evacuated safely, according to Raymond Hicks, the hotel's night manager, who called 911 and coordinated the evacuation.

Fire Chief Warren Briggs said the fire appears to have originated in Room 416 and spread into the fourth-floor corridor, causing smoke and water damage to adjacent rooms. The fire was contained to the fourth floor.

"We are still in the early stages of our investigation," Briggs said Monday morning. "We have not yet determined the cause of the fire."

The Belmont Hotel, built in 1889, is listed on the National Register of Historic Places and is one of King Street's most recognizable landmarks. Webb and his business partner, Gerald R. Tate, purchased and restored the hotel in 1978. The restoration was praised by preservation groups and featured in national publications.

Tate, reached by telephone Monday morning, said he was "devastated beyond words."

"Marcus was my partner for twenty-two years and my closest friend for even longer," Tate said. "He loved the Belmont more than anything. This is an unbearable loss for me, for his family, and for the city of Charleston."

Patricia Webb, the victim's wife, declined to comment through a family spokesperson.

The hotel will remain closed pending the fire investigation. Guests have been relocated to the Francis Marion Hotel and the Mills House.

Webb is survived by his wife, Patricia; a brother, Thomas Webb of Columbia; and a sister, Elaine Webb Morris of Atlanta.

Funeral arrangements are pending.

## BELMONT HOTEL FIRE RULED ACCIDENTAL;
## VICTIM'S WIFE QUESTIONED ABOUT WILL CHANGE

**By Margaret Calloway, Staff Writer**

CHARLESTON -- The Charleston Fire Department is expected to rule the deadly fire at the Belmont Hotel an accident, multiple sources told the Post and Courier on Tuesday, ending a nine-day investigation into the blaze that killed hotel co-owner Marcus Webb.

Fire Chief Warren Briggs declined to confirm the finding but said a final report would be filed by the end of the month.

"We have examined all physical evidence and conducted extensive interviews," Briggs said. "Our findings will speak for themselves."

Sources familiar with the investigation said the fire was likely caused by a lit cigarette left unattended while Webb slept. A cigarette butt was recovered near the victim's body.

Meanwhile, the Post and Courier has learned that Patricia Webb, the victim's wife, updated her husband's will just three days before the fire. Court records show Marcus Webb executed a new will on October 14, changing the distribution of his estate.

Attorney Samuel Cross, who prepared the will, called the timing "entirely coincidental" and said the changes were routine.

"The will was updated at Mr. Webb's direction to add a charitable bequest to the Charleston Arts Foundation," Cross said. "Mrs. Webb's share was not increased. Any suggestion otherwise is irresponsible."

Patricia Webb has cooperated fully with investigators, according to Charleston PD spokesperson Lt. Dan Morrison. She has not been named as a suspect.

"Mrs. Webb is a grieving widow who has answered every question asked of her," Cross said. "The focus of this investigation should be on the fire itself, not on a widow's inheritance."

Gerald Tate, Webb's business partner, told investigators he was attending the annual Charleston Yacht Club Autumn Gala at the time of the fire. His alibi has been confirmed by multiple witnesses, according to police sources.

**CHARLESTON WHISPERS**
*Your Guide to Who's Who and What's What*
By Cordelia Ashton

**Post and Courier -- Sunday, October 31, 1993**

---

Well, darlings, it has been quite a month in our fair city, and not the good kind of quite.

Gerald Tate, bless his heart, has been the picture of composure through it all. He's been seen at the Peninsula Grill, at the Yacht Club, at St. Michael's -- always in dark suits, always surrounded by friends, always saying exactly the right thing. One almost admires the performance. (Did I say that out loud? Forgive me. Grief takes many forms.)

Everyone has an opinion about the Belmont tragedy, it seems. At the garden club last Thursday, I counted no fewer than six separate theories being discussed over cucumber sandwiches. Theories about the wife. Theories about the night manager. Theories about a mysterious cash-paying guest. But the one theory no one will discuss in polite company is the one that involves the name Tate.

Right or wrong, there is a question that hangs over King Street like the Spanish moss on our live oaks: what really happened on the fourth floor of the Belmont Hotel?

A friend of mine who knows the hotel well -- quite well, in fact -- told me something last week that made me put down my champagne glass. I cannot print what she said, not yet, not without proof. But I will say this: the story we have been told about Marcus Webb and a lit cigarette does not satisfy the people who knew him best. And the speed with which this investigation is being closed ought to concern every citizen of Charleston.

Lies have a way of unraveling in this city, darlings. We are too small and too nosy for secrets to stay buried forever. Someone always talks. Someone always remembers. Someone always kept a copy.

Dearest Marcus deserved better than a two-paragraph ruling. He was not a man who sought the spotlight -- that was always Gerald's department -- but he was universally respected. The kind of man who remembered your name, asked about your children, and meant it when he said he hoped to see you again. The Belmont was his cathedral, and he tended it with a devotion that put the rest of us to shame.

Now, about that will business. Your humble columnist has it on excellent authority that the changes to Marcus's will were nothing more than a charitable addition -- the Charleston Arts Foundation, a cause dear to both Webbs. Patricia is not the scheming widow the rumor mill has made her. She is, by every account, a woman of impeccable character who is suffering greatly. Let us leave her in peace.

In the meantime, your correspondent will be watching. And listening. And remembering what she's been told.

More next week, if my editor allows it.

*Kisses and suspicions,*
*Cordelia*

## Evidence Item 4.4 -- Editorial, Charleston Post and Courier

**THE CHARLESTON POST AND COURIER**
*EDITORIAL*
*Thursday, November 4, 1993*

---

# THE BELMONT FIRE DESERVES A REAL INVESTIGATION

The Charleston Fire Department is poised to close the book on the fire that killed Marcus Webb at the Belmont Hotel on October 17. The official finding: accidental. The official cause: an unattended cigarette.

This newspaper believes the people of Charleston deserve more than a two-paragraph explanation for the death of a prominent citizen in one of our most historic buildings.

We do not allege foul play. We do not point fingers. But we do ask questions, and we believe those questions merit honest answers.

First: Marcus Webb's personal physician, his wife, his employees, and his friends all say the same thing -- Webb did not smoke. He never smoked. If the fire was caused by a cigarette, whose cigarette was it?

Second: We understand from sources familiar with the investigation that the fire exhibited characteristics inconsistent with a single accidental ignition point. We are not fire experts, but we know that when the physical evidence raises questions, those questions should be answered thoroughly and publicly.

Third: The speed of this investigation is troubling. It took the Warren Commission ten months to investigate the Kennedy assassination. It took Charleston less than three weeks to close the book on a fatal fire in a historic hotel owned by one of the city's most connected businessmen. The haste is conspicuous.

Fourth: The hotel's insurance policy was increased from $800,000 to $2.3 million just one month before the fire. We do not suggest this proves anything. But we do suggest it deserves explanation.

Fifth: This newspaper has received communications from multiple individuals with knowledge of the investigation who have expressed concern about external pressure being applied to investigators. We cannot independently verify these communications, but we take them seriously.

We call on Fire Chief Warren Briggs to release his complete investigation report, including any dissenting findings or minority opinions from his investigators. We call on the Charleston Police Department to confirm whether a criminal investigation is ongoing. And we call on the political leadership of this city to ensure that no one -- regardless of family name, social standing, or charitable donations -- is above scrutiny.

Marcus Webb deserved better. Charleston deserves the truth.

**A Good Man Deserves Answers**

To the Editor:

I worked at the Belmont Hotel for eleven years as a front desk clerk. Marcus Webb was the finest employer I ever had. He knew every employee's name, their children's names, and when someone was having a hard time, he noticed. He didn't just sign paychecks -- he cared.

I remember the day Mr. Webb hired Delilah Monroe to sing in the Blue Room. He told me, "Nancy, I just heard the future of Charleston jazz, and she's twenty-four years old." He was so proud of her. He was proud of all of us.

It breaks my heart to think the investigation into his death is being rushed. I don't know what happened that night, but I know Mr. Webb deserved more than three weeks and a shrug.

-- Nancy Whitfield, Charleston

---

**Leave Gerald Tate Alone**

To the Editor:

I am dismayed by the insinuations circling around Gerald Tate in connection with this tragedy. Gerald is a pillar of this community. He has given generously to the Charleston Museum, the Gibbes Art Gallery, the Medical University, and a dozen other institutions. To suggest he had anything to do with his partner's death is shameful and libelous.

The fire department investigated. They reached a conclusion. The police have confirmed no suspects. Let it rest.

Gerald Tate has devoted his life to this city. He deserves our support in his time of grief, not our suspicion.

-- Thomas R. Halsey III, Charleston

---

**Something Is Wrong Here**

To the Editor:

I am a retired fire investigator from Columbia. I have followed the Belmont Hotel fire coverage with increasing concern.

Two details trouble me. First, the reported fire behavior -- the speed and intensity of the blaze -- is difficult to reconcile with a single cigarette ignition in a well-maintained hotel room. Hotel curtain fabrics treated with flame retardant, as required by fire code, do not ignite easily from a small ignition source like a cigarette. They smolder; they do not flash. The reported fire behavior suggests a more aggressive ignition source, possibly involving an accelerant.

Second, the absence of any smoking materials among the victim's personal effects (aside from the single cigarette near his body) is a significant anomaly. In my thirty years of investigating fire deaths attributed to smoking, the victim always has additional smoking materials -- a pack, a lighter, an ashtray. Always. The absence of these materials should not be dismissed.

I urge the Charleston Fire Department to invite an independent review of their findings. The people of Charleston deserve confidence in the conclusion, whatever it may be.

-- Harold Jennings, Columbia, SC
*Retired, SC State Fire Marshal's Office*

---

**Enough Speculation**

To the Editor:

Your editorial of November 4 was irresponsible. The fire department has done its job. The police have done their job. A man died in a tragic accident. Must we turn every death into a conspiracy?

Leave the Webb family to grieve. Leave Mr. Tate to mourn his friend. And leave the rest of us to trust the institutions we pay taxes to support.

-- Beverly Calhoun, Mount Pleasant

---

**The Belmont Was His Life**

To the Editor:

I knew Marcus Webb for twenty years. We served together on the board of the Historic Charleston Foundation. He was a quiet man in a city that prizes volume, and an honest man in a business that often tests honesty.

He loved two things: that hotel and his wife. He was not a careless man. The idea that he fell asleep with a lit cigarette -- a cigarette, of all things -- defies everything I knew about him.

I do not make accusations. But I ask that the authorities not close this matter prematurely. The truth, whatever it is, should be pursued fully.

-- Dr. William Roper, Charleston

**THE CHARLESTON POST AND COURIER**
*Obituaries -- Wednesday, October 20, 1993*

---

**WEBB, Marcus Allen**

Marcus Allen Webb, 58, of Charleston, died October 17, 1993, at the Belmont Hotel.

Born March 15, 1935, in Columbia, SC, to the late Harold and Dorothy Carlisle Webb. A graduate of the University of South Carolina (1957), Mr. Webb devoted his life to the hospitality industry, building a distinguished career in hotel management in Columbia, Savannah, and Atlanta before co-founding the Webb-Tate Hospitality Group in 1978 and restoring the Belmont Hotel to its place as one of Charleston's premier landmarks.

Mr. Webb was a member of the Historic Charleston Foundation, the Charleston Hospitality Association, and a supporter of the Charleston Arts Foundation. He was known for his dedication to his employees, his meticulous attention to detail, and his deep love for the city of Charleston and its history.

He is survived by his wife, Patricia Davenport Webb of Charleston; a brother, Thomas H. Webb of Columbia; a sister, Elaine Webb Morris of Atlanta; and numerous nieces and nephews.

A funeral service was held October 23 at St. Philip's Church. Interment at Magnolia Cemetery.

In lieu of flowers, the family requests donations to the Charleston Arts Foundation.

J. Henry Stuhr Funeral Home is in charge of arrangements.

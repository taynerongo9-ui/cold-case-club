---

**VOSS'S ANNOTATION (attached sheet):**

*I obtained this receipt from King Street Cleaners on January 22, 1994. The manager, Margaret Rawlings, remembered the Tate order because of three things: the strong smoke smell, Mrs. Tate's unusual urgency about turnaround, and the fact that the perspiration stains were heavy -- "like the man had been running or exerting himself," as Rawlings put it.*

*Claire Tate told the cleaner the smoke was "from the fireplace at the event."*

*I verified with the Charleston Yacht Club facilities manager: the Autumn Gala was held in the Grand Ballroom. THE GRAND BALLROOM DOES NOT HAVE A FIREPLACE. The only fireplaces at the Yacht Club are in the Members' Lounge (closed during gala events, confirmed by club staff) and the Commodore's Library (private, second floor, accessible only by members with key access).*

*The Yacht Club was also non-smoking indoors as of 1992. The veranda allows smoking, but cigarette smoke and wood fire smoke are distinct -- and the cleaner specifically noted it was NOT cigarette smoke. "More like bonfire or wood fire." Like a building fire.*

*The perspiration stains: October 17 was a clear, 68-degree evening. The Grand Ballroom is climate-controlled. A man in a tuxedo at a seated dinner event does not produce heavy perspiration stains unless he has been physically active -- running, climbing stairs, carrying something, or simply panicking.*

*Gerald Tate's tuxedo reeked of heavy smoke on the night he claims he never left a non-smoking, fireplace-free ballroom. His shirt was soaked with sweat from an evening of sitting, eating, and "stepping out for air."*

*Where did the smoke come from, Gerald? Why were you sweating?*

*-- LV*

## Evidence Item 10.2 -- Delilah Monroe's Testimony

[PRINT FORMAT: Typed witness statement. Delilah's voice is warmer and more emotional than the police procedural format.]

---

CHARLESTON POLICE DEPARTMENT
CRIMES AGAINST PERSONS UNIT
WITNESS STATEMENT

**Case:** 93-HOM-0842
**Date:** January 15, 1994
**Witness:** Delilah Anne Monroe
**DOB:** 08/23/1964
**Occupation:** Singer/Musician, The Belmont Hotel Blue Room Lounge
**Address:** 44 Smith Street, Charleston, SC 29401

**Statement taken by:** Det. Lorraine Voss, Badge #2847

---

*I, Delilah Anne Monroe, provide the following statement voluntarily:*

I've been singing at the Belmont since 1989. Marcus Webb hired me for a Wednesday night tryout and I've been there every week since. Four nights a week, the Blue Room Lounge, 8 PM to midnight. Jazz standards, Ella, Sarah, Billie, Nina -- the greats. That room was my church. Candlelight on the tables, the smell of bourbon and gardenia, and those old walls holding sixty years of music inside them.

Marcus was more than my employer. He was the first person who believed I could do this for real. I grew up in North Charleston -- Park Circle, the rough side. My mama cleaned houses for families south of Broad. My daddy was gone before I could walk. My grandmother Nettie raised me on hymns and Mahalia Jackson records. Nobody where I came from became a singer. You became a teacher, a nurse, a secretary if you were lucky. You did not become a performer.

But Marcus heard me sing at a church fundraiser in 1988 -- Mount Moriah Baptist, we were raising money for the roof -- and he walked up to me afterward and said, "You have a gift, and I'm going to make sure people hear it." Just like that. No hesitation. No conditions. A white man I'd never met, standing in a Black church in North Charleston, telling me he was going to change my life.

And he did. He funded my first demo tape. He paid for voice lessons with Charles DuBois in Savannah -- a real jazz instructor, not just someone who teaches scales. He put my name on the marquee outside the hotel. He gave me a four-night residency that paid enough to quit substitute teaching. When I had trouble with a landlord who was trying to raise my rent illegally, Marcus called the landlord. When my car broke down, he lent me his. He never asked for anything in return except that I keep singing and keep working at my craft.

People talked, of course. People always talk. A white businessman and a young Black woman -- they assumed the worst. I heard the whispers at the hotel. I saw the looks from the ladies who came into the Blue Room with their husbands. But it wasn't like that. It was never like that. Marcus was like a father to me. The father I never had. He was fifty-three when he hired me and I was twenty-four, and from the first day, he treated me like a daughter. Not a charity case, not a project -- a daughter.

Patricia knew it too -- she and I got along fine. She used to come to my Friday night sets and sit at the bar with a glass of Sancerre. Sometimes she'd stay until closing and we'd talk about music, art, the world. She once told me that Marcus talked about my performances the way other men talked about football games -- with genuine excitement and attention to detail.

Now let me tell you what I know about what happened.

In the last few months before the fire, Marcus changed. He was worried. Not the normal hotel-business kind of worried -- something deeper. A heaviness. He was distracted, short-tempered sometimes, which wasn't like him at all. He lost weight -- I could see it in his face, in the way his jackets hung. He'd come into the Blue Room after closing, pour himself a bourbon, and just sit there in the dark while I packed up my music. Sometimes he'd talk. Sometimes he'd just sit.

One night in late September -- I think it was the 28th or 29th -- he stayed late and we talked. I was packing up my sheet music and he was on the third stool from the end -- his usual spot -- and he said, "Delilah, I've discovered something about Gerald that I can't ignore. Something bad." I asked him what. He shook his head and said, "Money. A lot of money. Going to places it shouldn't go."

I asked if he was going to confront Gerald. He said he already had, back in August. He said Gerald had denied everything at first, then changed. His face changed, his voice changed. Marcus said Gerald became "menacing." Not threatening outright -- Gerald Tate doesn't threaten. He adjusts. He shifts. He says things that sound reasonable on the surface but carry an edge underneath, like a knife in a velvet glove. Marcus said, "I've known Gerald for thirty-five years and I've never been afraid of him until now."

I asked him what he was going to do. He said he was going to do what was right. He said he had documentation and he was going to turn it over to the proper authorities. He said his lawyer was helping him. He said it like a man who has made peace with a decision that's going to cost him everything.

Then he said something I will never forget. He looked at me with those sad brown eyes and said, "If anything happens to me, Delilah, I want you to know it wasn't an accident."

I asked him what he meant by that. He just looked at me and said, "I hope I'm being dramatic. I probably am. But I needed to say it to someone."

Two and a half weeks later, he was dead.

The night of October 17, I was in the Blue Room. I started my set at 8:30 -- "Autumn Leaves," "My Funny Valentine," "Summertime." Normal night. Maybe twenty people in the lounge. I didn't know anything was wrong until the fire alarm went off around 10:12.

I got my people out through the side exit. Stood on King Street with the other guests and staff. The smoke was pouring from the fourth-floor windows. I could see the orange glow of the fire behind the glass. I could smell it -- not just wood smoke. Something chemical. Something wrong.

I knew Marcus was up there. I knew he worked late in 416 every night. And I knew -- I knew in my bones, standing on that sidewalk with the fire trucks screaming past -- I knew that Gerald Tate had done this.

Nobody from the police department interviewed me in 1993. Not once. I went to the station in November and asked to give a statement. The desk sergeant -- a young white man with a crew cut -- looked at me like I was wasting his time. He told me the case was closed. I said I had information. He said to write it in a letter and mail it in. I wrote a three-page letter. I described my conversations with Marcus, his fears, his exact words. I mailed it certified mail to the Charleston PD Crimes Against Persons Unit.

I never heard back.

Detective Voss found me in January 1994. She came to the Blue Room on a Wednesday night, sat at the bar, and listened to my whole set. After closing, she showed me her badge and said, "Miss Monroe, I've been wanting to talk to you for three months. Nobody told me you existed."

She's the first person in this whole city who actually wanted to hear what I had to say.

One more thing. A few years ago -- 1998, I think -- I sang at a benefit concert in a little town in Maine called Crestfall. A "lighthouse keeper's memorial" they called it. Cold, beautiful place. Full of ghosts. The lighthouse was on a cliff above the ocean and the wind was so strong you could barely stand. I sang "Someone to Watch Over Me" on a wooden stage in front of about a hundred people wrapped in blankets.

I thought of Marcus the whole time. He would have loved that place. Quiet. Honest. No pretense. No Charleston society masks. Just the ocean and the light.

Marcus Webb was murdered. I don't need a lab report to know that. I knew it the moment I smelled the smoke from across King Street on that awful night. And I have remembered what he told me. Every single day for thirty years.

---

*Signed: Delilah A. Monroe*
*Date: January 15, 1994*

*Witnessed: Det. L. Voss, Badge #2847*

---

[Voss's note:]
*"Delilah is the most important witness in this case and nobody talked to her for three months. She wrote a three-page letter to the department and it disappeared. I searched the records room. I found it in an 'unassigned correspondence' box, unopened. Three pages of testimony from the victim's closest confidante, sitting in a box, unread, while we closed the case."*

*"Her account of Marcus's state of mind directly supports the theory that he was killed to prevent him from reporting Tate's fraud. His statement -- 'if anything happens to me, it wasn't an accident' -- is as close to a dying declaration as we're going to get."*

*"Delilah Monroe deserved better from this department. Marcus Webb deserved better from this city. I'm ashamed of both. -- LV"*

## Evidence Item 10.3 -- Room 418 Guest Investigation (Martin Hale / "David Crane")

[PRINT FORMAT: Investigative summary document. This clears the "mysterious guest" red herring.]

---

CHARLESTON POLICE DEPARTMENT
CRIMES AGAINST PERSONS UNIT
INVESTIGATIVE SUPPLEMENT

**Case:** 93-HOM-0842
**Subject:** Investigation of Room 418 Occupant ("David Crane")
**Prepared by:** Det. Lorraine Voss, Badge #2847
**Date:** November 18, 1993

---

**BACKGROUND:**

A guest registered in Room 418 of the Belmont Hotel on October 17, 1993, under the name "David Crane." The guest paid cash, requested no bell service, and was described by night manager Raymond Hicks as "reluctant to evacuate" during the fire, gathering papers before leaving. The use of a false name and cash payment in a room adjacent to the victim's drew investigative attention.

**INVESTIGATION:**

Through hotel lobby surveillance footage (lobby camera on a separate system from floor security, remained operational), I identified the guest as a white male, approximately 35-40, carrying a leather messenger bag and a camera case.

On November 12, I showed the lobby footage to three sources in Charleston's journalism community. On November 14, reporter Sarah Kessler of the Charleston City Paper identified the man as her colleague **Martin Hale**, age 37, an investigative reporter who covers government and real estate.

I contacted Hale on November 15. After initially declining to speak, he agreed to an interview on condition that I protect his journalistic sources.

**FINDINGS:**

Martin Hale confirmed he checked into Room 418 under a false name on October 17, 1993. He stated he was investigating a story about **Charleston County zoning irregularities** involving several downtown hotel properties, including the Belmont. He was looking into allegations that hotel owners received favorable zoning variances in exchange for political contributions.

Hale stated his investigation focused on the **zoning board**, not on the hotel's internal finances. He was unaware of any fraud or embezzlement at the Belmont and had no knowledge of the Webb-Tate financial dispute.

Hale was in Room 418 from approximately 9:15 PM, reviewing documents. He heard nothing unusual before the fire alarm at 10:12 PM. He was reluctant to evacuate because he feared his research materials might be compromised. He took his papers with him when Hicks evacuated him.

Hale's zoning story was published in the Charleston City Paper on December 2, 1993. It does not mention the fire, Webb, or Tate.

**CONCLUSION:**

Martin Hale / "David Crane" has no connection to the fire or to Marcus Webb's death. His presence in Room 418 was coincidental. He is eliminated as a person of interest.

*-- Det. L. Voss*

## Evidence Item 10.4 -- Eva Chen's Chemical Smell Report (Expanded)

[PRINT FORMAT: Brief typed statement and hotel internal incident log. Eva's voice is careful and precise.]

---

BELMONT HOTEL -- INTERNAL INCIDENT LOG
**Date:** October 1, 1993
**Reported by:** Eva Chen, Housekeeping
**Room:** 416

*During regular cleaning of Room 416 at approximately 10:30 AM, I noticed an unusual chemical smell. Not normal cleaning products -- I know every cleaning product we use by smell. This was different. Stronger -- like medicine or chemical solvent. Sharp, slightly sweet underneath.*

*The smell was strongest near the side table where Mr. Webb keeps his bourbon decanter. I checked the decanter -- it appeared clean, no discoloration visible. The crystal stopper was in place. I checked the glasses -- clean, no residue. But the smell was there, hanging in the air around the table.*

*I also noticed that the decanter seemed fuller than usual. Mr. Webb typically keeps it about half full. That morning it was nearly three-quarters full. Someone had added to it.*

*I reported this to Mr. Hicks (on early shift that day) at 11:00 AM. He made a note in the log. I suggested he notify Mr. Webb. He said he would mention it when Mr. Webb came in.*

*I cleaned the room as usual. The smell faded with the windows open.*

*I did not notice the smell on my next scheduled cleaning (October 4). The decanter was back to its usual level.*

*-- Eva Chen*

---

**SUPPLEMENTARY STATEMENT -- Eva Chen, January 20, 1994**
**Taken by:** Det. Lorraine Voss

*Detective Voss showed me a bottle of chloral hydrate solution from a pharmacy reference collection. She opened it under controlled conditions and asked me to smell it from a safe distance.*

*That was the smell. That exact smell. The sharp chemical odor with a slight sweetness underneath. I am certain. That is what I smelled in Room 416 on October 1.*

*Detective Voss asked me about Mr. Tate's visit to the fourth floor on October 1. I checked my cleaning schedule -- yes, Mr. Tate signed in at the front desk at 1:45 PM that day. I saw him on the fourth floor near Room 420. I did not see him enter Room 416, but I was cleaning Room 422 from approximately 2:00 to 2:30 PM and would not have observed anyone entering 416 during that time.*

*I wish someone had listened to me in October. I told Mr. Hicks. I told him something smelled wrong. He wrote it in the log. And that was the end of it.*

*-- Eva Chen*

---

[Voss's note:]
*"Eva Chen identified the smell of chloral hydrate without being told what it was. She picked it out of a lineup of chemical samples. Her nose is as reliable as a laboratory instrument."*

*"October 1st. Sixteen days before the fire. Tate was testing the delivery method. He added chloral hydrate to the decanter and waited to see if Webb would notice the taste. Either Webb didn't drink from it that day (the decanter was fuller than usual -- suggesting Tate added liquid), or the dose was too small to produce effects."*

*"By October 17, Tate had refined his approach. Higher concentration, timed so that he could personally witness Webb drinking from the dosed decanter at 6:30 PM."*

*"Eva Chen tried to tell someone. Nobody listened. A housekeeper in a hotel. Invisible. Unheard. And she was the first person to detect the murder weapon."*

*"-- LV"*

*"But I know someone at the Yacht Club called the Belmont at 9:37 PM. And I know the security system went down at 9:38 PM. Draw your own conclusions. -- LV"*

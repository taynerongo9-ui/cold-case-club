## Evidence Item 10.2 — Delilah Monroe's Testimony

[PRINT FORMAT: Typed witness statement, but Delilah's voice is warmer and more emotional than the police procedural format. Her words should feel alive — this is someone who loved Marcus Webb and has carried this grief for decades.]

---

CHARLESTON POLICE DEPARTMENT
CRIMES AGAINST PERSONS UNIT
WITNESS STATEMENT

**Case:** 93-HOM-0842
**Date:** January 15, 1994
**Witness:** Delilah Anne Monroe
**DOB:** 08/23/1964
**Occupation:** Singer/Musician, The Belmont Hotel Blue Room Lounge
**Address:** 44 Smith Street, Charleston, SC 29401

**Statement taken by:** Det. Lorraine Voss, Badge #2847

---

*I, Delilah Anne Monroe, provide the following statement voluntarily:*

I've been singing at the Belmont since 1989. Marcus Webb hired me for a Wednesday night tryout and I've been there every week since. Four nights a week, the Blue Room Lounge, 8 PM to midnight. Jazz standards, Ella, Sarah, Billie, Nina — the greats. That room was my church.

Marcus was more than my employer. He was the first person who believed I could do this for real. I grew up in North Charleston — Park Circle, the rough side. My mama cleaned houses. My daddy was gone before I could walk. Nobody where I came from became a singer. But Marcus heard me sing at a church fundraiser in 1988 and he said, "You have a gift, and I'm going to make sure people hear it."

He funded my first demo tape. He paid for voice lessons. He put my name on the marquee outside the hotel. When I had trouble with a landlord, he called the landlord. When my car broke down, he lent me his. He never asked for anything in return except that I keep singing and keep working at my craft.

People talked, of course. People always talk. A white businessman and a young Black woman — they assumed the worst. But it wasn't like that. Marcus was like a father to me. The father I never had. Patricia knew it too — she and I got along fine. She used to come to my Friday night sets and sit at the bar with a glass of wine.

Now let me tell you what I know about what happened.

In the last few months before the fire, Marcus changed. He was worried. Not the normal hotel-business kind of worried — something deeper. He was distracted, short-tempered sometimes, which wasn't like him at all. He lost weight. He'd come into the Blue Room after closing, pour himself a bourbon, and just sit there in the dark while I packed up my music.

One night in late September — I think it was the 28th or 29th — he stayed late and we talked. He said, "Delilah, I've discovered something about Gerald that I can't ignore. Something bad." I asked him what. He shook his head and said, "Money. A lot of money. Going to places it shouldn't go."

I asked if he was going to confront Gerald. He said he already had. He said Gerald had denied everything and then — and Marcus used this exact word — Gerald had become "menacing." Not threatening outright, but the way he looked at Marcus, the way his voice changed. Marcus said, "I've known Gerald for thirty-five years and I've never been afraid of him until now."

I asked him what he was going to do. He said he was going to do what was right. He said he had documentation and he was going to turn it over to the proper authorities. He said his lawyer was helping him.

Then he said something I will never forget. He said, "If anything happens to me, Delilah, I want you to know it wasn't an accident."

I asked him what he meant by that. He just looked at me with those sad brown eyes and said, "I hope I'm being dramatic. I probably am. But I needed to say it to someone."

Two and a half weeks later, he was dead.

Nobody from the police department interviewed me in 1993. Not once. I went to the station in November and asked to give a statement. The desk sergeant told me the case was closed. I said I had information. He said to write it in a letter and mail it in. I did. I never heard back.

Detective Voss found me in January. She's the first person in this whole city who actually wanted to hear what I had to say.

Marcus Webb was murdered. I don't need a lab report to know that. I knew it the moment I smelled the smoke from across King Street on that awful night. I stood on the sidewalk and watched the firefighters and I knew in my bones that Gerald Tate had done this.

I can't prove it. But Marcus told me to remember. And I have. Every single day for thirty years.

---

*Signed: Delilah A. Monroe*
*Date: January 15, 1994*

*Witnessed: Det. L. Voss, Badge #2847*

---

[Voss's note:]
*"Delilah is the most important witness in this case and nobody talked to her for three months. She has no motive to lie, no financial interest, and her account of Marcus's state of mind directly supports the theory that he was killed to prevent him from reporting Tate's fraud. Her testimony about Marcus saying 'if anything happens to me, it wasn't an accident' is as close to a dying declaration as we're going to get. — LV"*

## Evidence Item 10.3 — Room 418 Guest Investigation

[PRINT FORMAT: Investigative summary document. This clears the "mysterious guest" red herring and reveals it was a journalist on an unrelated story.]

---

CHARLESTON POLICE DEPARTMENT
CRIMES AGAINST PERSONS UNIT
INVESTIGATIVE SUPPLEMENT

**Case:** 93-HOM-0842
**Subject:** Investigation of Room 418 Occupant ("David Crane")
**Prepared by:** Det. Lorraine Voss, Badge #2847
**Date:** November 18, 1993

---

**BACKGROUND:**

A guest registered in Room 418 of the Belmont Hotel on October 17, 1993, under the name "David Crane." The guest paid cash, requested no bell service, and was described by night manager Raymond Hicks as "reluctant to evacuate" during the fire, gathering papers before leaving. The use of a false name and cash payment in a room adjacent to the victim's drew investigative attention.

**INVESTIGATION:**

Through hotel lobby surveillance footage (lobby camera was on a separate system from the floor security and remained operational), I identified the guest as a white male, approximately 35-40, carrying a leather messenger bag and a camera case.

On November 12, I showed the lobby footage to three sources in Charleston's journalism community. On November 14, reporter Sarah Kessler of the Charleston City Paper identified the man as her colleague **Martin Hale**, age 37, an investigative reporter who covers government and real estate.

I contacted Hale on November 15. After initially declining to speak, he agreed to an interview on the condition that I not reveal his journalistic sources.

**FINDINGS:**

Martin Hale confirmed he checked into Room 418 under a false name on October 17, 1993. He stated he was investigating a story about **Charleston County zoning irregularities** involving several downtown hotel properties, including the Belmont. Specifically, he was looking into allegations that hotel owners had received favorable zoning variances in exchange for political contributions.

Hale stated his investigation focused on the **zoning board**, not on the hotel's internal financial practices. He was unaware of any fraud or embezzlement at the Belmont and had no knowledge of the Webb-Tate financial dispute.

Hale stated he was in Room 418 from approximately 9:15 PM onward, reviewing documents and making notes. He heard nothing unusual before the fire alarm sounded at 10:12 PM. He was reluctant to evacuate because he feared his unpublished research materials might be seized or compromised. He took his papers with him when Hicks evacuated him.

Hale's story on zoning irregularities was published in the Charleston City Paper on December 2, 1993. I reviewed the published article. It does not mention the Belmont fire, Marcus Webb, or Gerald Tate.

**CONCLUSION:**

Martin Hale / "David Crane" has no connection to the fire or to Marcus Webb's death. His presence in Room 418 was coincidental, related to an unrelated journalistic investigation. He is eliminated as a person of interest.

*— Det. L. Voss*

## Evidence Item 10.4 — Eva Chen's Chemical Smell Report

[PRINT FORMAT: Brief typed statement and a handwritten incident report from the hotel's internal log. Eva's voice is careful and precise — English is her second language but she is meticulous.]

---

BELMONT HOTEL — INTERNAL INCIDENT LOG
**Date:** October 1, 1993
**Reported by:** Eva Chen, Housekeeping
**Room:** 416

*During regular cleaning of Room 416 at approximately 10:30 AM, I noticed an unusual chemical smell. Not normal cleaning products. Stronger — like medicine or chemical solvent. The smell was strongest near the side table where Mr. Webb keeps his bourbon decanter. I checked the decanter and the glasses — they appeared clean. The smell was in the air, not from any visible source.*

*I reported this to Mr. Hicks (night manager, on early shift that day) at 11:00 AM. He made a note. No further action was taken.*

*I cleaned the room as usual and did not notice the smell on my next scheduled cleaning (October 4).*

*— Eva Chen*

---

**VOSS'S NOTE (attached):**

*October 1st. Sixteen days before the fire. The chemical smell near the bourbon decanter.*

*Chloral hydrate has a mildly pungent, slightly bitter odor. In concentrated form — such as the solution Flagg purchased from the Savannah veterinary supply — the smell would be noticeable in an enclosed room, especially to someone with Eva Chen's trained nose for cleaning products.*

*I believe Tate was testing the delivery method. He may have added a small amount of chloral hydrate to the decanter on October 1st to see if Webb would notice the taste or smell. Either Webb didn't drink from it that day, or the dose was too small to produce obvious effects.*

*By October 17th, Tate had refined his approach. He visited Room 416 at 3:40 PM that afternoon — Eva Chen saw him near the room. He had ample opportunity to dose the decanter. When he returned at 6:30 PM for "bourbon," the trap was set.*

*Eva tried to tell someone. Nobody listened.*

*— LV*

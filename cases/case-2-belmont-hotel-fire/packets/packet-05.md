### I. SUMMARY

On the evening of October 17, 1993, a structural fire occurred on the fourth floor of the Belmont Hotel, a five-story brick and timber commercial building at 412 King Street. The fire resulted in one fatality (Marcus A. Webb, age 58), three minor injuries (smoke inhalation, treated and released), and significant structural damage to the fourth floor, concentrated in Room 416 and the adjacent hallway.

This report presents the complete findings of the fire investigation conducted between October 18 and October 28, 1993.

### II. SCENE EXAMINATION

Room 416 is a premium suite measuring approximately 450 square feet, consisting of a main living area, a sleeping alcove, a private bathroom, and an entry closet. The room was furnished with period-appropriate hotel furniture including a settee, writing desk, wardrobe, bed, and side tables. The room had two windows facing east (King Street).

Upon examination, the following was observed:

**Fire Damage Pattern:**
The room exhibited catastrophic fire damage consistent with a sustained, high-temperature burn. Ceiling joists were exposed and charred. Floor boards showed deep alligator charring. The plaster walls were calcined (calcium deposits visible), indicating temperatures exceeding 1,000 degrees Fahrenheit in portions of the room.

**CRITICAL FINDING: TWO SEPARATE ORIGIN POINTS WERE IDENTIFIED.**

**Origin Point A:** Located at the east wall, centered on the window curtain area. The curtains (heavy brocade, per hotel inventory) showed a low-to-high burn pattern radiating from the base of the window treatment, approximately 18 inches above floor level. The "V" pattern on the wall plaster clearly indicates fire initiation at this location. Burn intensity was greatest at the base, diminishing as it rose вҖ” consistent with an ignition source at or near floor level.

**Origin Point B:** Located at the entry closet, on the west wall of the room near the hallway door. This closet contained coats, an umbrella stand, and stored linens. The burn pattern here showed a separate "V" origin, with the deepest charring at the base of the closet, approximately 12 inches above floor level. The pattern is independent of Origin Point A and could not have resulted from fire spread from Point A given the distance (approximately 22 feet), the room layout, and the burn progression timelines.

**Analysis:** A single accidental ignition source (e.g., a dropped cigarette) produces ONE origin point. The presence of TWO distinct, independent origin points is a strong indicator of intentional fire-setting. In my twenty-three years of fire investigation, I have never encountered a legitimate accidental fire with two clearly separated origins in a single room.

### III. ACCELERANT ANALYSIS

Samples were collected from both origin points and submitted to the South Carolina Law Enforcement Division (SLED) Forensic Laboratory for chemical analysis.

**Results (SLED Lab Report #93-FL-2287):**

- **Sample A-1 (Origin Point A, carpet and underlayment):** Positive for petroleum distillate, consistent with charcoal lighter fluid or similar commercial accelerant.
- **Sample A-2 (Origin Point A, curtain fabric remnant):** Positive for petroleum distillate.
- **Sample B-1 (Origin Point B, closet floor and shelf):** Positive for petroleum distillate, same chemical profile as Samples A-1 and A-2.
- **Control Sample C-1 (hallway carpet, unburned):** Negative for accelerant.
- **Control Sample C-2 (Room 414, unburned):** Negative for accelerant.

**Analysis:** The presence of petroleum-based accelerant at both origin points, combined with the dual-origin fire pattern, is consistent with deliberate fire-setting (arson). The accelerant profile matches commonly available commercial products (lighter fluid, barbecue starter) that can be purchased without restriction.

### IV. THE CIGARETTE

A partially consumed cigarette was recovered near the victim's right hand, on the floor between the settee and the room entry door. The cigarette was a Marlboro Red, king-size.

**Observations:**
- The cigarette showed burn patterns consistent with controlled smoking (even ash line), not with exposure to ambient fire.
- No cigarette pack, carton, lighter, matches, or ashtray was found in the room.
- The victim, per medical records and multiple witness statements, was a lifelong non-smoker.
- The positioning of the cigarette near the victim's hand, in conjunction with the above observations, suggests the cigarette was placed deliberately as part of staging.

### V. SPRINKLER AND SAFETY SYSTEMS

The Belmont Hotel was equipped with a wet-pipe sprinkler system installed during the 1982 renovation. Sprinkler heads in Room 416 activated during the fire but were insufficient to suppress the blaze due to the accelerant involvement.

The fourth-floor electronic security monitoring system (smoke detectors, door sensors) was offline at the time of the fire. The system log shows it was deactivated at 21:38 hours via remote dial-in access using an owner-level authorization code. The system was not manually reactivated. The building fire alarm (separate system, manual pull stations) remained operational.

### VI. CONCLUSION

Based on the physical evidence вҖ” specifically, the two independent fire origin points, the presence of petroleum-based accelerant at both locations, the planted cigarette inconsistent with the victim's known habits, and the deliberate deactivation of the fourth-floor security system вҖ” it is my professional opinion that this fire was deliberately set.

**Original classification: UNDETERMINED вҖ” SUSPICIOUS, pending further criminal investigation.**

## Evidence Item 5.3 вҖ” SLED Forensic Laboratory Analysis

[PRINT FORMAT: Official lab report. State agency letterhead. Technical language, tabulated results. Clean, clinical formatting.]

SOUTH CAROLINA LAW ENFORCEMENT DIVISION
FORENSIC SCIENCES LABORATORY
4400 Broad River Road, Columbia, SC 29210

**Lab Report Number:** 93-FL-2287
**Date of Analysis:** October 22-25, 1993
**Requesting Agency:** Charleston Fire Department
**Case Reference:** CFD Incident #93-4417
**Analyst:** Dr. Robert Kimura, Forensic Chemist

**EVIDENCE RECEIVED:**
Five (5) sealed evidence containers received on October 20, 1993, via chain of custody from Chief Warren Briggs, CFD.

**METHODOLOGY:**
Gas chromatography-mass spectrometry (GC-MS) analysis was performed on all samples to detect and identify ignitable liquid residues (ILR). Samples were prepared using activated charcoal strip adsorption/elution per ASTM E1412-91.

**RESULTS:**

| Sample ID | Location | Material | ILR Detected | Classification |
|-----------|----------|----------|-------------|----------------|
| A-1 | Origin Pt. A, floor | Carpet/pad | POSITIVE | Medium petroleum distillate |
| A-2 | Origin Pt. A, wall | Curtain remnant | POSITIVE | Medium petroleum distillate |
| B-1 | Origin Pt. B, closet | Wood/fabric | POSITIVE | Medium petroleum distillate |
| C-1 | Hallway, control | Carpet | NEGATIVE | вҖ” |
| C-2 | Room 414, control | Carpet | NEGATIVE | вҖ” |

**INTERPRETATION:**

Samples A-1, A-2, and B-1 each contained ignitable liquid residues classified as medium petroleum distillates. The chromatographic profiles of all three positive samples are consistent with each other, suggesting a common source product. The profile is consistent with commercially available charcoal lighter fluid or similar petroleum-based fire starter products.

The presence of ILR at two spatially separated locations within the same room, combined with negative control samples from adjacent areas, supports a finding of deliberate introduction of an ignitable liquid at both locations.

**CONCLUSION:**

The analytical findings are consistent with the intentional application of a petroleum-based accelerant at two locations within Room 416 of the Belmont Hotel. These findings should be considered in conjunction with the fire pattern analysis conducted by the investigating fire officer.

---

Dr. Robert Kimura, Ph.D.
Forensic Chemistry Section
SLED Forensic Sciences Laboratory

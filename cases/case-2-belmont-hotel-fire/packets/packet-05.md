### I. SUMMARY

On the evening of October 17, 1993, a structural fire occurred on the fourth floor of the Belmont Hotel, a five-story brick and timber commercial building at 412 King Street. The fire resulted in one fatality (Marcus A. Webb, age 58), three minor injuries (smoke inhalation, treated and released), and significant structural damage to the fourth floor, concentrated in Room 416 and the adjacent hallway.

This report presents the complete findings of the fire investigation conducted between October 18 and October 28, 1993.

### II. SCENE EXAMINATION

Room 416 is a premium suite measuring approximately 450 square feet, consisting of a main living area, a sleeping alcove, a private bathroom, and an entry closet. The room was furnished with period-appropriate hotel furniture including a settee, writing desk, wardrobe, bed, and side tables. The room had two windows facing east (King Street).

Upon examination, the following was observed:

**Fire Damage Pattern:**
The room exhibited catastrophic fire damage consistent with a sustained, high-temperature burn. Ceiling joists were exposed and charred to a depth of 1.5 inches. Floor boards showed deep alligator charring -- the large, shiny blisters characteristic of rapid, intense burning. The plaster walls were calcined (calcium deposits visible), indicating temperatures exceeding 1,000 degrees Fahrenheit in portions of the room. The thermal damage gradient indicates the fire burned at peak intensity for approximately 45-60 minutes before suppression.

**CRITICAL FINDING: TWO SEPARATE ORIGIN POINTS WERE IDENTIFIED.**

**Origin Point A:** Located at the east wall, centered on the window curtain area. The curtains (heavy brocade, per hotel inventory records -- a flame-retardant-treated fabric that requires sustained direct flame or accelerant to ignite) showed a low-to-high burn pattern radiating from the base of the window treatment, approximately 18 inches above floor level. The "V" pattern on the wall plaster clearly indicates fire initiation at this location. The apex of the V was approximately 42 inches above the floor, and the arms extended approximately 30 inches to either side. Burn intensity was greatest at the base, diminishing as it rose -- consistent with an ignition source at or near floor level, with an upward fire spread aided by the vertical fuel load of the curtain fabric.

The carpet beneath Origin Point A showed deep charring in an irregular pool pattern, approximately 24 inches in diameter. Pool patterns at the base of a V-pattern are a hallmark of poured liquid accelerant. The carpet pad beneath this area was completely consumed.

**Origin Point B:** Located at the entry closet, on the west wall of the room near the hallway door. This closet contained coats, an umbrella stand, and stored linens (per room inventory). The burn pattern here showed a separate "V" origin, with the deepest charring at the base of the closet, approximately 12 inches above floor level. The V-pattern extended upward through the closet frame and into the hallway-side wall above the closet. The pattern is independent of Origin Point A and could not have resulted from fire spread from Point A given the distance (approximately 22 feet), the room layout (including a bed and desk between the two points), and the burn progression timelines.

Analysis of the char depth at both origin points, using the standard one-inch-per-hour char rate for softwood, indicates that both fires were burning for approximately the same duration -- confirming they were set at approximately the same time.

**Analysis:** A single accidental ignition source (e.g., a dropped cigarette) produces ONE origin point. The presence of TWO distinct, independent origin points is a strong indicator of intentional fire-setting. In my twenty-three years of fire investigation, I have never encountered a legitimate accidental fire with two clearly separated origins in a single room.

Furthermore, the strategic placement of the two origin points is significant: Origin Point A (curtains, east wall) would create a dramatic visual fire visible from King Street, ensuring rapid detection and response. Origin Point B (closet, west wall near door) would block the only escape route and the hallway access point, ensuring that anyone inside Room 416 could not escape and that rescue personnel would face impassable conditions at the room entry.

This is not random. This is tactical.

### III. FIRE DYNAMICS ANALYSIS

**Rate of Fire Growth:**
Based on the damage patterns, witness accounts (fire alarm at 10:12 PM, Engine 7 arrival at 10:22 PM, full room involvement observed), and the behavior described by first responders, the fire in Room 416 progressed from ignition to flashover in approximately 8-12 minutes. This rate of growth is consistent with accelerant-assisted ignition in a furnished room. Without accelerant, a typical hotel room fire progresses from ignition to flashover in 20-30 minutes.

**Flashover Conditions:**
Flashover occurs when all combustible materials in a room simultaneously ignite due to radiant heat feedback. In Room 416, flashover would have produced temperatures of 1,100-1,400 degrees Fahrenheit at ceiling level and 600-800 degrees at floor level. These conditions are not survivable. Any occupant who was conscious would have approximately 2-3 minutes from initial fire detection (smoke, heat) to escape before conditions became lethal. An unconscious occupant would have no chance.

**Smoke Production:**
The fire produced heavy black smoke consistent with the combustion of petroleum-based accelerant mixed with standard room furnishings (wood, fabric, carpet). The dark, particulate-heavy smoke reduced visibility to zero on the fourth-floor corridor within 3-5 minutes of fire growth, consistent with first responder accounts.

### IV. ACCELERANT ANALYSIS

Samples were collected from both origin points and submitted to the South Carolina Law Enforcement Division (SLED) Forensic Laboratory for chemical analysis.

**Results (SLED Lab Report #93-FL-2287):**

- **Sample A-1 (Origin Point A, carpet and underlayment):** Positive for petroleum distillate, consistent with charcoal lighter fluid or similar commercial accelerant.
- **Sample A-2 (Origin Point A, curtain fabric remnant):** Positive for petroleum distillate.
- **Sample B-1 (Origin Point B, closet floor and shelf):** Positive for petroleum distillate, same chemical profile as Samples A-1 and A-2.
- **Control Sample C-1 (hallway carpet, unburned):** Negative for accelerant.
- **Control Sample C-2 (Room 414, unburned):** Negative for accelerant.

**Analysis:** The presence of petroleum-based accelerant at both origin points, combined with the dual-origin fire pattern, is consistent with deliberate fire-setting (arson). The accelerant profile matches commonly available commercial products (lighter fluid, barbecue starter) that can be purchased without restriction at any hardware store or gas station. The amount of accelerant used, based on the burn area and intensity, is estimated at 16-24 fluid ounces -- approximately one standard bottle of lighter fluid.

### V. THE CIGARETTE

A partially consumed cigarette was recovered near the victim's right hand, on the floor between the settee and the room entry door. The cigarette was a Marlboro Red, king-size.

**Observations:**
- The cigarette showed burn patterns consistent with controlled smoking (even ash line), not with exposure to ambient fire. This means the cigarette was smoked and then placed -- it was not an unsmoked cigarette that was ignited by the room fire.
- No cigarette pack, carton, lighter, matches, or ashtray was found in the room.
- The victim, per medical records and multiple witness statements, was a lifelong non-smoker who had a physical aversion to tobacco smoke.
- The positioning of the cigarette near the victim's hand, in conjunction with the above observations, suggests the cigarette was placed deliberately as part of staging.
- The Marlboro Red brand is the most popular cigarette in the United States. It is a generic choice -- the kind of cigarette someone who doesn't smoke would buy because they don't know what else to buy.

### VI. SPRINKLER AND SAFETY SYSTEMS

The Belmont Hotel was equipped with a wet-pipe sprinkler system installed during the 1982 renovation, compliant with NFPA 13 standards of the time. Sprinkler heads in Room 416 activated during the fire but were insufficient to suppress the blaze due to the accelerant involvement. Accelerant-fed fires release heat at a rate that exceeds the cooling capacity of standard wet-pipe sprinkler systems, particularly in the initial minutes of fire growth.

The fourth-floor electronic security monitoring system (SentryGuard SG-400, installed 1989 -- smoke detectors, door sensors, motion sensors) was offline at the time of the fire. The system log shows it was deactivated at 21:38:14 via remote dial-in access using an owner-level authorization code. The system was not re-armed. The building fire alarm (separate system -- manual pull stations and heat detectors) remained operational.

Had the electronic smoke detectors been active, they would have detected smoke in Room 416 within 2-3 minutes of fire initiation -- potentially 5-7 minutes before the hallway became impassable and 8-10 minutes before the manual alarm was pulled by a guest. This earlier detection could have provided sufficient time for a conscious occupant to escape or for hotel staff to attempt a rescue.

### VII. VICTIM ANALYSIS

The victim, Marcus A. Webb, was found face-down on the floor of Room 416, between the settee and the room entry door, approximately 8 feet from the door. The body's position and the absence of any evidence of attempted escape (no marks on door handle, no crawl pattern toward the exit, no disturbance at the windows) indicate that the victim was unconscious and immobile when the fire reached him.

His wristwatch stopped at 10:07 PM, consistent with heat damage as the fire approached the settee area. The room service tray on the side table showed the dinner had been consumed. The crystal bourbon decanter was shattered by heat (fragments recovered).

### VIII. CONCLUSION

Based on the physical evidence -- specifically, the two independent fire origin points, the presence of petroleum-based accelerant at both locations, the planted cigarette inconsistent with the victim's known habits, the deliberate deactivation of the fourth-floor security system, the tactical placement of fires to maximize destruction and prevent escape, and the victim's apparent unconsciousness -- it is my professional opinion that this fire was deliberately set with the intent to kill the occupant of Room 416 and destroy the contents of the room.

**Original classification: UNDETERMINED -- SUSPICIOUS, pending further criminal investigation.**

## Evidence Item 5.3 -- SLED Forensic Laboratory Analysis

[PRINT FORMAT: Official lab report. State agency letterhead. Technical language, tabulated results. Clean, clinical formatting.]

---

SOUTH CAROLINA LAW ENFORCEMENT DIVISION
FORENSIC SCIENCES LABORATORY
4400 Broad River Road, Columbia, SC 29210

**Lab Report Number:** 93-FL-2287
**Date of Analysis:** October 22-25, 1993
**Requesting Agency:** Charleston Fire Department
**Case Reference:** CFD Incident #93-4417
**Analyst:** Dr. Robert Kimura, Forensic Chemist

**EVIDENCE RECEIVED:**
Five (5) sealed evidence containers received on October 20, 1993, via chain of custody from Chief Warren Briggs, CFD.

**METHODOLOGY:**
Gas chromatography-mass spectrometry (GC-MS) analysis was performed on all samples to detect and identify ignitable liquid residues (ILR). Samples were prepared using activated charcoal strip adsorption/elution per ASTM E1412-91. Analysis was conducted per ASTM E1387-90.

**RESULTS:**

| Sample ID | Location | Material | ILR Detected | Classification |
|-----------|----------|----------|-------------|----------------|
| A-1 | Origin Pt. A, floor | Carpet/pad | POSITIVE | Medium petroleum distillate |
| A-2 | Origin Pt. A, wall | Curtain remnant | POSITIVE | Medium petroleum distillate |
| B-1 | Origin Pt. B, closet | Wood/fabric | POSITIVE | Medium petroleum distillate |
| C-1 | Hallway, control | Carpet | NEGATIVE | -- |
| C-2 | Room 414, control | Carpet | NEGATIVE | -- |

**INTERPRETATION:**

Samples A-1, A-2, and B-1 each contained ignitable liquid residues classified as medium petroleum distillates. The chromatographic profiles of all three positive samples are consistent with each other, suggesting a common source product. The profile is consistent with commercially available charcoal lighter fluid or similar petroleum-based fire starter products (e.g., Kingsford Match Light, Gulf Lite, or generic equivalents).

The presence of ILR at two spatially separated locations within the same room, combined with negative control samples from adjacent areas, supports a finding of deliberate introduction of an ignitable liquid at both locations.

The estimated volume of accelerant used, based on burn area calculations and residue concentrations, is 16-24 fluid ounces -- approximately one standard container of commercial lighter fluid.

**CONCLUSION:**

The analytical findings are consistent with the intentional application of a petroleum-based accelerant at two locations within Room 416 of the Belmont Hotel. These findings should be considered in conjunction with the fire pattern analysis conducted by the investigating fire officer.

---

Dr. Robert Kimura, Ph.D.
Forensic Chemistry Section

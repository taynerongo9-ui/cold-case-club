**PERSONAL HISTORY:**

Marcus Allen Webb was born in Columbia, South Carolina, to Harold Webb (d. 1971), a mill foreman, and Dorothy Webb, nee Carlisle (d. 1978), a schoolteacher. The eldest of three children. Raised in a working-class household in the Eau Claire neighborhood.

Webb attended the University of South Carolina on academic scholarship, graduating in 1957 with a degree in Business Administration. He met Gerald Tate at USC during their junior year. While Tate came from one of Charleston's oldest families, Webb came from nothing — a fact that shaped their partnership for the next thirty-five years.

After college, Webb worked in hotel management in Columbia, then Savannah, then Atlanta, building a reputation as a skilled operator who could turn struggling properties around. In 1978, Tate approached Webb with a proposal: purchase and restore the Belmont Hotel, a once-grand Charleston landmark that had fallen into disrepair. Tate would provide the capital and social connections; Webb would run the business.

The partnership was, by all accounts, successful for over a decade. The Belmont's restoration was celebrated in the Charleston press. Webb was respected in the hospitality community — a hands-on manager who knew every employee by name, personally inspected rooms, and treated the hotel as his life's work.

**PERSONAL LIFE:**

Webb married Patricia Davenport in 1991. It was his second marriage (first marriage to Susan Kelly, 1962-1974, ended in divorce, no children). Patricia owned the Davenport Gallery on Church Street, specializing in Lowcountry watercolors. Friends described the marriage as loving but sometimes strained by Webb's devotion to the hotel.

Webb had no children. His closest personal relationships outside his marriage were with Delilah Monroe, the Belmont's lounge singer (whom he mentored and considered a surrogate daughter), and Samuel Cross, his attorney and longtime confidant.

**HABITS AND CHARACTER:**

Colleagues and friends consistently describe Webb as hardworking, honest, detail-oriented, and private. He was not a socialite — unlike Tate, he avoided Charleston society events when possible. He preferred to work.

Webb kept Room 416 as his personal office and occasional overnight retreat. He would often work late into the evening there, reviewing hotel operations, reading, or listening to jazz records.

He was known to enjoy bourbon (Maker's Mark, neat) and was a moderate drinker — two drinks maximum per evening, according to multiple sources.

**He did not smoke.** Multiple sources confirm this, including his wife, his physician, hotel staff, and personal friends. This is addressed in detail in the attached medical records.

**FINANCIAL STATUS:**

Webb's personal assets at time of death were estimated at $1.2 million, including his 50% stake in the Belmont Hotel (valued at approximately $800,000), a residence on Tradd Street, personal savings, and a modest investment portfolio managed by Cross.

His will, updated October 14, 1993, left his estate to Patricia Webb, with a secondary bequest of $50,000 to the Charleston Arts Foundation. [NOTE: The will update has drawn media attention. See Packet 4.]

## Evidence Item 3.3 — Webb & Tate Business History

[PRINT FORMAT: Typed summary document, formatted as a background research memo. Looks like it was prepared by a paralegal or research assistant. Clean, factual, slightly dry.]

---

RESEARCH MEMORANDUM
Prepared for: Det. L. Voss, Charleston PD
Re: Webb-Tate Partnership — Business History
Date: November 2, 1993

**PARTNERSHIP FORMATION:**

The Belmont Hotel was purchased on April 12, 1978, by the Webb-Tate Hospitality Group, LLC, a South Carolina limited liability company formed by Marcus A. Webb and Gerald R. Tate. Purchase price: $340,000 (the property was in severe disrepair).

Financing: Tate provided $250,000 from family trust assets. Webb contributed $90,000 in personal savings and secured a $200,000 renovation loan from First Charleston Bank, personally guaranteed by both partners.

Ownership: 50/50 split. The operating agreement designated Webb as Managing Partner (day-to-day operations) and Tate as Financial Partner (accounting, banking, investor relations).

**RENOVATION AND GROWTH:**

The Belmont underwent a comprehensive renovation from 1978 to 1982 at a total cost of approximately $600,000. The project was featured in Southern Living (March 1983) and Historic Preservation Magazine (Fall 1983). The hotel was listed on the National Register of Historic Places in 1984.

By 1985, the Belmont was profitable, generating approximately $1.2M in annual revenue with a net margin of 12-15%. Occupancy rates averaged 78% annually, well above the Charleston market average.

**FINANCIAL MANAGEMENT:**

Per the operating agreement, Gerald Tate maintained exclusive control over the hotel's financial accounts, bookkeeping, vendor relationships, and tax filings. Marcus Webb had access to financial records but, by his own admission to attorney Samuel Cross, "trusted Gerald with the money side."

This division of responsibilities is significant. It gave Tate sole and unsupervised access to the hotel's accounts for fifteen years.

**THE RIFT:**

According to Cross, Webb first became suspicious of financial irregularities in March 1993 when a routine accountant's review flagged discrepancies between vendor invoices and actual services rendered. Webb spent the spring and summer of 1993 quietly investigating.

By July 1993, Webb had compiled evidence of what he believed was systematic embezzlement and money laundering totaling approximately $1.4 million over eight years. He shared this evidence with Cross and stated his intention to report the findings to the Internal Revenue Service.

Cross advised Webb to secure his documentation before confronting Tate. Webb reportedly confronted Tate in August 1993 despite this advice. The nature of that conversation is known only through Webb's account to Cross: Tate denied everything and became "cold and threatening."

Webb kept copies of the financial documents in Room 416 and with Cross's office. The copies in Room 416 were destroyed in the fire.

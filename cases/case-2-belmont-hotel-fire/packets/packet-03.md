## Evidence Item 3.1 -- Marcus Webb Biographical Profile

[PRINT FORMAT: Typed dossier-style document. Charleston PD header. Clean typewriter font. Attached paper-clipped "photo" -- a printed description card since we can't use real photos. Single staple, top left.]

---

CHARLESTON POLICE DEPARTMENT
CRIMES AGAINST PERSONS UNIT
VICTIM PROFILE

**Case:** 93-HOM-0842
**Prepared by:** Det. Lorraine Voss, Badge #2847
**Date:** October 24, 1993

**SUBJECT:** Marcus Allen Webb

**Date of Birth:** March 15, 1935
**Date of Death:** October 17, 1993
**Age at Death:** 58
**Race/Sex:** White / Male
**Height:** 6'1" **Weight:** 195 lbs
**Hair:** Gray, full, neatly trimmed
**Eyes:** Brown
**Distinguishing marks:** Surgical scar, left knee (1987, ACL repair)

**PERSONAL HISTORY:**

Marcus Allen Webb was born in Columbia, South Carolina, to Harold Webb (d. 1971), a foreman at the Olympia Cotton Mill, and Dorothy Webb, nee Carlisle (d. 1978), a schoolteacher at Eau Claire Elementary. The eldest of three children (brother Thomas, b. 1938; sister Elaine, b. 1941). Raised in a working-class household in the Eau Claire neighborhood of Columbia. Attended Dreher High School, where he lettered in basketball and worked summers at the mill alongside his father.

Webb attended the University of South Carolina on academic scholarship, graduating in 1957 with a degree in Business Administration. He met Gerald Tate at USC during their junior year. While Tate came from one of Charleston's oldest and wealthiest families, Webb came from nothing -- a fact that shaped their partnership for the next thirty-five years.

After college, Webb worked in hotel management in Columbia (Columbia Marriott, assistant manager, 1957-1963), then Savannah (DeSoto Hilton, operations director, 1963-1970), then Atlanta (Peachtree Plaza, general manager, 1970-1977). He developed a reputation in the hospitality industry as a turnaround specialist -- a hands-on operator who could take a struggling property and make it profitable through attention to detail, staff development, and genuine care for the guest experience.

In 1978, Tate approached Webb with a proposal: purchase and restore the Belmont Hotel, a once-grand Charleston landmark that had fallen into severe disrepair. Tate would provide the capital and social connections; Webb would run the business. The partnership was formed on a handshake before it was formed on paper.

The Belmont's restoration was Marcus Webb's masterwork. He personally supervised every aspect of the renovation -- selecting period-appropriate furnishings, restoring original heart pine floors, sourcing antique light fixtures from estate sales across the Lowcountry. He learned the history of the building so thoroughly that he could give impromptu tours to guests, explaining the architectural details of every hallway. "Marcus loved that hotel more than some men love their wives," his brother Thomas said. "It wasn't a business to him. It was a living thing."

**PERSONAL LIFE:**

Webb married Patricia Davenport in 1991. It was his second marriage (first marriage to Susan Kelly, 1962-1974, ended in divorce, no children). Patricia owned the Davenport Gallery on Church Street, specializing in Lowcountry watercolors. Friends described the marriage as loving but sometimes strained by Webb's devotion to the hotel. Patricia sensed he was keeping secrets in the months before his death -- he was, but about the fraud, not about another woman.

Webb had no children. His closest personal relationships outside his marriage were with Delilah Monroe, the Belmont's lounge singer (whom he mentored and considered a surrogate daughter), and Samuel Cross, his attorney and longtime confidant.

**HABITS AND CHARACTER:**

Colleagues and friends consistently describe Webb as hardworking, honest, detail-oriented, and private. He was not a socialite -- unlike Tate, he avoided Charleston society events when possible. He preferred to work.

Webb kept Room 416 as his personal office and occasional overnight retreat. He would often work late into the evening there, reviewing hotel operations, reading history and biography, or listening to jazz records on a portable player. The room was furnished to his personal taste -- a writing desk, a settee, bookshelves, his bourbon decanter.

He was known to enjoy bourbon (Maker's Mark, neat) and was a moderate drinker -- two drinks maximum per evening, according to multiple sources.

**He did not smoke.** Multiple sources confirm this, including his wife, his physician, hotel staff, and personal friends. This is addressed in detail in the attached medical records.

He ordered the same dinner from room service almost every evening he worked late: grilled flounder, rice, and sweet tea. A working-class meal from a man who could have ordered anything on the menu. The mill foreman's son never fully left Columbia.

**FINANCIAL STATUS:**

Webb's personal assets at time of death were estimated at $1.2 million, including his 50% stake in the Belmont Hotel (valued at approximately $800,000), a residence on Tradd Street, personal savings of approximately $180,000, and a modest investment portfolio managed by Cross.

His will, updated October 14, 1993, left his estate to Patricia Webb, with a secondary bequest of $50,000 to the Charleston Arts Foundation. [NOTE: The will update has drawn media attention. See Packet 4.]

## Evidence Item 3.4 -- Webb & Tate Business History

[PRINT FORMAT: Typed summary document, formatted as a background research memo. Looks like it was prepared by a paralegal or research assistant. Clean, factual, slightly dry.]

---

RESEARCH MEMORANDUM
Prepared for: Det. L. Voss, Charleston PD
Re: Webb-Tate Partnership -- Business History
Date: November 2, 1993

**PARTNERSHIP FORMATION:**

The Belmont Hotel was purchased on April 12, 1978, by the Webb-Tate Hospitality Group, LLC, a South Carolina limited liability company formed by Marcus A. Webb and Gerald R. Tate. Purchase price: $340,000 (the property was in severe disrepair).

Financing: Tate provided $250,000 from family trust assets. Webb contributed $90,000 in personal savings and secured a $200,000 renovation loan from First Charleston Bank, personally guaranteed by both partners.

Ownership: 50/50 split. The operating agreement designated Webb as Managing Partner (day-to-day operations) and Tate as Financial Partner (accounting, banking, investor relations).

**RENOVATION AND GROWTH:**

The Belmont underwent a comprehensive renovation from 1978 to 1982 at a total cost of approximately $600,000. The project was featured in Southern Living (March 1983) and Historic Preservation Magazine (Fall 1983). The hotel was listed on the National Register of Historic Places in 1984.

By 1985, the Belmont was profitable, generating approximately $1.2M in annual revenue with a net margin of 12-15%. Occupancy rates averaged 78% annually, well above the Charleston market average.

**FINANCIAL MANAGEMENT:**

Per the operating agreement, Gerald Tate maintained exclusive control over the hotel's financial accounts, bookkeeping, vendor relationships, and tax filings. Marcus Webb had access to financial records but, by his own admission to attorney Samuel Cross, "trusted Gerald with the money side." This trust was understandable given their 35-year friendship, but it also meant that Tate operated without meaningful oversight for fifteen years.

Webb's focus was operational: he hired and trained staff, maintained the property, managed guest relations, and ensured the hotel's reputation. He was, by all accounts, exceptional at this work. But his operational focus meant the financial side of the business was a black box he rarely opened.

**THE RIFT:**

According to Cross, Webb first became suspicious of financial irregularities in March 1993 when a routine external accountant's review flagged discrepancies between vendor invoices and actual services rendered. Specifically, the accountant noted that the hotel's linen expenditures -- approximately $78,000/year -- were roughly 250% of what a 72-room hotel would normally spend. The accountant recommended that Webb investigate.

Webb spent the spring and summer of 1993 quietly pulling vendor files from the business office (Room 420) and conducting his own review. He discovered that "Coastal Textile Services" -- which billed the hotel $4,000-6,000 per month -- had no listed address, no phone number, no employees, and no filing with the SC Secretary of State. He found similar anomalies with "Lowcountry Consulting Group."

By July 1993, Webb had compiled evidence of what he believed was systematic embezzlement and money laundering totaling approximately $1.4 million over eight years. He shared this evidence with Cross and stated his intention to report the findings to the Internal Revenue Service.

Cross advised Webb to secure his documentation before confronting Tate. Webb reportedly confronted Tate in August 1993 despite this advice. The nature of that conversation is known only through Webb's account to Cross: Tate denied everything and became "cold and menacing."

Webb kept copies of the financial documents in Room 416 and with Cross's office. The copies in Room 416 were destroyed in the fire.

---

CHARLESTON POLICE DEPARTMENT
WITNESS STATEMENT (EXCERPT)

**Case:** 93-HOM-0842
**Date:** October 22, 1993
**Witness:** Patricia Anne Webb, nee Davenport
**Statement taken by:** Det. Lorraine Voss, Badge #2847

[...]

**DET. VOSS:** Mrs. Webb, can you tell me about your husband's daily routine?

**P. WEBB:** Marcus was at the hotel nearly every day. He'd arrive by eight in the morning and often wouldn't come home until nine or ten at night. He kept Room 416 as his office -- he said he thought better there than at a desk. Sometimes he'd stay overnight if he was working late. I learned to live with it. The Belmont was his first love. I was his second. [Pause] I don't say that bitterly. It was simply true. I knew it when I married him.

**DET. VOSS:** Was there anything unusual about his behavior in recent weeks?

**P. WEBB:** Yes. Marcus was... distracted. More than usual. He was losing weight -- not eating properly. He'd come home and go straight to his study and close the door. Sometimes I'd hear him on the phone late at night. Once, I heard him say, "I don't care what it costs, Samuel. I want this done right." I assume that was his attorney.

**DET. VOSS:** Did he tell you what was troubling him?

**P. WEBB:** Not directly. Marcus was protective of me. He didn't want to burden me with hotel business. But I could tell something was wrong between him and Gerald. They'd been like brothers for thirty years. Suddenly Marcus wouldn't mention Gerald's name without his jaw tightening. I asked him once, about a month ago, if everything was all right with the partnership. He said -- and I remember this exactly -- "The partnership is over, Patricia. Gerald just doesn't know it yet."

**DET. VOSS:** Did he elaborate?

**P. WEBB:** No. But the look on his face frightened me. Not angry. Sad. As if he'd lost something he could never get back.

[...]

**DET. VOSS:** One more question. The will change on October 14 -- can you explain that?

**P. WEBB:** [Sighs] It was Marcus's idea. He wanted to add the Charleston Arts Foundation as a beneficiary. He said -- let me remember his exact words -- he said, "I want to leave something behind that matters, in case anything goes sideways." I asked him what he meant by "sideways." He kissed my forehead and said not to worry. [Long pause] "In case anything goes sideways." I've replayed those words every night since the fire.

---

[Voss's note:]
*"Patricia Webb is not a suspect. She is a woman who lost her husband and was made a scapegoat by a media that preferred a scandalous widow to an uncomfortable truth. The will change was Marcus's way of preparing for the worst. He knew what Gerald was capable of. He was putting his affairs in order. -- LV"*

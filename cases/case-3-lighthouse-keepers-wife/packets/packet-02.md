## Evidence Piece 1: Lighthouse History

[PRINTED ON: Cream-colored paper with slightly yellowed edges. Serif book font. Formatted as a book excerpt with page numbers and running header.]

*Excerpt from* **Lights of the Maine Coast: A History**
*by Professor Harold T. Kimball, University of Maine Press, 1958*

---

**DUNMORE POINT LIGHT**

The lighthouse at Dunmore Point, three miles north of Crestfall village in Cumberland County, was constructed in 1887 by the United States Lighthouse Board at a cost of $18,000. It replaced an earlier signal — a simple iron basket fire maintained by local fishermen atop a granite cairn — that had marked the treacherous approach to Crestfall Harbor since the 1830s. The basket fire was tended on a volunteer basis by generations of Crestfall men, most notably the Dunmore family, for whom the point was eventually named. Captain Josiah Dunmore, who lobbied Congress for eleven years to fund a proper lighthouse, died in 1886, three months before construction began. He never saw his name on the point, but his persistence ensured that it would be there.

The granite for the lighthouse foundation was quarried from the headland itself — a practical decision that also had the poetic effect of making the lighthouse literally part of the rock on which it stands. The bricks were manufactured at the Houghton Kiln in Bath and shipped up the coast by schooner. Construction took fourteen months and employed twenty-three men, twelve of whom were Crestfall locals. The project was supervised by Mr. Horace Leighton of the Lighthouse Board's First District office in Portland.

The tower is a conical brick structure standing sixty-seven feet from base to lantern room, painted white with a single red horizontal band at mid-height — the "daymark" that allows mariners to identify the light by sight during daylight hours. The light itself is a fourth-order Fresnel lens manufactured by Henri-Lepaute of Paris in 1886, a masterwork of precision optics that focuses the light from a single kerosene flame into a beam visible at a distance of fourteen nautical miles in clear conditions. The lens is mounted on a clockwork mechanism that rotates it continuously, producing the characteristic pattern of one white flash every ten seconds. The clockwork is weight-driven, similar in principle to a grandfather clock: a heavy weight on a cable descends slowly through the tower, and the keeper must climb to the mechanism room every four hours during the night to rewind it.

A fog signal — a diaphone horn powered by compressed air — was added in 1911 following a series of groundings in the approaches to Crestfall Harbor. The horn produces a deep, resonant blast every thirty seconds that can be heard at a distance of five nautical miles and felt, as a physical vibration, at a distance of one. During prolonged fog events, the horn may run continuously for days. The compressor requires regular maintenance, and the sound is, by universal testimony, deeply oppressive to anyone living within earshot. Mrs. Eliza Craddock, wife of the third keeper, returned to Portland after a single winter in 1893, writing to her sister that "the sound of the sea never stops, not even in sleep, and the fog horn is the voice of madness itself."

The keeper's dwelling, attached to the tower's south face, is a modest Cape Cod-style cottage of one and one-half stories, with clapboard siding, a slate roof, and a central chimney. It contains a kitchen, a sitting room, two bedrooms (upstairs), and a small water closet added during renovations in 1934. The dwelling is adequate but isolated; the nearest neighbor is more than two miles distant, and the road to Crestfall is unpaved and frequently impassable during winter storms. Supplies are delivered by road when conditions permit and by boat when they do not. The supply vessel from the Coast Guard depot at Portland calls approximately once per month with kerosene, lamp wicks, cleaning supplies, and official correspondence.

Dunmore Point has had twelve keepers since 1887. The position has never been considered desirable. The isolation is profound, the winters punishing, and the sea — visible from every window of the cottage, audible from every room — is a constant, indifferent companion that offers beauty and menace in equal measure, often simultaneously. Several keepers' wives have found the posting intolerable. In addition to Mrs. Craddock, Mrs. Alice Thornton (wife of the seventh keeper, 1921-1928) reportedly suffered a nervous collapse during the winter of 1924 and was taken to Portland by boat in a February storm, wrapped in blankets and unable to speak.

The current keeper, Edward T. Marsh, assumed the position in 1957. He is, by all accounts, well suited to the work — a quiet man who prefers the company of machinery to that of people, and who finds in the routine demands of the light a purpose that the wider world has not provided him. His wife, Catherine, has adapted to lighthouse life with greater grace than many of her predecessors, involving herself in the Crestfall community — attending church functions, participating in the library book club — and maintaining what visitors describe as a warm and inviting home despite the isolation. She is known locally for her watercolor paintings of the coastline and for her collection of pressed wildflowers gathered from the headland.

The lighthouse at Dunmore Point remains an active aid to navigation, though the United States Coast Guard, which assumed responsibility for all lighthouses in 1939, has indicated that automation may be considered within the decade. If automated, Dunmore Point would join the growing number of Maine lights that no longer require a human keeper — a development that some regard as progress and others as the quiet extinction of a way of life that has defined the New England coast for two centuries.

## Evidence Piece 3: Keeper's Cottage Description

[PRINTED ON: Standard police report paper. Typewriter font.]

**CRESTFALL POLICE DEPARTMENT**
**SUPPLEMENTAL REPORT — CASE 62-0041**
**Subject: Description of Keeper's Dwelling, Dunmore Point**
**Officer: Constable R. Hale**
**Date: March 10, 1962**

Visited the keeper's dwelling at Dunmore Point on March 10, 1962, to complete my description of the premises for the case file.

**Exterior:** Cape Cod style, white clapboard, attached to the south face of the lighthouse tower. Slate roof, central chimney. Red front door with brass knocker (anchor motif). Small covered porch with two wooden chairs. Garden (dormant, winter) along the south wall — appears well-tended even in the off season, with mulched beds and a low stone wall to protect from wind. Stone path from porch to cliff path. No exterior lighting between the cottage and the cliff path — the only light would come from the cottage windows and, when operating, the lighthouse beam itself, which sweeps across the headland every ten seconds.

**Interior — Ground Floor:**

*Kitchen:* Clean and well-ordered. Wood-burning stove (Glenwood, c. 1940) with chrome trim, well-maintained. Icebox (not electric refrigerator — the cottage has limited electrical service from a generator). Dishes from Saturday evening dinner still on the drying rack — four place settings, mismatched stoneware. Half a pot of coffee, cold, on the stove. A wooden spice rack on the wall with twelve jars, hand-labeled in feminine handwriting. A tide table pinned to the wall beside the window, with certain dates circled in pencil.

*Sitting Room:* Modest but comfortable. Upholstered settee (faded floral print), two armchairs (one clearly Edward's — more worn, positioned facing the window). Stone fireplace, fire burned to ash. Mantelpiece holds a clock (stopped — not wound), a brass ship's bell, and a framed photograph of a young couple in front of a lighthouse (see Evidence Piece 4). Bookshelf with approx. 50 volumes — novels (Agatha Christie, Daphne du Maurier, Rebecca, My Cousin Rachel, several Brontes, complete Jane Austen, a dog-eared paperback of The Moonstone), tide tables, lighthouse service manuals, a Bible (King James, worn), and two volumes of Shakespeare's Complete Works. A small writing desk by the window with a blotter, pen, ink, stationery, and **one sealed envelope on the desk, stamped and addressed to "Mrs. Margaret Sullivan, 14 Elm Street, Bangor, Maine."** I did not open the letter, as it did not appear relevant to the incident. [NOTE: This letter was mailed by Edward with Catherine's other personal effects. It is the March 6 letter to Maggie.]

*Notable:* Several watercolor paintings on the walls — seascapes and wildflower studies, all apparently by Mrs. Marsh. The quality is good — she had a real eye for color and light. A wooden press containing dried flowers and ferns was on a side table beside the settee, with a small notebook listing each specimen. A basket of knitting (cream-colored yarn, half-finished scarf) beside Catherine's chair.

**Interior — Upper Floor:**

*Master Bedroom:* Bed made on the deceased's side — hospital corners, pillow smooth. Mr. Marsh's side unmade — he had been on watch. Mrs. Marsh's nightgown (white cotton, lace at the collar) folded on her pillow, untouched. She had not changed for bed. A hairbrush on the dresser with several auburn hairs. A small framed watercolor of wildflowers on the wall above the bed. A Bible on the nightstand on Mrs. Marsh's side, with a pressed flower (lavender) as a bookmark at Psalm 27. One pair of house shoes beneath the bed on Mrs. Marsh's side.

*Guest Room:* Bed unmade. Bottle of Old Crow bourbon on the nightstand, approximately one-quarter full. Glass with amber residue. Mr. William Marsh stated he retired at approximately 1930 hrs and "had a few drinks and fell asleep." Mrs. Helen Marsh stated she joined him at approximately 2100 hrs. The window faces west — away from the cliff path.

**Interior — Lighthouse Tower (accessible from cottage via interior door):**

The tower is accessed through a heavy wooden door in the back wall of the sitting room. A spiral iron staircase ascends 67 feet to the lantern room, with a landing at the mechanism room level (approximately 50 feet).

Watch room at top of tower contains the Fresnel lens and its clockwork mechanism, a desk, a wooden chair, the keeper's log, a small oil heater, and a tin cup. Mr. Marsh spends his night watches here — approximately 7:30 PM to 6:00 AM — monitoring the light, winding the clockwork every four hours, and recording conditions in the log. The windows provide a 360-degree view of the surrounding ocean and headland.

Below the watch room, the mechanism room houses the clockwork weight system that rotates the lens. This room is crowded with gears, cables, the weight mechanism, and a workbench with tools. There are several built-in compartments in the walls, originally intended for storing spare parts and supplies. The room is dimly lit by a single bare bulb. I did not conduct a thorough search of this room, as it appeared to contain only mechanical equipment and was not relevant to the incident.

*[Signed] Constable R. Hale*

[PRODUCTION NOTE: Hale's admission that he didn't search the mechanism room is critical — Catherine's diary is hidden there. Also note: Catherine's nightgown folded on her pillow (she never went to bed), the cold coffee on the stove (she was planning to stay up), the sealed letter to Maggie sitting in plain sight, the Bible bookmarked at Psalm 27 ("The Lord is my light and my salvation; whom shall I fear?"), and the bourbon bottle nearly empty in the guest room (William was unconscious). Every detail in this room description is either a clue or a piece of character.]

## Evidence Piece 8: Lighthouse Keeper's Regulations

[PRINTED ON: Stiff card stock, slightly yellowed. Official U.S. Coast Guard formatting. The kind of regulations posted on a wall.]

**UNITED STATES COAST GUARD**
**INSTRUCTIONS TO KEEPERS OF LIGHTS AND FOG SIGNALS**
*(Abridged — Posted at Station)*

**I. THE LIGHT**
1. The keeper shall ignite the light at sunset and extinguish it at sunrise each day without exception.
2. The lens and prisms shall be cleaned daily. The lamp shall be trimmed and filled before each lighting.
3. The clockwork mechanism shall be wound at intervals not exceeding four (4) hours during the period of operation.
4. The keeper shall maintain a continuous watch in the lantern room or watch room during all hours of darkness.

**II. THE FOG SIGNAL**
5. The fog signal shall be operated whenever visibility falls below five (5) nautical miles.
6. Once commenced, the signal shall not be discontinued until visibility exceeds five nautical miles and conditions are stable.
7. The keeper shall record all fog signal operations in the station log, including times of commencement and discontinuation.

**III. THE LOG**
8. The keeper shall maintain the station log with entries at minimum four (4) times daily: 0600, 1200, 1800, 2400 hours.
9. Entries shall record: weather conditions, temperature, wind direction and speed, sea state, and all operational activities.
10. The keeper may add personal remarks but is reminded that the log is an official government record and may be inspected at any time.

**IV. GENERAL DUTIES**
11. The keeper shall maintain the station, including the dwelling, grounds, and all structures, in good order.
12. The keeper shall render assistance to vessels in distress to the extent possible without abandoning the light.
13. The keeper shall not be absent from the station for more than four (4) hours without arranging a relief.
14. **The keeper shall not consume intoxicating liquors while on watch.**

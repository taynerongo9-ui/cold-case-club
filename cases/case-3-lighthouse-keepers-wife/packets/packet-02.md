**DUNMORE POINT LIGHT**

The lighthouse at Dunmore Point, three miles north of Crestfall village, was constructed in 1887 by the United States Lighthouse Board at a cost of $18,000. It replaced an earlier signal — a simple iron basket fire maintained by local fishermen — that had marked the treacherous approach to Crestfall Harbor since the 1830s.

The tower is a conical brick structure standing sixty-seven feet from base to lantern room, painted white with a single red horizontal band at mid-height. The light itself is a fourth-order Fresnel lens manufactured by Henri-Lepaute of Paris, producing a characteristic pattern of one white flash every ten seconds, visible at a distance of fourteen nautical miles in clear conditions. A fog signal — a diaphone horn powered by compressed air — was added in 1911 following a series of groundings in the approaches to Crestfall Harbor.

The keeper's dwelling, attached to the tower's south face, is a modest Cape Cod-style cottage of one and one-half stories, with clapboard siding, a slate roof, and a central chimney. It contains a kitchen, a sitting room, two bedrooms, and a small water closet added during renovations in 1934. The dwelling is adequate but isolated; the nearest neighbor is more than two miles distant, and the road to Crestfall is unpaved and frequently impassable during winter storms.

Dunmore Point has had twelve keepers since 1887. The position has never been considered desirable. The isolation is profound, the winters punishing, and the sea — visible from every window — is a constant, indifferent companion. Wives of keepers have historically found the posting difficult. Mrs. Eliza Craddock, wife of the third keeper, returned to Portland after a single winter, writing to her sister that "the sound of the sea never stops, not even in sleep, and the fog horn is the voice of madness itself."

The current keeper, Edward T. Marsh, assumed the position in 1957. He is, by all accounts, well suited to the work — a quiet man who prefers the company of machinery to that of people. His wife, Catherine, has adapted to lighthouse life with greater grace than many of her predecessors, involving herself in the Crestfall community and maintaining what visitors describe as a warm and inviting home despite the isolation.

The lighthouse at Dunmore Point remains an active aid to navigation. However, the United States Coast Guard has indicated that automation may be considered within the decade, a development that would end seventy years of continuous human presence on the headland.

## Evidence Piece 3: Keeper's Cottage Description

[PRINTED ON: Standard police report paper. Typewriter font.]

**CRESTFALL POLICE DEPARTMENT**
**SUPPLEMENTAL REPORT — CASE 62-0041**
**Subject: Description of Keeper's Dwelling, Dunmore Point**
**Officer: Constable R. Hale**
**Date: March 10, 1962**

Visited the keeper's dwelling at Dunmore Point on March 10, 1962, to complete my description of the premises for the case file.

**Exterior:** Cape Cod style, white clapboard, attached to the south face of the lighthouse tower. Slate roof. Red front door. Small covered porch. Garden (dormant, winter). Stone path from porch to cliff path. No exterior lighting between the cottage and the cliff path — the only light would come from the cottage windows and, when operating, the lighthouse beam itself.

**Interior — Ground Floor:**

*Kitchen:* Clean and well-ordered. Wood-burning stove (Glenwood, c. 1940). Icebox. Dishes from Saturday evening dinner still on the drying rack — four place settings. Half a pot of coffee, cold, on the stove.

*Sitting Room:* Modest but comfortable. Upholstered settee, two armchairs. Stone fireplace, fire burned to ash. Bookshelf with approx. 50 volumes — novels (mostly mysteries and English literature), tide tables, lighthouse service manuals. A small writing desk by the window with a blotter, pen, ink, and stationery. **One sealed envelope on the desk, stamped and addressed to "Mrs. Margaret Sullivan, 14 Elm Street, Bangor, Maine."** I did not open the letter, as it did not appear relevant to the incident. [NOTE: This letter was mailed by Edward with Catherine's other personal effects. It is the March 6 letter to Maggie.]

*Notable:* Several watercolor paintings on the walls — seascapes and wildflower studies. Mrs. Marsh was evidently an accomplished amateur artist. A wooden press containing dried flowers and ferns was on a side table.

**Interior — Upper Floor:**

*Master Bedroom:* Bed made on the deceased's side. Mr. Marsh's side unmade — he had been on watch. Mrs. Marsh's nightgown folded on her pillow, untouched. She had not changed for bed.

*Guest Room:* Bed unmade. Bottle of Old Crow bourbon on the nightstand, approximately one-quarter full. Glass with residue. Mr. William Marsh stated he retired at approximately 1930 hrs and "had a few drinks and fell asleep." Mrs. Helen Marsh stated she joined him at approximately 2100 hrs.

**Interior — Lighthouse Tower (accessible from cottage via interior door):**

Watch room at top of tower contains the lens mechanism, a desk, a chair, the keeper's log, and a small oil heater. Mr. Marsh spends his night watches here.

Below the watch room, the mechanism room houses the clockwork weight system that rotates the lens. This room is crowded with machinery and is not regularly occupied. I did not conduct a thorough search of this room.

*[Signed] Constable R. Hale*

[PRODUCTION NOTE: Hale's admission that he didn't search the mechanism room is significant — Catherine's diary is hidden there. Also note the sealed letter to Maggie Sullivan sitting in plain sight on the desk, unremarked upon.]

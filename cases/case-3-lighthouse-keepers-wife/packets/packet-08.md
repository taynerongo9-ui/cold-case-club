## Evidence Piece 1: Discovery of the Diary

[PRINTED ON: Cold Case Club analysis paper. Modern document providing context.]

**COLD CASE CLUB — INVESTIGATIVE ANALYSIS**
**Discovery of Catherine Marsh's Private Diary**

---

In the original incident report (Packet 1, Evidence Item #7), a small brass key was found in Catherine Marsh's coat pocket. Edward Marsh did not recognize it. Constable Hale did not investigate its purpose. The key was returned to Edward with Catherine's other personal effects and forgotten.

In April 1963, Agnes Doyle was at the lighthouse collecting more of Catherine's belongings when she found herself drawn to the lighthouse tower. She had been reading Catherine's letter to Maggie Sullivan (recovered from the cottage desk and forwarded to Maggie, who shared it with Agnes). In the letter, Catherine referenced her "special place" — "up high, where no one thinks to look, among the machinery."

Agnes searched the mechanism room — the cramped, gear-filled chamber below the watch room that houses the clockwork weight system for the rotating lens. Behind the main drive gear housing, she found a small brass-latched compartment built into the wall, originally intended for storing spare parts. The compartment was locked. Agnes tried the brass key from Catherine's coat.

It fit.

Inside, Agnes found:
- A small leather-bound diary (approximately 5" x 7", green cover)
- A loose envelope from St. Agnes Orphanage, Bangor, addressed to "Vera Novak" (this is a duplicate — Catherine had taken the original from Helen's coat and kept a copy of the address)
- A folded piece of paper with Catherine's handwritten notes about Helen's background claims and the inconsistencies she had found

The diary contains entries spanning November 1961 through March 7, 1962. Not all entries relate to Helen — many are personal reflections, notes about paintings, observations of the sea. The entries relevant to the investigation are reproduced below.

## Evidence Piece 2: Catherine's Diary — Selected Entries

[PRINTED ON: Pages designed to look torn from a small leather diary. Handwritten in Catherine's elegant script. Some entries have small sketches in the margins — a seashell, a bird, a flower. The entries about Helen are written in smaller, tighter handwriting, as if Catherine were being careful even with herself.]

---

**November 18, 1961**

The sea was wild today. I stood at the window in the watch room and watched the waves throw themselves at the rocks as though they were angry at the land for existing. Edward says I romanticize the ocean. He says the ocean doesn't feel anything — it just moves. But I think he's wrong. I think everything that moves, feels.

I am painting again. A watercolor of the view from the lantern room at sunset. The colors are impossible — I cannot mix the exact shade of gold that the light makes when it catches the Fresnel lens. But I am trying.

I feel more like myself than I have in months. The darkness is lifting. Not gone, but lifting. Like fog.

---

**December 9, 1961**

William and Helen for the weekend. Helen brought a fruitcake from some Portland bakery — very fine, very expensive. She does this. She brings gifts that are slightly too generous, as though she is purchasing goodwill. Edward says I am uncharitable. Perhaps I am.

But I notice things about Helen that trouble me. Small things. Tonight at dinner, William mentioned their wedding — the flowers Helen had chosen. Helen said they were lilies. William said, "No, darling, they were roses. White roses." Helen laughed and said, "Of course, roses. I don't know where my mind was."

A bride who forgets her wedding flowers?

---

**December 22, 1961**

Christmas preparations. I have been making gifts — a scarf for Mama, a pipe rest for Edward (carved from driftwood, rather rough, but he'll appreciate the effort). For William and Helen, I knitted a pair of mittens. Helen will not wear them. She wears leather gloves, always, even indoors. As if she is afraid of leaving fingerprints.

I am being unfair again. She is probably just cold.

---

**January 14, 1962**

A thought that will not leave me alone.

Last month Helen told Mrs. Callahan at church that her maiden name was Stewart. But I distinctly remember William introducing her to Mama at their first visit as "Helen Hennessey." I mentioned this to Edward. He said I must be mistaken. Perhaps I am.

But I am not.

---

**January 28, 1962**

Helen claims to be from Kennebunk. She says she attended Kennebunk High School. Last week, I wrote to the Kennebunk Public Library and asked whether they had a copy of the 1949 high school yearbook — the year Helen would have graduated, if she is the age she claims. The librarian was very kind. She looked up "Helen Hennessey" in the yearbook.

There is no Helen Hennessey in the 1949 Kennebunk High School yearbook. Nor in 1948 or 1950.

It could mean nothing. She might have attended under a different name. She might have the year wrong. People misremember.

But combined with everything else — the flowers, the maiden name, the way she watches me — I am beginning to wonder whether Helen is who she says she is at all.

---

**February 12, 1962**

Dorothy Pembrook told me something today that has turned my suspicions into something harder. Something close to certainty.

Helen has been receiving mail at general delivery in Crestfall under the name "V. Novak." Not Helen Marsh, not Helen Hennessey, not Helen Stewart. V. Novak. Dorothy says the letters come from a P.O. Box in Boston and, on one occasion, from St. Agnes Orphanage in Bangor.

An orphanage.

Helen has told everyone she grew up with parents in Kennebunk. But she receives mail from an orphanage under a different name.

I need to think carefully about what this means.

---

**February 25, 1962**

William and Helen visited this weekend. I have been watching Helen more carefully than ever, and I think she has noticed. She is watching me too. We are two women circling each other across the dinner table, each pretending to be at ease.

Tonight, while Helen was in the sitting room and Edward and William were talking in the kitchen, I hung Helen's coat by the fire to dry — it had been raining. A letter fell from the pocket. I picked it up. The envelope was addressed to **Vera Novak, P.O. Box 2247, Boston, Massachusetts.** The return address was **St. Agnes Home, 47 State Street, Bangor, Maine.**

I did not open the letter. But I copied down the addresses before replacing it in her coat.

Vera Novak. That is the name. V. Novak. Vera.

Who is Vera Novak? And if Helen IS Vera Novak, then who is the woman my brother-in-law married?

---

**February 28, 1962**

I wrote to St. Agnes Orphanage today. I asked whether a woman named Vera Novak had been a resident there. I said I was a relative trying to locate her for a family matter. I do not know if they will respond.

I also went to the Crestfall Library and looked through old Portland Press Herald copies. I was searching for any mention of a Helen Hennessey or Helen Marsh. I did not find what I was looking for, but I found something else. A small notice in the October 1958 edition:

*"MARSH, Helen R., 27, of Boston, died October 14, 1958, in a single-vehicle automobile accident on Route 1 in Wiscasset. Miss Marsh was a secretary. She is survived by no immediate family."*

Helen Marsh died in 1958.

William married Helen Marsh in 1960.

My hands are shaking as I write this.

---

**March 2, 1962**

I have not slept well in days. I keep turning this over and over.

The Helen Marsh that William married is not the Helen Marsh who died in October 1958. She cannot be. The real Helen Marsh is dead. The woman living in Portland, sleeping in William's bed, sitting at our dinner table — she is someone else. She is Vera Novak. She took a dead woman's name and married my husband's brother.

But why?

The money. It must be the money. Grandfather Silas's trust. William inherits when he is married and Edward turns thirty-five. William is already past thirty-five. The only thing standing between William — and his wife — and that money is time. Two more years until Edward qualifies, and then the trust pays out.

Vera Novak married William Marsh to get the Marsh money. And she is using a dead woman's identity to do it.

I must tell Edward. But I am afraid of what this will do to William. He loves Helen — or the woman he believes is Helen. This will destroy him.

---

**March 6, 1962**

I have written to Maggie. I have told her enough that if something happens, there is a record. I feel foolish writing that — "if something happens" — as though I am a character in one of my mystery novels. But Vera has been watching me with an intensity that frightens me. At dinner last visit, she caught me staring at her, and the look she gave me — just for a moment, before the mask came back — was not the look of a sister-in-law. It was the look of a woman calculating a threat.

William and Helen arrive tomorrow for the weekend. I will speak with Edward first, privately, and then we will confront Helen together.

God give me courage.

---

**March 7, 1962**

They arrived at half three. William is in good spirits. Helen is wearing a new blue dress and a smile that does not reach her eyes. I went to confession this afternoon and spoke with Father Connolly. I could not tell him everything — not the name, not the details — but I told him enough. He told me to pray and to trust my judgment.

I will speak with Edward after his watch. No — I will speak with Helen first. Alone. I want to give her the chance to explain. Perhaps there is an explanation I have not considered. Perhaps I am wrong about all of it.

But I am not wrong. I know I am not wrong.

This is the last entry.

---

[PRODUCTION NOTE: The diary is the emotional core of the case. Catherine's voice should be vivid, intelligent, and increasingly frightened. The progression from idle curiosity (December) to active investigation (January-February) to near-certainty (March) mirrors a detective's process. She's piecing together the same puzzle the subscriber is solving. The March 7 entry — written less than 24 hours before her death — should be devastating. Print on pages that look torn from a small, well-worn diary. Handwriting should be Catherine's established script but with increasing tension in the later entries — tighter spacing, heavier pen pressure, occasional words underlined.]

**SKETCH: LIGHTHOUSE MECHANISM ROOM — HIDING PLACE**
*Drawn by Agnes Doyle from memory, 1963*

[PRODUCTION NOTE: A simple cross-section sketch showing the lighthouse tower interior. The mechanism room is below the watch room. The sketch shows the large clockwork drive gear housing (labeled), and behind it, a small wall-mounted compartment (labeled "BRASS LATCH — LOCKED" with an arrow). The compartment is roughly the size of a shoebox. A note in Agnes's handwriting: "Catherine hid her diary here. The brass key from her coat pocket (Item #7 in police evidence) opens this compartment. ## Evidence Piece 4: Agnes Doyle's Letter to the State Police

[PRINTED ON: Personal stationery, typed. Agnes has upgraded from handwritten notes to typed letters — she is getting more formal, more organized, more relentless.]

Mrs. Agnes M. Doyle
22 Maple Street
Crestfall, Maine

May 3, 1963

Lieutenant James P. Farnsworth
Maine State Police — Criminal Investigation Division
36 Hospital Street
Augusta, Maine 04330

Dear Lieutenant Farnsworth,

I have written to your office three times previously regarding the death of my daughter, Catherine Anne Marsh, at Dunmore Point, Crestfall, on March 8, 1962. I have received no substantive response. I am writing a fourth time because I have now obtained evidence that was not available to Constable Hale, and which I believe constitutes grounds for reopening this case.

Enclosed please find:

1. A copy of my daughter's private diary, recovered from a hidden compartment in the Dunmore Point Lighthouse mechanism room. The diary documents Catherine's discovery that her sister-in-law, Helen Marsh, is living under a false identity. The name "Vera Novak" and the connection to St. Agnes Orphanage in Bangor are described in detail.

2. A copy of Catherine's letter to Mrs. Margaret Sullivan of Bangor, dated March 6, 1962 — two days before Catherine's death — in which Catherine states: "I've found something about Helen that changes everything."

3. A statement from Dorothy Pembrook, Crestfall Postmistress, confirming that Helen Marsh collected mail at general delivery under the name "V. Novak."

4. A copy of the National Weather Service observation report for March 8, 1962, confirming that fog cleared by 4:00 PM — hours before Catherine died. The official ruling of accidental death in fog is contradicted by the weather record.

5. A statement from Captain Josiah Briggs, lobsterman, confirming that conditions on the evening of March 8 were clear, with no fog.

I am not a detective. I am a sixty-year-old schoolteacher. But I can read, and I can reason, and what I have read and reasoned tells me this: my daughter discovered that Helen Marsh is a fraud, and she was killed to keep that secret.

I ask again — for the fourth time — that the Maine State Police open an investigation into the death of Catherine Anne Marsh.

Respectfully but firmly,
Mrs. Agnes M. Doyle

---

*[Handwritten at the bottom, in red pen, apparently added by Agnes after receiving a response:]*

*Received form letter dated May 28, 1963. "Thank you for your concern. The matter has been reviewed and does not meet the threshold for state investigation at this time." They did not even read the enclosures. The envelope was returned unopened.*

---

[PRODUCTION NOTE: Agnes's letter is meticulous and devastating. She has done the police's job for them and they won't even open the envelope. Her red-pen addition at the bottom should make subscribers furious on her behalf.]

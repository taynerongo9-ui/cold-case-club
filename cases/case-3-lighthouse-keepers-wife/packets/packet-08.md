## Evidence Piece 1: Discovery of the Diary

[PRINTED ON: Cold Case Club analysis paper.]

**COLD CASE CLUB — INVESTIGATIVE ANALYSIS**
**Discovery of Catherine Marsh's Private Diary**

---

In the original incident report (Packet 1, Evidence Item #7), a small brass key was found in Catherine Marsh's coat pocket. Edward Marsh did not recognize it. Constable Hale did not investigate its purpose. The key was returned to Edward with Catherine's personal effects on March 25, 1962, and placed in a dish on the kitchen windowsill at the cottage, where it sat undisturbed for over a year.

In April 1963, Agnes Doyle — who had by then been conducting her own investigation for more than a year — visited the lighthouse. She had been reading Catherine's letter to Maggie Sullivan (the March 6 letter, which Edward had mailed with Catherine's other effects and Maggie had shared with Agnes). In the letter, Catherine referenced her "special place" — "up high, where no one thinks to look, among the machinery."

Agnes knew her daughter. She knew Catherine's childhood habit of hiding treasured things in high, unlikely places — the inkwell in the schoolhouse, the rafters of the Doyle shed, a hollow fence post in the garden. She asked Edward about the tower, and he led her up the spiral staircase — sixty-seven feet of iron steps that made Agnes's knees ache — past the watch room to the mechanism room below.

The mechanism room is a cramped, dim chamber crowded with the clockwork weight system that rotates the Fresnel lens: massive brass gears, steel cables, the weight mechanism, and a workbench with tools. The room smells of machine oil and cold stone. A single bare bulb hangs from the ceiling. Behind the main drive gear housing, Agnes found a small brass-latched compartment built into the wall, approximately the size of a shoebox. The compartment had been part of the original construction — intended for storing spare parts and supplies.

The compartment was locked. Agnes asked Edward about the brass key from Catherine's coat. He retrieved it from the kitchen windowsill.

It fit.

Inside, Agnes found:
- A small leather-bound diary (approximately 5" x 7", green cover, gold-edged pages)
- A loose envelope from St. Agnes Orphanage, Bangor, addressed to "Vera Novak" at P.O. Box 2247, Boston (Catherine had taken the envelope from Helen's coat and kept it)
- A folded piece of paper with Catherine's handwritten notes listing Helen's background claims and the inconsistencies she had found
- A pressed sprig of rosemary (symbolism: remembrance)

Agnes sat on the floor of the mechanism room and read the diary by the light of the single bulb. She read it twice. Then she climbed down the sixty-seven steps with the diary pressed against her chest like a shield and sat in Catherine's chair in the sitting room and wept — not from grief, though grief was there, but from vindication. Her daughter had seen the truth, documented the truth, and hidden the proof in the most unlikely place in the compound. And Agnes had found it.

Constable Hale had been in this room. He had written in his supplemental report: "I did not conduct a thorough search of this room." Catherine's testimony was twenty feet from his head, locked in a compartment he never tried, opened by a key he never questioned.

## Evidence Piece 2: Catherine's Diary — Selected Entries

[PRINTED ON: Pages designed to look torn from a small leather diary. Handwritten in Catherine's elegant script. Some entries have small sketches in margins — a seashell, a bird, a wildflower. Later entries are in tighter, smaller handwriting.]

---

**November 18, 1961**

The sea was wild today. I stood at the window in the watch room and watched the waves throw themselves at the rocks as though they were angry at the land for existing. Edward says I romanticize the ocean. He says the ocean doesn't feel anything — it just moves. But I think he's wrong. I think everything that moves, feels. The tide comes in and the tide goes out and the water touches the same rocks ten thousand times, and if that isn't something like devotion, I don't know what is.

I am painting again. A watercolor of the view from the lantern room at sunset — the moment when the Fresnel lens catches the last horizontal light and the whole tower fills with gold. The colors are impossible. I have mixed every shade of yellow and amber and ochre on my palette and none of them is right. The light that the lens makes is not a color you can buy in a tube. It is a color that only exists in that room, at that hour, for that moment, and then it is gone. But I am trying.

I feel more like myself than I have in months. The darkness is lifting. Not gone — I don't think it will ever be entirely gone; I think it lives in me now, a permanent tenant — but it is quieter. I can hear the other things again: the gulls, the wind, the scratch of my brush on paper, the sound of Edward winding the clockwork at ten o'clock, steady as a heartbeat.

I am coming back. I am here.

---

**December 9, 1961**

William and Helen for the weekend. Helen brought a fruitcake from a Portland bakery — very fine, very expensive, wrapped in gold foil. She does this. She brings gifts that are slightly too generous, as though she is purchasing goodwill. A bottle of French wine for Edward (who drinks bourbon and would not know French wine from dishwater). A silk scarf for me (lovely, but I live in a lighthouse — where would I wear silk?). The gifts are always slightly wrong — not in kind, but in scale. They are the gifts of a woman who has studied what people like but does not intuitively understand it.

At dinner, William mentioned their wedding — the flowers Helen had chosen. Helen said they were lilies. William said, "No, darling, they were roses. White roses. I remember because I pricked my finger on a thorn and bled on my cuff." Helen laughed and said, "Of course, roses. I don't know where my mind was."

A bride who forgets her wedding flowers. I have thought about this for three days and I cannot explain it away. I remember every detail of my wedding — the dress (Mama's, altered), the flowers (sea lavender and beach roses from the headland, bound with ribbon), the hymns (Amazing Grace and Be Thou My Vision), the way Edward's hands shook when he said his vows. These are not things a woman forgets. Unless she was not really there. Unless the wedding was a performance rather than a sacrament.

I am being suspicious. Uncharitable. Perhaps Helen simply has a poor memory. Perhaps some brides forget.

But I don't think so.

---

**December 22, 1961**

Christmas preparations. I have been making gifts — a scarf for Mama, a pipe rest for Edward carved from driftwood (rather rough, but he will appreciate the effort), a bookmark for Maggie made from pressed fern. For William and Helen, I knitted a pair of mittens. Helen will not wear them. She wears leather gloves, always, even indoors — at the dinner table, in the sitting room, as if she is afraid of touching things directly. Of leaving prints. Of being traced.

I am being unfair. She is probably just cold. Portland people feel the cold differently from Crestfall people. We are accustomed to it; they are merely subjected to it.

But the gloves bother me.

---

**January 14, 1962**

A thought that will not leave me alone, no matter how many times I push it away.

Last month, Helen told Mrs. Callahan at church that her maiden name was Stewart. I was standing near enough to hear it clearly. But I distinctly remember — vividly, with certainty — that when William first brought Helen to Crestfall in the autumn of 1960, he introduced her to Mama as "Helen Hennessey." Not Stewart. Hennessey.

I mentioned the discrepancy to Edward. He said I must be mistaken. "People get confused about names," he said. "It doesn't mean anything."

Perhaps he is right. But I am not mistaken. I have my mother's memory for detail, and I heard what I heard. A woman's maiden name is not something that changes.

Unless she has more than one.

---

**January 28, 1962**

I have done something I am not sure I should have done. I wrote to the Kennebunk Public Library. Helen claims to have attended Kennebunk High School — she has mentioned it several times, always casually, always in passing, as one does when establishing a background that is meant to be taken for granted. I asked the librarian whether they had copies of the high school yearbooks from the late 1940s, and whether a Helen Hennessey appeared in any of them.

The librarian was very kind. She checked the 1948, 1949, and 1950 yearbooks — the years Helen would have graduated, depending on her exact age.

There is no Helen Hennessey in any of them. No Helen Stewart either, for that matter. No Helen at all who matches the description.

This could mean nothing. She might have attended under a different name — her married name, if she was married before, which she says she wasn't. She might have the graduation year wrong. People misremember. And Kennebunk is a real town with a real high school, and the librarian confirmed it exists, so at least the school is real, even if Helen's presence there is not.

But combined with the flowers, the maiden name, the gloves, the gifts that are always slightly wrong — I am beginning to wonder whether Helen Marsh is who she says she is at all.

---

**February 12, 1962**

Dorothy Pembrook told me something today that has turned my suspicions into something harder. Something close to certainty.

Helen has been receiving mail at general delivery in Crestfall under the name "V. Novak." Not Helen Marsh. Not Helen Hennessey. Not Helen Stewart. V. Novak. Dorothy says the letters come from a P.O. Box in Boston (2247) and, on one occasion, from St. Agnes Orphanage in Bangor.

An orphanage.

Helen has told everyone she grew up with parents in Kennebunk. But she receives mail from an orphanage under a different name.

I walked home on the cliff path with the wind in my face and the sea booming below and my mind was racing so fast I could barely keep my footing. V. Novak. An orphanage in Bangor. A woman who doesn't know her own wedding flowers and can't keep her maiden name straight and wears gloves as though she's hiding her hands.

I need to think carefully about what this means. I need to think very carefully.

---

**February 25, 1962**

William and Helen visited this weekend. I have been watching Helen more carefully than ever, and I believe she has noticed. She is watching me too. We are two women circling each other across the dinner table, each pretending to be at ease, each taking the other's measure. I smiled at her and she smiled at me and neither of us meant it and both of us knew it.

Tonight, while Helen was in the sitting room and Edward and William were talking in the kitchen about the boathouse repairs, I took Helen's coat from the hook to hang it by the fire to dry — it had been raining on their drive up. A letter fell from the inside pocket. An envelope.

I picked it up. My hands were shaking.

The envelope was addressed to **Vera Novak, P.O. Box 2247, Boston, Massachusetts.** The return address was **St. Agnes Home, 47 State Street, Bangor, Maine.**

I stood by the fire with that envelope in my hand and the rain running down the windows and the sound of Edward's voice in the kitchen, and I knew — with a certainty that settled into my bones like cold — that Helen Marsh is Vera Novak. She is a woman from an orphanage in Bangor who has taken another person's name and married my husband's brother.

I did not open the letter. I wanted to — God knows I wanted to — but I could not bring myself to read another person's mail, even a person who is living under false pretenses. Instead, I copied down both addresses in my notebook and replaced the letter in her coat pocket. My hands were steady by then. Steadier than they had any right to be.

Vera Novak. That is the name. V. Novak. Vera.

Who is Vera Novak? And if Helen IS Vera Novak, then who is the woman my brother-in-law married?

---

**February 28, 1962**

I wrote to St. Agnes Orphanage today. I asked whether a woman named Vera Novak had been a resident there. I said I was a relative trying to locate her for a family matter. It was not entirely a lie — if Vera is Helen, then she is family, after a fashion. A fraudulent fashion.

I also went to the Crestfall Library and asked Mrs. Quinn to bring out the Portland Press Herald archives from 1958. I was searching for any mention of Helen Marsh. I spent two hours turning fragile pages, my eyes aching, the microfiche machine humming. I did not find what I expected. I found something worse.

A small notice in the October 1958 edition. Deaths and obituaries. Seven words that changed everything:

*"MARSH, Helen R., 27, of Boston, died October 14, 1958, in a single-vehicle automobile accident on Route 1 in Wiscasset. Miss Marsh was a secretary. She is survived by no immediate family."*

Helen Marsh died in 1958.

William married Helen Marsh in 1960.

My hands are shaking as I write this. The pen is trembling on the page and I can see the ink wobbling in my letters and I do not care about my penmanship right now because I have just found the proof that the woman in my brother-in-law's house is not his wife. She is someone else entirely. She is an orphan named Vera Novak who stole a dead woman's name and married William Marsh and sits at my dinner table wearing a dead girl's smile.

God help me. God help us all.

---

**March 2, 1962**

I have not slept well in days. I lie beside Edward in the dark and listen to the clockwork ticking in the tower above our heads and I turn this over and over like a stone I cannot put down.

The Helen Marsh that William married is not the Helen Marsh who died in October 1958. She cannot be. The real Helen Marsh is dead — buried in Boston, alone, with three people at her funeral, none of them family. The woman living in Portland, sleeping in William's bed, sitting at our dinner table, watching me with those careful eyes — she is someone else. She is Vera Novak. An orphan. A secretary. A woman who saw an opportunity in a dead girl's name and took it.

But why? Why go to such lengths? Why steal an identity, build a life on it, marry a man under false pretenses?

The money. It must be the money. Grandfather Silas's trust. The Marsh Family Trust, which pays out when both Edward and William are married and Edward turns thirty-five. William is already past thirty-five. He is married — or believes he is. The only thing standing between William and his $60,000 is Edward's age. Two more years. And then the trust pays out, and Vera — as William's wife — gets access to the money.

Vera Novak married William Marsh to get the Marsh money. She is using a dead woman's identity to do it. The marriage is a fraud. The identity is stolen. The trust will be distributed based on a lie.

And I am the only person who knows.

I must tell Edward. But I am afraid — not of Helen, not yet, though perhaps I should be — but of what this will do to William. He loves Helen. He truly loves her, or the woman he believes she is. This will destroy him. It will destroy the family. And yet the truth is the truth, and my mother taught me that the truth is a debt you owe to the people you love, even when — especially when — it is painful to pay.

---

**March 6, 1962**

I have written to Maggie. I have told her enough that if something happens, there is a record. I feel foolish writing that — "if something happens" — as though I am Miss Marple instead of a lighthouse keeper's wife who reads too many mystery novels. But Vera has been watching me with an intensity that frightens me. At dinner last visit, she caught me staring at her across the table, and the look she returned — just for a moment, before the mask came back — was not the look of a sister-in-law. It was the look of a woman calculating a threat.

I have also hidden this diary in the mechanism room, in the compartment behind the drive gear. The brass key is in my coat pocket. If anything happens to me — and nothing is going to happen to me, I am being dramatic, but IF — the diary is here, and the key is with me, and Maggie knows where to look.

William and Helen arrive tomorrow for the weekend. I will speak with Edward first, privately, before his watch. Then we will confront Helen together.

God give me courage.

## Evidence Piece 4: Agnes Doyle's Letter to the State Police

[PRINTED ON: Typed, personal stationery.]

Mrs. Agnes M. Doyle
22 Maple Street
Crestfall, Maine

May 3, 1963

Lieutenant James P. Farnsworth
Maine State Police — Criminal Investigation Division
36 Hospital Street
Augusta, Maine 04330

Dear Lieutenant Farnsworth,

I have written to your office three times previously regarding the death of my daughter, Catherine Anne Marsh, at Dunmore Point, Crestfall, on March 8, 1962. My first letter was dated March 28, 1962. My second was dated June 15, 1962. My third was dated October 4, 1962. I received no substantive response to any of them — only form acknowledgments that my letters had been "received and noted."

I am writing a fourth time because I have now obtained evidence that was not available to Constable Hale, and which I believe constitutes grounds for reopening this case as a homicide investigation.

Enclosed please find:

1. A copy of my daughter's private diary, recovered from a hidden compartment in the Dunmore Point Lighthouse mechanism room. The diary documents Catherine's discovery that her sister-in-law, Helen Marsh, is living under a false identity. The real name is Vera Novak, and the connection to St. Agnes Orphanage in Bangor is established beyond doubt.

2. A copy of Catherine's letter to Mrs. Margaret Sullivan of Bangor, dated March 6, 1962 — two days before her death — in which Catherine states she has discovered something about Helen that "changes everything."

3. A statement from Dorothy Pembrook, Crestfall Postmistress, confirming that Helen Marsh collected mail at general delivery under the name "V. Novak."

4. A copy of the National Weather Service observation report for March 8, 1962, confirming that fog cleared by 4:00 PM — five hours before Catherine died. The official ruling of accidental death in fog is contradicted by the official weather record.

5. A statement from Captain Josiah Briggs confirming clear conditions at the harbor all evening of March 8.

6. The medical report of Dr. Thomas Greer documenting bilateral grip bruising on Catherine's upper arms inconsistent with an accidental fall.

I am not a detective, Lieutenant. I am a sixty-year-old schoolteacher who has spent the last year doing the work that Constable Hale should have done and that your office should have supervised. I can read, and I can reason, and what I have read and reasoned tells me this: my daughter discovered that Helen Marsh is a fraud. She was killed to keep that secret. The fog was gone. The bruises are real. The diary is in her own hand.

I ask — for the fourth time — that the Maine State Police open an investigation into the death of Catherine Anne Marsh.

Respectfully but firmly,
Mrs. Agnes M. Doyle

---

*[Handwritten at the bottom, in red pen:]*

*Received form letter dated May 28, 1963. "Thank you for your concern. The matter has been reviewed and does not meet the threshold for state investigation at this time. We recommend you contact your local law enforcement." They did not read the enclosures. I know this because the enclosures were returned to me — still sealed in their envelope, the tape unbroken. They sent back my daughter's diary without reading a word of it.*

*I will write again. I will write until they listen or until I die.*

## Evidence Piece 7: Catherine's Final Diary Entry

[PRINTED ON: Single diary page, printed separately for emphasis. Heavier handwriting, slight tremor.]

**March 7, 1962**

William and Helen arrived at half past three. William is in good spirits as always — he has brought bourbon and stories from Portland and that booming laugh that fills the cottage like a wave filling a tide pool. He does not know what I know. He does not know that the woman beside him is not his wife — not legally, not truly, not in any way that matters.

Helen is wearing a new blue dress and a smile that does not reach her eyes. She complimented my chowder recipe and asked about my painting and laughed at the right moments and said all the correct things, and I watched her do it the way I watch the lighthouse mechanism turn — with an appreciation for the precision and a keen awareness that it is all machinery.

I went to confession this afternoon. I told Father Connolly everything — the name, the orphanage, the death certificate. He listened without speaking, his hands folded, his face growing more grave with every sentence. When I finished, he said: "This is a very serious matter, Catherine. Are you certain of your facts?" I said I was. He said: "Then you must act on them. But act carefully. And act with someone beside you."

After confession, in the parish hall, I told him what I could say outside the seal — about a person in the family living under false pretenses, about a fraud. He advised me to speak with Edward. I will.

I WILL speak with Edward. I tried this morning — I went to him in the kitchen before the others were awake and said, "Edward, I need to talk to you about Helen. Something is very wrong." And he said — I could have predicted it, I have loved this man for seven years and I know every word he will say before he says it — he said, "Can't it wait? I need to check the compressor before the fog comes in." And I said, "No, it can't wait." And he said, "All right. Tonight, then. After my watch."

Tonight, then. After his watch. Which means after midnight. Which means another evening of sitting across the table from Vera Novak and pretending I do not know what I know.

No. I will not wait until after midnight. I will speak with Helen first. Alone. Tonight, after Edward goes to the tower and William drinks himself to sleep. I will give her the chance to explain. Perhaps — perhaps — there is an explanation I have not considered. Perhaps the orphanage letters are innocent. Perhaps the death certificate is a coincidence. Perhaps a bride really can forget her wedding flowers.

But I am not wrong. I know I am not wrong. The evidence is in this diary, in the addresses I copied, in the newspaper obituary, in the pattern of small lies that add up to one enormous fraud. And tonight I will face the woman behind the fraud and tell her that I see her.

I am frightened. I will not write that I am not, because this diary is the place where I have promised myself to tell the truth, and the truth is that I am frightened. Not of violence — Helen is not a violent woman, or at least she has never seemed to be. But of the unknown. Of what happens when you pull the last thread and the whole garment comes apart. Of what it will do to William. Of what it will do to Edward. Of what it will do to this family, which is held together by love and silence and the rhythm of the light, and which may not survive the truth.

But the truth is a debt. Mama taught me that. And I am going to pay it.

This is the last entry I will make before I speak with Helen. If I am wrong about all of it, I will write tomorrow with relief and embarrassment and probably a very large glass of Edward's bourbon. If I am right — and I am right — then tomorrow I will write about the hardest conversation of my life, and the beginning of the end of something, and perhaps the beginning of something else.

Pray for me.

---

*There are no more entries.*

## Evidence Piece 9: Letter from Agnes to Edward

[PRINTED ON: Personal stationery, handwritten in Agnes's firm schoolteacher's hand.]

April 8, 1963

Dear Edward,

I found Catherine's diary.

It was in the mechanism room, behind the drive gear, in a locked compartment. The brass key from her coat pocket — the one Constable Hale did not bother to investigate — opened it.

You need to read what is in this diary, Edward. Our Catherine — your wife, my daughter — spent the last weeks of her life investigating Helen. She discovered that Helen is not who she says she is. The diary is detailed, precise, and devastating.

I am not going to tell you what to do. You are a grown man and you will make your own decisions. But I am going to tell you what I believe: I believe Catherine was killed because of what she found. I believe she confronted Helen on the cliff path that night. And I believe Helen pushed her.

Come to my house when you are ready. I will have tea and the diary and whatever strength I can give you.

I love you, Edward. You were a good husband to my daughter. You loved her, even if you did not always know how to show it. She knew. She always knew.

Agnes

## Evidence Piece 1: The Orphanage Letter

[PRINTED ON: Institutional letterhead — plain, slightly stiff paper. "St. Agnes Home for Children" with a simple cross logo. Typed in an older typewriter font, slightly uneven. The envelope should be included as a separate piece — addressed to "Miss Vera Novak, P.O. Box 2247, Boston, Massachusetts" with a forwarding note in postal handwriting: "FWD to: V. Novak, c/o General Delivery, Crestfall, ME." This is the letter Catherine found in Helen's coat on February 25, 1962.]

**ST. AGNES HOME FOR CHILDREN**
*47 State Street, Bangor, Maine*
*Telephone: Bangor 2-4100*
*Operated by the Sisters of Mercy*

January 8, 1962

Miss Vera Novak
P.O. Box 2247
Boston, Massachusetts

Dear Vera,

Thank you for your letter of December 28 and for the generous donation enclosed. Your continued support of St. Agnes is a blessing, and the children benefit greatly from your kindness.

You asked, as you have before, whether we have been able to locate any information regarding your birth parents. I regret to tell you that our records from 1931 contain no additional details beyond what we have previously shared. You were brought to St. Agnes as an infant of approximately two weeks by a woman who identified herself only as an aunt. She left no name, no address, and no documentation. The name "Novak" was assigned to you by Sister Margaret, who was on duty that evening, after a family she had known in her hometown.

I understand that this must be a disappointment. Many of our former residents carry this same burden — the not knowing. But I want you to know, as I have told you before, that the Sisters remember you well and with great affection. You were a bright and determined child, and whatever path your life has taken since leaving us, I hope it has brought you some measure of the happiness you deserve.

Sister Bernadette, who taught you piano, is still with us, though her health is declining. She asked me to tell you that she remembers your playing and that she prays for you.

If there is anything further we can do, please do not hesitate to write.

Yours in Christ,

Sister Mary Kathleen O'Brien
Director, St. Agnes Home for Children

---

[PRODUCTION NOTE: This letter is poignant. Vera is not a monster — she's a woman with no family, no roots, no identity, who has been sending money back to the orphanage that raised her. The letter humanizes her without excusing what she did. Catherine found this letter in Helen's coat, and it was the piece that confirmed "V. Novak" was not a friend — it was Helen herself. Print on institutional-looking letterhead. Include the envelope as a separate physical piece with the forwarding address visible.]

## Evidence Piece 3: The Real Helen Marsh

[PRINTED ON: Cold Case Club analysis paper with attached newspaper clipping reproduction.]

**COLD CASE CLUB — INVESTIGATIVE ANALYSIS**
**Profile: Helen Ruth Marsh (the real Helen Marsh)**

---

**Born:** March 3, 1931, in Kittery, Maine
**Died:** October 14, 1958, in Wiscasset, Maine

Helen Ruth Marsh was the only child of Robert and Edna Marsh (no relation to the Crestfall Marshes — Marsh is a common surname in Maine). She grew up in Kittery, attended Kittery schools, and moved to Boston in 1953 to work as a secretary at Pemberton & Associates, a small law firm on State Street.

Helen was, by all accounts, a quiet and unremarkable young woman. She had few close friends, no siblings, and both parents died before she was twenty-five (Robert in 1952, Edna in 1954). She was, in effect, alone in the world — a fact that would later make her identity easy to steal.

Helen met William Marsh socially in Boston in 1957 through mutual acquaintances. They went on several dates but the relationship did not develop seriously before Helen's death.

On October 14, 1958, Helen was driving alone on Route 1 near Wiscasset, Maine, when her car left the road and struck a tree. She died at the scene. The accident was attributed to wet road conditions. There was no foul play.

**The identity theft:** Vera Novak was working as a secretary at a different Boston law firm when she learned of Helen's death — likely through the legal community grapevine or through the small obituary notice in the Boston Globe. The key factors that made Helen's identity attractive to Vera:

1. Helen had no surviving close family — no one to say "that's not Helen."
2. Helen was approximately the same age and general physical type as Vera (dark-haired, slender).
3. Helen had a casual connection to William Marsh — providing a plausible basis for "reconnecting."
4. Helen's personal documents (Social Security card, birth certificate) could be obtained through the law firm or public records.

Vera spent approximately eighteen months building the Helen Marsh identity before approaching William in Portland in early 1960. By then, enough time had passed that William's memory of the real Helen was hazy, and the "Helen" who reappeared was charming, attentive, and eager to reconnect.

They married in June 1960.

---

| Nov 1958 - Feb 1960 | Boston, MA | No employment record under "Vera Novak" | P.O. Box 2247 (new) | **15-month gap.** Vera is becoming Helen. Obtaining documents, building the new identity. |
| **Mar 1960** | **Portland, ME** | Appears as **"Helen Marsh"** | William Marsh's address | "Reconnects" with William. They marry June 1960. |
| Jun 1960 - present | Portland, ME | Homemaker | 84 Brackett St. (William's residence) | Vera Novak ceases to exist on paper. Helen Marsh takes her place. |

**Analysis:** The fifteen-month gap between Helen Marsh's death (October 1958) and "Helen's" reappearance in Portland (March 1960) is the transformation period. During these months, Vera obtained Helen's birth certificate, Social Security card, and other identity documents. She studied Helen's background, her connection to William Marsh, and the Marsh family finances. This was not an impulsive act — it was a carefully planned assumption of identity.

The P.O. Box (2247, Boston) was Vera's bridge between her two lives — the only remaining thread connecting Vera Novak to Helen Marsh. It was this thread that Dorothy Pembrook noticed, that Catherine Marsh pulled, and that ultimately cost Catherine her life.

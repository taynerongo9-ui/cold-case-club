## Evidence Piece 1: The Orphanage Letter

[PRINTED ON: Institutional letterhead. Include a separate physical ENVELOPE addressed to "Miss Vera Novak, P.O. Box 2247, Boston, Massachusetts" with a postal forwarding note: "FWD to: V. Novak, c/o General Delivery, Crestfall, ME."]

**ST. AGNES HOME FOR CHILDREN**
*47 State Street, Bangor, Maine*
*Telephone: Bangor 2-4100*
*Operated by the Sisters of Mercy*

*Ref: 44.8012N-068.7778W*

January 8, 1962

Miss Vera Novak
P.O. Box 2247
Boston, Massachusetts

Dear Vera,

Thank you for your letter of December 28 and for the generous donation of twenty dollars enclosed. Your continued support of St. Agnes is a blessing, and the children benefit greatly from your kindness. We have used your donation and others to purchase new blankets for the dormitory, which were sorely needed — the old ones had been mended so many times they were more thread than wool.

You asked, as you have before, whether we have been able to locate any information regarding your birth parents. I regret to tell you that our records from 1931 contain no additional details beyond what we have previously shared with you. You were brought to St. Agnes on February 14, 1931, as an infant of approximately two weeks, by a woman who identified herself only as "an aunt." She was middle-aged, dark-haired, well-dressed, and visibly distressed. She said: "Her name is Vera. Please take care of her." She left no surname, no address, no documentation of any kind. She did not return, and despite inquiries by the Sisters at the time, she was never identified.

The name "Novak" was assigned to you by Sister Margaret, who was on duty that evening. She chose the name from a family she had known in her childhood town of New Prague, Minnesota. It was, she told me later, the first name that came to her mind. I have always regretted that we could not give you a name that meant something more — but then, you have made the name your own, and perhaps that is meaning enough.

I understand that this must be a disappointment, as it has been each time you have asked. Many of our former residents carry this same burden — the not knowing, the absence where a family should be. I have watched children grow to adulthood in these halls, and the ones who were never claimed carry a wound that no amount of care or education can fully heal. You were one of those children, Vera, and I have thought of you often over the years.

But I want you to know, as I have told you before, that the Sisters remember you well and with great affection. You were a bright and determined child — perhaps too determined, I sometimes thought, too watchful, too skilled at reading the currents of adult emotion and adjusting your sails accordingly. I worried about you. I worried that the world would take your gifts and use them against you, or that you would use them against yourself. I hope I was wrong.

Sister Bernadette, who taught you piano from age eight until you left us, is still with us at St. Agnes, though her health is failing. She is eighty-one now and her hands are too stiff for the keys, but her mind is sharp and her memory is long. She asked me to tell you that she remembers your playing — Chopin, particularly the nocturnes — and that she prays for you every night before vespers. She said: "Tell Vera I still hear her playing when the house is quiet."

If there is anything further we can do for you, or if you would like to visit St. Agnes, you would be most welcome. We are not much changed — the building is older, the Sisters are fewer, the children are just as loud and just as hungry and just as in need of love.

Yours in Christ,

Sister Mary Kathleen O'Brien
Director, St. Agnes Home for Children

---

[PRODUCTION NOTE: This letter humanizes Vera profoundly. She has been sending money back to the orphanage, asking about her birth parents year after year, and the nuns remember her with genuine affection. The coordinate reference number (44.8012N, 068.7778W) is the Cipher 5 Easter egg — these are the geographic coordinates of Bangor, Maine. Print on institutional letterhead. Include the envelope as a separate physical piece.]

**MEDICAL HISTORY:** Childhood diseases (measles 1936, chicken pox 1938). No significant health issues. Dental work (2 fillings) 1945. Height at age 18: 5'7". Weight: 128 lbs.

**ADOPTION HISTORY:** Never adopted. Showed to three prospective families (1933, 1935, 1937). All passed. Notes suggest the child was "too old" by the second showing and "too self-possessed" by the third — prospective parents wanted younger, more visibly needy children.

---

**EDUCATION:** Attended St. Agnes School (on premises), grades 1-12. Above-average student. Particular aptitude for reading, composition, and music (piano). Certificate of completion, June 1949.

**BEHAVIORAL NOTES (selected):**

- *Age 6 (1937):* "Vera is a quiet child who observes more than she speaks. She is well-liked by the other children but forms few close attachments. She has an unusual ability to adapt her behavior to her surroundings — cheerful with the Sisters, assertive with older children, protective of younger ones. A chameleon." — Sr. Margaret

- *Age 8 (1939):* "Vera has begun piano lessons with Sr. Bernadette and shows real talent. She practices without being asked — an unusual trait in a child her age. When I asked her why she works so hard at it, she said: 'Because when I play, I'm someone.' I found this both touching and troubling." — Sr. Mary Kathleen

- *Age 10 (1941):* "Vera asked me today why she doesn't have a mother. I told her that God loves all His children equally. She looked at me with those dark eyes and said: 'Then why did He give me to strangers?' I had no good answer. None of us do." — Sr. Bernadette

- *Age 14 (1945):* "Vera has become adept at presenting different faces to different people. This concerns me. She tells each Sister what that Sister wants to hear. She is polite to visitors, charming to donors, gentle with the younger children, and shrewd with her peers. She is not dishonest, exactly — she does not tell lies so much as she performs truths selectively. She chooses which version of herself to present, and she does it with a sophistication that I have not seen in a child her age. I worry about what happens when a young woman learns that the truth is less useful than a well-told fiction." — Sr. Mary Kathleen

- *Age 17 (1948):* "Vera has expressed interest in leaving St. Agnes after completing her schooling. She has no family to go to and no prospects beyond what we can arrange. She is intelligent enough for college but we have no funds for it and the scholarship opportunities for orphans are vanishingly small. She says she wants to be a secretary — 'because secretaries know everything and no one notices them.' I fear she will disappear into the world and we will lose track of her. She has a gift for reinvention that is either her greatest strength or her greatest danger. I pray she uses it wisely." — Sr. Mary Kathleen

**DATE OF DEPARTURE:** June 15, 1949 (aged out of program)
**DEPARTING POSSESSIONS:** One suitcase (provided by St. Agnes), two dresses, one coat, undergarments, toiletries, certificate of completion, rosary (gift from Sr. Bernadette), $12.00 (gift from the Sisters' personal funds)
**FORWARDING ADDRESS:** YWCA, Congress Street, Bangor, Maine
**SUBSEQUENT CONTACT:** Sporadic letters and modest donations, 1949-1962. Address changed multiple times. Last known address: P.O. Box 2247, Boston, MA.

## Evidence Piece 3: The Real Helen Marsh

[PRINTED ON: Cold Case Club analysis paper with newspaper clipping.]

**COLD CASE CLUB — INVESTIGATIVE ANALYSIS**
**Profile: Helen Ruth Marsh (the real Helen Marsh)**

---

**Born:** March 3, 1931, Kittery, York County, Maine
**Died:** October 14, 1958, Wiscasset, Lincoln County, Maine

Helen Ruth Marsh was the only child of Robert and Edna Marsh of Kittery (no relation to the Crestfall Marshes — Marsh is among the most common surnames in coastal Maine). She grew up in Kittery, attended Kittery schools, and moved to Boston in 1953 to work as a secretary at Pemberton & Associates, a small law firm on State Street.

Helen was, by all available accounts, a quiet and unremarkable young woman. She had few close friends. Both parents died before she was twenty-five: Robert of a heart attack in 1952, Edna of cancer in 1954. She had no siblings, no aunts or uncles who maintained contact, no cousins who claimed her. She was, in the most literal and devastating sense, alone in the world — a fact that made her identity uniquely vulnerable to theft.

Helen met William Marsh socially in Boston in 1957 through mutual acquaintances at a dinner party hosted by a colleague at Pemberton & Associates. William was in Boston on insurance business. They went on two dates — dinner and a movie, a walk along the Charles River. The relationship did not develop. Helen stopped returning William's calls. William, not a man to press, let it go.

On October 14, 1958, Helen was driving alone on Route 1 near Wiscasset, returning from a weekend trip to visit her parents' graves in Kittery. Her car — a 1955 Ford Fairlane — left the road on a wet curve and struck an oak tree. She died at the scene. The accident was attributed to speed and wet conditions. There was no foul play.

Three people attended her funeral: her employer, his wife, and a colleague. The funeral home noted: "A quiet burial for a quiet woman."

**The vulnerability:** Helen had no surviving family, few friends, and a modest paper trail. Her death was noted in a small obituary in the Boston Globe and in no other newspaper. She was buried at Mount Hope Cemetery in Boston under a simple headstone. Within weeks of her death, her apartment was cleared, her employment records closed, and her existence faded into the mundane archives of a city that processes thousands of deaths a year. She was, in every meaningful sense, forgotten.

It was this forgottenness that Vera Novak exploited.

## Evidence Piece 6: Letter from Sister Mary Kathleen to Agnes

[PRINTED ON: Institutional stationery, handwritten.]

September 15, 1963

Dear Mrs. Doyle,

Thank you for your letter regarding Vera Novak. I have read it with great distress, and I wish to respond as fully and honestly as I can.

Yes, Vera Novak was a resident of St. Agnes from infancy until age eighteen. She was one of our children — I use that phrase with all the weight it carries. She was raised by the Sisters, educated in our school, and sent into the world with nothing but a suitcase, a certificate, and twelve dollars from the nuns' personal savings. That is not enough. It was never enough. And I have spent twenty-five years as director of this institution knowing that it is not enough and being unable to do more.

You ask whether I believe Vera is capable of what you describe. I will answer honestly: I believe Vera Novak is capable of almost anything. She was the most adaptable child I have ever known — and I have known hundreds. She could become whatever a situation required. This is a survival skill, Mrs. Doyle. Children who are abandoned learn to read the room before they can read a book. They learn that the self is negotiable, that identity is performance, that love is earned through usefulness rather than given through grace. Vera learned these lessons earlier and more thoroughly than most.

But I do not believe she was born cruel. I believe the world was cruel to her first, and she learned its language.

I am not excusing what you believe she has done. If she harmed your daughter, then she must answer for it — before the law and before God. But I ask you to understand that the woman you are pursuing was once a two-week-old infant left on a desk by a stranger who did not even stay long enough to give her a surname. Everything Vera became, she became because no one ever told her she was enough as she was.

I will pray for your daughter. And I will pray for Vera. And I will pray for all of us who failed them both.

In Christ's sorrow,
Sister Mary Kathleen O'Brien

## Evidence Piece 8: Coordinate Cipher Note

[PRINTED ON: Cold Case Club analysis paper.]

**COLD CASE CLUB — INVESTIGATOR'S NOTE**
**The File Reference Number**

Alert investigators may have noticed that the St. Agnes orphanage letter (Evidence Piece 1) bears an unusual file reference: **Ref: 44.8012N-068.7778W**

This is not a standard institutional filing code. These are geographic coordinates.

44.8012 N, 68.7778 W corresponds to: **Bangor, Maine** — the location of St. Agnes Home for Children.

Whether this was an intentional coding by someone at St. Agnes (perhaps Sister Mary Kathleen, who was sharper than her institutional stationery suggested) or simply an artifact of the filing system, the coordinates confirm what the evidence already shows: the trail leads to Bangor. To an orphanage. To a girl named Vera.

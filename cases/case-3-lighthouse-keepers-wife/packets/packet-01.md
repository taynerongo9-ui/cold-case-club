## Evidence Piece 1: Case Reopening Letter

[PRINTED ON: Cold Case Club letterhead. Heavy cream stock. Typed in serif font with red "CLASSIFIED" stamp across top corner. Embossed CC logo at bottom.]

**COLD CASE CLUB**
*Case File #003*

Dear Investigator,

The file you are about to open has been closed for over sixty years.

On the morning of March 9, 1962, a lighthouse keeper named Edward Marsh descended from his watch in the tower at Dunmore Point, three miles north of Crestfall, Maine. He expected to find his wife, Catherine, asleep in their bed. The bed was made on her side. Her nightgown lay folded on her pillow, untouched. Her coat was not on the hook by the door. One pair of shoes was missing.

Edward searched the cottage. He searched the fog signal building. He searched the oil house. Then he walked to the cliff path, and he looked down.

Catherine Anne Marsh, age twenty-nine, lay on the granite rocks eighty feet below, at the edge of the incoming tide. She had been dead for approximately eight hours. Her wristwatch, shattered against the stone, had stopped at 9:14 PM.

The local constable — a decent man, a war veteran, a faithful public servant with no experience investigating violent death — arrived at the scene to find Edward cradling his wife's body on the rocks. Edward had climbed down to her an hour earlier and had not moved since. The scene was compromised. The body had been moved. The constable had no forensic training, no forensic equipment, and no reason to suspect anything beyond what appeared to be a terrible accident.

He ruled it an accident. Catherine, he concluded, had gone walking on the cliff path in dense fog and lost her footing. The path was narrow. There was no railing. The night had been foggy. It was tragic but explicable. The case was closed within two weeks.

There was only one problem with this theory: there was no fog.

The lighthouse keeper's own log — the meticulous, hourly record that every keeper was required to maintain — documents that the fog cleared at 8:00 PM that evening. The National Weather Service observation station at Crestfall Harbor confirms visibility of more than ten miles from 4:00 PM onward. A local lobsterman mending traps at the harbor that night stated he could see the lighthouse beam clearly and that the fog horn — which had been running for thirty hours — was silent. The three-quarter moon was up. The sky was clear.

Catherine Marsh did not stumble in the fog. Something else happened on that cliff path.

Catherine's mother, Agnes Doyle, spent the remaining twenty-two years of her life trying to tell someone. She was a schoolteacher — precise, methodical, relentless. She wrote forty-seven letters to the state police, the attorney general, and two successive governors of Maine. She gathered evidence. She took statements from witnesses. She found her daughter's hidden diary in a locked compartment inside the lighthouse mechanism room. She compiled a case file that would have done credit to a professional investigator.

No one listened. The state police returned her evidence packet unopened. The attorney general sent form letters. The governors did not respond at all. Agnes Doyle died on March 8, 1984 — the twenty-second anniversary of Catherine's death — with a box of evidence under her bed and the word "murder" on her lips.

We believe Agnes was right.

Over the next six months, you will receive twelve evidence packets containing the original investigative materials, witness statements, medical records, personal correspondence, and documents that Constable Hale either overlooked or never obtained. Some of these materials have never been examined together. Some contradictions have never been noticed. Some evidence has been waiting sixty years for someone with the patience and the perception to see what it means.

Pay attention to everything. The weather. The timestamps. The small details that don't quite fit. The people who should have spoken and didn't. The questions that were never asked.

Your task is to determine what truly happened on the night of March 8, 1962, on the cliff path at Dunmore Point.

Who killed Catherine Marsh? And why?

The evidence will guide you. Trust your instincts. Question everything — especially the official story.

Good luck, Investigator. Catherine has been waiting a long time.

*Cold Case Club — Investigative Division*

## Evidence Piece 2: Original Incident Report

[PRINTED ON: Aged goldenrod paper. Typewriter font (Courier). Official form layout with fields. Some words x'd out and retyped, as if done on a manual typewriter. Coffee ring stain in upper right corner. Stamp: "CASE CLOSED — MARCH 22, 1962"]

**CRESTFALL POLICE DEPARTMENT**
**INCIDENT REPORT**

**Case No.:** 62-0041
**Date of Report:** March 9, 1962
**Reporting Officer:** Constable Robert J. Hale, Badge #3
**Incident Type:** Unattended Death — Accidental

---

**DECEASED:** Catherine Anne Marsh (nee Doyle)
**Date of Birth:** June 14, 1933
**Age:** 29 years
**Address:** Dunmore Point Lighthouse, Lighthouse Road, Crestfall, Maine
**Occupation:** Homemaker
**Next of Kin:** Edward T. Marsh (husband), Agnes M. Doyle (mother, of 22 Maple Street, Crestfall)

**DATE OF INCIDENT:** March 8, 1962 (estimated)
**DATE DISCOVERED:** March 9, 1962, approx. 0615 hrs
**LOCATION:** Base of cliff, north face, Dunmore Point, Crestfall, Maine
**MANNER REPORTED:** Telephone call from Edward T. Marsh to Crestfall Police, 0632 hrs

---

**NARRATIVE:**

At 0632 hours on March 9, 1962, I received a telephone call from Edward T. Marsh, lighthouse keeper at Dunmore Point, reporting that he had discovered the body of his wife, Catherine A. Marsh, at the base of the cliff approximately 40 yards north of the boathouse stairs.

Mr. Marsh was in considerable distress during the call. He stated that he had found his wife at the bottom of the cliff and that she was dead. I instructed him to remain at the scene and not to move the body further. He stated he had already moved the body — he had turned her over and held her. I advised him that I would be there as quickly as possible.

I telephoned Dr. Thomas Greer at his residence and requested he meet me at Dunmore Point. I then proceeded to the lighthouse by automobile, arriving at the compound at 0745 hours.

Mr. Marsh was present at the cliff base with the body. He was seated on the rocks, holding his wife's hand. He appeared to have been crying. He stated he had been with her for approximately one hour before calling for assistance. He stated he had climbed down the boathouse stairs upon discovering her from the cliff path above. The body had been moved from its original position — Mr. Marsh stated he had turned her over, closed her eyes, and held her against his chest.

**DESCRIPTION OF SCENE:** The deceased was found on the rocks at the base of Dunmore Point cliff, approximately 80 feet below the cliff path. She was wearing a wool coat (dark green, one button missing — button not recovered), a gray cotton dress, stockings (one torn at the knee), and one shoe (brown leather, low heel, right foot). The second shoe was recovered on the cliff path above, approximately 12 feet from the cliff edge, at a point where the path narrows to approximately 3 feet and the ground is uneven with loose gravel over exposed granite. No railing or barrier exists at this section of the path.

The deceased's hair was loose and damp from sea spray. Her right arm was at her side; her left arm had been placed across her abdomen by Mr. Marsh. Her eyes were closed (also by Mr. Marsh). A wristwatch (Timex, ladies' model) on the deceased's left wrist had stopped at 9:14 PM — presumably the time of impact.

The rocks at the cliff base are rough granite, with tidal pools. At the time of my arrival (0745), the tide was low and receding. High tide would have reached the location of the body, and if discovery had been delayed significantly, the body might have been carried out by the current, which runs northeast along this stretch of coast.

**WEATHER CONDITIONS (night of March 8):** Dense fog reported throughout the day and evening. Visibility significantly reduced. [SEE CONSTABLE'S NOTE: Weather conditions were assessed based on general reports from townspeople. Lighthouse weather log was not consulted at time of initial report. See supplemental note filed March 11, 1962.]

**PERSONS PRESENT AT LIGHTHOUSE COMPOUND, NIGHT OF MARCH 8:**
1. Edward T. Marsh — husband, lighthouse keeper. States he was in the tower on evening watch from approx. 1930-0600 hrs. States he did not leave the tower during his watch. States he heard nothing unusual.
2. William R. Marsh — brother-in-law, visiting from Portland with his wife. States he retired to the guest room at approx. 1930 hrs and consumed whiskey. States he slept through the night and was unaware of any disturbance. States he woke at approximately 0630 to the sound of Edward calling for help from outside the cottage.
3. Helen V. Marsh — sister-in-law (wife of William), visiting from Portland. States she went to bed at approx. 2100 hrs. States she heard nothing unusual. States she was awakened by William at approximately 0630.

**PHYSICAL EVIDENCE:** The deceased sustained injuries consistent with a fall from significant height, including multiple fractures and contusions. Dr. Thomas Greer examined the body at scene (0900 hrs) and at his office (1100 hrs) and will provide a separate medical report (see Packet 5). Dr. Greer noted bruising on the deceased's upper arms but stated this could be consistent with impact during the fall. [NOTE: Dr. Greer expressed some reservation about this interpretation. I have included his concern in the record but do not believe it changes the overall finding.]

**ADDITIONAL NOTE:** The deceased's mother, Mrs. Agnes Doyle, arrived at the lighthouse at approximately 1000 hrs, having been notified by telephone. Mrs. Doyle was highly emotional and stated repeatedly that her daughter "would never have fallen" and "knew that path better than anyone alive." I noted Mrs. Doyle's concern but do not find it inconsistent with a grieving mother's natural reluctance to accept an accidental explanation.

**CONCLUSION:** Based on the physical evidence, witness statements, and weather conditions, it is my determination that Catherine A. Marsh died as a result of an accidental fall from the cliff path at Dunmore Point while walking in conditions of severely reduced visibility due to fog. The narrow path, uneven terrain, and absence of protective barriers at the fall point all support this conclusion.

No evidence of foul play was found at the scene. No further investigation is warranted at this time.

**Case Status:** CLOSED — Accidental Death
**Date Closed:** March 22, 1962

*Signed,*
*Constable Robert J. Hale*
*Crestfall Police Department*

## Evidence Piece 3: Hand-Drawn Map of Dunmore Point

[PRINTED ON: Heavy off-white paper. Illustrated map in a hand-drawn style with ink lines, as though sketched by a police officer or surveyor. Compass rose in corner. Scale bar at bottom. Key landmarks labeled with clean lettering. Weathered edges.]

**MAP: DUNMORE POINT LIGHTHOUSE COMPOUND & ENVIRONS**
*Prepared for Case File 62-0041*
*Drawn by Constable R. Hale, March 10, 1962*

**Key Features Labeled:**

- **Dunmore Point Lighthouse** (tower, 67 ft.) — northernmost point of headland. Conical brick, white with red horizontal band at mid-height. Fourth-order Fresnel lens.
- **Keeper's Cottage** — attached to lighthouse base, south-facing. Cape Cod style, one and one-half stories, white clapboard, slate roof, central chimney. Red front door. Two bedrooms (upper floor), kitchen, sitting room (ground floor). Interior door connects to lighthouse tower stairway.
- **Fog Signal Building** — 30 yards west of tower. Houses diaphone horn and compressor.
- **Oil House** — small stone structure, 15 yards south of tower. Stores kerosene and lamp oil. Iron door, slightly rusted hinges.
- **Boathouse** — at cliff base, accessible via wooden stairs (87 steps, steep). Small structure for storage and shore access.
- **Cliff Path** — unpaved footpath running along headland from lighthouse compound south to Lighthouse Road (approx. 1/4 mile). Width varies from 3 to 5 feet. No railing at any point. Surface: loose gravel over exposed granite.
- **"X" — LOCATION WHERE SHOE WAS FOUND** — on cliff path, approx. 200 yards from cottage, at a point where path narrows to 3 feet. Ground here slopes slightly toward cliff edge. Uneven surface. Cliff edge drops approximately 80 feet to rocks below.
- **"X" — LOCATION WHERE BODY WAS FOUND** — at base of cliff, directly below shoe location, on granite rocks 40 yards north of boathouse stairs. Approximately 6 feet from waterline at low tide.
- **Lighthouse Road** — unpaved road connecting cliff path to Main Road into Crestfall (3 miles). Single lane. Frequently impassable in winter storms.
- **Crestfall Village** — 3 miles south. Population: ~1,200. Harbor, Main Street, general store, post office, St. Brendan's Church.
- **Ocean** — North Atlantic. Currents run northeast along this stretch of coast. Rocky bottom, strong undertow.

**Notations in Hale's handwriting:**

*"Path extremely narrow at fall point. Ground slopes toward edge. Loose gravel — easy to lose footing, especially in fog or dark. No moon visible at time of incident per fog conditions."*

[PRODUCTION NOTE: The last notation is WRONG — there was a three-quarter moon and no fog. Hale wrote this on March 10, still operating under his incorrect assumption about the weather. Map should look hand-drawn and period-appropriate. Use ink-style illustration. Include dotted line showing the cliff path. Mark the two "X" locations prominently. The narrowness of the path at the fall point should be visually clear. Include small illustrations of the lighthouse, cottage, and boathouse.]

## Evidence Piece 5: Supplemental Weather Note

[PRINTED ON: Police report paper, typewriter font. Brief addendum filed two days after the initial report.]

**CRESTFALL POLICE DEPARTMENT**
**SUPPLEMENTAL NOTE — CASE 62-0041**
**Subject: Weather Conditions, Night of March 8, 1962**
**Officer: Constable R. Hale**
**Date: March 11, 1962**

---

Upon review of my initial report, I wish to add the following note regarding weather conditions.

In my incident report filed March 9, I described the weather on the evening of March 8 as "dense fog" with "visibility significantly reduced." This assessment was based on reports from several Crestfall residents who recalled foggy conditions during the day of March 8 and described the weather generally as "a foggy day."

I have since spoken with Mr. Arthur Kimball, harbor master and cooperative weather observer for the National Weather Service, who informs me that while fog was present during the morning and early afternoon of March 8, conditions cleared significantly by late afternoon. Mr. Kimball states that he reopened the harbor at approximately 4:00 PM due to improved visibility and that conditions remained clear through the evening.

I have not yet consulted the lighthouse keeper's weather log for the evening in question. Mr. Marsh has been in a state of considerable distress and I have not wished to disturb him further.

I do not believe this information changes the substance of my finding. The cliff path at Dunmore Point is hazardous in any conditions — fog, darkness, or simple inattention could cause a fall. The deceased may have walked the path before conditions fully cleared, or she may have simply stumbled in the dark. The path has no lighting and the ground is uneven.

This note is added for completeness of the record.

*Constable R. Hale*
*March 11, 1962*

[PRODUCTION NOTE: This supplemental note is devastating in hindsight. Hale was TOLD the fog had cleared. He acknowledged it. And then he rationalized it away: "fog, darkness, or simple inattention." He never checked the lighthouse log. He never revised his conclusion. He filed this note — which should have reopened the investigation — as a footnote and moved on. The subscriber should read this and realize that the case nearly broke open on Day 3 and then closed again because one man chose not to look further.]

Body found. Cancel MP report. Proceed to Dunmore Point.

*[And below that, in pencil, much later:]*

I should have filed this first. I should have treated it as a disappearance before I treated it as an accident. By the time I arrived, I had already decided what had happened. A man shouldn't decide before he looks.

— R.H., sometime after 1984

[PRODUCTION NOTE: The pencil note at the bottom was added by Hale years after the fact — the reference to "after 1984" suggests it was written after Agnes Doyle's death, perhaps triggered by guilt. This document shows Hale's investigative instincts were sound — he started a missing persons report — but he abandoned procedure the moment Edward reported finding the body. The pencil confession is one of the quietest, most powerful moments in the case.]

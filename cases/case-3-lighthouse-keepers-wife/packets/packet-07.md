## Evidence Piece 1: Dorothy Pembrook's Testimony

[PRINTED ON: Lined notebook paper, handwritten in a round, careful hand.]

**Statement of Dorothy Anne Pembrook**
**Postmistress, Crestfall, Maine**
**Given to Mrs. Agnes Doyle**
**June 14, 1963**

---

Agnes has asked me to write down everything I know about Helen Marsh's mail, and I will do it, though it has taken her asking three times before I found the courage. I want it understood that I am not a gossip. People say I am, and I suppose there is some truth in it, but there is a difference between noticing things because you are nosy and noticing things because it is your job to handle other people's correspondence and some things simply cannot be overlooked. I am the postmistress of Crestfall. I have held this position since 1938 — twenty-five years. Every piece of mail that enters or leaves this town passes through my hands. I sort it, I stamp it, I file it, I deliver it when the weather is too bad for people to come in themselves. I know the handwriting of every person in this town. I know who writes to whom and how often and from where. This is not snooping. This is professional competence.

Now. Helen Marsh — William's wife — she doesn't live in Crestfall, she lives in Portland, so I wouldn't normally handle her mail at all. But William and Helen visit Edward at the lighthouse with some regularity — every month or six weeks — and when they visit, Helen sometimes has mail forwarded to our office for pickup, or she arranges for items to be sent to Crestfall rather than to her Portland address. This in itself is not remarkable. Visitors do this occasionally. What is remarkable is the name on the mail.

I first noticed something peculiar in September 1961. A letter arrived at the Crestfall post office addressed to "V. Novak, c/o General Delivery, Crestfall, Maine." Now, we don't get much general delivery in Crestfall — this isn't a tourist town, and everyone who lives here has a proper address or a post office box. General delivery is for transients and travelers, and we don't see many of those between September and June. So a general delivery letter stands out.

I set it aside in the general delivery slot. Two days later, Helen Marsh came in — she and William were visiting that weekend — and asked if there was any general delivery mail. I showed her the letter addressed to V. Novak, and she took it. She said, quite smoothly, that it was for a friend who had asked her to pick it up. I thought that was a bit odd — why would a friend in Boston have mail sent to general delivery in Crestfall, Maine, instead of to Helen's Portland address? — but I am not the police, and people's mail is their own business, so I did not question it further.

Then it happened again in November 1961. Another letter, same format — "V. Novak, c/o General Delivery, Crestfall, Maine." This time I noted the return address: P.O. Box 2247, Boston, Massachusetts. Helen picked it up during her November visit. Same story about the friend.

In January 1962, a third letter came. This time the return address was different: St. Agnes Home, 47 State Street, Bangor, Maine. I know St. Agnes — it's the orphanage on State Street. My cousin's girl, Patty Fowler, volunteered there in the mid-fifties, helping with the younger children. I recognized the address immediately. And I thought: why is a woman from Portland having orphanage mail sent to general delivery in Crestfall under a name that isn't hers?

Helen collected this letter in January during their visit. She didn't mention the friend this time. She just asked for general delivery, took the letter, and left. She was wearing white leather gloves, I remember, and she handled the letter carefully, as though she didn't want to touch it directly.

I should have kept this to myself. I know that. A postmistress has an obligation to confidentiality, and I violated it, and Catherine Marsh may have died because of my loose tongue. I will carry that for the rest of my life. But on February 12, 1962, Catherine came into the post office to collect the lighthouse mail and buy stamps, and we were chatting — she always chatted with me, she was the friendliest of the lighthouse wives I've served — and I mentioned, more casually than I should have, that Helen had been picking up mail under a different name.

I said something like: "Your sister-in-law has a mysterious correspondent. Someone named Novak — V. Novak. Gets letters from that orphanage up in Bangor and a post box in Boston."

Catherine went very still. I can see it now — the way her face changed. Not shocked, exactly. More like... confirmed. As though I had told her something she had already suspected but hadn't been able to prove. She asked me to repeat the name. I said, "Novak. V. Novak." She asked if I had any of the letters. I did not — Helen had collected them all. Catherine asked if I remembered the exact return addresses. I gave her the Bangor address (St. Agnes Home, 47 State Street) and the Boston P.O. Box number (2247).

Catherine thanked me, bought her stamps (twenty of them — more than usual), and left. She was distracted as she walked out. Almost excited, though that seems the wrong word for what she must have been feeling. She walked out of my post office and down the street toward Lighthouse Road, and I watched her through the window until she disappeared around the bend, and I did not see Catherine Marsh alive again after that day.

After Catherine died, I thought about our conversation constantly. I lay awake at night wondering whether I should tell Constable Hale what I had told Catherine. But Hale had already closed the case. He had not asked me about mail — he had not asked me about anything. And I was afraid. If Helen was involved in something illegal, if the mail was connected to Catherine's death, then by telling Catherine what I knew, I had set in motion the chain of events that led to her falling — or being pushed — from that cliff. I did not want to be responsible. I did not want to be the person who had, however innocently, gotten Catherine killed.

I am telling Agnes now because she asked, and because she has been asking for months, and because I cannot look at her in church every Sunday and know that I am keeping a secret that might help her find the truth about her daughter's death. I should have spoken sooner. I carry that weight and I will carry it until I die.

One more thing. In March 1962 — after Catherine's death — I received one more letter for V. Novak at general delivery. Helen did not come to collect it. It sat in my office for thirty days and then was returned to sender per postal regulations. The return address was P.O. Box 2247, Boston, Massachusetts. I wrote the number down because by then I was suspicious. I wrote down everything.

**The mail for V. Novak stopped after Catherine's death.** Helen never arranged for general delivery in Crestfall again. As if she no longer needed the connection. Or as if the person she had been writing to — the person she had BEEN — was no longer relevant.

Dorothy Pembrook
June 14, 1963

P.S. — Agnes also asked me whether I had ever forwarded mail to unusual destinations. I should note that in August 1961, a tourist couple passing through Crestfall asked me to forward a letter to "Ashford, Oregon." I remember because I had to look it up — I'd never heard of the place. Small town, the woman said. Some sort of theatre community. Nothing to do with Helen or Catherine. But Agnes asked me to write down everything, so I am writing everything.

## Evidence Piece 3: Helen Marsh Character Sketch

[PRINTED ON: Cold Case Club analysis paper. Compiled from multiple residents.]

**COLD CASE CLUB — INVESTIGATIVE ANALYSIS**
**Character Profile: Helen Marsh (Mrs. William Marsh)**
*Compiled from statements by Crestfall residents, 1963-1964*
*Original interviews conducted by Agnes Doyle*

---

**Physical Description:** Dark-haired, approximately 5'7", slender, attractive. Always well-dressed — tailored dresses, good shoes, leather gloves even indoors. Applied makeup carefully. Carried herself with what multiple witnesses described as "poise" or "control." Mrs. Harriet Bowen: "She looked as if she were always about to leave — as if Crestfall were a waiting room and she was just passing through."

**General Impression:**

- *Mrs. Harriet Bowen:* "Helen was charming. She always knew the right thing to say. But there was something practiced about it — like an actress who has memorized her lines very well but hasn't quite made the part her own. I never felt I was seeing the real woman. I felt I was seeing a performance."

- *Mr. Ralph Garrett (general store):* "Nice enough lady. Bought expensive things when she was in town — the good coffee, imported cheese, things we don't sell much of. Paid cash always. Never put anything on account like everyone else. That struck me as odd — everyone in Crestfall has an account. But Helen always had cash ready, exact change usually. As if she didn't want her purchases on any record."

- *Mrs. Margaret Callahan (church auxiliary):* "Helen came to Mass with William when they visited, but she didn't seem comfortable in the church. She didn't know the responses — she was always half a beat behind, moving her lips after everyone else. I assumed she might be Protestant, but William said she was raised Catholic. Well, she didn't act like any Catholic I've ever known. She held the rosary like she'd never touched one before."

- *Arthur Kimball (harbor master):* "Didn't have much interaction with the lady myself. She wasn't the type to come down to the harbor. But I'll tell you this — she asked a lot of questions about the Marsh family whenever she visited. About Captain Silas, about the family history, about the shipping business, about Edward and William's inheritance. William told me once she was 'fascinated by family history,' but looking back, she was digging. She wanted to know about the money."

- *Josiah Briggs (lobsterman):* "I saw her once on the cliff path, watching the sea. Not the way Catherine watched it — Catherine looked at the sea like she was painting it in her head. Helen looked at it like she was measuring it. Calculating something. She gave me a nod when I passed but her eyes went right through me."

**Background (as told by Helen to various residents):**

Helen claimed to be from Kennebunk, Maine. She said her parents were deceased — father a fisherman, mother a homemaker. She said she attended Kennebunk High School and later worked as a secretary in Portland before meeting William.

**Inconsistencies noted:**

- *Dorothy Pembrook:* "She said Kennebunk, but when I mentioned the Shore Road clambake — it's a Kennebunk institution, every child in town goes — she looked blank. Just for a second, then she covered it. But I saw the blankness."

- *Agnes Doyle:* "Catherine told me in January that Helen had claimed her parents were buried at Green Lawn Cemetery in Kennebunk. Catherine checked. There are no Marshes buried at Green Lawn. And no Hennesseys either, for that matter."

- *Mrs. Callahan:* "She told me her maiden name was Stewart. But William told Ralph Garrett at the store that her maiden name was Hennessey. One of them was confused, or one of them was lying."

- *Mrs. Bowen:* "I asked her once about her family — just making conversation — and she said her mother had made the most wonderful apple pie. Then later that same visit, she told Catherine her mother 'couldn't cook to save her life.' Small thing. But I noticed."

**Relationship with Catherine:**

- *Agnes Doyle:* "Catherine tried to like Helen. She truly did. But she told me in late 1961 that something about Helen 'didn't sit right.' She said Helen watched her — closely, constantly — as if monitoring what Catherine knew. Catherine said: 'It's like being observed by someone taking notes. She studies me the way I study a painting — looking for details, for things that don't match. Except I'm the painting and she's the one deciding whether the composition is right.'"

**Behavior after Catherine's death:**

- *Mrs. Bowen:* "At the funeral, she was composed. Not a tear. William was a wreck — sobbing, couldn't get through the service. But Helen stood straight as a rod, hands folded, face like marble. I thought at the time: that's impressive self-control. Now I think: that's a woman with nothing to mourn."

- *Dorothy Pembrook:* "She stopped having mail sent to Crestfall after Catherine died. No more V. Novak letters. As if that part of her life was over — as if the threat had been removed and she no longer needed the connection."

## Evidence Piece 4: Gerald Whitfield Statement (Red Herring Resolution)

[PRINTED ON: State government letterhead.]

**STATE OF MAINE**
**DEPARTMENT OF TRANSPORTATION**
**HIGHWAY DIVISION**

**Memorandum**

**To:** File
**From:** Gerald R. Whitfield, Highway Surveyor
**Date:** April 15, 1962
**Re:** Route 1A Extension Survey — Crestfall Coastal Section

---

This memorandum documents my surveying activities in the Crestfall area during February 1962.

I was assigned to conduct preliminary surveying for the proposed Route 1A coastal extension between Bath and Crestfall, a project under consideration since 1959. My work in Crestfall took place February 5-23, 1962.

My activities required surveying the coastal terrain along the proposed route, including sections near Dunmore Point Lighthouse. I was in the vicinity of the lighthouse on February 8, 12, 15, and 19. On February 12, I spoke with Mr. Edward Marsh at the lighthouse. He was courteous, offered me coffee, and showed me the view from the tower. His wife was not present — she had walked to town.

I understand that my presence near the lighthouse was noted by Crestfall residents and that an "unfamiliar stranger with surveying equipment" became a subject of local speculation following Mrs. Marsh's death in March. I wish to confirm: my activities were entirely related to highway surveying. I had no interaction with Mrs. Marsh. I was not in the Crestfall area at any time in March 1962. My final day of work in Crestfall was February 23.

The Route 1A extension was subsequently shelved due to budget constraints and remains unfunded.

*Gerald R. Whitfield*
*Highway Surveyor, Grade III*

## Evidence Piece 5: Dorothy's Diary Entry

[PRINTED ON: Personal diary paper, round handwriting.]

**Dorothy Pembrook — Personal Diary**
**February 12, 1962**

*Catherine Marsh came in today for stamps and the lighthouse mail. We talked for a while — she is looking so much better than she did last year, more color in her face, more light in her eyes. I mentioned the V. Novak business to her — Helen's strange mail — and I wish now that I hadn't. Catherine's face changed. She went very still, very focused, the way she looks when she's painting and she's trying to get a color exactly right. She asked me questions — careful questions, specific questions. The name. The addresses. How many letters. How often.*

*She left the post office and walked toward home, and as I watched her through the window I had the oddest feeling — a chill, as if someone had opened a door to the winter. I thought: I have just told that woman something important. Something she has been waiting to hear.*

*I hope it is nothing. I hope Helen's mail is innocent and Catherine's curiosity is simply her nature. But I do not think so. Catherine does not ask questions idly. She asks questions the way her mother asks questions — because she intends to find the answers.*

## Evidence Piece 7: General Delivery Regulations

[PRINTED ON: Official U.S. Post Office regulation excerpt.]

**UNITED STATES POST OFFICE DEPARTMENT**
**POSTAL OPERATIONS MANUAL — SECTION 157**
**General Delivery Service**

**157.1** General delivery service is available at all post offices for the receipt of mail by transient persons or persons not permanently residing at an address within the delivery area.

**157.2** Persons claiming general delivery mail must present satisfactory identification. Acceptable forms: driver's license, employer identification, Social Security card, military identification.

**157.4** Unclaimed general delivery mail shall be held for thirty (30) days from date of receipt, after which it shall be returned to sender with appropriate marking.

*[Dorothy Pembrook's note:]*
*"Helen never showed identification when she collected the Novak letters. I should have asked. The regulations say I must. But she was William Marsh's wife — a known person — and she said the letters were for a friend, and I did not want to be difficult. I was not difficult. I was accommodating. And Catherine is dead."*

## Evidence Piece 8: Helen's Letter to William (1971)

[PRINTED ON: Quality stationery, typed. Cold, precise.]

April 3, 1971

William,

I am leaving. I have instructed my attorney, Mr. David Pierce of 18 Congress Street, Portland, to draw up the divorce papers. You will receive them within the week. I ask that you sign them promptly and without contest. I am requesting an equitable division of marital assets.

I am moving to California. I will not provide a forwarding address. Do not attempt to find me.

We had nine years together. Some of them were good. I am sorry that they are ending this way, but they were always going to end this way. You should know that I was fond of you — genuinely fond. You are a kind man, William. Kinder than you know. But kindness was never going to be enough.

Do not look through my things after I leave. There is nothing there that will make you happier.

Helen

*[Agnes Doyle's note, paper-clipped to the letter:]*
*"William gave me this letter in October 1971, along with his statement. He did look through her things, of course. He found the birth certificate, the Social Security card, the orphanage letters, the newspaper clipping. Helen — Vera — told him not to look, knowing he would. Perhaps she wanted him to know. Perhaps even she was tired of the lie."*

*"Do not look through my things after I leave. There is nothing there that will make you happier." She was right about that much.*

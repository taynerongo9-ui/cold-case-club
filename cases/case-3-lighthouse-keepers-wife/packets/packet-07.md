## Evidence Piece 1: Dorothy Pembrook's Testimony

[PRINTED ON: Lined notebook paper, handwritten in a round, careful hand. Dorothy writes the way she speaks — in long, winding sentences full of qualifications and parentheticals. She's a gossip, but an honest one.]

**Statement of Dorothy Anne Pembrook**
**Postmistress, Crestfall, Maine**
**Given to Mrs. Agnes Doyle**
**June 14, 1963**

Agnes asked me to write down what I know about Helen Marsh's mail, and I will do it, though I want it understood that I do not make a habit of reading other people's correspondence. I am the postmistress. I handle every piece of mail that comes through Crestfall, and I can't help noticing things. That is the nature of the position.

Now. Helen Marsh — William's wife — she doesn't live in Crestfall, she lives in Portland, so I wouldn't normally see her mail at all. But William and Helen visit Edward at the lighthouse with some regularity, and when they do, Helen sometimes has mail forwarded to our office for pickup, or she receives items here that she has apparently arranged in advance to be sent to Crestfall rather than to her Portland address.

I first noticed something peculiar in September 1961. A letter arrived at the Crestfall post office addressed to "V. Novak, c/o General Delivery, Crestfall, Maine." We don't get much general delivery — this isn't a tourist town — so it stood out. I set it aside. When Helen Marsh came in that afternoon (she and William were visiting that weekend), she asked if there was any general delivery mail. I showed her the letter addressed to V. Novak, and she took it. She said it was for a friend and she had agreed to pick it up. I thought that was a bit odd — why would a friend have mail sent here rather than to Portland? — but I didn't question it.

Then it happened again in November 1961. Another letter, same thing — "V. Novak, c/o General Delivery, Crestfall, Maine." Helen picked it up. Same story about a friend.

In January 1962, a third letter came. This time, I noticed the return address: St. Agnes Home, Bangor, Maine. I know St. Agnes — it's the orphanage on State Street. My cousin's girl volunteered there in the fifties. I thought: why is a woman in Portland having orphanage mail sent to general delivery in Crestfall under a name that isn't hers?

I probably should have kept this to myself. But in February — February 12, I believe — Catherine Marsh came into the post office to collect the lighthouse mail and buy stamps. We were chatting, and I mentioned — I shouldn't have, but I did — that Helen had been picking up mail under a different name. I said something like, "Your sister-in-law has a mysterious correspondent. Someone named Novak. Gets letters from that orphanage in Bangor."

Catherine went very still. She asked me to repeat the name. I said, "Novak. V. Novak." Catherine asked if I had any of the letters. I did not — Helen had collected them all. Catherine asked if I remembered the exact return address. I told her: St. Agnes Home, State Street, Bangor.

Catherine thanked me, bought her stamps, and left. She seemed distracted. Almost excited, if that's the right word. As if a piece of a puzzle had fallen into place.

I did not see Catherine again after that day.

After she died, I thought about our conversation many times. I wondered if I should tell Constable Hale what I had told Catherine. But Hale had already closed the case, and he had not asked me about mail, and I was not sure it mattered. And honestly, I was afraid. If Helen was involved in something, I did not want to be the one pointing the finger.

I am telling Agnes now because she asked, and because she deserves to know. I should have spoken sooner. I carry that.

One more thing. In March 1962 — after Catherine's death — I received one more letter for V. Novak at general delivery. Helen did not come to collect it. It sat in my office for thirty days and then was returned to sender per postal regulations. The return address was a P.O. Box in Boston. Not the orphanage this time — just a P.O. Box number. I wrote the number down because by then I was suspicious. **P.O. Box 2247, Boston, Massachusetts.**

*Dorothy Pembrook*
*June 14, 1963*

---

[PRODUCTION NOTE: Dorothy's testimony is gold. She's the kind of witness investigators dream of — she notices everything and remembers details. The P.O. Box number is a specific, verifiable detail that makes this feel real. Her guilt about not speaking sooner adds emotional weight. Print in her handwriting style — round, careful, schoolgirl-neat.]

## Evidence Piece 3: Helen Marsh Character Sketch

[PRINTED ON: Cold Case Club analysis paper. Modern document compiling impressions from multiple Crestfall residents, gathered by Agnes Doyle in 1963-1964.]

**COLD CASE CLUB — INVESTIGATIVE ANALYSIS**
**Character Profile: Helen Marsh (Mrs. William Marsh)**
*Compiled from statements by Crestfall residents, 1963-1964*
*Original interviews conducted by Agnes Doyle*

---

**Physical Description:** Dark-haired, approximately 5'7", slender, attractive. Always well-dressed — notably more so than other women in Crestfall. Wore gloves even indoors ("as if she were always about to leave," per Mrs. Bowen). Applied makeup carefully. Carried herself with what multiple witnesses described as "poise" or "control."

**General Impression:**

- *Mrs. Harriet Bowen (neighbor):* "Helen was charming. She always knew what to say. But there was something... practiced about it. Like she had rehearsed being friendly. I never felt I was seeing the real woman."

- *Mr. Ralph Garrett (general store):* "Nice enough lady. Bought expensive things when she was in town — the good coffee, imported cheese, things we don't sell much of. Paid cash always. Never put anything on account like everyone else does."

- *Mrs. Margaret Callahan (church auxiliary):* "Helen came to Mass with William when they visited, but she didn't seem comfortable. She didn't know the responses. I assumed she might be Protestant, but William said she was Catholic. Raised Catholic, he said. Well, she didn't act like it."

- *Arthur Kimball (harbor master):* "Didn't have much interaction with her. She wasn't the type to come down to the harbor. I'll say this — she asked a lot of questions about the Marsh family. About Captain Silas, about the family history, about Edward and William's inheritance. I thought she was just interested, but looking back, she was digging."

**Background (as told by Helen to various residents):**

Helen claimed to be from Kennebunk, Maine. She said her parents were deceased — father was a fisherman, mother a homemaker. She said she attended Kennebunk High School and later worked as a secretary in Portland before meeting William.

**Inconsistencies noted:**

- *Dorothy Pembrook:* "She said she grew up in Kennebunk, but when I mentioned the Shore Road clambake — every Kennebunk child knows about it — she looked blank. She covered it, but I saw the blankness."

- *Agnes Doyle:* "Catherine told me in January that Helen had said her parents were buried at Green Lawn Cemetery in Kennebunk. Catherine looked it up. There are no Marshes buried at Green Lawn. Catherine didn't tell Helen she had checked."

- *Mrs. Callahan:* "She told me her maiden name was Stewart. But William told Ralph Garrett her maiden name was Hennessey. I suppose one of them was mistaken, but it struck me as odd."

**Relationship with Catherine:**

- *Agnes Doyle:* "Catherine tried to like Helen. She truly did. But she told me in late 1961 that something about Helen 'didn't sit right.' She said Helen watched her — closely, constantly — as if monitoring what Catherine knew or noticed. Catherine said, 'It's like being observed by someone taking notes.'"

**Behavior after Catherine's death:**

- *Mrs. Bowen:* "She seemed... relieved? No, that's too strong. But she didn't seem grief-stricken, either. She was composed at the funeral. Very composed. William was a mess — crying, couldn't get through the service. But Helen stood straight as a rod and didn't shed a tear."

- *Dorothy Pembrook:* "She stopped having mail sent to Crestfall after Catherine died. No more V. Novak letters. As if she didn't need them anymore."

## Evidence Piece 4: Gerald Whitfield Statement (Red Herring Resolution)

[PRINTED ON: Official state government letterhead. Typewriter font.]

**STATE OF MAINE**
**DEPARTMENT OF TRANSPORTATION**
**HIGHWAY DIVISION**

**Memorandum**

**To:** File
**From:** Gerald R. Whitfield, Highway Surveyor
**Date:** April 15, 1962
**Re:** Route 1A Extension Survey — Crestfall Coastal Section

---

This memorandum is to document my surveying activities in the Crestfall area during February 1962.

I was assigned to conduct preliminary surveying for the proposed Route 1A coastal extension between Bath and Crestfall, a project under consideration by the Highway Division since 1959. My work in the Crestfall area took place over three weeks in February 1962, specifically February 5-23.

My work required me to survey the coastal terrain along the proposed route, which included sections near Dunmore Point Lighthouse. I was in the vicinity of the lighthouse on February 8, 12, 15, and 19. On February 12, I spoke briefly with Mr. Edward Marsh at the lighthouse to explain my presence. He was courteous and offered me coffee, which I accepted.

I understand that several Crestfall residents noted my presence and that an "unfamiliar stranger" near the lighthouse became a matter of local discussion following the death of Mrs. Marsh in March. I wish to confirm that my activities were entirely related to highway surveying. I had no interaction with Mrs. Marsh and was not in the Crestfall area at any time in March 1962.

The Route 1A extension project was subsequently shelved due to budget constraints and has not been revived.

*Gerald R. Whitfield*
*Highway Surveyor, Grade III*

---

[PRODUCTION NOTE: This document cleanly resolves the "mysterious stranger" red herring. It's placed here — Packet 7, past the midpoint — so that subscribers who were pursuing this lead can set it aside and refocus on Helen. The bureaucratic tone of a government memo is a nice contrast to the emotional testimonies elsewhere in the packet.]

## Evidence Piece 3: Timeline Reconstruction

[PRINTED ON: Cold Case Club analysis paper — modern document, clean design. This is presented as the Club's own reconstruction based on available evidence.]

**COLD CASE CLUB — INVESTIGATIVE ANALYSIS**
**Timeline Reconstruction: Evening of March 8, 1962**

*Compiled from: Lighthouse keeper's log, fog signal record, National Weather Service data, witness statements*

---

**1600 hrs (4:00 PM)** — Fog dissipates. Visibility at Crestfall Harbor: 8+ miles. Weather observer Arthur Kimball reopens the harbor.

**1800 hrs (6:00 PM)** — Edward ignites the lighthouse lamp at dusk. Records "mostly clear" conditions. Dinner in the keeper's cottage: Edward, Catherine, William, Helen. Edward notes it was a "quiet evening." William has brought a bottle of Old Crow bourbon.

**1930 hrs (7:30 PM)** — Edward goes to the lighthouse tower for his evening watch (standard shift: ~7:30 PM to 6:00 AM). William retires to the guest room with the bourbon.

**2000 hrs (8:00 PM)** — Edward records: *"Clear, 37°F, wind S 5. Fog cleared. Discontinued fog signal."* Weather Service confirms: visibility 10+ miles. Three-quarter moon. **The fog had been gone for four hours.**

**~2030-2100 hrs (8:30-9:00 PM)** — [GAP IN RECORD] Helen states she "went to bed at approximately 2100 hrs." William states he "fell asleep" after drinking. No one accounts for Catherine's movements during this window.

**~2100-2200 hrs (9:00-10:00 PM)** — [ESTIMATED TIME OF DEATH] Based on Dr. Greer's examination (body temperature, rigor mortis onset), Catherine died approximately 8-10 hours before discovery at 6:15 AM — placing death between approximately 8:15 PM and 10:15 PM. Catherine's stopped wristwatch reads **9:14 PM.**

**2400 hrs (midnight)** — Edward's log entry: *"Clear, 33°F, calm. All in order. On watch. House is dark. Everyone asleep, I assume."* He does not know Catherine is dead at the base of the cliff.

**0615 hrs (6:15 AM), March 9** — Edward finishes dawn watch. Catherine is not in bed, not in the house. Her coat and one pair of shoes are gone. He searches and discovers her body.

---

**ANALYTICAL NOTE:**

The official finding rests on the premise that Catherine walked the cliff path *in dense fog* and fell due to poor visibility. However:

1. The National Weather Service recorded fog dissipating by **4:00 PM** — five hours before the estimated time of death.
2. Edward Marsh's lighthouse log records fog clearing and the fog signal being discontinued at **8:00 PM** — at least one hour before death.
3. Weather conditions at the estimated time of death: **clear skies, 10+ miles visibility, three-quarter moon.**

**If there was no fog, why did Catherine fall?**

This question was never asked by Constable Hale. His report states weather conditions were "assessed based on general reports from town" — he never consulted the official weather record or the lighthouse log, both of which were readily available.

## Evidence Piece 4: Statement of Captain Josiah Briggs

[PRINTED ON: Aged paper, handwritten style (neat but informal). This is a statement given to Agnes Doyle in 1963, not to police — Agnes was conducting her own investigation.]

**Statement of Josiah Briggs**
**Lobsterman, Crestfall, Maine**
**Given to Agnes Doyle, April 1963**

Mrs. Doyle asked me to write down what I remember about the night of March 8, 1962, on account of Catherine. I will do my best.

I was down at the harbor that evening mending traps. I had been stuck inside all day because of the fog, and by late afternoon it had cleared up nice, so I went down to get some work done. Arthur Kimball reopened the harbor around four o'clock, and I remember thinking I might go out in the morning if the weather held.

I was at the harbor from about five o'clock until past nine. It was a fine evening — cool but clear. I could see the lighthouse beam sweeping the whole time, clear as day. The fog horn was NOT blowing. I remember because it had been going for most of two days straight and the quiet was a relief.

At no point during the evening was there any fog. Not a wisp. The moon was up and I could see clear across the harbor to Ram Island, which is a good three miles. You could not ask for better visibility.

I heard about Catherine the next morning. When Constable Hale said she fell in the fog, I thought that was peculiar, because there was no fog that evening. I said as much to a few people at Garrett's store, but no one seemed interested. Hale had already made up his mind.

I am sorry I did not say more at the time. I liked Catherine. She was a good woman.

*Josiah Briggs*
*April 14, 1963*

[PRODUCTION NOTE: This statement is powerful because it's an independent eyewitness confirming the weather data. Briggs had no reason to lie. His statement also shows that people in town knew the fog story didn't add up — but no one pushed back against the official ruling.]

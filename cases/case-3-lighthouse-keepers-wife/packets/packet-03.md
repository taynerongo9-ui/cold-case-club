## Evidence Piece 3: Timeline Reconstruction

[PRINTED ON: Cold Case Club analysis paper — modern document, clean design. Presented as the Club's own reconstruction based on available evidence.]

**COLD CASE CLUB — INVESTIGATIVE ANALYSIS**
**Timeline Reconstruction: Evening of March 8, 1962**

*Compiled from: Lighthouse keeper's log, fog signal record, National Weather Service data, witness statements, physical evidence*

---

**1400 hrs (2:00 PM)** — Fog signal recommenced after brief clearing. Edward notes fog "coming and going."

**1530 hrs (3:30 PM)** — William and Helen Marsh arrive at the lighthouse for a weekend visit, driving from Portland. Edward notes their arrival in the evening log entry.

**1555 hrs (3:55 PM)** — Arthur Kimball reopens Crestfall Harbor. Visibility 8+ miles and improving.

**1600 hrs (4:00 PM)** — Fog dissipates entirely. Weather Service records: visibility 8+ miles, mostly clear. **From this point forward, there is NO FOG.**

**1742 hrs (5:42 PM)** — Sunset. Three-quarter moon rises in the east.

**1800 hrs (6:00 PM)** — Edward ignites the lighthouse lamp at dusk. Records "mostly clear" conditions, wind S 10. Dinner in the keeper's cottage: Edward, Catherine, William, Helen. Catherine has made fish chowder. Edward notes "quiet evening." He also records that Catherine "seemed distracted" and had told him that morning she wanted to "talk about Helen" — that "something is very wrong." Edward told her they would talk after his watch.

**1930 hrs (7:30 PM)** — Edward goes to the lighthouse tower for his evening watch (standard shift: 7:30 PM to 6:00 AM). He will not descend until morning. William retires to the guest room with the bottle of Old Crow bourbon. Helen's movements during this period are unaccounted for in her statement — she says only that she "went to bed at approximately 2100 hrs."

**2000 hrs (8:00 PM)** — **CRITICAL:** Edward records in the lighthouse log: *"Clear, 37F, wind S 5. Fog cleared. Discontinued fog signal."* Weather Service confirms: visibility 10+ miles. Three-quarter moon above the horizon. Arthur Kimball at the harbor can see the lighthouse beam clearly from three miles away. Captain Josiah Briggs, mending traps at the harbor, confirms clear conditions with no fog horn audible. **The fog had been gone for four hours.**

**~2030 hrs (8:30 PM)** — William Marsh, by his own statement, falls asleep in the guest room after consuming most of a bottle of bourbon. He will not wake until approximately 6:30 AM the following morning. He is unconscious for the entire critical period.

**~2030-2100 hrs (8:30-9:00 PM)** — [GAP IN RECORD] This is the window during which Catherine and Helen go to the cliff path. Helen states she "went to bed at approximately 2100 hrs." William states Helen "joined him" in the guest room at about 9:00 PM. If both are true, Helen was somewhere other than the guest room between approximately 8:00 PM and 9:00 PM. No one accounts for Catherine's movements during this period.

**~2100-2115 hrs (9:00-9:15 PM)** — [ESTIMATED TIME OF DEATH] Based on Dr. Greer's examination (body temperature, rigor mortis onset), Catherine died approximately 8-10 hours before discovery at 6:15 AM — placing death between approximately 8:15 PM and 10:15 PM. Catherine's stopped wristwatch reads **9:14 PM**, consistent with impact. **Conditions at 9:14 PM: clear sky, visibility 10+ miles, three-quarter moon, temperature 36F, light winds. No fog. No rain. No obstruction to vision.**

**~2130 hrs (9:30 PM)** — [ESTIMATED] Helen returns to the cottage. She goes to the guest room and gets into bed beside William, who is unconscious.

**2400 hrs (midnight)** — Edward's log entry: *"Clear, 33F, calm. All in order. On watch. House is dark. Everyone asleep, I assume."* He does not know Catherine is dead at the base of the cliff, eighty feet below the path she walked every day of her married life.

**0600 hrs (6:00 AM), March 9** — Edward extinguishes the light at dawn.

**0615 hrs (6:15 AM)** — Edward descends from the tower. Catherine is not in bed. Her nightgown is on the pillow — she never changed for bed. Her coat is gone. He searches the cottage, the outbuildings. He walks the cliff path. He looks down.

**0630 hrs** — Edward's log: *"God help me. I have found her."*

**0632 hrs** — Edward telephones Crestfall Police. Constable Hale begins filling out a missing persons report, then cancels it when Edward reports finding the body.

**0745 hrs** — Hale arrives at Dunmore Point.

---

**ANALYTICAL NOTE:**

The official finding rests on the premise that Catherine walked the cliff path *in dense fog* and fell due to poor visibility. However:

1. The National Weather Service recorded fog dissipating by **4:00 PM** — five hours before the estimated time of death.
2. Edward Marsh's lighthouse log records fog clearing and the fog signal being discontinued at **8:00 PM** — at least one hour before death.
3. The fog signal operation record confirms the horn was shut down at 8:00 PM after thirty continuous hours.
4. The harbor master could see the lighthouse beam from three miles away at 8:00 PM.
5. An independent witness (Captain Briggs) confirms clear conditions at the harbor all evening.
6. Weather conditions at the estimated time of death: **clear skies, 10+ miles visibility, three-quarter moon, temperature 36F.**

**If there was no fog, why did Catherine fall?**

This question was never asked by Constable Hale. His report states weather conditions were "assessed based on general reports from town." He acknowledged two days later (supplemental note, March 11) that the harbor master had told him the fog cleared in the afternoon, but he dismissed this as immaterial, writing: "The cliff path is hazardous in any conditions." He never consulted the official weather record. He never checked the lighthouse log — the most detailed weather record within three miles, maintained by the husband of the deceased.

The fog was the foundation of the accidental death ruling. Without the fog, the ruling collapses. And the fog was gone.

## Evidence Piece 4: Statement of Captain Josiah Briggs

[PRINTED ON: Aged paper, handwritten in a neat but informal hand. Statement given to Agnes Doyle in 1963, not to police.]

**Statement of Josiah Briggs**
**Lobsterman, Crestfall, Maine**
**Given to Mrs. Agnes M. Doyle**
**April 14, 1963**

---

Mrs. Doyle has asked me to write down what I remember about the night of March 8, 1962, on account of her daughter Catherine. I will do my best. I am not a man much given to writing but I owe it to Catherine and to Agnes, who has been a friend to me and to every man in this town for as long as I can remember.

I am a lobsterman. I have fished out of Crestfall Harbor for thirty-one years, since I was sixteen. I know the weather along this coast the way I know the shape of my own hands. I pay attention to fog because fog is a lobsterman's enemy — you can lose your boat, your traps, and your life in fog if you are not careful. So when I tell you about the weather on a particular night, you may take it as the word of a man who has staked his livelihood on reading the sky correctly for three decades.

On the morning of March 8, the fog was thick as I have ever seen it. You could not see the end of the wharf from the harbor master's office. Arthur Kimball closed the harbor at first light. I spent the morning in my shed on the wharf, splicing line and mending traps. There was nothing else to do.

Around noon, the fog started to break. By two o'clock, I could see the harbor mouth. By three-thirty, I could see Ram Island, which is a good three miles across the bay. Arthur reopened the harbor just before four. The sun came through and it turned into as fine an afternoon as you could ask for in March.

I went down to the harbor at about five o'clock to continue my trap work. I was there until past nine — close to nine-thirty. I had my hurricane lantern and I was working in the open air because the evening was pleasant for the season. Cool, yes, but clear and calm.

During the entire time I was at the harbor, the following conditions prevailed:

1. **The sky was clear.** Not a cloud from one horizon to the other after about four o'clock. Stars came out bright after dark. The moon — near full, a bit lopsided — was up and shining on the water.

2. **There was no fog.** Not a wisp. Not a tendril. Not a ghost of fog anywhere along the coast from the time it cleared in the afternoon until I went home after nine-thirty. I have been told that Catherine fell "in the fog." There was no fog. I will swear to this on any Bible you put in front of me.

3. **The fog horn at Dunmore Point was NOT blowing.** I am certain of this because the horn had been going for most of two days straight and the sudden silence was a relief so profound that I remarked on it to Arthur Kimball when he walked past the harbor around seven. "Thank the Lord that horn's quit," I said, and Arthur agreed. The horn stopped sometime around eight o'clock, I believe, which matches with the fog clearing completely.

4. **I could see the lighthouse beam** sweeping across the headland from the harbor. The beam was clear and bright. You can only see the beam clearly when the air is clear — in fog, it turns into a diffuse glow. On the night of March 8, it was a sharp, clean beam, sweeping the way it's supposed to. That tells you everything you need to know about the visibility.

5. **I could see clear across the harbor to Ram Island**, which is three miles. I could see the running lights of Pete Morrison's boat coming in from the southeast, which means visibility was at least five or six miles. You cannot see Ram Island in fog. You cannot see a boat at five miles in fog. It was not foggy.

When I heard about Catherine the next morning, I felt sick. She was a good woman — kind, quiet, always had a smile for a rough old fisherman like me. She used to paint watercolors of the harbor and once she gave me one for Christmas — my boat, the *Agnes D.* (named for my mother, not Mrs. Doyle, though Agnes Doyle has always been pleased by the coincidence). I hung it in my kitchen and it is still there.

When Constable Hale said Catherine fell in the fog, I knew it was wrong. I said as much to a few people at Garrett's store the following week. Ralph Garrett said he thought the same thing but didn't want to make trouble. Harriet Bowen said it was best not to speak ill of the dead or to stir up the family's grief. I said I wasn't speaking ill of anyone — I was saying the weather was clear. But people in Crestfall don't like to contradict the constable, and Hale had made up his mind, and that was that.

I should have gone to Hale directly. I should have insisted. But I am a lobsterman, not a detective, and I did not think it was my place to tell the constable his business. I was wrong about that. It was everyone's place. Catherine was everyone's neighbor.

Agnes, I am sorry I did not say more at the time. I liked your daughter. She deserved better from this town and she deserved better from me.

*Josiah Briggs*
*April 14, 1963*

*P.S. — If it matters, my boat log for March 8 shows I was at the wharf from 1700 to 2130 hrs and I noted "clear, calm, good moon" at 2000 hrs. I keep a log the way Edward keeps his — it's a habit of the sea.*

[PRODUCTION NOTE: Briggs is the independent eyewitness who demolishes the fog theory. He's credible — a thirty-year fisherman whose life depends on reading weather accurately. His statement is powerful because it's not just about the weather; it's about the community's failure to speak up. The P.S. about his own boat log is a nice touch — another contemporaneous record confirming clear conditions.]

## Evidence Piece 7: Fog Signal Regulations Extract

[PRINTED ON: Official Coast Guard document, small format, regulation-style.]

**U.S. COAST GUARD — FIRST DISTRICT**
**STANDING ORDER: FOG SIGNAL OPERATIONS**
*Applicable to all manned light stations*

**1.** The fog signal shall be activated when visibility falls below five (5) nautical miles as determined by the keeper's observation.

**2.** Once activated, the signal shall run continuously until visibility exceeds five nautical miles and the keeper determines that conditions are stable and unlikely to deteriorate within one hour.

**3.** The keeper shall record the exact time of activation and deactivation in both the station log and the Fog Signal Operation Record.

**4.** **Falsification of fog signal records is a federal offense.** The fog signal log is a legal record used in maritime accident investigations, insurance claims, and navigational disputes. Inaccurate entries may result in disciplinary action, dismissal, and criminal prosecution.

**5.** The keeper's fog signal record is presumed accurate in any legal proceeding unless contradicted by other credible evidence.

*[Handwritten note in margin, in Edward's hand:]*
*"I have never falsified an entry. The horn goes on when the fog comes and off when it clears. My record is exact."*

[PRODUCTION NOTE: This regulation establishes two things: first, that Edward's fog signal log is a legal record that he had every reason to keep accurate (and criminal penalties for falsifying); second, that his record of shutting down the horn at 8 PM is therefore reliable evidence. Edward's marginal note reinforces his conscientiousness. This is the keeper who underlined the regulation about not drinking on watch — he takes his records seriously.]

## Evidence Piece 8: Analytical Note — The Fog Discrepancy

[PRINTED ON: Cold Case Club analysis paper. Modern document.]

**COLD CASE CLUB — INVESTIGATIVE ANALYSIS**
**Analytical Note: The Fog Discrepancy**

---

The official ruling in Case 62-0041 rests on a single premise: that Catherine Marsh walked the cliff path at Dunmore Point in dense fog and fell to her death due to poor visibility. Constable Hale's incident report states: "Dense fog reported throughout the day and evening. Visibility significantly reduced."

This premise is false.

Five independent sources of evidence — any one of which was available to Constable Hale had he checked — confirm that the fog cleared hours before Catherine's death:

| Source | Time Fog Cleared | Record Type | Available to Hale? |
|--------|------------------|-------------|-------------------|
| National Weather Service (Crestfall Station) | 1600 hrs (4:00 PM) | Official government record | Yes — on file at harbor master's office |
| Lighthouse keeper's log (Edward Marsh) | 2000 hrs (8:00 PM) | Official station record | Yes — in the lighthouse tower |
| Fog signal operation record | 2000 hrs (discontinued) | Official station record | Yes — in the lighthouse tower |
| Harbor master's log (Arthur Kimball) | 1555 hrs (harbor reopened) | Official harbor record | Yes — harbor master's office |
| Eyewitness (Capt. Josiah Briggs) | Clear all evening | Personal testimony | Yes — Briggs was at the harbor and spoke to others about the weather |

Constable Hale checked **none** of these sources before closing the case. He acknowledged in a supplemental note (March 11, 1962) that Kimball had told him the fog cleared in the afternoon, but dismissed this as immaterial.

**Catherine Marsh died at approximately 9:14 PM** (per her stopped wristwatch). At 9:14 PM on March 8, 1962:
- Visibility was 10+ miles
- The sky was clear
- A three-quarter moon was above the horizon
- There was no precipitation
- Wind was light (5 mph from the south)
- The fog horn had been silent for over an hour
- The lighthouse beam was visible from Crestfall Harbor, three miles away

Catherine Marsh knew the cliff path at Dunmore Point intimately. She had walked it in every condition for five years. On the evening of March 8, conditions were as good as they ever get on the Maine coast in March. She did not stumble in fog, because there was no fog.

**If Catherine did not fall accidentally, then she was either pushed or she jumped. The evidence in the remaining packets will address both possibilities.**

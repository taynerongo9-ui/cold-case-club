## Evidence Piece 1: Last Will and Testament of Captain Silas Marsh

[PRINTED ON: Heavy legal paper, aged cream color. Formal legal typeface. Embossed seal of "Bancroft & Wells, Attorneys at Law, Portland, Maine." Should look like a proper legal document from the late 1950s.]

**LAST WILL AND TESTAMENT**
**OF**
**SILAS EZEKIEL MARSH**

---

*I, Silas Ezekiel Marsh, of Crestfall, in the County of Cumberland, State of Maine, being of sound mind and memory, do hereby make, publish, and declare this as my Last Will and Testament, revoking all previous wills and codicils.*

[...]

**ARTICLE IV — THE MARSH FAMILY TRUST**

Section 1. I hereby direct my Executor to establish a trust, to be known as the Marsh Family Trust, funded with the sum of ONE HUNDRED AND TWENTY THOUSAND DOLLARS ($120,000.00) from the proceeds of the sale of Marsh Shipping Company and such other assets as remain in my estate after payment of debts, taxes, and specific bequests.

Section 2. The Trust shall be administered by the law firm of Bancroft & Wells, Portland, Maine, or its successor, as Trustee.

Section 3. The principal of the Trust shall be divided equally between my two grandsons, EDWARD THOMAS MARSH and WILLIAM ROBERT MARSH, subject to the conditions set forth in Section 4 below.

**Section 4. CONDITIONS OF DISTRIBUTION:**

*(a)* No distribution shall be made to either beneficiary until BOTH beneficiaries have attained the age of thirty-five (35) years.

*(b)* Each beneficiary must be lawfully married at the time of distribution. A beneficiary who is unmarried, divorced, or whose marriage has been annulled shall forfeit his share, which shall pass to the remaining beneficiary.

*(c)* Each beneficiary must be of "sound reputation" in the community, as determined in the sole discretion of the Trustee.

*(d)* Upon both beneficiaries meeting the above conditions, the Trust principal shall be divided equally — SIXTY THOUSAND DOLLARS ($60,000.00) to each beneficiary.

*(e)* If either beneficiary fails to meet the conditions by the time the younger beneficiary reaches age forty (40), the entire Trust principal shall be distributed to the qualifying beneficiary. If neither qualifies, the principal shall be donated to the Crestfall Seamen's Benevolent Society.

**Section 5.** During the period before distribution, the Trust income (interest and dividends) shall be reinvested in the principal.

[...]

*IN WITNESS WHEREOF, I have hereunto set my hand and seal this 15th day of March, 1955.*

*Silas Ezekiel Marsh*

*Witnessed by:*
*Harold T. Bancroft, Esq.*
*Miss Patricia Henley (secretary)*

---

[PRODUCTION NOTE: The critical clause is Section 4(b) — each beneficiary must be "lawfully married." If Vera's identity fraud is discovered, her marriage to William is void (marriage obtained through fraud). William would be considered unmarried, forfeiting his share. More importantly, the exposure would likely trigger a criminal investigation, ending Vera's freedom. The stakes are total: money, marriage, liberty. That's motive for murder.]

## Evidence Piece 4: William Marsh's Statement, 1971

[PRINTED ON: Handwritten on plain white paper, in a rough masculine hand. This statement was given to Agnes Doyle in 1971, after William and Vera's divorce, when the truth finally began to surface.]

**Statement of William Robert Marsh**
**Given to Mrs. Agnes Doyle**
**October 22, 1971**

---

Agnes,

You were right. About all of it. I am sorry it took me so long to see it. I am sorry it took losing her to see it.

Helen — I cannot call her Vera, even now, even knowing what I know — left me in April of this year. She said she wanted a divorce. No explanation. She took half of everything, including most of what remained of the trust money, and moved to California. She left no forwarding address. Her attorney in Portland handled the paperwork. I signed the papers because I had no fight left in me.

After she left, I found things in the house. Things she had hidden. A birth certificate in the name of Vera Novak. A Social Security card in the same name. Letters from an orphanage in Bangor. And a newspaper clipping — from the Boston Globe, 1958 — about the death of a woman named Helen Marsh.

I sat in my kitchen and read that clipping and I understood everything. The woman I married was not the woman I thought she was. She had taken a dead woman's name and married me, and she had done it for the money.

I thought about calling the police. I thought about it for three days. And then I didn't. Because if I called the police, they would investigate, and they would ask about Catherine, and I would have to face the possibility that Helen — Vera — killed your daughter. And I could not face that. I am a coward, Agnes. I have always been a coward. Edward got the courage and I got the charm, and neither one of us ended up with what we needed.

I do not know if Vera killed Catherine. I was asleep that night. I had drunk most of a bottle of bourbon and I slept like a dead man. I heard nothing. I saw nothing. When I woke in the morning, Edward was screaming.

But I know this: Catherine was asking questions about Helen. I noticed it at that last dinner — Catherine watching Helen with those sharp eyes of hers, the way she watched everything, missing nothing. And Helen watching Catherine right back. Two women circling each other. I thought it was just family tension. I was a fool.

If Vera killed Catherine to protect her secret, then the trust money that paid for my house and my car and my comfortable life was bought with your daughter's blood. I do not know how to live with that.

You may do whatever you wish with this statement. Show it to the police. Print it in the newspaper. I don't care anymore. I should have listened to you years ago.

William

---

[PRODUCTION NOTE: William's statement is the confession of a man who has finally stopped lying to himself. It's not a legal confession — he didn't witness anything — but it's a moral one. He knew something was wrong. He chose not to look. The line about Edward getting the courage and William getting the charm is the kind of self-knowledge that only comes after everything has been lost. Print in rough handwriting on plain paper — a man writing at his kitchen table with nothing left to lose.]

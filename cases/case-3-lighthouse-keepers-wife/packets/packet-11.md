## Evidence Piece 1: Last Will and Testament of Captain Silas Marsh

[PRINTED ON: Heavy legal paper, aged cream. Embossed seal: "Bancroft & Wells, Attorneys at Law, Portland, Maine."]

**LAST WILL AND TESTAMENT**
**OF**
**SILAS EZEKIEL MARSH**

---

*I, Silas Ezekiel Marsh, of Crestfall, in the County of Cumberland, State of Maine, being of sound mind and memory, though advanced in years and troubled in body, do hereby make, publish, and declare this as my Last Will and Testament, revoking all previous wills and codicils.*

**ARTICLE I — DECLARATION**

I am a widower. My beloved wife, Margaret Rose Marsh, predeceased me in the year 1948. My only son, Robert Edward Marsh, predeceased me in 1935. I am survived by two grandsons: Edward Thomas Marsh, born 1929, and William Robert Marsh, born 1926, both of whom I love deeply and for whom I have great hope, though not without reservation.

**ARTICLE II — SPECIFIC BEQUESTS**

Section 1. To the Crestfall Seamen's Benevolent Society, the sum of FIVE THOUSAND DOLLARS ($5,000.00), to be used for the relief of widows and orphans of Crestfall fishermen lost at sea.

Section 2. To St. Brendan's Catholic Church, Crestfall, the sum of TWO THOUSAND DOLLARS ($2,000.00), for the maintenance of the church building and the support of its pastor.

Section 3. To my housekeeper, Mrs. Doris Whitcomb, the sum of ONE THOUSAND DOLLARS ($1,000.00), with my gratitude for twenty-two years of faithful service and excellent pie.

Section 4. My personal effects, including my sea chest, navigational instruments, ship's logs, and the portrait of the schooner *Crestfall Pride* that hangs in the front parlor, to Edward Thomas Marsh, who alone of my grandsons understands the sea.

**ARTICLE III — RESIDUARY ESTATE**

All remaining assets, including the proceeds of the sale of Marsh Shipping Company, real property, bank accounts, and investments, shall constitute the Residuary Estate, from which debts, taxes, and the above bequests shall be paid. The remainder shall fund the Trust established in Article IV.

**ARTICLE IV — THE MARSH FAMILY TRUST**

Section 1. I hereby direct my Executor to establish a trust, to be known as the Marsh Family Trust, funded with the sum of ONE HUNDRED AND TWENTY THOUSAND DOLLARS ($120,000.00) from the Residuary Estate.

Section 2. The Trust shall be administered by the law firm of Bancroft & Wells, Portland, Maine, or its successor, as Trustee.

Section 3. The principal of the Trust shall be divided equally between my two grandsons, EDWARD THOMAS MARSH and WILLIAM ROBERT MARSH, subject to the conditions set forth in Section 4 below.

**Section 4. CONDITIONS OF DISTRIBUTION:**

*(a)* No distribution shall be made to either beneficiary until BOTH beneficiaries have attained the age of thirty-five (35) years.

*(b)* Each beneficiary must be lawfully married at the time of distribution. A beneficiary who is unmarried, divorced, or whose marriage has been annulled shall forfeit his share, which shall pass to the remaining beneficiary.

*(c)* Each beneficiary must be of "sound reputation" in the community, as determined in the sole discretion of the Trustee. I intend this provision to exclude from benefit any grandson who has fallen into habitual drunkenness, criminality, or moral turpitude. I do not wish to reward dissolution.

*(d)* Upon both beneficiaries meeting the above conditions, the Trust principal shall be divided equally — SIXTY THOUSAND DOLLARS ($60,000.00) to each beneficiary, plus any accumulated income.

*(e)* If either beneficiary fails to meet the conditions by the time the younger beneficiary reaches age forty (40), the entire Trust principal shall be distributed to the qualifying beneficiary. If neither qualifies, the principal shall be donated to the Crestfall Seamen's Benevolent Society.

**Section 5.** During the period before distribution, the Trust income shall be reinvested in the principal.

**Section 6.** I impose these conditions not out of spite but out of love. I watched my son, Robert, destroy himself with drink and idleness because he was given money before he was given purpose. I will not make the same mistake with my grandsons. Marriage steadies a man. Age teaches him. Together, they may produce the kind of men I hoped Robert would be and was not.

[...]

*IN WITNESS WHEREOF, I have hereunto set my hand and seal this 15th day of March, 1955.*

*Silas Ezekiel Marsh*

*Witnessed by:*
*Harold T. Bancroft, Esq.*
*Miss Patricia Henley (secretary)*

---

[PRODUCTION NOTE: The critical clause is Section 4(b) — "lawfully married." If Vera's fraud is exposed, the marriage is void. William becomes unmarried under the trust terms, forfeiting his share. More: criminal investigation follows. Vera loses everything. Section 4(c) about "sound reputation" also bears on Edward's drinking — but the trust attorney determined that Edward's widower status satisfied the marriage requirement and his drinking did not rise to "habitual drunkenness" in a legal sense. Section 6 — Silas's explanation of his reasoning — is both paternalistic and poignant. He was trying to protect his grandsons from their father's fate. He could not have imagined that his protective conditions would create the motive for murder.]

*Note: Edward Marsh is known to drink heavily. I have considered whether this constitutes "habitual drunkenness" under Section 4(c). In my judgment, it does not — Mr. Marsh maintains employment, pays his debts, and has not been arrested or publicly disgraced. Captain Marsh intended Section 4(c) to exclude dissolute behavior, not to punish a grieving man for seeking comfort in a bottle. I note this for the record in case the determination is questioned.

**NOTE ON EDWARD MARSH'S MARRIAGE STATUS:** Edward's wife died in 1962. He has not remarried. The trust requires beneficiaries to be "lawfully married at the time of distribution." After consultation with the probate court, I have determined that Edward's widower status satisfies this condition. The clause was intended to ensure the grandsons were settled men — not to penalize a man for his wife's death. Edward was lawfully married. His wife's death does not retroactively invalidate that marriage.

**PROJECTED DISTRIBUTION:** Upon Edward's 35th birthday in 1964.

**ADDENDUM (handwritten by Bancroft):**

*I have been contacted by Mrs. Agnes Doyle, mother of the late Catherine Marsh, who alleges that William Marsh's wife "Helen" is not who she claims to be. Mrs. Doyle asserts that "Helen Marsh" is actually an orphan named Vera Novak who stole a dead woman's identity. Mrs. Doyle has provided documentation including Catherine Marsh's diary, postal records, and a newspaper obituary for the real Helen Marsh, who died in 1958.*

*I advised Mrs. Doyle that trust administration is not the appropriate venue for criminal allegations and recommended she contact law enforcement.*

*However — for this file only — I wish to note the legal consequences if Mrs. Doyle's allegations were substantiated:*

*1. A marriage obtained through identity fraud is voidable under Maine law.*
*2. If annulled, William would be considered unmarried at the time of distribution.*
*3. Under Section 4(b), his share would pass to Edward.*
*4. More significantly: Vera Novak would face criminal charges for fraud, identity theft, and potentially other offenses depending on investigation.*
*5. The trust distribution itself could be challenged and potentially reversed.*

*I note these consequences for the file. I take no investigative action. That is not my role. But I observe that Mrs. Doyle's documentation is meticulous and her arguments are compelling, and if I were a younger man with fewer obligations to professional neutrality, I might be inclined to listen more carefully to a schoolteacher who has clearly done her homework.*

*— H.T.B.*

## Evidence Piece 4: William Marsh's Statement, 1971

[PRINTED ON: Handwritten on plain white paper, rough masculine hand.]

**Statement of William Robert Marsh**
**Given to Mrs. Agnes Doyle**
**October 22, 1971**

---

Agnes,

You were right. About all of it. I am sorry it took me so long to see it — or rather, to stop pretending I didn't see it.

Helen left me in April. She said she wanted a divorce. No explanation, no argument, no tears. She was calm the way she was always calm — controlled, composed, watching me from behind those careful eyes to see how I would react. I reacted the way I always react to things that frighten me: I agreed. I signed the papers. I let her take what she wanted. She took half of everything, including most of what remained of the trust money, and moved to California. She left no forwarding address.

After she was gone, I found things in the house. Things she had hidden, though not particularly well — perhaps she wanted me to find them. Perhaps even Helen — Vera — was tired of the lie.

In the back of her closet, in a hatbox:
- A birth certificate in the name of Vera Novak, from Bangor, Maine
- A Social Security card in the same name
- Three letters from St. Agnes Home for Children, Bangor
- A newspaper clipping from the Boston Globe, dated October 1958, reporting the death of a woman named Helen Ruth Marsh in a car accident

I sat in my kitchen with that clipping in my hand and I understood everything. I understood it the way you understand a joke that everyone else has been laughing at for years — suddenly, completely, and with a shame so profound it felt like drowning. The woman I married was not the woman I thought she was. She had taken a dead woman's name, a dead woman's Social Security number, a dead woman's place in the world, and she had done it for money. My grandfather's money. The Marsh family trust.

I thought about calling the police. I thought about it for three days. I sat at my kitchen table and I drank and I thought, and at the end of three days I hadn't called. Because if I called the police, they would investigate. And if they investigated, they would ask about Catherine. And I would have to face the possibility that Helen — that Vera — killed your daughter.

And I could not face that. I am a coward, Agnes. I have always been a coward. Edward got the courage, quiet and stubborn and unshakeable. I got the charm. And neither one of us ended up with what we needed. Edward needed to say more. I needed to see more. Catherine needed both of us and we failed her — Edward by silence and I by blindness.

I do not know for certain that Vera killed Catherine. I was asleep that night. I had drunk most of a bottle of bourbon and I slept like a dead man through the worst night of my family's life. I heard nothing. I saw nothing. When I woke in the morning, Edward was outside the cottage making a sound I had never heard before — a sound like a man trying to breathe underwater.

But I know this: Catherine was asking questions about Helen. I noticed it at that last dinner — the way Catherine watched Helen across the table with those sharp gray-green eyes, the ones that saw everything, that noticed the flowers and the maiden names and the gloves and the things that didn't add up. And Helen watching Catherine right back, smiling that careful smile, saying all the right things in all the right ways while her eyes calculated and measured and weighed. Two women circling each other like boxers in a ring, each knowing what the other knew, each waiting for the first blow.

I saw this. I saw it and I told myself it was family tension. I told myself women were complicated. I poured another glass of bourbon and I went to bed and I left them to it.

If Vera killed Catherine to protect her secret — and Agnes, in my heart I believe she did — then the trust money that paid for my house, my car, my comfortable, stupid, unexamined life was bought with your daughter's blood. I do not know how to live with that. I have been trying for six months and I have not found a way.

You may do whatever you wish with this statement. Show it to the police. Print it in the newspaper. I don't care anymore. I should have listened to you years ago. You came to me with the diary and the letters and the evidence, and I nodded and I said the right things and I did nothing. Because doing something would have meant admitting that my marriage was a fraud, that my wife was a stranger, and that my brother's wife died because I was too charming and too stupid and too drunk to notice the monster sleeping beside me.

I am sorry, Agnes. I am sorry for Catherine. I am sorry for Edward. I am sorry for the years you spent shouting into the silence while I covered my ears.

William

## Evidence Piece 6: Lighthouse Commission Letter

[PRINTED ON: Official U.S. Coast Guard letterhead.]

**UNITED STATES COAST GUARD**
**FIRST DISTRICT — PORTLAND, MAINE**

August 22, 1965

Mr. Edward T. Marsh
Dunmore Point Lighthouse
Crestfall, Maine

Dear Mr. Marsh:

This letter is to inform you that the United States Coast Guard has approved the automation of Dunmore Point Light Station, effective October 1, 1965.

Upon automation, the position of Lighthouse Keeper will be eliminated. You are hereby released from your duties as of October 1, 1965, with our sincere gratitude for eight years of faithful service.

Your service record has been exemplary. The station logs maintained under your tenure are among the most thorough and accurate in the First District. Inspector Fletcher has asked me to note particularly the quality of your lens maintenance and your conscientious operation of the fog signal. You have been a credit to the traditions of the Lighthouse Service.

We understand that this transition will require you to vacate the keeper's dwelling. You may remain in residence until November 1, 1965, to arrange alternative housing.

Thank you, Mr. Marsh, for keeping the light.

Respectfully,
Captain William H. Drake
Commander, First Coast Guard District

*[Edward's handwritten note on the letter:]*
*"Eight years. Catherine was with me for five of them. The best five."*

## Evidence Piece 7: Trust Correspondence

[PRINTED ON: Legal letterhead, typed.]

**BANCROFT & WELLS**
*18 Exchange Street, Portland, Maine*

June 3, 1963

Mrs. Agnes M. Doyle
22 Maple Street
Crestfall, Maine

Dear Mrs. Doyle:

Thank you for your letter of May 15 and the enclosed documentation regarding your allegations concerning the identity of Mrs. Helen Marsh.

While I have reviewed the materials you provided — including excerpts from your daughter's diary, postal records, and the newspaper obituary of a Helen Marsh who died in 1958 — I must reiterate that the administration of the Marsh Family Trust is a legal matter, not an investigative one. It is not within the scope of my duties as Trustee to verify the identities of the beneficiaries' spouses beyond what is presented in official documentation (marriage certificate, identification).

I strongly recommend that you present your evidence to the appropriate law enforcement authorities. If a criminal investigation results in findings relevant to the trust, I will of course take appropriate action at that time.

I am sorry for your loss, Mrs. Doyle. I knew your daughter only slightly, but she struck me as a woman of unusual intelligence and character.

Sincerely,
Harold T. Bancroft, Esq.

*[Agnes's note in red pen:]*
*"Another man who sees the truth and looks away. 'Not within the scope of my duties.' Catherine's death was not within the scope of anyone's duties, it seems. No one's job. No one's responsibility. No one's problem but mine."*

## Evidence Piece 8: Agnes Doyle's Final Letter

[PRINTED ON: Typed, personal stationery. The last letter in Agnes's campaign.]

Mrs. Agnes M. Doyle
22 Maple Street
Crestfall, Maine

January 7, 1983

The Honorable Joseph E. Brennan
Governor, State of Maine
State House
Augusta, Maine 04333

Dear Governor Brennan:

I am writing to you because I have written to everyone else.

Over the past twenty-one years, I have written forty-six letters to law enforcement, legal authorities, and elected officials regarding the murder of my daughter, Catherine Anne Marsh, at Dunmore Point, Crestfall, Maine, on March 8, 1962. This is letter forty-seven. I have received form replies, boilerplate sympathies, and bureaucratic silences. I have not received justice.

I will not repeat the full case here. My previous letters contain the evidence — the diary, the weather records, the medical report, the testimony of the postmistress, the death certificate of the real Helen Marsh, the trust documents, the confession of William Marsh. I have enclosed a summary with this letter. I ask only that someone read it.

I am seventy-eight years old, Governor. My health is declining. I have arthritis in my hands that makes typing painful and writing impossible on my worst days. I may not be able to write many more letters. But I will write this one, and I will say what I have said forty-six times before:

My daughter was murdered. The evidence proves it. The killer was never investigated, never charged, never held accountable. This is a failure of justice so profound that it shames every institution in this state that was supposed to prevent it.

I am not asking for sympathy. I am asking for action. Open the case. Read the evidence. Follow the truth where it leads.

Respectfully and for the last time,
Agnes M. Doyle

*[No response was received. Agnes Doyle died on March 8, 1984.]*

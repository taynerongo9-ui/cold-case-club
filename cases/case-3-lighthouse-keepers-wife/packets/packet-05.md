# Packet 5: "The Body"
## Theme: Medical Evidence — The Bruising That Doesn't Fit

**Packet contains:**
1. Dr. Thomas Greer's examination report
2. Crime scene description — Constable Hale's detailed notes
3. Dr. Greer's private letter to the State Medical Examiner (unsent)
4. Constable Hale's interview notes — Edward Marsh

## Evidence Piece 1: Dr. Greer's Examination Report

[PRINTED ON: Medical form paper, typewriter font. Header for "Thomas A. Greer, M.D. — General Practice — Crestfall, Maine." Formal medical language but accessible — Greer is a small-town GP, not a forensic pathologist.]

**MEDICAL EXAMINATION REPORT**

**Physician:** Thomas A. Greer, M.D.
**Date of Examination:** March 9, 1962 (at scene, 0900 hrs; at office, 1100 hrs)
**Decedent:** Catherine Anne Marsh, female, age 29
**Referred by:** Constable Robert J. Hale, Crestfall Police Department
**Case Number:** 62-0041

---

**EXTERNAL EXAMINATION:**

The body is that of a well-nourished Caucasian female, approximately 5 feet 5 inches in height, estimated weight 125 pounds, with auburn hair and gray-green eyes. The body was examined first at the scene (base of Dunmore Point cliff) and subsequently at my office in Crestfall.

**Clothing:** Dark green wool coat (one button missing — button not recovered at scene). Gray cotton dress. Undergarments. One stocking torn. One brown leather shoe, low heel, on the right foot. Left shoe absent (recovered separately on cliff path above).

**Rigor mortis:** Well established in all extremities, consistent with death approximately 8-10 hours prior to examination.

**Body temperature:** Significantly cooled, consistent with outdoor exposure at ambient temperature of approximately 33-37°F over 8-10 hours. Core temperature not measurable with available equipment.

---

**INJURIES — HEAD AND NECK:**

- Massive contusion and laceration to the right occipital region (back of head), with underlying skull fracture palpable through the wound. This is the primary fatal injury, consistent with striking the rocks below the cliff.
- Small contusion to the left temporal region (left temple), approximately 2 cm in diameter. This injury is notably LESS severe than the occipital wound and appears to have occurred separately. **Note: This contusion could represent a blow received BEFORE the fall, or an injury sustained during the fall itself. I cannot determine the sequence with certainty.**
- Abrasion to the right cheek and chin, consistent with contact with rough stone.

**INJURIES — TORSO:**

- Multiple rib fractures, left side (ribs 4-8), consistent with impact.
- Probable internal hemorrhage (abdomen distended, firm on palpation). Without autopsy facilities, I cannot determine specific organ damage.

**INJURIES — EXTREMITIES:**

- Fracture of the right femur (thigh bone), compound, with bone visible through skin.
- Fracture of the left wrist (Colles' fracture pattern), consistent with an outstretched hand attempting to break a fall.
- Multiple lacerations and abrasions to both hands and forearms.

**— SIGNIFICANT FINDING —**

- **Bilateral bruising to the upper arms.** Both arms display contusions on the lateral and anterior surfaces of the upper arms (biceps/deltoid region), approximately 4-6 inches below the shoulder joints. The bruising on each arm shows a pattern of **four parallel elongated marks on the outer surface and a single broader mark on the inner surface**, consistent with the pattern produced by a **gripping human hand** — four fingers on the outside, thumb on the inside.
- The bruising is BILATERAL — present on BOTH arms in a symmetrical pattern, suggesting the deceased was **gripped firmly on both arms simultaneously, from the front, by a person facing her.**
- The coloration of these contusions (dark purple-red, with defined margins) suggests they are relatively fresh — inflicted within hours of death, not days.
- **I wish to state clearly: this bruising pattern is NOT consistent with injuries typically sustained in an uncontrolled fall from height.** In a fall, one might expect random contusions from striking outcroppings during descent, but symmetrical bilateral gripping bruises on the upper arms are, in my medical opinion, indicative of another person having forcibly gripped the deceased shortly before or at the time of death.

---

**CAUSE OF DEATH:** Blunt force trauma to the head consistent with a fall from significant height (approximately 80 feet).

**MANNER OF DEATH:** I defer to Constable Hale's determination. However, I wish to note my concern regarding the upper arm bruising described above, which I believe warrants further investigation.

*[Signed] Thomas A. Greer, M.D.*
*March 9, 1962*

---

[PRODUCTION NOTE: Greer's report should be the moment subscribers sit up straight. The bruising description is detailed and damning. He's clearly uncomfortable with the accident ruling but doesn't have the authority or courage to overrule the constable. His careful phrasing — "I wish to state clearly" — is as close to shouting as a man of his temperament gets.]

## Evidence Piece 2: Crime Scene Description

[PRINTED ON: Police report paper. Typewriter font.]

**CRESTFALL POLICE DEPARTMENT**
**SUPPLEMENTAL REPORT — CASE 62-0041**
**Subject: Detailed Scene Description — Cliff Base**
**Officer: Constable R. Hale**
**Date: March 9, 1962**

---

Arrived at Dunmore Point at 0745 hrs. Mr. Edward Marsh met me at the cottage and led me down the boathouse stairs to the cliff base. The stairs are wooden, steep, and in fair condition. 87 steps. At the base, I proceeded north along the rocks approximately 40 yards to where the body of Catherine Marsh was lying.

**BODY POSITION (as found by this officer):** The body had been moved from its original position by Mr. Marsh, who stated he had turned his wife over and held her. As observed by me, the deceased was lying on her back on a flat section of granite rock, approximately 6 feet from the waterline. Her coat was open. Her hair was loose and wet from sea spray. Her right arm was at her side; her left arm was across her abdomen. Her eyes were closed — Mr. Marsh stated he had closed them.

**ORIGINAL POSITION (per Mr. Marsh):** Mr. Marsh stated he found his wife lying face down on the rocks, approximately 3 feet from where she was when I observed her. He stated he turned her over, saw that she was dead, and "held her for a long while" before climbing back up to the cottage to telephone for help.

**SURROUNDING AREA:** The rocks at the cliff base are granite, rough and uneven, with tidal pools. At the time of my arrival, the tide was low and the body was well above the waterline. High tide would have reached the body's location, meaning if discovery had been delayed, the body might have been carried out to sea.

**CLIFF FACE:** The cliff at this point is approximately 80 feet high, nearly vertical, with some rocky outcroppings. No vegetation. Seabird nesting ledges visible at approximately 30 and 50 feet.

**CLIFF PATH ABOVE:** I climbed back up and examined the cliff path at the point directly above where the body was found. The path here is approximately 3 feet wide, unpaved, with loose gravel and exposed rock. The edge of the cliff is not marked or protected in any way. A single shoe (brown leather, left foot, low heel) was found approximately 12 feet from the cliff edge, on the path.

**ADDITIONAL OBSERVATIONS:**
- No signs of struggle on the cliff path (scuffing, disturbed gravel, etc.), though this is difficult to assess on such terrain.
- No blood on the cliff path.
- No personal items other than the shoe.
- The section of path where the shoe was found is a known hazard — the ground slopes slightly toward the cliff edge and is uneven.

**CONCLUSION:** The physical evidence is consistent with the deceased having walked the cliff path, lost her footing, and fallen from the edge. The presence of only one shoe suggests she may have stumbled, losing the shoe, and then fallen.

*[Signed] Constable R. Hale*

[PRODUCTION NOTE: Hale is describing the scene accurately but interpreting it through his predetermined conclusion. Note that he says "no signs of struggle" but also admits the terrain makes this "difficult to assess." A more experienced investigator would have recognized that an 80-foot cliff requires very little struggle to become a crime scene.]

## Evidence Piece 3: Dr. Greer's Unsent Letter

[PRINTED ON: Personal stationery — "Thomas A. Greer, M.D." printed at top. Handwritten in a careful physician's hand. The letter is creased as though folded and unfolded many times. Found in Dr. Greer's papers after his death in 1979.]

**Thomas A. Greer, M.D.**
*14 Harbor Street, Crestfall, Maine*

March 15, 1962

Dr. Robert J. Hanson
Chief Medical Examiner
State of Maine
Augusta, Maine

Dear Dr. Hanson,

I write to you regarding a case that I have been unable to put from my mind.

On March 9 of this year, I examined the body of Mrs. Catherine Marsh, age 29, of Dunmore Point, Crestfall. The death has been ruled accidental — a fall from the cliff path at the lighthouse. I conducted the only medical examination, as Crestfall has no morgue, no autopsy facilities, and no forensic capability. The body was released for burial on March 11.

I am troubled by what I found.

The fall injuries were extensive and consistent with the eighty-foot drop. I have no quarrel with the proximate cause of death. What troubles me is the bruising I observed on both of Mrs. Marsh's upper arms. The bruising is bilateral and symmetrical — four elongated marks on the outer arm and a broader mark on the inner arm, on both sides. I have been a physician for twenty-two years. I have seen many bruises. These are grip marks. Someone seized this woman by both arms with considerable force, and they did so within hours of her death.

Constable Hale has accepted my report but has attributed the bruising to "the fall itself." I lack the forensic expertise to argue the point with scientific authority, which is why I am writing to you. In your professional opinion, is bilateral symmetrical grip bruising on the upper arms consistent with injuries sustained in an uncontrolled fall from a cliff? I believe it is not, but I want to be certain before I press the matter.

I want to do right by this woman. I have lived in Crestfall for eighteen years, and Catherine Marsh was a neighbor and a friend. If there is more to her death than an accident, I owe it to her to say so.

I would be grateful for your guidance.

Respectfully yours,

Thomas A. Greer, M.D.

---

*[Handwritten note at bottom, in different ink, apparently added later:]*

*This letter was never sent. I wrote it and I held it and in the end I did not have the courage. Hale had closed the case. The family wanted peace. Who was I to reopen the wound? But the bruises are there in my notes, and they will speak for themselves if anyone ever thinks to look.*

*God forgive me.*

*— T.G., April 1962*

---

[PRODUCTION NOTE: This is one of the most emotionally powerful pieces in the entire case. Greer KNEW something was wrong. He wrote the letter that could have cracked the case open — and never sent it. His confession at the bottom is devastating. Print the main letter in careful handwriting. The postscript should be in shakier, smaller writing — a man arguing with his own conscience.]

## Evidence Piece 4: Constable Hale's Interview — Edward Marsh

[PRINTED ON: Police report paper, typewriter font. Q&A format.]

**CRESTFALL POLICE DEPARTMENT**
**WITNESS STATEMENT — CASE 62-0041**
**Subject: Edward T. Marsh (husband of deceased)**
**Interviewed by: Constable R. Hale**
**Date: March 9, 1962, 1400 hrs**
**Location: Keeper's Cottage, Dunmore Point**

---

**Q:** Mr. Marsh, can you tell me about the evening of March 8? Start with dinner.

**A:** We had dinner at six. Catherine, myself, William, and Helen. It was a quiet meal. Catherine made fish chowder. She was a fine cook.

**Q:** How was the mood at dinner? Any arguments, disagreements?

**A:** No. Quiet. Catherine was... she seemed thoughtful. She'd been that way for a few days. Not unhappy, just — like she had something on her mind. I didn't press her on it. I should have.

**Q:** Had you and Catherine been getting along?

**A:** [Pause.] We'd had our difficulties. Last year was hard. Catherine lost a baby, and she — it took something out of her. Out of both of us. I won't lie to you, Bob. I drank too much. We argued. But things were getting better. She was painting again. She seemed more like herself. I thought we were coming through it.

**Q:** What happened after dinner?

**A:** I went up to the tower around half seven. That's my routine — evening watch runs until dawn. William took his bottle and went to the guest room. He was half in the bag already. Helen — I don't know what Helen did. She was in the sitting room when I left.

**Q:** And Catherine?

**A:** She was clearing the dishes. She said she might read for a while. She said goodnight to me at the door to the tower stairs. She said — [Pause.] She said, "Be careful up there, Edward." She always said that. Every night.

**Q:** When did you next see Catherine?

**A:** [Long pause.] When I found her. At the bottom of the cliff. At quarter past six this morning.

**Q:** You didn't come down from the tower at all during the night?

**A:** No. My watch is my watch. I stay in the watch room from half seven until six. That's the job.

**Q:** Did you hear anything unusual during the night? A cry, a shout?

**A:** No. But the watch room is sixty feet up, the windows are thick, and when the fog horn is running — [Stops.] Wait. The fog horn wasn't running. I shut it down at eight o'clock when the fog cleared. So it was quiet. But I didn't hear anything. The cottage is below me and to the south. The cliff path runs north. If Catherine — if something happened on the path, I might not have heard it from the tower. Especially if I was focused on the log or the mechanisms.

**Q:** Did Catherine have any reason to walk the cliff path at night?

**A:** Not that I know of. She's walked it at all hours, but usually only if she's going to town or coming back. There was no reason to go to town that evening.

**Q:** Mr. Marsh, is there anyone who might have wished to harm your wife?

**A:** [Pause.] No. Everyone loved Catherine. She was — she was the good one. Between the two of us, she was the good one.

**Q:** One more question. Do you recognize this key? [Shows Item #7, small brass key found in Catherine's coat pocket.]

**A:** No. I've never seen it. Catherine never mentioned it. I don't know what it opens.

---

*Statement concluded at 1435 hrs.*
*Witness appeared distraught. Appeared to have been drinking. Cooperative throughout.*

*[Signed] Constable R. Hale*

[PRODUCTION NOTE: Edward's testimony should break hearts. He's a man in shock. The detail about Catherine saying "Be careful up there" every night is the kind of specific, mundane tenderness that makes loss feel real. Note that Edward himself corrects the fog horn detail mid-statement — he realizes the horn wasn't running that night, meaning he should have been able to hear something. This haunts him.]

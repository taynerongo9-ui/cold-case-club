## Evidence Piece 1: Dr. Greer's Examination Report

[PRINTED ON: Medical form paper, typewriter font. Header: "Thomas A. Greer, M.D. — General Practice — Crestfall, Maine."]

**MEDICAL EXAMINATION REPORT**

**Physician:** Thomas A. Greer, M.D.
**Date of Examination:** March 9, 1962 (at scene, 0900 hrs; at office, 1100 hrs)
**Decedent:** Catherine Anne Marsh, female, age 29
**Referred by:** Constable Robert J. Hale, Crestfall Police Department
**Case Number:** 62-0041

---

**EXTERNAL EXAMINATION:**

The body is that of a well-nourished Caucasian female, approximately 5 feet 5 inches in height, estimated weight 125 pounds, with auburn hair (loose, damp from sea spray) and gray-green eyes. The body was examined first at the scene (base of Dunmore Point cliff, 0900 hrs) and subsequently at my office in Crestfall (1100 hrs). The body was transported from the scene by Mr. Edward Marsh and Mr. William Marsh, with my assistance, via the boathouse stairs and then by automobile to my office on Harbor Street.

**Clothing:** Dark green wool coat (one button missing — third from top, button not recovered). Gray cotton dress (torn at left shoulder seam, consistent with tearing during the fall or on rocks). Undergarments intact. One stocking torn at the right knee with abrasion visible beneath. One brown leather shoe, low heel, on the right foot. Left shoe absent (recovered separately on cliff path above by Constable Hale). Wedding band, gold, plain, on left ring finger. Wristwatch (Timex, ladies' model) on left wrist, crystal shattered, hands stopped at 9:14 PM.

**Rigor mortis:** Well established in all extremities, consistent with death approximately 8-10 hours prior to examination at 0900 hrs. This places time of death between approximately 2300 hrs March 8 and 0100 hrs March 9 by rigor alone, though I note that the stopped wristwatch (9:14 PM) suggests an earlier time of death, which is consistent if we account for the accelerating effect of cold ambient temperatures (33-37F) on rigor development.

**Body temperature:** Significantly cooled, consistent with outdoor exposure at ambient temperatures of approximately 33-37F over 8-10 hours. Core temperature not measurable with available equipment. I do not have a long-shaft thermometer suitable for this purpose. This is a limitation of my examination.

---

**INJURIES — HEAD AND NECK:**

- Massive contusion and laceration to the right occipital region (back of head), approximately 8 cm in length, with underlying skull fracture palpable through the wound. This is the primary fatal injury, consistent with striking the rocks below the cliff at high velocity. Brain matter visible at wound margins. This injury alone would have been rapidly fatal.
- Small contusion to the left temporal region (left temple), approximately 2 cm in diameter, with slight swelling but no laceration. This injury is notably LESS severe than the occipital wound and appears to have occurred separately. **Note: This contusion could represent a blow received BEFORE the fall — consistent with being struck on the side of the head — or an injury sustained during the fall itself (striking an outcropping on the cliff face during descent). I cannot determine the sequence with certainty, but I wish to record that the appearance of this contusion is qualitatively different from the other injuries: it is more localized, more defined in its margins, and less traumatic than what I would expect from cliff-face impact.**
- Abrasion to the right cheek and chin, consistent with contact with rough stone. Gravel particles embedded in the wound.
- No injuries to the neck consistent with strangulation or manual compression.

**INJURIES — TORSO:**

- Multiple rib fractures, left side (ribs 4-8), consistent with lateral impact on rocks.
- Probable internal hemorrhage (abdomen distended and firm on palpation). Without autopsy facilities, I cannot determine specific organ damage. I suspect splenic rupture based on the left-sided rib fractures and abdominal distension.
- Bruising over the left hip (iliac crest), consistent with impact.

**INJURIES — EXTREMITIES:**

- Fracture of the right femur (thigh bone), compound, with bone visible through the skin of the anterior thigh. A devastating injury that would have been immediately incapacitating.
- Fracture of the left wrist (Colles' fracture pattern), consistent with an outstretched hand attempting to break a fall. **Note: This injury is significant. A Colles' fracture occurs when a person extends their hand to arrest a fall. It suggests the deceased was conscious and reactive during at least part of the descent — she tried to catch herself. This is inconsistent with a person who was unconscious or incapacitated before the fall (as might occur with a severe pre-fall head blow) but consistent with a person who was pushed and fell while fully alert.**
- Multiple lacerations and abrasions to both hands and forearms, consistent with contact with rough rock surfaces.
- Fingernails: intact on both hands. No tissue or fibers visible beneath the nails. I did not scrape the nails for evidence, as I had no collection materials and was not operating under the assumption of foul play at the time of examination. **I note this omission with regret.**

**--- SIGNIFICANT FINDING ---**

- **Bilateral bruising to the upper arms.** Both arms display contusions on the lateral and anterior surfaces of the upper arms (biceps/deltoid region), approximately 4-6 inches below the shoulder joints. The bruising on each arm shows a pattern of **four parallel elongated marks on the outer surface and a single broader mark on the inner surface**, consistent with the pattern produced by a **gripping human hand** — four fingers on the outside, thumb on the inside.

- The bruising is BILATERAL — present on BOTH arms in a symmetrical pattern, suggesting the deceased was **gripped firmly on both arms simultaneously, from the front, by a person facing her.**

- The coloration of these contusions (dark purple-red, with defined margins) suggests they are relatively fresh — inflicted within hours of death, not days. There is no yellowing at the margins, which would indicate older bruising.

- The grip pattern is approximately consistent with an adult hand of moderate size — smaller than a large man's hand, I would estimate, though I acknowledge this is subjective without forensic measurement tools.

- **I wish to state clearly: this bruising pattern is NOT consistent with injuries typically sustained in an uncontrolled fall from height.** In a fall, one might expect random contusions from striking outcroppings during descent, but symmetrical bilateral gripping bruises on the upper arms — identical in placement, pressure pattern, and coloration on both sides — are, in my medical opinion, indicative of another person having forcibly gripped the deceased shortly before or at the time of death.

- I raised this concern verbally with Constable Hale during my scene examination. Constable Hale stated that the bruising "could have resulted from the fall itself." I did not press the point at the scene, as Mr. Marsh was present and in extreme distress, and I did not wish to create additional anguish. I am pressing the point now, in this report, for the record.

---

**CAUSE OF DEATH:** Blunt force trauma to the head (occipital region) consistent with a fall from significant height (approximately 80 feet), resulting in massive skull fracture and brain injury.

**MANNER OF DEATH:** I defer to Constable Hale's determination regarding the classification of manner. However, I wish to note my serious concern regarding the upper arm bruising described above, the left temporal contusion of uncertain origin, and the overall pattern of injuries, which I believe warrant further investigation by a qualified forensic pathologist. I strongly recommend that the State Medical Examiner's office be consulted.

*[Signed] Thomas A. Greer, M.D.*
*March 9, 1962*

## Evidence Piece 2: Crime Scene Description

[PRINTED ON: Police report paper. Typewriter font.]

**CRESTFALL POLICE DEPARTMENT**
**SUPPLEMENTAL REPORT — CASE 62-0041**
**Subject: Detailed Scene Description — Cliff Base**
**Officer: Constable R. Hale**
**Date: March 9, 1962**

---

Arrived at Dunmore Point at 0745 hrs. Mr. Edward Marsh met me at the cottage. He was standing on the porch in his shirtsleeves despite the cold (30F). His hands were shaking. His shirt was stained with what appeared to be blood — his wife's, presumably, from holding her. He led me down the boathouse stairs to the cliff base without speaking. The stairs are wooden, steep, and in fair condition, though three treads are loose (as noted in the recent Coast Guard inspection). 87 steps. At the base, I proceeded north along the rocks approximately 40 yards to where the body of Catherine Marsh was lying.

**BODY POSITION (as found by this officer):** The body had been moved from its original position by Mr. Marsh, who stated he had turned his wife over, closed her eyes, and held her. As observed by me, the deceased was lying on her back on a flat section of granite rock, approximately 6 feet from the waterline. Her coat was open. Her hair was loose and wet from sea spray. Her right arm was at her side; her left arm was across her abdomen (positioned by Mr. Marsh). Her eyes were closed (also by Mr. Marsh). Her expression was — I note this only because it struck me at the time — not pained or frightened. She looked peaceful, though this may be attributable to the passage of time and the effects of cold on the musculature.

**ORIGINAL POSITION (per Mr. Marsh):** Mr. Marsh stated he found his wife lying face down on the rocks, approximately 3 feet from where she was when I observed her. Her left arm had been beneath her body. Her right arm was extended above her head. Mr. Marsh stated he turned her over, saw that she was dead ("her eyes were open and she wasn't there"), and "held her for a long while" before climbing back up to the cottage to telephone for help. He estimated he was with her for approximately one hour before calling.

**SURROUNDING AREA:** The rocks at the cliff base are rough, dark granite, with tidal pools containing seaweed, barnacles, and small crabs. Broken shells scattered across the surface. Sea spray reaches this area regularly — the rocks were damp. At the time of my arrival, the tide was low and receding (low tide approximately 0600 per the tide table). High tide at 0604 would have reached the body's location, meaning the water had only recently receded. Several small pools of seawater remained on the flat rock surface near the body. If discovery had been delayed by even one hour, the tide might have shifted the body or carried it out to sea entirely.

**CLIFF FACE:** The cliff at this point is approximately 80 feet high, nearly vertical, composed of dark granite with horizontal banding. Some rocky outcroppings at approximately 30 and 50 feet — seabird nesting ledges. A smear of what appeared to be fabric (green, matching the deceased's coat) was visible on the outcropping at 30 feet. No vegetation on the cliff face. The rock is wet from spray and condensation.

**CLIFF PATH ABOVE:** I climbed back up via the boathouse stairs and walked the cliff path to the point directly above where the body was found. The path here is approximately 3 feet wide, consisting of loose gravel and exposed rock. The ground slopes slightly toward the cliff edge — perhaps 5 degrees. The edge of the cliff is not marked or protected. A single shoe (brown leather, left foot, low heel, matching the shoe on the deceased's right foot) was found on the path approximately 12 feet from the cliff edge, lying on its side, toe pointing away from the cliff.

**ADDITIONAL OBSERVATIONS:**
- No clear signs of struggle on the cliff path (scuffing, disturbed gravel, drag marks). However, I note that the terrain is rough and irregular, making it difficult to distinguish normal wear from evidence of a confrontation. A more experienced investigator with proper equipment might find more than I was able to.
- No blood on the cliff path.
- No personal items other than the shoe on the path.
- The section of path where the shoe was found is a known hazard — the ground slopes toward the cliff edge and the surface is uneven.
- I noted no footprints. The surface is too hard (exposed rock and gravel) to retain footprints.
- The cottage's exterior lights were off. The only illumination on the path would have been the lighthouse beam (sweeping every 10 seconds) and moonlight. On a clear night with a three-quarter moon, visibility on the path would have been adequate to see one's footing. In fog, it would not.

**CONCLUSION:** The physical evidence is consistent with the deceased having walked the cliff path, lost her footing, and fallen from the edge. The presence of only one shoe suggests she may have stumbled, losing the shoe, and then lost her balance. However, I am obliged to note that the physical evidence is also consistent with other scenarios, and the scene was compromised by Mr. Marsh before my arrival.

*[Signed] Constable R. Hale*

## Evidence Piece 3: Dr. Greer's Unsent Letter

[PRINTED ON: Personal stationery — "Thomas A. Greer, M.D." Handwritten. Creased as though folded and unfolded many times. Found in Greer's papers after his death in 1979.]

**Thomas A. Greer, M.D.**
*14 Harbor Street, Crestfall, Maine*

March 15, 1962

Dr. Robert J. Hanson
Chief Medical Examiner
State of Maine
Augusta, Maine

Dear Dr. Hanson,

I write to you regarding a case that I have been unable to put from my mind since the morning I knelt on the rocks at the base of Dunmore Point and examined the body of a woman I had known for five years.

On March 9 of this year, I examined the body of Mrs. Catherine Marsh, age 29, of Dunmore Point, Crestfall. The death has been ruled accidental — a fall from the cliff path at the lighthouse. I conducted the only medical examination, as Crestfall has no morgue, no autopsy facilities, and no forensic capability of any kind. The body was released for burial on March 11. No autopsy was performed. I recognize now that this was an error, but at the time, the constable had already determined the manner of death, and I had neither the authority nor the facilities to insist otherwise.

I am troubled by what I found, and I am writing to you because I lack the forensic expertise to interpret my findings with the scientific certainty that the situation demands.

The fall injuries were extensive and consistent with the eighty-foot drop. I have no quarrel with the proximate cause of death. What troubles me is the bruising I observed on both of Mrs. Marsh's upper arms. The bruising is bilateral and symmetrical — four elongated marks on the outer arm and a broader mark on the inner arm, on both sides, in a pattern I can only describe as a gripping hand. I have been a physician for twenty-two years. I have treated injuries from falls, from machinery, from fights at the harbor tavern. I know what bruises look like when they are caused by impact with objects, and I know what they look like when they are caused by human hands. These are grip marks. Someone seized this woman by both arms with considerable force, from the front, and they did so within hours of her death.

I also noted a small contusion on the left temple — localized, defined, qualitatively different from the massive fatal wound on the back of the head. It could be a blow delivered before the fall. Or it could be an impact during the fall itself. I cannot be certain. But combined with the arm bruising, it concerns me.

Constable Hale has accepted my report but has attributed the bruising to "the fall itself." I expressed my reservations at the scene, but I did not press them as forcefully as I should have. There were reasons for my reticence — the husband was present, the scene was emotional, and I am not accustomed to contradicting officers of the law. These reasons feel hollow to me now.

In your professional opinion: is bilateral symmetrical grip bruising on the upper arms, with a four-finger-and-thumb pattern, consistent with injuries sustained in an uncontrolled fall from a cliff? I believe it is not, but I want to be certain before I press the matter further.

I also wish to ask: should I have insisted on an autopsy? Could fingernail scrapings have provided evidence of another person's involvement? I did not collect scrapings at the time because I was not operating under an assumption of foul play. I fear I may have lost evidence that cannot be recovered.

I want to do right by this woman, Dr. Hanson. I have lived in Crestfall for eighteen years, and Catherine Marsh was a neighbor and a friend. She brought me shortbread at Christmas. She once painted a watercolor of my office for my waiting room — the harbor view from my window. If there is more to her death than an accident, I owe it to her to say so, even if saying so makes me unpopular in a town that wants this to be over.

I would be grateful for your guidance. I would welcome a telephone call or a visit, if you are able.

Respectfully yours,

Thomas A. Greer, M.D.

---

*[Handwritten note at bottom, in different ink, apparently added months later:]*

*This letter was never sent. I wrote it and I held it and I carried it in my jacket pocket for two weeks and in the end I put it in this drawer. Hale had closed the case. The funeral was over. William and Helen had gone back to Portland. Edward was drinking himself to death. Agnes was writing her letters. Everyone wanted it to be finished. And I thought: who am I to reopen this wound? I am a small-town doctor who delivers babies and sets broken bones and treats influenza. I am not a forensic pathologist. Perhaps I am wrong about the bruises. Perhaps the fall really did cause them and I am seeing patterns that are not there.*

*But the bruises are there in my notes. They are clear and they are real and they are in the shape of hands. They will speak for themselves if anyone ever thinks to look.*

*God forgive me for my cowardice. I knew, and I said nothing.*

*— T.G., April 1962*

## Evidence Piece 4: Interview — Edward Marsh

[PRINTED ON: Police report paper, Q&A format.]

**CRESTFALL POLICE DEPARTMENT**
**WITNESS STATEMENT — CASE 62-0041**
**Subject: Edward T. Marsh (husband of deceased)**
**Interviewed by: Constable R. Hale**
**Date: March 9, 1962, 1400 hrs**
**Location: Keeper's Cottage, Dunmore Point**

---

**Q:** Mr. Marsh, I appreciate you speaking with me. I know this is a difficult time. Can you tell me about yesterday evening? Start with dinner.

**A:** We had dinner at six. Catherine, myself, William, and Helen. It was a quiet meal. Catherine made fish chowder — she always made chowder when William visited because it's his favorite. She made cornbread too. She was a fine cook. [Pause.] I keep talking about her like she's still here.

**Q:** Take your time. How was the mood at dinner? Any arguments, disagreements?

**A:** No. Quiet. Catherine was... she seemed thoughtful. Distracted, maybe. She'd been that way for a few days. Not unhappy, just — like she had something on her mind. A problem she was working through. I didn't press her on it. I should have.

**Q:** Had you and Catherine been getting along?

**A:** [Long pause.] We'd had our difficulties. Last year was hard — the hardest of our marriage. Catherine lost a baby in the spring, and she — it broke something in her. She went quiet in a way I'd never seen. She stopped painting. Stopped walking to town. She'd sit in the chair by the window and watch the sea for hours and not say a word. I didn't know how to help her. I'm not good with words — I never have been. I should have taken her to Portland, to a doctor who could help. Instead I... I drank. I drank too much. We argued. I won't lie to you, Bob. It was bad. But I never raised a hand to her. Not once. Not ever.

**Q:** I understand. And more recently?

**A:** Things were better. Getting better. She was painting again — this watercolor she'd been working on for weeks, a view from the lantern room. She was writing letters. Pressing flowers. She seemed more like herself. I thought we were coming through it. [Pause.] I thought we had time.

**Q:** What happened after dinner yesterday?

**A:** I went up to the tower around half seven. That's my routine — evening watch runs until dawn. It's my job and I take it seriously. I wound the clockwork, checked the lamp, started my log entries for the evening. William took his bottle and went to the guest room — he was half in the bag already, pardon my saying so. Helen — I don't know what Helen did. She was in the sitting room when I left. Reading a magazine, I think.

**Q:** And Catherine?

**A:** She was clearing the dishes. She said she might read for a while, or write a letter. She said goodnight to me at the door to the tower stairs. She said — [Pause. Witness visibly struggling.] She said, "Be careful up there, Edward." She always said that. Every night. Five years of nights, and every single one she said "Be careful up there, Edward." And I always said "I will." And then I went up and she... [Cannot continue for several moments.]

**Q:** When did you next see Catherine?

**A:** [Long pause.] This morning. Quarter past six. At the bottom of the cliff. On the rocks. Face down. [Pause.] I turned her over and her eyes were open and they were looking at something I couldn't see. I closed them. I sat down on the rocks and I held her and I said, "I'm here, Catherine. I'm here now." As though that meant anything. As though being there after meant anything at all.

**Q:** You didn't come down from the tower at all during the night?

**A:** No. My watch is my watch. I stay in the watch room from half seven until six. That's the job. I wind the clockwork every four hours. I record the conditions. I monitor the light. I do not leave the tower during a watch.

**Q:** Did you hear anything unusual during the night? A cry, a shout?

**A:** No. But — [Stops. Thinks.] Wait. The fog horn wasn't running. I shut it down at eight o'clock when the fog cleared. So it was quiet. But I didn't hear anything. The watch room is sixty feet up, the windows are thick glass, and even with the horn off, the wind and the sea make their own noise. The cottage is below me and to the south. The cliff path runs north. If Catherine — if something happened on the path, I might not have heard it from the tower. Especially if I was focused on the log or the mechanisms. [Pause.] But the horn wasn't running. It was quiet. I should have heard something. If she cried out — if she — [Cannot continue.]

**Q:** Did Catherine have any reason to walk the cliff path at night?

**A:** Not that I know of. She's walked it at all hours, in all weather, but usually only if she's going to town or coming back. There was no reason to go to town that evening. Unless she wanted to speak with someone. Unless she needed air. Unless... I don't know, Bob. I don't know why she was out there.

**Q:** Mr. Marsh, is there anyone who might have wished to harm your wife?

**A:** [Pause.] No. Everyone loved Catherine. She was — she was the good one. Between the two of us, she was the good one. She was the light in this house, and I don't mean the one in the tower.

**Q:** One more question. Do you recognize this key? [Shows Item #7, small brass key found in Catherine's coat pocket.]

**A:** No. I've never seen it. Catherine never mentioned it. I don't know what it opens. It's not for any lock in the cottage that I know of.

---

*Statement concluded at 1435 hrs.*
*Witness appeared distraught. Appeared to have been drinking (odor of alcohol, though this is not unusual for the witness). Cooperative throughout. No indication of deception, though the witness is not the most forthcoming individual by nature.*

*[Signed] Constable R. Hale*

## Evidence Piece 5: Interview — Helen Marsh

[PRINTED ON: Police report paper, Q&A format.]

**CRESTFALL POLICE DEPARTMENT**
**WITNESS STATEMENT — CASE 62-0041**
**Subject: Helen V. Marsh (sister-in-law of deceased)**
**Interviewed by: Constable R. Hale**
**Date: March 9, 1962, 1500 hrs**
**Location: Keeper's Cottage, Dunmore Point**

---

**Q:** Mrs. Marsh, thank you for your time. Can you tell me about yesterday evening?

**A:** Of course. We had dinner at six — Catherine made a lovely chowder. After dinner, Edward went to the tower for his watch and William went to the guest room. He'd been drinking, I'm afraid. He enjoys his bourbon when we visit.

**Q:** And you? What did you do after dinner?

**A:** I helped Catherine clear the dishes. We talked for a little while — about nothing in particular. Then I sat in the sitting room and read a magazine. I believe it was a Ladies' Home Journal that Catherine had borrowed from someone in town. After about an hour, I went up to bed.

**Q:** What time was that?

**A:** Approximately nine o'clock. Perhaps a few minutes before.

**Q:** Did Catherine say anything to you about going outside? Going for a walk?

**A:** No. When I went upstairs, she was still in the sitting room. She was sitting at the writing desk, looking at some papers. She said goodnight to me.

**Q:** What was Catherine's mood that evening?

**A:** She seemed... quiet. Thoughtful. Catherine was often quiet — it was her nature. I didn't think anything of it at the time.

**Q:** Did you hear anything unusual during the night? Any sound from outside — a cry, footsteps?

**A:** No. Nothing at all. I'm a sound sleeper, and William was — well, he was snoring rather loudly, if I'm honest. I didn't hear anything until William shook me awake this morning when Edward was calling from outside.

**Q:** Mrs. Marsh, how was your relationship with Catherine?

**A:** We were cordial. Friendly. I wouldn't say we were close — Catherine was a quiet woman and I'm more of a city person, so we didn't always have a great deal to talk about. But I liked her. She was a kind person. A talented painter. I admired her ability to live out here — I couldn't do it myself.

**Q:** Had Catherine seemed troubled recently? Worried about anything?

**A:** Not that I noticed. She seemed fine to me. But as I said, we weren't terribly close. She may have been troubled about something without my being aware.

**Q:** Is there anything else you can think of that might be relevant?

**A:** No, I'm sorry. I wish I could be of more help. This is a terrible thing. Poor Edward.

---

*Statement concluded at 1520 hrs.*
*Witness was composed and cooperative. Well-dressed. Calm. Answered all questions directly. No indication of distress beyond what is appropriate. No inconsistencies noted.*

*[Signed] Constable R. Hale*

[PRODUCTION NOTE: Helen's statement is a masterclass in controlled performance. She is calm, helpful, slightly distant — the perfect concerned-but-not-too-involved sister-in-law. But there are micro-contradictions for close readers: She says she went to bed "at approximately nine o'clock" but William says she "joined him" at about 9:00 PM — meaning she came from somewhere, arriving at 9. Where was she between dinner and 9 PM? She says she was reading in the sitting room, but the unasked question is: when did she leave the sitting room? She says Catherine was "sitting at the writing desk, looking at some papers" when she said goodnight — what papers? And she says she "didn't hear anything" — but the fog horn had just been shut down after thirty hours. The silence itself was notable. A more careful investigator would have pressed on the timeline between 8:00 and 9:00 PM.]

## Evidence Piece 6: Interview — William Marsh

[PRINTED ON: Police report paper, Q&A format.]

**CRESTFALL POLICE DEPARTMENT**
**WITNESS STATEMENT — CASE 62-0041**
**Subject: William R. Marsh (brother-in-law of deceased)**
**Interviewed by: Constable R. Hale**
**Date: March 9, 1962, 1530 hrs**
**Location: Keeper's Cottage, Dunmore Point**

---

**Q:** Mr. Marsh, I'm sorry to trouble you. Can you tell me about last evening?

**A:** God, yes. I'll tell you whatever you need. [Witness appears shaken, has been crying.] We had dinner — Catherine's chowder, best in the county. After that, Edward went up to the tower like he does, and I — well, I'll be honest with you, Bob, I took the bottle and went to bed. I'd been driving most of the day and I was tired, and I'm not much for sitting in silence while Edward watches the sea and Catherine reads her books. So I had a few drinks and I lay down. I was asleep by — I don't know, eight-thirty? Nine? I don't remember clearly.

**Q:** When did Helen come to bed?

**A:** I don't know exactly. I was already asleep. Or mostly asleep. I think she came in around nine — I half-remember her getting into bed, but I couldn't tell you the time precisely. I was fairly deep in the bourbon by then.

**Q:** Did you hear anything during the night?

**A:** Not a thing. I slept like the dead. [Stops. Winces at his own words.] I'm sorry. I didn't mean — Christ. I slept through the whole night. I didn't hear a sound until this morning when Edward was outside the cottage, shouting. He was shouting Catherine's name. I got up and went outside and he was standing on the cliff path with his face white as the lighthouse, and he said, "She's at the bottom, Will. She's at the bottom." And I — I didn't believe him at first. I thought he must be confused or still asleep. But he wasn't. She was there.

**Q:** How was your relationship with Catherine?

**A:** I loved Catherine. She was a wonderful woman — too good for my brother, I always said, though Edward is a good man in his way. She was warm and clever and she made that lighthouse feel like a home instead of a prison. I'm — I can't believe she's gone. I keep thinking she's going to walk through that door with a tray of coffee and tell us all to stop being mournful.

**Q:** Did Catherine seem troubled or unhappy recently?

**A:** Well, she had that rough patch last year — the miscarriage, and then the dark spell afterward. I was worried about her. Edward was worried too, though he'd never say so — he'd just drink more, which is his way of worrying, God love him. But when Helen and I visited in January, Catherine seemed better. Brighter. She showed me a watercolor she was working on and she was excited about it in a way I hadn't seen in months. So I thought she was coming back to herself. I really did.

**Q:** One more question. Is there anyone who might have wanted to harm Catherine?

**A:** [Emphatic] No. Absolutely not. Everyone loved her. You'd have to be a monster to hurt Catherine.

---

*Statement concluded at 1550 hrs.*
*Witness emotional but cooperative. Appeared genuinely distraught. Odor of alcohol noted (likely from last night's consumption). No inconsistencies noted.*

*[Signed] Constable R. Hale*

[PRODUCTION NOTE: William's statement is genuine emotion from a man who is not very perceptive. He loved Catherine but he didn't notice anything because William doesn't notice things — it's his defining flaw. His statement about Helen coming to bed "around nine" is the only indication of Helen's movements that evening, and it contradicts Helen's claim to have been reading in the sitting room until she went upstairs. If Helen arrived in the bedroom at 9:00, and the last account of Catherine alive is Helen saying goodnight to her in the sitting room — the question is: did Helen go directly from the sitting room to the bedroom, or did she go somewhere else first?]

## Evidence Piece 7: Dr. Greer's Journal Entry

[PRINTED ON: Personal diary paper, handwritten in a physician's careful hand.]

**Personal Journal — Thomas A. Greer**
**March 10, 1962**

I cannot sleep. It is past midnight and I am sitting at my desk with a glass of brandy and the image of Catherine Marsh's arms in my mind. I examined those arms today. I held them in my hands and looked at the bruises and I knew — I KNEW — that someone had seized her. The marks are as clear as if someone had dipped their hands in ink and pressed them to her skin. Four fingers on the outside. Thumb on the inside. Both arms. Symmetrical. Fresh.

Hale says it's the fall. Hale says a lot of things. Hale is a decent man who wants this to be over, and I understand that impulse because I feel it too. We are a small town. We know these people. The thought that one of the people sitting in that cottage — Edward, William, Helen — might have seized Catherine by the arms and thrown her off a cliff is so monstrous that the mind recoils from it. It is easier to believe in fog and a misstep and a terrible accident. It is easier to believe that the world is clumsy rather than cruel.

But the bruises are there. And I am a doctor, and my training tells me what they mean, and I cannot unknow it no matter how much I might wish to.

I have written a letter to the State Medical Examiner. I have not sent it yet. I do not know if I will. Ruth says I should, and Ruth is usually right, but Ruth does not have to live in this town afterward and face the people I will have accused, indirectly, of violence. Ruth says: "Tom, if you don't speak, who will?" And I say: "Agnes will. Agnes is already speaking." And Ruth says: "Agnes is a mother. You are a doctor. It is different."

She is right. It is different. I have the professional authority that Agnes lacks, and my silence lends credibility to the accident theory that my report contradicts. I am, in effect, allowing Hale to use my name to close a case that my own findings should have kept open.

I will decide tomorrow. Tonight I will finish my brandy and try to stop seeing the bruises.

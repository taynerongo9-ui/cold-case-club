**DIRECTOR'S NOTE**

Ibsen wrote *A Doll's House* in 1879, but its questions are as urgent today as they were a century ago. What happens when a woman discovers that the life she's been living is built on deception? What does she owe to a marriage that has kept her comfortable but never free?

Nora's final act — walking out the door, closing it behind her — remains one of the most revolutionary moments in theatre. She chooses truth over comfort. She chooses herself.

I dedicate this production to everyone who has ever stayed silent when they should have spoken. The door is always there. You just have to be brave enough to open it.

— Daniel Cross, Artistic Director

## Evidence Item 2: Witness Statement — Margaret Ellis, Bartender

[PRODUCTION NOTES: Official Ashford PD witness statement form. Typewritten with handwritten signature. Aged cream paper.]

---

**ASHFORD POLICE DEPARTMENT**
Witness Statement

**Case Number:** 87-0114
**Date:** January 16, 1987
**Time:** 2:15 PM
**Interviewing Officer:** Det. Frank Callahan
**Witness:** Margaret Ellis
**DOB:** 06/14/1957
**Address:** 18 Elm Street, Apt. 3B, Ashford, OR
**Occupation:** Bartender, The Stagecoach Tavern, 55 Main Street

---

**STATEMENT:**

I've been bartending at The Stagecoach for six years. I know most of the regulars and I know most of the faces in this town, even the ones who don't drink. Eleanor Hargrove was not a regular. She came in maybe once a month, usually with her friend Lillian or sometimes with that crowd from the gallery. She was always polite. Good tipper. Quiet drinker.

On the night of January 14, she came in alone around quarter past ten. The theatre crowd had been filtering in since about ten — opening night always sends people our way after. But Eleanor wasn't with the theatre group. She came in by herself, sat at the far end of the bar near the window, and ordered a gin. No ice. I remember because most people take their gin with tonic or at least ice, and she specifically said "no ice." Like she'd thought about it.

She was wearing a nice coat — tan or camel colored, long, looked expensive. She kept it on for a while, like she wasn't planning to stay. Eventually she took it off and draped it over the stool next to her.

She was distracted. Kept looking at the door. Every time it opened, her head snapped up. She was waiting for someone. I've been doing this long enough to know what that looks like. Whoever it was never showed.

She had two drinks. The first one she nursed. The second one she ordered at about 11 o'clock and finished it faster. She didn't talk to anyone. A couple of people said hello to her — the Prescotts, I think, and maybe Tom Wheeler — but she just smiled and nodded. Didn't invite conversation.

I want to add something that I think is relevant. Four days earlier, on January 10, there was an incident at the bar. Richard Hargrove came in around 9 PM, clearly looking for someone. He found Daniel Cross — the new theatre director — sitting at a table near the back. Richard walked straight up to him and started talking in a low voice. I couldn't hear all of it, but it got louder. Richard said something like "stay away from my wife" and "I know what you are." Cross tried to keep it calm, said something like "this isn't the place." Richard grabbed his collar. I had to come out from behind the bar. I told Richard to leave or I was calling the police. He left. Cross looked shaken. He finished his drink and left about ten minutes later.

I'm telling you this because you asked if anything unusual had happened recently involving Eleanor Hargrove or her husband. That was unusual. Richard Hargrove is usually Mr. Charm. I'd never seen him like that.

Back to the 14th — Eleanor left around 11:30. She put her coat back on, left a ten on the bar for two seven-dollar drinks, and walked out toward Main Street. She turned left, which would take her back toward the theatre, not toward the bridge. That's the last I saw of her.

She wasn't crying. She wasn't upset, exactly. She was... tense. Alert. Like she was bracing for something.

[Signed] Margaret Ellis
[Date] 01/16/87

## Evidence Item 3: Witness Statement — Robert Kline, Morning Jogger

[PRODUCTION NOTES: Same official statement form as above.]

---

**ASHFORD POLICE DEPARTMENT**
Witness Statement

**Case Number:** 87-0114
**Date:** January 15, 1987
**Time:** 8:30 AM
**Interviewing Officer:** Officer James Whitfield
**Witness:** Robert Kline
**DOB:** 11/02/1942
**Address:** 220 Birch Lane, Ashford, OR
**Occupation:** Insurance adjuster, Pacific Northwest Mutual

---

**STATEMENT:**

I jog the Morrison Creek Trail every morning, rain or shine. Have for the past eight years. My route takes me from Birch Lane down to Creek Road, across the bridge, and back — about three miles round trip. I usually start between 6:30 and 6:45 AM.

This morning, January 15, I left the house at 6:40 AM. It was still mostly dark — first light was just coming in. Temperature was brutal, probably high twenties. No wind to speak of, which is unusual for January.

I reached Morrison Creek Bridge at approximately 6:47 AM. I noticed something on the east railing of the bridge from maybe thirty yards out. As I got closer, I could see it was a coat. A dark-colored woman's coat, folded over the top rail.

I stopped. I'll be honest — my first thought was that someone had jumped. That bridge has a history. I looked over the railing. The water was dark, moving at its usual winter pace. I didn't see anything in the water — no person, no clothing, nothing floating.

The coat was folded once, lengthwise, and laid across the top of the railing. It wasn't bundled up or crumpled. It was placed there. It struck me as deliberate, almost careful. The kind of thing a person does when they're putting a coat down and planning to pick it up again — except no one was around.

I didn't touch it. I jogged to the payphone at the Creek Road gas station — about a quarter mile east — and called the police at 6:52 AM. Officer Whitfield arrived at 7:30. I waited at the bridge until he got there. Nobody else came by in that time.

I want to note: I jog this route every morning. I crossed this bridge yesterday morning, January 14, at approximately the same time. There was no coat on the bridge yesterday. Whatever happened, it happened between 6:45 AM on January 14 and 6:47 AM on January 15.

I did not know Eleanor Hargrove personally. I've seen her around town — at the gallery, at community events. I recognized her photograph when it was shown on the news.

[Signed] Robert Kline
[Date] 01/15/87

## Evidence Item 4: Witness Statement — Patricia Yoon, Coat Check Attendant

[PRODUCTION NOTES: Same official statement form. Patricia's statement is briefer and more casual in tone — she's a 20-year-old college student.]

---

**ASHFORD POLICE DEPARTMENT**
Witness Statement

**Case Number:** 87-0114
**Date:** January 17, 1987
**Time:** 10:00 AM
**Interviewing Officer:** Det. Frank Callahan
**Witness:** Patricia Yoon
**DOB:** 09/18/1966
**Address:** 304 University Ave, Apt. 12, Ashford, OR
**Occupation:** Student (Southern Oregon University) / Part-time coat check & ensemble, Monarch Theatre

---

**STATEMENT:**

I was working the coat check at the Monarch Theatre on January 14 for the opening night of *A Doll's House*. I also had a small part in the play — I played Anne-Marie, the nursemaid — so I was doing double duty. I'd take coats before the show, do my scenes, and then hand coats back after.

It was a busy night. Opening nights always are. I probably checked about sixty or seventy coats. I had the system down — you hand me your coat, I give you a numbered stub, I hang the coat on the corresponding hook in the back room.

I remember Mrs. Hargrove checking in her coat. She came in early, maybe ten to eight. She was wearing a really beautiful camel-colored coat — it looked like expensive wool, very classic. I complimented her on it and she smiled and said thank you. I gave her stub number 47. I remember because I'd been keeping a little notebook that night — it was only my third shift working coat check and I wanted to make sure I didn't mess up any of the big-ticket items. Nice coats, fur stoles, that kind of thing. So I wrote down "47 — camel wool coat, Mrs. H" in my notebook.

She never came back to pick it up. After the show, I was swamped with people collecting their coats, and then I had to help strike the set. By the time I locked up the coat check room around midnight, there were about a dozen unclaimed items. The camel coat was one of them. Per theatre policy, unclaimed items go in the lost and found closet. If no one claims them in ninety days, they get donated.

I didn't think anything of it at the time. People forget their coats all the time, especially after opening nights when everyone's excited and there's wine flowing at the reception. It wasn't until I heard the news about Mrs. Hargrove that I thought about it again.

I should mention — I did see Mrs. Hargrove during the show. She was sitting in the fourth or fifth row, center section. I could see her from the wings during my scenes. She seemed engaged with the play. I didn't notice anything unusual about her behavior.

After the show, I saw her briefly in the lobby. She declined the invitation to the after-party — Mr. Cross had invited everyone — and headed for the door. That would have been around 10:05 or 10:10 PM. She did not stop at the coat check.

The next time she would have come back for her coat would have been later that night — possibly the 11:42 PM appearance on the security camera, which I understand shows someone at the side entrance. The coat check room is accessible from the side hallway. But I had already locked up by then, so she would have needed a key or someone to let her in.

That's all I can remember. I still have my notebook if you need it.

[Signed] Patricia Yoon
[Date] 01/17/87

## Evidence Item 5: Detective Callahan's Initial Case Notes

[PRODUCTION NOTES: Yellow legal pad, handwritten in blue ballpoint. More extensive than Packet 1 notes. Numbered observations. Some words underlined for emphasis.]

---

**Personal Case Notes — Det. F. Callahan**
**Case #87-0114 — Hargrove**
**January 16-17, 1987**

Day two. Interviews started. Here's where I am.

**THE TIMELINE (as best I can reconstruct):**

6:30 PM — Eleanor leaves home (per Richard's statement)
7:48 PM — Coat checked at Monarch Theatre (per Yoon)
8:00 PM — Play begins. Eleanor in attendance, 4th-5th row center
10:05 PM — Play ends. Eleanor declines after-party, leaves theatre
10:15 PM — Eleanor arrives at The Stagecoach (per Margaret Ellis)
11:30 PM — Eleanor leaves The Stagecoach, turns left toward theatre
11:42 PM — Security camera: woman matching Eleanor's description at theatre side door

THEN WHAT?

Gap: 11:42 PM to 6:47 AM (coat discovered). That's seven hours unaccounted for.

**OBSERVATIONS:**

1. Three witnesses, three different impressions. Margaret Ellis says Eleanor was "tense, alert." Patricia Yoon says she seemed "engaged" and normal during the show. Robert Kline just found the coat. Nobody saw her between 11:42 PM and the discovery of the coat.

2. The coat. I keep coming back to the coat. Whitfield's report says "neatly placed, not thrown." That matches Kline's description — "folded once, lengthwise." Suicides don't fold their coats. I've seen four jumpers off that bridge and not one of them took time to neatly fold anything.

3. Richard Hargrove's alibi. He says he was at the office until midnight. His office is at 12 Commerce Street — three blocks from the Monarch Theatre, four blocks from the bridge. Nobody can confirm he was at the office. His secretary, Donna Walsh, left at 5:30 PM. The building has no security system and no cameras. He says he went straight home after midnight and went to bed without checking on Eleanor. Possible. But convenient.

4. The Stagecoach incident on Jan 10. Richard confronting Daniel Cross. "Stay away from my wife." So there's an affair, or at least Richard believes there is. This gives Richard a motive — but a motive for what? If Eleanor committed suicide, the affair is just context. If she didn't commit suicide, the affair plus the confrontation plus no alibi equals a very different picture.

5. The car keys. I can't let go of this. If you're walking to a bridge to end your life, why do you leave your keys in the ignition? You don't care about your car anymore. But you also don't deliberately place the keys like you're coming back. This detail feels staged — like someone wanted the car to be easily found and identified.

6. Weather. January 14 was cold. High of 34, low of 26. At 11:42 PM it would have been around 28 degrees. Eleanor supposedly walked 0.6 miles from the theatre to the bridge in 28-degree weather. If she was wearing her coat to the bridge, how did she walk from the theatre? The Stagecoach to the theatre is only two blocks — manageable. But the theatre to the bridge in that cold, in dress shoes? After a play and two gins?

**WHAT I NEED:**
- Richard's phone records for Jan 14
- Security camera tape from Monarch Theatre (VHS — quality is bad)
- Canvas of downtown — anyone awake after midnight?
- Background on the marriage. What were they really like behind closed doors?
- Follow up on Daniel Cross — who is this guy?

**GUT FEELING:**
Something is wrong here. I don't know what yet, but this doesn't read like a suicide. It reads like someone wanted it to look like one.

— FC

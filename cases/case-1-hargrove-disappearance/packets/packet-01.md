## Evidence Item 1: Welcome Letter from Detective Sarah Chen

[PRODUCTION NOTES: Modern letterhead — Ashford Police Department, printed on white bond paper. Professional, clean design. Badge/department seal in header.]

---

**ASHFORD POLICE DEPARTMENT**
Criminal Investigations Division — Cold Case Unit
47 Municipal Drive, Ashford, Oregon 97201

March 18, 2024

RE: Case File #87-0114 — The Disappearance of Eleanor Hargrove

To the Investigative Consultant:

My name is Detective Sarah Chen, and I've been assigned to reopen a case that has haunted this town for thirty-seven years.

On January 14, 1987, Eleanor Hargrove — age 34, wife, art gallery owner, daughter — attended the opening night of a play at the Monarch Theatre in downtown Ashford, Oregon. She was last seen on a security camera at 11:42 PM. The next morning, her navy coat was found on Morrison Creek Bridge. Her car sat in the theatre parking lot, keys still in the ignition.

No body was ever recovered.

The original investigation lasted nineteen days. It was led by Detective Frank Callahan, a good cop who I believe was under enormous pressure to close this case quietly. The official ruling was presumed suicide. Eleanor's family never accepted that conclusion. Neither did Callahan, though he never said so publicly — until now.

Three weeks ago, we received an anonymous tip that has given us reason to believe Eleanor Hargrove did not take her own life. I can't share the details of that tip yet — you'll receive it when the time is right — but I can tell you this: we found her.

After thirty-seven years, we found her. And she wasn't in that creek.

Detective Callahan, now 82 years old, has turned over his personal case notes — observations he kept separate from the official file. Notes that document things that didn't add up. Things that were ignored or explained away under pressure from people with influence in this town.

I'm sending you everything. The original case file. The witness statements. The evidence logs. Callahan's private notes. And the new material that has emerged in 2024. I need fresh eyes on this. The people of Ashford deserve the truth, and Eleanor Hargrove deserves justice.

Over the coming months, you'll receive evidence packets as I process and release materials from both the 1987 investigation and our current one. Some of what you'll see is straightforward. Some of it isn't. There are contradictions. There are lies. And there is at least one critical clue that was missed the first time around.

Three witnesses gave three different stories about that night. Someone in this case is not telling the truth.

Your job is to figure out who — and why.

Start with the original missing persons report and the map of Ashford. Familiarize yourself with the geography. In a town this small, distance matters. Who could have been where, and when — that's going to be important.

Welcome to the case. I hope you're better at this than we were.

Detective Sarah Chen
Badge #4471
Ashford Police Department — Cold Case Unit

P.S. — Detective Callahan asked me to include a personal note. He said, and I quote: "Tell them to pay attention to the small things. The coat. The timing. The money. I missed it. Don't let them miss it too."

**Spouse:** Richard Hargrove (notified 01/15/87 at 9:00 AM)

---

**CIRCUMSTANCES OF DISAPPEARANCE**

On January 15, 1987, at approximately 6:47 AM, civilian Robert Kline (M, 44, 220 Birch Lane) was jogging along the Morrison Creek Trail and observed a woman's navy blue wool coat draped over the east railing of Morrison Creek Bridge. Kline reported the coat appeared deliberately placed, not dropped or blown by wind. He contacted APD at 6:52 AM.

Responding officer (Whitfield) arrived at scene at 7:30 AM. Coat was recovered and tagged as evidence (Item #87-0114-001). No identification found in coat pockets. One tissue, unused. One theater ticket stub, torn. No wallet, no keys.

Vehicle check of surrounding area revealed a 1984 Volvo 240, color silver, Oregon plate KFJ-447, parked in the Monarch Theatre lot approximately 0.6 miles from the bridge. Vehicle registered to Eleanor R. Hargrove. Keys found in ignition. Doors unlocked. Purse on passenger seat containing wallet ($43 cash, driver's license, two credit cards), lipstick, compact mirror, and a folded theater program for *A Doll's House* (Monarch Theatre, Opening Night, January 14, 1987).

**Last confirmed sighting:** Security camera footage from Monarch Theatre shows a woman matching Eleanor Hargrove's description exiting the east side door at 11:42 PM on January 14, 1987. Image quality is poor (VHS recording, low light) but clothing and build are consistent.

**Husband's statement (summary):** Richard Hargrove states he last saw his wife at approximately 6:30 PM on January 14 when she left their home to attend the theatre. He states he worked at his office (Hargrove Development Corp., 12 Commerce Street) until approximately midnight, then returned home. He states he assumed Eleanor was sleeping in the guest bedroom, which she "sometimes did after late evenings." He was unaware she had not returned until contacted by police.

---

**INITIAL SEARCH EFFORTS**

- Morrison Creek searched by dive team on 01/15/87 and 01/16/87. Creek depth at bridge: 14 feet. Current: moderate. Water temperature: 38°F. No remains or additional personal items recovered.
- Shoreline search conducted 01/15 through 01/20 along 4-mile stretch downstream. Nothing recovered.
- K-9 unit deployed 01/16. Dogs tracked scent from theatre parking lot to bridge. No scent trail beyond bridge.
- Interviews conducted with theatre staff, Stagecoach tavern employees, neighboring residents.

---

**REPORTING OFFICER NOTES (Whitfield):**

The coat on the bridge was neatly placed — folded once over the top rail, as if someone had taken it off and laid it there with care. In my experience, personal items left by jumpers are usually dropped, not arranged. The scene felt staged to me, but I am not an investigator and defer to the detective assigned.

The theatre parking lot is a 12-minute walk from the bridge. The route passes through downtown Ashford, which would have been deserted after midnight. No witnesses have come forward to report seeing anyone walking that route.

## Evidence Item 4: Detective Callahan's Introductory Notes

[PRODUCTION NOTES: Yellow legal pad paper, handwritten in blue ballpoint pen. Slightly messy but legible. These are personal notes, not official documents — they should feel informal and unguarded.]

---

**Personal Case Notes — Det. F. Callahan**
**Case #87-0114 — Hargrove**
**January 15, 1987**

Started this one at 7:45 AM. Whitfield called me at home. Woman's coat on Morrison Creek Bridge, car in the theatre lot. I've worked four suicides off that bridge in eleven years. This one doesn't feel right.

Got to the bridge at 8:10. Coat is a navy blue peacoat, women's size 8. Good quality — not cheap. Folded neatly over the rail. That's the first thing. People who jump don't fold their coats. They drop them, shrug them off, sometimes they don't even take them off. This was placed. Deliberately.

Car is a silver Volvo in the Monarch lot. Keys in the ignition, like she planned to come right back. Purse on the passenger seat. $43 in her wallet. Whoever heard of a suicide leaving $43 in their car?

Went to notify the husband at 9 AM. Richard Hargrove. I know him — everybody in Ashford knows him. Builds half the houses in this county. Rotary Club, youth baseball, church every Sunday. Real pillar-of-the-community type.

He answered the door in a bathrobe. Looked surprised. Said he assumed Eleanor was sleeping in the guest room because she "sometimes does that after late nights." Didn't check on his wife all morning. Didn't notice her car wasn't in the garage. I found that odd, but people grieve differently, and shock does strange things.

His reaction when I told him: controlled. Very controlled. Some people scream. Some people go quiet. Hargrove went still for about three seconds and then asked very calmly, "What do you need from me?" Textbook composure. Maybe that's just who he is. Maybe.

Three things bothering me already:

1. The coat. Too neat. Too deliberate.
2. The keys. Why leave keys in the ignition if you're walking to a bridge?
3. The husband didn't notice his wife never came home.

I've got interviews starting tomorrow. Theatre staff, the tavern, neighbors. Somebody saw something. In a town this size, somebody always sees something.

— FC

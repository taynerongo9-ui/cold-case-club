**ORIGINAL STATEMENT (January 21, 1987):**

Eleanor was my best friend. We met at Reed College in 1971. She was the smartest person I'd ever met, and the most stubborn, and the kindest. When she told me she was marrying Richard Hargrove and moving to Ashford, I tried to talk her out of it. Not because of Richard specifically — I didn't know him well enough then. Because Eleanor was a Portland person, a city person, and Ashford is a small town that runs on gossip and old money.

She talked me into moving here too. We opened the gallery in 1982. It was her dream.

I need to tell you about the money. On January 2nd, Eleanor came to the gallery early and told me she'd found something while reviewing our insurance documents. Over $200,000 in wire transfers to Cascade Holdings LLC over six months. She didn't know what Cascade Holdings was. The transfers were all authorized by Richard.

Eleanor was shaken. She said she felt like she was "living inside a lie." I told her to talk to a lawyer before she talked to Richard. I gave her the name of David Wexler in Portland.

She opened a safe deposit box at First Pacific Bank — I went with her on January 12th. She also told me she was keeping notes in her diary — "in code, just in case." Eleanor had studied cryptography at Reed. She said if anything happened to her, the diary would explain everything.

I told Detective Callahan all of this on January 21st. He listened. He took notes. Two weeks later, the case was closed.

Nobody ever looked into the money.

[Signed] Lillian Chen, 01/21/87

---

**SUPPLEMENTAL STATEMENT (March 8, 2024):**

I've waited thirty-seven years to give this statement.

When I told Detective Callahan about the embezzlement in 1987, he believed me. But the case was closed before he could act on it. I was told my statement was "noted but not determinative" and that the financial matters were "a civil issue."

A civil issue. Eleanor found $340,000 stolen from her accounts, and they called it a civil issue.

I kept the gallery open. I renamed it Chen Gallery in 1990. I kept Eleanor's records — the ledgers, the insurance documents, the margin notes — in a fireproof safe. I knew someday someone would ask.

Detective Chen asked.

I want to add something I didn't say in 1987 because I was afraid. On January 13th — the day before Eleanor disappeared — she called me at home around 9 PM. She was whispering. She said, "He knows. Richard knows I found the accounts." I asked how. She said she didn't know. She said he'd been acting differently all day — cold, watchful, controlled.

I told her to leave the house. She said she had opening night the next evening and she didn't want to cause a scene.

That was the last time I spoke to Eleanor.

I should have driven to her house that night and dragged her out. I should have called the police. I have carried the weight of that conversation for thirty-seven years.

Please find out what happened to her. For Eleanor. For Ruth, who died in 2019 without knowing the truth. And for me, because I am 74 years old and I would like to stop carrying this before I die.

[Signed] Lillian Chen, 03/08/24

## Evidence Item 7: Pigpen Cipher Reference Card

[PRODUCTION NOTES: Print as a small card (3x5) on card stock. Styled as a page from a cryptography textbook or hobbyist reference.]

---

**THE PIGPEN CIPHER**
*A Substitution Cipher Guide*

The pigpen cipher (also called the Freemason's cipher or Tic-Tac-Toe cipher) replaces each letter with a symbol based on its position in a grid.

**The Grid:**

```
 A | B | C        J . K . L
---+---+---       .........
 D | E | F        M . N . O
---+---+---       .........
 G | H | I        P . Q . R
```

Second grid (with dots):
```
 S . T . U
 .........
 V . W . X
 .........
 Y . Z
```

Each letter is represented by the portion of the grid that surrounds it. Letters in dotted grids include a dot.

**Example:** "HELLO" would be encoded as the grid segments for H, E, L, L, O.

**To decode:** Match each symbol to its position in the grid above.

*"The pigpen cipher has been used by secret societies, prisoners of war, and schoolchildren. Its beauty is in its simplicity." — David Kahn, The Codebreakers*

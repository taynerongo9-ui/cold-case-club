**DET. SARAH CHEN — ANNOTATION (2024):**

*Cascade Holdings LLC was registered on July 15, 1986 — the same month the first wire transfers from the Hargrove joint account began. The P.O. Box in Portland was rented under Victor Hargrove's name. The LLC has no employees, no physical office, no publicly listed phone number, and — critically — no real estate holdings. Despite its stated purpose of "real estate acquisition, development, and management," Cascade Holdings never purchased, developed, or managed a single property.*

*The $340,000 wired to Cascade Holdings was deposited into a Bank of Portland account (#XXXX-7291) and subsequently withdrawn in cash over a period of months. The cash trail ends there.*

*Richard Hargrove's name appears nowhere on the Cascade Holdings documents. His brother Victor is the sole listed manager and director. However, analysis of the filing reveals that the articles were prepared by William Crane, Esq. — the same attorney who represented Richard Hargrove during the 1987 investigation.*

*Victor Hargrove, when reached by investigators in February 2024, declined to answer questions about Cascade Holdings. He died on April 2, 2024.*

*— SC*

## Evidence Item 4: Witness Statement — Lillian Chen

**DOB:** 02/14/1950

**Address (2024):** 206 Cedar Street, Ashford, OR
**Occupation:** Co-owner, Hargrove Gallery (now Chen Gallery), 88 Main Street

---

**ORIGINAL STATEMENT (January 21, 1987):**

Eleanor was my best friend. We met at Reed College in 1971 — I was two years ahead of her, but we were in the same art history seminar and we clicked immediately. She was the smartest person I'd ever met, and the most stubborn, and the kindest. When she told me she was marrying Richard Hargrove and moving to Ashford, I tried to talk her out of it. Not because of Richard specifically — I didn't know him well enough then to have an opinion. Because Eleanor was a Portland person, a city person, and Ashford is a small town that runs on gossip and old money and people minding each other's business.

She talked me into moving here too, eventually. We opened the gallery in 1982. It was her dream. She did most of the curating, I did the business side. We made it work.

I need to tell you about the money. On January 2nd of this year, Eleanor came to the gallery early — before opening — and told me she'd found something while reviewing our insurance documents. She'd needed to verify the value of our joint assets for a policy renewal, so she'd pulled the bank statements for the household savings account. She found over $200,000 in wire transfers to a company called Cascade Holdings LLC over the past six months.

She didn't know what Cascade Holdings was. Neither did I. She said Richard had never mentioned it. The transfers were all authorized by Richard's signature alone.

Eleanor was shaken. She said she felt like she was "living inside a lie." She asked me what she should do. I told her to talk to a lawyer before she talked to Richard. I gave her the name of an attorney in Portland — David Wexler, a college friend of mine who does financial law.

I don't know if she contacted Wexler. She said she wanted to "gather more evidence first." She opened a safe deposit box at First Pacific Bank — I went with her on January 12th. She put documents inside but didn't show me what they were.

She also told me she was keeping notes in her diary — but "in code, just in case." Eleanor had studied cryptography as an elective at Reed. She was always fascinated by codes and ciphers. She said if anything happened to her, the diary would explain everything.

I told Detective Callahan all of this on January 21st. He listened. He took notes. He said he would look into the financial records. Two weeks later, the case was closed.

Nobody ever looked into the money.

[Signed] Lillian Chen
[Date] 01/21/87

---

**SUPPLEMENTAL STATEMENT (March 8, 2024):**

I've waited thirty-seven years to give this statement. I want to say that clearly: thirty-seven years.

When I told Detective Callahan about the embezzlement in 1987, he believed me. I could see it in his eyes. He understood what I was telling him — that Richard had a $340,000 reason to keep Eleanor quiet. But the case was closed before he could act on it. I was told, by a department liaison, that my statement was "noted but not determinative" and that the financial matters were "a civil issue, not a criminal one."

A civil issue. Eleanor found $340,000 stolen from her own accounts, and they called it a civil issue.

I kept the gallery open. I renamed it Chen Gallery in 1990, because the Hargrove name made me sick. I kept Eleanor's records — the gallery ledgers, the insurance documents, the notes she'd made in the margins about discrepancies. I kept them in a fireproof safe in my office because I knew, someday, someone would ask.

Detective Chen asked.

I want to add something I didn't say in 1987 because I was afraid. On January 13th — the day before Eleanor disappeared — she called me at home around 9 PM. She was whispering. She said, "He knows. Richard knows I found the accounts." I asked her how. She said she didn't know — maybe he saw her looking through the statements, maybe he checked the bank and saw she'd opened a safe deposit box. She said he'd been acting differently all day — cold, watchful, controlled.

I told her to leave the house. I told her to come stay with me. She said she had the opening night at the Monarch the next evening and she didn't want to cause a scene. She said she'd be careful.

That was the last time I spoke to Eleanor.

I have carried the weight of that conversation for thirty-seven years. I should have driven to her house that night and dragged her out. I should have called the police. I should have done something more than what I did, which was nothing.

Please find out what happened to her. For Eleanor. For Ruth, who died in 2019 without ever knowing the truth. And for me, because I am 74 years old and I would like to stop carrying this before I die.

[Signed] Lillian Chen
[Date] 03/08/24

## Evidence Item 5: Detective Callahan's Notes — The Money

[PRODUCTION NOTES: Yellow legal pad, handwritten. Short and bitter.]

---

**Personal Case Notes — Det. F. Callahan**
**Case #87-0114 — Hargrove**
**January 21, 1987**

Lillian Chen came in today and handed me a motive on a silver platter.

$340,000. Shell company. Victor Hargrove's name on the papers. Eleanor found out and was gathering evidence. She opened a safe deposit box. She was keeping a coded diary. She was planning to go to a lawyer.

And then she disappeared.

I pulled the wire transfer records from First Pacific (they cooperated — small-town bank, no subpoena needed for a missing persons case). Six transfers, all authorized by Richard. All going to Cascade Holdings LLC. The LLC is registered to Victor Hargrove at a Portland P.O. box.

The brothers are in this together. Richard steals the money, Victor launders it through the shell company. Eleanor finds out, and suddenly she ends up dead on a bridge. Except she's NOT on the bridge — only her coat is.

This is it. This is the motive. Richard wasn't afraid of the affair. He wasn't even afraid of Eleanor leaving him. He was afraid of Eleanor going to the authorities about the embezzlement. That's criminal. That's prison. That's the end of Richard Hargrove's life as he knows it.

I went to Briggs. I showed him the bank statements. I showed him the LLC filing. I told him we needed to pull Richard's phone records, search the lake house, bring Victor in for questioning.

Briggs said: "Frank, you have a missing woman and a family dispute over money. That's not evidence of murder."

I said: "Three hundred and forty thousand dollars is not a family dispute."

He said: "Without a body, you don't have a murder."

He's right about the law. He's wrong about everything else.

The case closes in eleven days. I don't have enough time, and I don't have enough authority, and the Hargrove name hangs over this town like a goddamn shadow.

— FC

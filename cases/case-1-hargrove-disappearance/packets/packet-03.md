## Evidence Item 1: Police Interview Transcript — Richard Hargrove

[PRODUCTION NOTES: Official APD interview transcript format. Typewritten on white paper with department header. Transcript uses standard Q/A format with timestamps. Some lines have Callahan's handwritten margin notes in red pen.]

---

**ASHFORD POLICE DEPARTMENT**
Interview Transcript

**Case Number:** 87-0114
**Date:** January 16, 1987
**Time:** 10:00 AM — 11:47 AM
**Location:** Ashford Police Department, Interview Room 2
**Interviewing Officer:** Detective Frank Callahan
**Subject:** Richard Arthur Hargrove
**DOB:** 05/11/1944
**Address:** 14 Lakeview Drive, Ashford, OR
**Occupation:** President, Hargrove Development Corporation
**Subject Status:** Voluntary interview. Not under arrest. Declined attorney.
**Recording:** Audio cassette, Tape #87-0114-INT-003

---

**[TRANSCRIPT BEGINS — 10:00 AM]**

**CALLAHAN:** Thank you for coming in, Mr. Hargrove. I know this is a difficult time. For the record, you've agreed to speak with me voluntarily and you've declined to have an attorney present. Is that correct?

**HARGROVE:** That's correct. I want to help in any way I can. I just want to find Eleanor.

**CALLAHAN:** When did you last see your wife?

**HARGROVE:** Tuesday evening. January 14th. She left for the theatre around 6:30. She was excited about opening night — she'd been looking forward to it. She's very involved with the arts community in Ashford. The gallery, the theatre, all of it.

**CALLAHAN:** Did she say anything about her plans for after the performance?

**HARGROVE:** She mentioned there might be an after-party at the theatre. I told her to have a good time. That was the last conversation we had.

[CALLAHAN MARGIN NOTE: *"Have a good time." Not "I love you." Not "be safe." Noted.*]

**CALLAHAN:** What did you do that evening?

**HARGROVE:** I had work to catch up on. We have a new development project launching — Ashford Commons, on the east side. Lot of paperwork, permits, contractor agreements. I went to the office around 7 and worked until about midnight.

**CALLAHAN:** Anyone else at the office?

**HARGROVE:** No. My secretary, Donna, left at 5:30. I was alone.

**CALLAHAN:** Did you make or receive any phone calls from the office?

**HARGROVE:** I may have made a few calls. Business calls. I'd have to check.

[CALLAHAN MARGIN NOTE: *Check phone records. Who did he call?*]

**CALLAHAN:** What happened when you got home?

**HARGROVE:** I came in through the garage. The house was dark. Eleanor's car wasn't in the garage, but that wasn't unusual — she sometimes parks in the driveway when she comes home late, so she doesn't have to bother with the garage door. I assumed she was home and had gone to bed.

**CALLAHAN:** You didn't check on her?

**HARGROVE:** No. She sometimes sleeps in the guest bedroom after late evenings out. She says — said — that she doesn't want to wake me when she comes in late. I didn't want to disturb her.

**CALLAHAN:** So you went to bed without confirming your wife was home.

**HARGROVE:** Yes. I was tired. I'd been working since seven in the morning. I brushed my teeth and went to bed.

**CALLAHAN:** When did you realize she wasn't home?

**HARGROVE:** When your officer knocked on my door at nine o'clock this morning.

[CALLAHAN MARGIN NOTE: *Twelve hours. His wife is missing for twelve hours and he doesn't notice.*]

**CALLAHAN:** Mr. Hargrove, I have to ask — how would you describe your marriage?

**HARGROVE:** [pause] We're a good team. We've been married nine years. Like any marriage, we've had our ups and downs. But Eleanor and I respect each other. We built a life together in this town.

**CALLAHAN:** Has Eleanor ever expressed suicidal thoughts? Any history of depression?

**HARGROVE:** She's had periods of... melancholy. She can be emotional. The gallery business has its stresses, and she takes things personally. But suicidal? No. I wouldn't say that.

[CALLAHAN MARGIN NOTE: *"Melancholy." "Emotional." "Takes things personally." Notice how he characterizes her.*]

**CALLAHAN:** I need to ask you about Daniel Cross.

**HARGROVE:** [long pause] What about him?

**CALLAHAN:** Witnesses say you confronted him at The Stagecoach tavern on January 10th. Four days before Eleanor disappeared.

**HARGROVE:** That was... a misunderstanding. I'd heard some talk around town. People gossip in a small town, Detective. I heard that Cross had been spending time with Eleanor — at the gallery, at events. I overreacted. I went to talk to him and I let my temper get the better of me. I shouldn't have done it publicly.

**CALLAHAN:** What exactly did you say to him?

**HARGROVE:** I told him to keep his distance from my wife. That's all.

**CALLAHAN:** A witness says you grabbed his collar.

**HARGROVE:** I may have. I was upset. But it was over in thirty seconds. The bartender asked me to leave and I left. I regret it.

**CALLAHAN:** Did you confront Eleanor about the rumors?

**HARGROVE:** Eleanor and I discussed it. She said there was nothing going on. I believed her. [pause] I believe her.

**CALLAHAN:** Did she seem different in recent weeks? Any change in behavior?

**HARGROVE:** She'd been... preoccupied. Distracted. I attributed it to the gallery — they had a new exhibition going up. And the holidays were stressful. Her mother calls constantly with opinions about everything. But Eleanor is — was — a private person. She didn't always share what was on her mind.

**CALLAHAN:** Was there anything financially unusual recently? Any large purchases, withdrawals, changes in accounts?

**HARGROVE:** Not that I'm aware of. I handle most of the business finances through my company. Eleanor manages the gallery accounts separately. Our joint personal accounts — mortgage, savings — those are routine. I can provide statements if you need them.

[CALLAHAN MARGIN NOTE: *He offered the statements before I asked. Either he has nothing to hide, or he's already prepared for the question.*]

**CALLAHAN:** One more question, Mr. Hargrove. The coat we found on the bridge — it's a navy blue peacoat. Does that belong to Eleanor?

**HARGROVE:** Yes. That's her coat. She wears it — she wore it all the time. It's her everyday coat.

**CALLAHAN:** Did she wear it to the theatre on January 14th?

**HARGROVE:** I... I think so. I assume so. I didn't pay close attention when she left. She usually wears that coat in winter.

[CALLAHAN MARGIN NOTE: *"I assume so." He doesn't actually know.*]

**CALLAHAN:** Thank you, Mr. Hargrove. We may need to speak again.

**HARGROVE:** Of course. Anything you need. I just want to find my wife, Detective.

**[TRANSCRIPT ENDS — 11:47 AM]**

## Evidence Item 3: Neighbor's Statement — Dorothy Marsh

[PRODUCTION NOTES: Official witness statement form, typewritten. Dorothy Marsh is an elderly neighbor — her statement is chatty and discursive, reflecting how she actually talks.]

---

**ASHFORD POLICE DEPARTMENT**
Witness Statement

**Case Number:** 87-0114
**Date:** January 18, 1987
**Time:** 3:00 PM
**Interviewing Officer:** Det. Frank Callahan
**Witness:** Dorothy Marsh
**DOB:** 04/22/1920
**Address:** 12 Lakeview Drive, Ashford, OR
**Occupation:** Retired (former Ashford Elementary librarian)

---

**STATEMENT:**

I've lived at 12 Lakeview Drive for thirty-one years — since my late husband Gerald built the house in 1956. The Hargroves moved in next door at number 14 in 1978, right after they married. Eleanor was a lovely young woman. Very polished, very gracious. She brought me a casserole the first week they moved in. Who does that anymore?

You asked about the marriage, so I'll tell you what I observed, but I want to say first that I'm not one to gossip, and I don't want to speak ill of anyone during what must be a terrible time.

The first few years, they seemed happy enough. They entertained often — dinner parties, garden parties in summer. Richard was always the host, very charming, very much the center of attention. Eleanor was more of the quiet organizer — she'd make everything beautiful and then stand in the background while Richard worked the room.

Things changed, I'd say, around 1983 or '84. I noticed Eleanor spending more time at the gallery. She'd leave early, come home late. Not in a sneaking-around way — more like she preferred being there to being home. The dinner parties stopped. Richard was traveling more for his developments, and when he was home, the house was very quiet. Not the good kind of quiet.

I'll tell you something I've never told anyone, because it didn't seem like my business at the time. One night — this would have been October or November of last year, 1986 — I was in my garden around ten o'clock. I couldn't sleep. My roses needed checking. And I heard shouting from the Hargrove house. Richard's voice, mostly. I couldn't make out the words, but the tone was unmistakable. He was furious about something. I heard something break — glass, I think. And then it went quiet.

The next morning, Eleanor was in her garden too, and she had her sunglasses on even though it was overcast. I asked if she was all right. She smiled and said she'd had a headache. She was wearing a long-sleeved blouse even though we'd been having an unusually warm autumn. I don't know what to make of that. Maybe nothing. Maybe something.

I also want to say this: in the weeks before Eleanor went missing — the last two or three weeks of December and into January — she seemed different. More purposeful, if that makes sense. Like she was making decisions. She was cleaning out the garage one weekend, boxing things up. She brought me a set of encyclopedias she said she didn't need anymore. It struck me as the kind of thing you do when you're planning a change.

I don't believe Eleanor would have harmed herself. I know people say you never really know what someone is going through, and that's true. But Eleanor was a fighter. She was a woman who opened an art gallery in a town that didn't think it needed one, and she made it succeed. That's not someone who gives up.

The morning after she disappeared — January 15th — I looked out my window and Richard was standing in the driveway in his bathrobe. Just standing there, looking at the empty space where Eleanor's car should have been. He stood there for maybe two minutes. Then he went inside. The police came about an hour later.

One last thing. I've noticed a silver pickup truck — not Richard's Ford Bronco, a different truck — parked at the Hargrove house several times late at night over the past few months. Always after midnight, always gone by morning. I don't know whose truck it was. I assumed it was work-related. But it seemed odd.

[Signed] Dorothy Marsh
[Date] 01/18/87

---

**DET. CALLAHAN NOTE (attached separately):**

*Dorothy Marsh is 66 years old, sharp as a tack, and not the type to make things up. Three items of interest here:*

*1. The shouting and possible glass breaking — October/November 1986. Eleanor's sunglasses and long sleeves the next day. This is concerning.*

*2. Eleanor boxing things up, cleaning out the garage, giving away possessions. This COULD suggest suicidal behavior — putting affairs in order. But it also looks like someone preparing to leave.*

*3. The silver pickup truck. Richard drives a '86 Ford Bronco, dark blue. Eleanor drives the Volvo. Who owns a silver pickup? Need to check — could be Victor Hargrove's vehicle. Or a contractor. Or someone else entirely.*

*— FC*

## Evidence Item 4: Detective Callahan's Case Notes — The Husband

[PRODUCTION NOTES: Yellow legal pad, handwritten, blue ballpoint. More frustrated tone than previous notes.]

---

**Personal Case Notes — Det. F. Callahan**
**Case #87-0114 — Hargrove**
**January 18, 1987**

Four days in. I've interviewed Richard Hargrove, and here's my problem: he's either innocent and genuinely in shock, or he's the most controlled liar I've ever sat across from.

Every answer was measured. Every pause was calculated. When I asked about the marriage, he used the word "team." Not "love." Not "partner." "Team." And when I asked about Eleanor's emotional state, he went straight to "melancholy" and "emotional" — words that plant the seed of instability without him having to say "crazy" or "depressed." He's building a narrative. Whether it's the truth or a story, I can't tell yet.

His alibi is tissue paper. Alone at the office from 7 PM to midnight. No witnesses, no cameras, no phone logs yet. His office is a five-minute drive from the theatre, the tavern, AND the bridge. He could have gone anywhere that night and nobody would know.

But here's what really bothers me: he didn't notice his wife was gone. For twelve hours. His wife doesn't come home, her car isn't in the garage, and he doesn't think to check? He says she "sometimes sleeps in the guest room" — fine, but he didn't notice in the morning? He didn't see her at breakfast? He didn't hear her moving around the house?

The Hargroves have no children. It's a two-person household. When one of the two isn't there, you notice. Unless you already know she's not coming back.

The neighbor, Dorothy Marsh, paints a picture of a marriage that was crumbling. Shouting in October, possible signs of physical altercation (sunglasses, long sleeves), Eleanor cleaning house like she was preparing for something. Add in the confrontation with Daniel Cross at The Stagecoach, and you've got a man who was losing control of his wife and his image — the two things Richard Hargrove seems to care about most.

But I've got no body. I've got no forensic evidence of foul play. I've got a coat on a bridge and a husband with a bad alibi. The chief is already talking about wrapping this up. The Hargroves are a founding family in this town. Richard's father built half of downtown. The family donates to everything — the police benevolent fund included.

Nobody is telling me to drop this. Nobody has to. The message is clear enough.

The silver pickup truck from Marsh's statement — I checked. Victor Hargrove drives a 1985 Chevy Silverado, silver. What was Victor doing at his brother's house after midnight on multiple occasions?

I'm going to pull phone records tomorrow. If Richard made calls from that office, it proves he was there. If he didn't, it proves nothing — but the silence would be interesting.

— FC

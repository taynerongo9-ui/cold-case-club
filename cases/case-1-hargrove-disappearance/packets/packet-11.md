## Evidence Item 1: The Anonymous Letter

[PRODUCTION NOTES: Handwritten in shaky elderly hand on plain white paper. Envelope postmarked Portland, Feb 10, 2024. No return address.]

---

**ASHFORD POLICE DEPARTMENT — EVIDENCE TAG**
Item #: 24-0212-001
Description: Anonymous letter, postmarked Portland OR 02/10/2024
Received: February 12, 2024

---

**[LETTER — shaky handwriting:]**

To the Chief of Police,

I don't know how to write this letter. I've been trying for thirty-seven years. I'm writing it now because I'm dying and I don't want to take this to my grave.

Eleanor Hargrove did not kill herself.

I know this because I was there. Not when it happened — I wasn't in the room. But I was part of it. I helped. God forgive me, I helped.

She's not in the creek. She was never in the creek. She's at 1987 Millbrook Road. Under the foundation. The east corner, near where the loading dock was.

I know the building is coming down. When they take up the foundation, they'll find her. I'm telling you now so you know what to look for.

I enclosed a photograph. You can see the soil — even after all these years, the ground settled differently. A foundation man would notice.

I can't give my name. Not because I'm protecting myself — it's too late for that. But there's someone else involved, still alive, with grandchildren. What I'm about to do will destroy them. I accept that. But I can't put my name on it. Maybe when I'm gone, someone will find my own letter and finish what I've started.

I'm sorry about Eleanor. She didn't deserve what happened. She was a good woman who found out something she wasn't supposed to find out. That's all it was. Money. It was always about the money.

I go to sleep every night and I see her face. I have for thirty-seven years. Maybe after this letter, I'll stop seeing it. Probably not.

Do the right thing. I couldn't. But you still can.

---

**CHIEF WHITFIELD'S NOTE:**

*I was the first officer on scene at Morrison Creek Bridge on January 15, 1987. I wrote that the coat was "neatly placed, not thrown." Nobody listened.*

*When this letter arrived, I read it three times. Then I authorized the reopening within the hour.*

*I know who wrote this. The person still alive is Richard. The person writing is Victor.*

*Victor was diagnosed with pancreatic cancer in November 2023. He died April 2, 2024 — seven weeks after we received this letter. Among his effects, his daughter found a full written confession.*

*For now: we dug.*

*— Chief James Whitfield*

## Evidence Item 8: Vigenere Cipher Reference Card

[PRODUCTION NOTES: A reference card with the Vigenere square and instructions. Styled as an educational reference.]

---

**THE VIGENERE CIPHER**
*A Polyalphabetic Substitution Cipher*

Unlike the Caesar cipher (which uses one shift), the Vigenere cipher uses a KEYWORD to determine different shifts for different letters.

**How it works:**
1. Write the plaintext message.
2. Write the keyword repeatedly below it, letter by letter.
3. Each keyword letter determines the shift for the corresponding plaintext letter: A=0, B=1, C=2... Z=25.

**Example (keyword: KEY):**
```
Plaintext:  H E L L O
Keyword:    K E Y K E
Shift:     10  4 24 10  4
Ciphertext: R I J V S
```

**To decode:** Reverse the process. Subtract each keyword letter's value from the ciphertext letter.

**The Vigenere Square:** [Include a 26x26 letter grid showing all possible shifts]

*"The Vigenere cipher was called 'le chiffre indechiffrable' — the unbreakable cipher — for three centuries. It was broken by Charles Babbage in the 1850s. No code is truly unbreakable. Only undiscovered."*

## Evidence Item 9: Dr. Susan Park — 2024 Statement

[PRODUCTION NOTES: A typed statement from the retired medical examiner, now in her late 70s. She waited 37 years to be vindicated. Brief, precise, and quietly furious.]

---

**Statement of Dr. Susan Park, M.D.**
Retired Douglas County Medical Examiner
March 20, 2024

I was asked if I would like to make a statement regarding the recovery and identification of Eleanor Hargrove's remains. I would.

On January 30, 1987, I submitted a memorandum to Chief Arnold Briggs objecting to the proposed finding of probable suicide. I cited the absence of remains, the absence of toxicology, the absence of a suicide note, the statistical impossibility of a body not being recovered from Morrison Creek within 72 hours, and the staging indicators identified by the responding officer. My memorandum was filed. It was not discussed. It was not shared with the press. Two days later, the case was closed.

I am told that my memorandum has now been entered into the reopened case file as evidence of institutional negligence. Good.

I practiced forensic pathology for thirty-five years. I examined thousands of bodies and testified in hundreds of cases. In all that time, the Hargrove case was the only one where I was asked to endorse a finding I knew to be scientifically unsupportable. I refused. It cost me nothing professionally — I answered to the county, not to the chief — but it cost me personally. I spent thirty-seven years knowing that a woman's murder had been classified as a suicide because the alternative was inconvenient for powerful people.

The remains recovered from 1987 Millbrook Road show a fractured hyoid bone — consistent with manual strangulation. This is what I would have found if I had been allowed to examine a body in 1987. But there was no body to examine, because the body was under a building built by the man who killed her.

I do not blame Detective Callahan. He fought as hard as he could. I blame the system that chose expedience over evidence, and the people who benefited from Eleanor Hargrove's silence.

I am 78 years old. I am glad I lived to see this corrected. Ruth Addams did not. That will haunt me for whatever time I have left.

[Signed] Dr. Susan Park, M.D.

## Evidence Item 11: Patricia Yoon-Nakamura — 2024 Statement

[PRODUCTION NOTES: A modern witness statement from the former coat check attendant. Patricia is now 57 and a high school drama teacher in Bend. Her voice is mature, reflective, carrying the weight of a detail she never knew mattered.]

---

**ASHFORD POLICE DEPARTMENT**
Witness Statement (Follow-up)

**Case Number:** 24-0301
**Date:** March 5, 2024
**Witness:** Patricia Yoon-Nakamura (formerly Patricia Yoon)
**Age:** 57
**Occupation:** Drama teacher, Bend High School, Bend, OR

---

**STATEMENT:**

When Detective Chen called me, I almost didn't answer. Unknown number, Ashford area code. I haven't lived in Ashford since 1988. But something made me pick up.

She asked about the Monarch Theatre. About a night in January 1987. About a coat.

I remember that night perfectly. It was my third shift working coat check and I was nervous about messing up someone's fur or cashmere, so I kept a little notebook — just in case. I wrote down the expensive coats with their stub numbers and owners. Mrs. Hargrove had a beautiful camel coat. She complimented my earrings — turquoise teardrops my mother gave me for Christmas. I wrote it all down: "47 — camel wool coat, Mrs. H (Eleanor Hargrove) — Beautiful coat! She was so nice. Complimented my earrings."

When she disappeared, I thought about the coat. I remembered it was camel — tan, elegant, the kind of coat you wear to opening night. I heard on the news that they found a navy coat on the bridge. I thought: that's not the same coat. But I was twenty years old and nobody asked me, and by the time Detective Callahan called about the notebook, I couldn't find it.

I looked. I really looked. I went to my parents' house in Portland and tore apart three closets. But I'd just moved apartments and everything was in boxes and I couldn't find the notebook in time. The case closed. I went back to school. Life went on.

I found the notebook in 2003 when my parents sold their house. It was in a box labeled "COLLEGE STUFF" in the attic. I flipped through it and there it was — January 14, 1987, stub 47, camel wool coat. I thought about calling the police. I thought about it for a long time. But the case was closed. It had been sixteen years. What would they do?

So I put the notebook in my own attic. And I waited.

Twenty-one more years. Detective Chen found me and I had that notebook in my hands within an hour. I climbed into the attic in my work clothes and came back down covered in dust and holding a spiral-bound notebook from 1987.

One line changed everything, she told me. Entry number 47.

I teach drama to high school kids now. I tell them that the details matter — in acting, in directing, in life. You don't know which detail will matter until it does. You write everything down. You pay attention.

I wrote it down. I paid attention. And thirty-seven years later, it mattered.

I wish I'd found the notebook sooner. I wish Detective Callahan had been able to see it. But it survived. And so did the truth.

[Signed] Patricia Yoon-Nakamura
[Date] 03/05/24

**THE MURDER OF ELEANOR HARGROVE**

Eleanor Ruth Hargrove was murdered on January 15, 1987, at approximately 1:00 AM, at the Hargrove Lake Property on Route 26. She was killed by her husband, Richard Arthur Hargrove, age 42, with the assistance of his brother, Victor Anthony Hargrove, age 39.

The motive was financial. Over eighteen months, Richard embezzled $340,000 from the couple's joint savings through Cascade Holdings LLC — a shell company registered in Victor's name. The money covered losses from the failed Ridgeview Project in Bend. Eleanor discovered the embezzlement in late December 1986, confided in Lillian Chen on January 2, and began gathering evidence. She opened a safe deposit box, kept a coded diary, and drafted a letter to an attorney. She never sent it.

On January 13, she wrote her final coded diary entry: "RICHARD KNOWS I FOUND THE ACCOUNTS."

**THE NIGHT OF JANUARY 14**

Eleanor checked her camel coat at the Monarch Theatre at 7:48 PM (stub #47) and attended *A Doll's House*. After the show, she went to The Stagecoach tavern (10:15-11:30 PM) to meet Daniel Cross, who never arrived. She returned to the theatre at 11:42 PM for her coat.

Richard intercepted her in the parking lot. He convinced her to come to the lake house. Victor was already there.

Between 12:45 and 1:15 AM, Eleanor was killed. Victor's confession: Eleanor refused to back down. She told them she had copies in a safe deposit box and a lawyer would have them by Monday. Richard strangled her. Victor stood in the doorway and did nothing.

**THE COVER-UP**

They buried Eleanor at the Ashford Commons construction site (1987 Millbrook Road) in a pre-dug trench. Richard retrieved Eleanor's navy peacoat from 14 Lakeview Drive and placed it on Morrison Creek Bridge. He drove her Volvo to the theatre parking lot.

**THE CRITICAL MISTAKE**

Eleanor wore her camel coat to the theatre. Richard didn't know — he grabbed the navy coat from the closet. Patricia Yoon's coat check notebook, recovered in 2024, proved the discrepancy: "#47 — camel wool coat, Mrs. H."

One line in a college student's notebook. That's what broke this case.

**THE AFTERMATH**

Richard filed construction permits six days later. The foundation was poured by mid-February. The case was closed as suicide on February 2. Eleanor's mother fought the ruling until her death in 2019.

In February 2024, Victor — dying of cancer — sent an anonymous letter identifying the burial location. Eleanor's remains were recovered on March 1. Richard was arrested on March 15. Victor died on April 2, leaving a seven-page confession.

Richard Hargrove was indicted on April 18, 2024, on charges of Murder in the First Degree, Fraud, Abuse of Corpse, Tampering with Physical Evidence, and Hindering Prosecution. Bail denied.

**THE PEOPLE**

Daniel Cross left Ashford in March 1987 and returned to San Francisco. He never married. When contacted in 2024, he wept. He had kept Eleanor's letter for thirty-seven years.

Thomas Brewer continued as groundskeeper until Richard's arrest. He was 89 years old. When told his testimony had been vindicated: "I always knew what I heard. I just couldn't get nobody to believe me."

Lillian Chen still operates Chen Gallery at 88 Main Street. She was the first person I called after the DNA confirmation. "Tell Ruth," was the first thing she said. When told Ruth had passed: "Then tell the world."

Case closed.

Detective Sarah Chen
Badge #4471

## Evidence Item 3: Victor Hargrove's Confession Letter (Excerpted)

[PRODUCTION NOTES: Handwritten on lined paper, shaky hand. Seven pages excerpted.]

---

**ASHFORD POLICE DEPARTMENT — EVIDENCE TAG**
Item #: 24-0408-001
Description: Confession letter, Victor A. Hargrove (deceased 04/02/2024)
Note: EXCERPTED — Full document is Exhibit 14, State of Oregon v. Richard Hargrove

---

**[EXCERPTS — Victor's handwriting:]**

I am writing this because I owe it to Eleanor and to the truth and to my own soul, if I still have one.

My name is Victor Anthony Hargrove. On January 15, 1987, I helped my brother conceal the murder of his wife. I was present when she died. I did not stop it. I helped him hide her body. I have lied about this for thirty-seven years.

[...]

The money was Richard's idea. He told me we needed a holding company to cover the Ridgeview losses. He said we'd pay it back. He said Eleanor would never know. It took eighteen months and $340,000 and then Eleanor found out and Richard said we had a problem.

[...]

He called me on January 14. He said Eleanor had confronted him. He said she told him she was going to the police. He said "we need to handle this." I asked what that meant. He said "come to the lake house."

I drove there that afternoon. I didn't know what I was agreeing to. Or maybe I did and I couldn't face it.

[...]

She came with Richard around 12:30 in the morning. She was wearing her nice coat — the tan one. She saw me and she knew immediately. She said, "So it's both of you."

Richard told her to sit down. She refused. She said she had copies in a safe deposit box and a lawyer would have them by Monday. She told him it was over.

Richard grabbed her arm. She pulled away. He grabbed her again. She slapped him. And then he put his hands around her throat and he didn't let go.

I stood there. I didn't move. I watched my brother kill his wife and I did nothing. That is the worst thing I have ever done.

[...]

We drove her to the Millbrook Road site. There was a trench already dug. We buried her. Richard said the foundation would cover it within two weeks and no one would ever know.

He was right. For thirty-seven years.

[...]

Richard went home and got Eleanor's blue coat — the navy one. He put it on the bridge. I drove her car to the theatre. We walked home separately. It was done by 4 AM.

I went back to Portland and checked into the Heathman. I had dinner with Peter Drummond and smiled and talked about real estate while Eleanor's blood was still under my fingernails.

[...]

I have lived with this for thirty-seven years. I see her face when I close my eyes. I hear her voice saying "So it's both of you." I hear the silence after.

Richard never showed remorse. He went on with his life. He built his buildings. He coached baseball and went to church. He married again in 1992 and divorced in 2001.

I never married. How do you build a life when you've helped destroy someone else's?

[...]

I am dying. I sent the anonymous letter. I am sending this confession. I cannot undo what I did, but I can make sure the truth survives me.

Eleanor Hargrove did not kill herself. She was murdered by her husband for the crime of discovering his lies. She was brave and she was right and she deserved to live.

I'm sorry, Eleanor. I am so sorry.

Victor Anthony Hargrove
March 28, 2024

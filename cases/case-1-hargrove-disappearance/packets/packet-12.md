---

**THE MURDER OF ELEANOR HARGROVE**

Eleanor Ruth Hargrove was murdered on January 15, 1987, at approximately 1:00 AM, at the Hargrove Lake Property on Route 26, forty minutes outside Ashford. She was killed by her husband, Richard Arthur Hargrove, age 42, with the assistance of his brother, Victor Anthony Hargrove, age 39.

The motive was financial. Over a period of approximately eighteen months (July 1986 through January 1987), Richard Hargrove embezzled $340,000 from the couple's joint savings account, funneling the money through a shell company called Cascade Holdings LLC, registered in Victor's name, to fund personal investments and cover debts from a failed development project that neither brother had disclosed to their investors or family.

Eleanor discovered the embezzlement in late December 1986 while reviewing financial documents for her art gallery's insurance renewal. She confided in her best friend and business partner, Lillian Chen, on January 2, 1987. She began gathering evidence — bank statements, the LLC registration, her own coded diary entries — and opened a safe deposit box at First Pacific Bank on January 12 to store them. She drafted a letter to an attorney but never sent it.

On January 13, Eleanor wrote her final coded diary entry: "RICHARD KNOWS I FOUND THE ACCOUNTS." She believed Richard had become aware of her discovery. She was right.

**THE NIGHT OF JANUARY 14, 1987**

At 7:48 PM, Eleanor checked her camel wool winter coat at the Monarch Theatre coat check and attended the opening night performance of *A Doll's House*. She wore coat check stub #47.

After the performance, she went to The Stagecoach tavern, where bartender Margaret Ellis served her gin, no ice, between approximately 10:15 and 11:30 PM. Eleanor was waiting for Daniel Cross, the theatre director, with whom she was having an affair. Cross never arrived — he was at the theatre's after-party.

Eleanor left the tavern at 11:30 PM and returned to the Monarch Theatre to retrieve her camel coat from coat check. She was captured on a security camera at 11:42 PM at the theatre's side entrance.

Richard Hargrove intercepted Eleanor in the theatre parking lot. Earlier that day, Richard had called Victor at 4:12 PM from his office to coordinate. Victor had driven from Portland to the Hargrove Lake Property, arriving at 3:45 PM (observed by groundskeeper Thomas Brewer), then returned to Portland by evening to establish his alibi.

Richard convinced Eleanor to accompany him to the lake house to "discuss the situation." The forty-minute drive put them at the property between 12:15 and 12:30 AM. Victor was already there, having driven back from Portland a second time that night.

Between 12:45 and 1:15 AM, a violent confrontation occurred. According to Victor's posthumous confession, Eleanor refused to back down. She told Richard she was going to the police, the bank, and a lawyer. She told him it was over.

Richard strangled Eleanor. Victor's confession states he was in the room but did not participate in the killing itself. He did, however, help with what came next.

**THE COVER-UP**

Between 1:30 AM and approximately 4:00 AM, Richard and Victor executed a plan they had discussed earlier that day:

1. They wrapped Eleanor's body and transported it in Victor's truck to the Ashford Commons construction site at 1987 Millbrook Road, where Richard had been planning a development project. A trench had been pre-dug on the site as part of preliminary site work. They buried Eleanor in the east corner of what would become the building's foundation.

2. Richard retrieved Eleanor's navy blue peacoat from their home at 14 Lakeview Drive. He drove to Morrison Creek Bridge and placed the coat on the east railing — folded neatly, deliberately, to suggest a woman had removed her coat before jumping.

3. Richard drove Eleanor's silver Volvo from the lake house to the Monarch Theatre parking lot, placed the keys in the ignition, and walked the 1.3 miles home.

**THE CRITICAL MISTAKE**

Richard did not know what coat Eleanor had worn to the theatre. She had left the house at 6:30 PM; he had left for his office shortly after. He assumed she had worn her everyday navy peacoat — the coat everyone associated with Eleanor. He took it from the front closet and staged it on the bridge.

But Eleanor had worn her camel coat — her dress coat, appropriate for opening night at the theatre. She had checked it at the coat check at 7:48 PM and retrieved it at 11:42 PM.

The coat on the bridge was not the coat Eleanor wore that night. It was planted.

This discrepancy was noted — almost — by Detective Frank Callahan, who suspected the coat was wrong based on bartender Margaret Ellis's description of Eleanor wearing a "nice tan coat." But Callahan was never able to confirm it. The theatre's coat check records were destroyed after ninety days. The case was closed before Callahan could locate Patricia Yoon's personal notebook.

That notebook survived for thirty-seven years. When we found Patricia Yoon-Nakamura in Bend, Oregon, in 2024, she still had it. Entry #47: "Camel wool coat, women's — Mrs. H (Eleanor Hargrove)."

One line in a college student's practice notebook. That's what broke this case open.

---

**THE AFTERMATH**

Richard Hargrove filed construction permits for Ashford Commons on January 20, 1987 — six days after Eleanor's death. The foundation was poured by mid-February, sealing Eleanor beneath concrete. The building stood for thirty-seven years.

The case was ruled probable suicide on February 2, 1987. Eleanor's mother, Ruth Addams, fought the ruling until her death in 2019. Detective Callahan kept his personal case notes for thirty-seven years, waiting for someone to ask for them.

In February 2024, Victor Hargrove — dying of pancreatic cancer at age 76 — sent an anonymous letter to the Ashford Police Department identifying the location of Eleanor's remains. His guilt, which he had carried since 1987, finally outweighed his fear of his brother.

The case was reopened on March 1, 2024. Eleanor's remains were positively identified via DNA on March 1. Richard Hargrove was arrested on March 15, 2024, at his home on Lakeview Drive, thirty-seven years and two months after he murdered his wife in the same house.

Victor Hargrove died on April 2, 2024. His written confession was discovered by his daughter among his personal effects.

Richard Hargrove was indicted by a Douglas County grand jury on April 18, 2024, on charges of Murder in the First Degree, Fraud, and Obstruction of Justice.

Daniel Cross left Ashford in March 1987 and returned to San Francisco. He never married. When contacted by investigators in 2024, he wept. He had kept Eleanor's letter for thirty-seven years.

Thomas Brewer continued to work as groundskeeper of the Hargrove Lake Property until Richard's arrest. He was 89 years old. When told that his testimony had been vindicated, he said: "I always knew what I heard. I just couldn't get nobody to believe me."

Lillian Chen still operates the gallery — now called Chen Gallery — at 88 Main Street in Ashford. She was the first person Detective Chen called after the DNA confirmation. "Tell Ruth," was the first thing Lillian said. When told that Ruth had passed in 2019, Lillian was silent for a long time. Then she said: "Then tell the world."

That is what this report intends to do.

Case closed.

Detective Sarah Chen
Badge #4471
Ashford Police Department

**[EXCERPTED CONFESSION — Victor Hargrove's handwriting:]**

I am writing this because I owe it to Eleanor and I owe it to the truth and I owe it to my own soul, if I still have one.

My name is Victor Anthony Hargrove. I am the brother of Richard Arthur Hargrove. On January 15, 1987, I helped my brother conceal the murder of his wife, Eleanor Ruth Hargrove. I was present when she died. I did not stop it. I helped him hide her body. I have lied about this for thirty-seven years.

[...]

The money was Richard's idea. It was always Richard's idea. He told me we needed a holding company to park cash from a development deal that was going sideways — the Ridgeview project, which had gone over budget by $400,000. He said we'd pay it back. He said Eleanor would never know. He said it would take six months.

It took eighteen months and $340,000 and then Eleanor found out and Richard said we had a problem.

[...]

He called me on January 14 at the office. He said Eleanor had confronted him that morning. He said she told him she was going to the police. He said "we need to handle this." I asked him what that meant. He said "come to the lake house."

I drove to the lake house that afternoon. I didn't know what I was agreeing to. I swear I didn't know. Or maybe I did and I couldn't face it. I don't know anymore.

[...]

She came to the lake house with Richard around 12:30 in the morning. She was still wearing her nice coat — the tan one, the one she wore to fancy events. She saw me and she knew immediately. She said, "So it's both of you."

Richard told her to sit down and be reasonable. She said she was done being reasonable. She said she was done being quiet and done being managed and done being the wife who looked the other way. She said she had copies of everything in a safe deposit box and that a lawyer would have them by Monday.

Richard grabbed her arm. She pulled away. He grabbed her again. She slapped him. And then — God forgive me — he put his hands around her throat and he didn't let go.

I stood there. I didn't move. I didn't help her and I didn't stop him. I stood in the doorway of that kitchen and I watched my brother kill his wife and I did nothing. That is the worst thing I have ever done and it is the thing I will die knowing.

[...]

We drove her body to the Millbrook Road site. Richard had been planning the Ashford Commons project for months. There was already a trench dug for a retaining wall test. We buried her there. Richard said the foundation would go over it within two weeks and no one would ever know.

He was right. For thirty-seven years, he was right.

[...]

Richard went back to the house and got Eleanor's blue coat — the navy one she wore every day. He put it on the bridge. I drove her car to the theatre parking lot. We walked home separately. It was done by 4 AM.

I went back to Portland and checked into the Heathman again. I had dinner with Peter Drummond to make sure someone could say they'd seen me. I sat across from Peter and smiled and talked about real estate while Eleanor's blood was still under my fingernails.

[...]

I have lived with this for thirty-seven years. I have thought about Eleanor every single day. I see her face when I close my eyes. I hear her voice saying "So it's both of you." I hear the sound Richard's hands made. I hear the silence after.

Richard never showed remorse. Not once. He went on with his life. He built his buildings. He joined the Rotary Club and coached baseball and went to church. He married again in 1992 — a woman named Patricia, who is kind and who has no idea what her husband is. They divorced in 2001.

I never married. I couldn't. How do you build a life with someone when you've helped destroy someone else's?

[...]

I am dying. The doctors say weeks. I sent the anonymous letter to the police. I am sending this confession to the district attorney. I cannot undo what I did, but I can make sure the truth survives me.

Eleanor Hargrove did not kill herself. She was murdered by her husband for the crime of discovering his lies. She was brave and she was right and she deserved to live.

I'm sorry, Eleanor. I am so sorry.

Victor Anthony Hargrove
March 28, 2024

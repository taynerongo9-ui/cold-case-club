# SEARCH FOR MISSING WOMAN ENTERS SECOND DAY

## Coat found on Morrison Creek Bridge; husband questioned

**By GEORGE PRESTON, Staff Writer**

ASHFORD — Police divers returned to Morrison Creek yesterday for a second day of searching after a woman's coat was found draped over the railing of Morrison Creek Bridge early Wednesday morning, raising fears for the safety of a prominent Ashford woman.

Eleanor Hargrove, 34, co-owner of the Hargrove Gallery on Main Street, has been missing since Tuesday evening, when she attended the opening night performance of *A Doll's House* at the Monarch Theatre. She was last seen on a security camera leaving the theatre at approximately 11:42 PM.

Her navy blue peacoat was discovered on the east railing of Morrison Creek Bridge at 6:47 AM Wednesday by a passing jogger, Robert Kline. Her 1984 Volvo was found in the Monarch Theatre parking lot with the keys in the ignition.

Detective Frank Callahan of the Ashford Police Department, who is leading the investigation, confirmed that divers have searched a two-mile stretch of Morrison Creek downstream from the bridge. Water temperature is approximately 38 degrees, with a moderate current flowing south.

"We are conducting a thorough search of the creek and the surrounding area," Callahan said at a brief press conference Wednesday afternoon. "At this point, we are treating this as a missing persons case. We have not reached any conclusions about what happened to Mrs. Hargrove."

Richard Hargrove, Eleanor's husband and president of Hargrove Development Corporation, was interviewed by police Wednesday morning. He is not considered a suspect at this time.

"I'm asking anyone with information to please come forward," Richard Hargrove said in a statement released through his attorney, William Crane. "Eleanor is the light of my life. I just want her to come home."

Friends and family have expressed shock at the disappearance. Lillian Chen, Eleanor's business partner at the Hargrove Gallery, said she spoke with Eleanor on Monday and nothing seemed amiss.

"Eleanor is strong, intelligent, and full of life," Chen said, her voice breaking. "I refuse to believe the worst. Something happened to her, and I want answers."

Ruth Addams, Eleanor's mother, has traveled from Portland to Ashford to assist in the search. "My daughter would not do this," Addams told the Herald outside the police station. "I know my child. Something is very wrong."

Morrison Creek Bridge, a wooden pedestrian and vehicle bridge on Creek Road, has been the site of at least four suicides in the past decade, according to county records. However, police emphasized that no determination has been made regarding the circumstances of Hargrove's disappearance.

Anyone with information is asked to contact the Ashford Police Department at (503) 555-0147.

# HUSBAND QUESTIONED IN HARGROVE CASE

## Police call Richard Hargrove "person of interest" as search enters eighth day

**By GEORGE PRESTON, Staff Writer**

ASHFORD — The investigation into the disappearance of Eleanor Hargrove took a dramatic turn this week as police confirmed that her husband, real estate developer Richard Hargrove, has been designated a "person of interest" in the case.

Detective Frank Callahan said the designation does not mean Richard Hargrove is a suspect or has been accused of any crime. "A person of interest is someone we believe may have information relevant to our investigation," Callahan said. "We have questions that require answers."

The move comes one week after Eleanor Hargrove, 34, was last seen leaving the Monarch Theatre on the evening of January 14. Despite an extensive search involving police divers, K-9 units, and over forty volunteers, no trace of Eleanor Hargrove has been found beyond the navy coat recovered from Morrison Creek Bridge.

Richard Hargrove, 42, has retained prominent Portland attorney William Crane, who issued a statement calling the designation "premature and irresponsible."

"Richard Hargrove has cooperated fully with investigators from the moment this nightmare began," Crane said. "He voluntarily submitted to an interview, offered to provide financial records, and has opened his home for inspection. This designation serves only to distract from the search for Eleanor."

Sources close to the investigation, who spoke on condition of anonymity, said police are examining Richard Hargrove's whereabouts on the night of January 14 and early morning of January 15. Hargrove has told police he was working alone at his office until midnight. Police have not been able to independently verify this claim.

The same sources indicated that police are also investigating the state of the Hargroves' marriage, including an alleged public confrontation between Richard Hargrove and Monarch Theatre director Daniel Cross at The Stagecoach tavern on January 10.

Cross, reached by telephone, declined to comment.

Meanwhile, the search for Eleanor Hargrove continues. Dive operations in Morrison Creek have been suspended due to deteriorating weather conditions, but shore teams continue to canvas the area downstream.

Ruth Addams, Eleanor's mother, traveled from Portland to Ashford this week and made an emotional appeal for information.

"My daughter did not do this to herself," Addams told the Herald. "Eleanor was not depressed. She was not unstable. She was a vibrant, determined woman with everything to live for. Someone did this to her, and I will not rest until I know who."

# CASE CLOSED: HARGROVE DEATH RULED PROBABLE SUICIDE

## Police end 19-day investigation; family disputes finding

**By GEORGE PRESTON, Staff Writer**

ASHFORD — The Ashford Police Department officially closed its investigation into the disappearance of Eleanor Hargrove on Monday, ruling her death a probable suicide based on the weight of circumstantial evidence.

Detective Frank Callahan, who led the investigation, said the determination was made after "a thorough review of all available evidence, witness statements, and the circumstances surrounding Mrs. Hargrove's disappearance."

"While no body has been recovered, the physical evidence — including the coat found on Morrison Creek Bridge, the abandoned vehicle, and the absence of any indication of foul play — is consistent with a self-inflicted death," Callahan said in a prepared statement read at police headquarters. He took no questions.

The ruling comes after a 19-day search that included dive teams, K-9 units, aerial surveillance, and extensive witness interviews. Police interviewed more than thirty individuals, including Richard Hargrove, who was designated a "person of interest" last week. That designation has been lifted.

"We are satisfied that Mr. Hargrove has cooperated fully with our investigation," Police Chief Arnold Briggs said. "There is no evidence to suggest the involvement of any third party in Mrs. Hargrove's death."

Chief Briggs acknowledged that the case is being closed without a body. "This is not unprecedented," he said. "In cases where the circumstances strongly suggest suicide and no evidence of foul play exists, a probable determination can be made."

The ruling was immediately challenged by Eleanor Hargrove's family. Ruth Addams, her mother, held an impromptu press conference outside the police station, accompanied by her attorney.

"This is a travesty," Addams said. "Nineteen days. They gave my daughter nineteen days. They couldn't even find her, and they've decided she killed herself. No body, no note, no history of suicidal behavior — and they close the case because it's convenient."

Addams announced her intention to file a formal complaint with the Oregon State Police and to request an independent review of the investigation.

Dr. Susan Park, the Douglas County Medical Examiner, issued a brief statement noting that her office "was not asked to make a formal determination" and that "in the absence of remains, a definitive cause of death cannot be established." The statement appeared to distance her office from the police ruling, though Dr. Park declined to elaborate.

William Crane, attorney for Richard Hargrove, released a statement on behalf of his client: "Richard Hargrove is devastated by the loss of his wife. He respects the police department's thorough work and hopes the community will now allow the Hargrove family to grieve in peace."

Daniel Cross, the Monarch Theatre director whose name surfaced during the investigation, could not be reached for comment. Theatre staff said Cross has taken a leave of absence.

Lillian Chen, Eleanor's business partner, said she intends to keep the Hargrove Gallery open. "Eleanor built something beautiful here," Chen said. "I won't let it disappear along with her."

# LOCAL WOMAN MISSING

## Police have suspended the search for Eleanor Hargrove, 34

**By GEORGE PRESTON, Staff Writer**

ASHFORD — Nearly ten months after Eleanor Hargrove, 34, disappeared following a night at the Monarch Theatre, the Ashford Police Department confirmed that all active search efforts have been permanently suspended.

"The case remains classified as closed — probable suicide," a department spokesperson said. "There has been no new evidence to warrant reopening the investigation."

Hargrove was last seen on January 14, 1987, exiting the Monarch Theatre at 11:42 PM. Her coat was found on Morrison Creek Bridge the following morning. Her body has never been recovered.

Her husband, real estate developer Richard Hargrove, remains a "person of interest" in the case, though police have emphasized he has not been charged with any crime and has cooperated with investigators. Hargrove's attorney, William Crane, has previously called the designation "unfair and unwarranted."

Ruth Addams, Eleanor's mother, continues to advocate for the case to be reopened. In a letter to the Herald published last month, Addams wrote: "I will never accept that my daughter chose to end her life. The Ashford Police Department failed Eleanor, and they know it."

The Hargrove Gallery on Main Street continues to operate under the management of Lillian Chen. The Monarch Theatre's current season is underway under a new artistic director; Daniel Cross departed Ashford in March.

Richard Hargrove's Ashford Commons development, on the east side of town, is nearing completion and is expected to open for occupancy in early 1988.

## Evidence Item 5: Letters to the Editor — Ruth Addams

[PRODUCTION NOTES: Two letters, printed as newspaper clippings.]

---

**LETTERS TO THE EDITOR**

**The Ashford Daily Herald — February 15, 1987**

**My Daughter Deserves Better**

To the Editor:

I am Ruth Addams, mother of Eleanor Hargrove.

I am writing because I have no other recourse. The Ashford Police Department closed my daughter's case after nineteen days. Nineteen days. They found no body. They found no suicide note. They found no history of depression or mental illness. They found nothing — and they called it suicide.

I have filed a formal complaint with the Oregon State Police. I have written to the governor's office. I have spoken to every reporter who will listen. And I will continue to do so until someone — anyone — takes a proper look at what happened to my daughter.

Eleanor was not a woman who gave up. She graduated summa cum laude from Reed College. She opened an art gallery in a town that had never had one. She organized community arts programs for children. She was planning a new exhibition for February. You don't plan exhibitions when you're planning to die.

I am told that the Hargrove family is influential in this town. I am told that some questions are better left unasked. I reject that. My daughter's life mattered. Her death — if she is dead — deserves a full and honest investigation.

I am asking the people of Ashford: if you know something, say something. Eleanor cannot speak for herself. Someone must speak for her.

Ruth Addams
Portland, Oregon

---

**The Ashford Daily Herald — October 4, 1987**

**Nine Months and No Answers**

To the Editor:

Nine months ago, my daughter Eleanor vanished from the streets of your town. Nine months. I have written eleven letters to the Ashford Police Department. I have received three responses, all of them form letters. I have requested the complete case file under the Oregon Public Records Act. My request was denied on the grounds that the case is "administratively closed but technically open."

I want the people of Ashford to know that I am not going away. I know some of you wish I would. I know some of you find my persistence uncomfortable. I know that the Hargrove name carries weight in this community, and I know that asking questions about Richard Hargrove is not a popular thing to do.

But I have one question that no one has been able to answer: if my daughter walked to that bridge and jumped, where is she? Morrison Creek is fourteen feet deep at the bridge. The current is moderate. Bodies surface. Bodies are found. In every single case of a drowning in that creek — and I have researched every one — the body was recovered within seventy-two hours.

Eleanor's body has never been found. Not in one day. Not in nineteen. Not in nine months. She is not in that creek. She was never in that creek.

So where is my daughter?

I will keep asking until someone answers.

Ruth Addams
Portland, Oregon

## Evidence Item 6: Detective Callahan's Notes — The Closing

[PRODUCTION NOTES: Yellow legal pad, handwritten, blue ballpoint. This entry is emotional — Callahan is angry and conflicted.]

---

**Personal Case Notes — Det. F. Callahan**
**Case #87-0114 — Hargrove**
**February 2, 1987 — The day they closed it**

Chief Briggs called me into his office at 8 AM. Told me the case was being closed as probable suicide. Told me to read the prepared statement at the press conference and take no questions. I asked him why. He said the evidence supported it.

I said, "What evidence? We don't have a body."

He said, "That's exactly right. We don't have a body. We don't have a crime scene. We don't have a witness to anything. We have a coat on a bridge and a history of suicides at that location. What we don't have is the resources to keep this open indefinitely."

I asked about Richard Hargrove's alibi — unverified. About the phone records I was still waiting on. About the marriage problems. About the neighbor's testimony regarding shouting and possible violence.

Briggs said, "Frank, you've got a woman in a troubled marriage who may have been having an affair, who was under stress, and who left her coat on a suicide bridge. Sometimes the simplest explanation is the right one."

I wanted to say: sometimes the simplest explanation is the one that requires the least amount of work. But I didn't. I read the statement. I took no questions. I went back to my desk and I sat there for an hour staring at the wall.

Ruth Addams was outside the station when I left. She was standing in the rain with her attorney. She looked at me and I couldn't meet her eyes. She said, "You know, don't you?"

I didn't answer. Because she's right. I do know. I know this isn't a suicide. I know the coat was placed. I know the keys in the ignition were staged. I know Richard Hargrove is lying about something. I know his brother Victor's truck was at the house late at night. I know Eleanor Hargrove was not in that creek.

But I can't prove any of it. And the department has made its decision.

I'm keeping these notes. All of them. Every observation, every doubt, every question that nobody wanted asked. Maybe someday they'll matter.

Ruth Addams told me she'd never stop fighting. I believe her. I just hope she lives long enough to see the truth.

I've been a cop for twenty-two years. This is the first case I'm ashamed of.

— FC

## Evidence Item 9: Detective Callahan's Notes — The Public Story

[PRODUCTION NOTES: Yellow legal pad, handwritten. Short, bitter.]

---

**Personal Case Notes — Det. F. Callahan**
**Case #87-0114 — Hargrove**
**January 22, 1987**

George Preston from the Daily Herald called me today for a quote. I gave him the standard line. He asked if Richard Hargrove was a suspect. I said "person of interest." He asked what the difference was. I said, "About fifty thousand dollars in attorney fees."

Preston laughed. I didn't.

The paper is doing its job — reporting what we tell them. But what we're telling them is the sanitized version. The public story is: missing woman, coat on bridge, husband cooperating, search continues. The real story is: embezzled money, staged evidence, a brother's truck at the house at midnight, and a department that doesn't want to look too hard at the family that built this town.

I notice the Herald ran the Ashford Commons construction permit story as a sidebar to the main Hargrove article. "In an unrelated development." Unrelated. Six days after his wife disappears, the man files construction permits. The reporter doesn't see the connection. Why would he? Nobody's told him to look.

Ruth Addams is writing letters. She'll keep writing them. She's the only person in this story who isn't afraid of the Hargrove name.

I wish I could say the same about myself.

— FC

## Evidence Item 1: Witness Statement — Thomas Brewer

[PRODUCTION NOTES: Official APD form. Brewer's voice is simple, hesitant, careful.]

---

**ASHFORD POLICE DEPARTMENT**
Witness Statement

**Case Number:** 87-0114
**Date:** January 17, 1987
**Time:** 9:00 AM
**Location:** Hargrove Lake Property, Route 26, Mile Marker 14
**Witness:** Thomas Earl Brewer
**DOB:** 12/08/1934
**Occupation:** Groundskeeper/Caretaker, Hargrove Properties (since 1980)

---

**STATEMENT:**

I'll tell you what I heard. I can't tell you what it means. That's your job.

I live in the cottage on the Hargrove lake property. Been living there seven years. Mr. Hargrove — Richard — he hired me in 1980 to keep up the grounds, the lake house, the fencing, the dock. It's a full-time job in summer and about half-time in winter. I don't go into town much. I keep to myself.

The night of January 14, I went to bed around 10 PM. I don't have a television. I read for a while — Louis L'Amour, *Hondo* — and turned the light off around 10:30.

Something woke me up. I checked the clock — 1:15 AM. January 15.

I heard voices from the main house. The lake house is about 200 yards from my cottage. Sound carries out here, especially in winter when the air is still. I couldn't hear words. But I could hear the tone. It was an argument. Loud. Two voices, maybe three. One was high — a woman's voice, I'm pretty sure. The others were lower.

It went on for maybe fifteen or twenty minutes. Then it stopped. Not gradually — it just stopped. Like someone turned off a radio.

After that, I heard a car engine. Maybe two. Vehicles moving on the gravel drive. I heard them pull away, heading toward Route 26. I looked out my window but it was dark — too many trees. I couldn't see the vehicles.

I thought about going over to check, but Mr. Hargrove doesn't like me coming to the main house uninvited. He's made that clear. So I went back to bed.

The next day, I walked over to the lake house around 8 AM to check on a pipe. The house was empty. No vehicles. Everything looked normal except — the rug in the front room had been moved. It's a big oriental rug that Mrs. Hargrove picked out. Usually centered under the coffee table. That morning it was shifted about two feet toward the kitchen. Like it had been pulled or bunched up and then straightened out but not put back right.

I also noticed the back door was unlocked. Mr. Hargrove always locks it. And the kitchen faucet was dripping — I turned it off.

I told Mr. Hargrove what I'd heard on January 16 when he came to the lake house. He looked at me for a long time and then he said, very calmly, "Tom, I called Eleanor from the house phone that night. We argued on the phone. That's what you heard."

I said, "It sounded like more than one person in the room."

He said, "You were two hundred yards away and half asleep. It was a phone call. Let's leave it at that."

I understood what he was saying. He was telling me what happened, and he was telling me to agree with it. He didn't threaten me. He didn't have to. This is his property. This is my home.

But I'm telling you what I heard, Detective. It wasn't a phone call. There were people in that house. And one of them was a woman.

[Signed] Thomas E. Brewer
[Date] 01/17/87

---

**DET. CALLAHAN NOTE:**

*Brewer is scared. He's a decent man in an impossible position. I believe every word he said.*

*1:15 AM. Eleanor was supposedly on the bridge by midnight. If Brewer heard arguing at 1:15 — and one voice was a woman — then Eleanor was alive more than an hour after she allegedly jumped. And she was at the lake house, 40 minutes outside town.*

*This blows the suicide timeline apart. But Brewer's background is going to be a problem. — FC*

## Evidence Item 6: Detective Callahan's Notes — The Groundskeeper

[PRODUCTION NOTES: Yellow legal pad, handwritten.]

---

**Personal Case Notes — Det. F. Callahan**
**Case #87-0114 — Hargrove**
**January 22, 1987**

Thomas Brewer heard an argument at 1:15 AM at the lake house. At least two voices, possibly three. One was a woman's.

If that woman was Eleanor, she was alive at 1:15 AM. The suicide theory says she was in the water before 12:15 AM.

These two things cannot both be true.

Victor's truck was at the lake house at 3:45 PM that same day. Phone records show one call from Richard's office at 4:12 PM to Victor — and then nothing.

So either Richard sat in silence for eight hours — or he left the office long before midnight and lied about it.

Here's what I think happened:

4:12 PM — Richard calls Victor. They coordinate.
3:45 PM — Victor at the lake house. Prepares.
Evening — Eleanor attends the play, goes to the tavern, returns for her coat.
11:42 PM — Eleanor at the theatre.
~12:15 AM — Richard intercepts Eleanor. Drives to lake house.
~12:45-1:15 AM — Confrontation. Eleanor killed.
~2:00-4:00 AM — Suicide staged. Coat on bridge. Volvo to theatre.

The timeline works. But I have no body, no forensics, and my key witness has a 1971 assault conviction.

I feel like I'm watching someone get away with murder in slow motion.

— FC

## Evidence Item 10: Donna Walsh Statement — Richard's Secretary

[PRODUCTION NOTES: Brief statement from Richard's secretary at Hargrove Development. She corroborates Callahan's suspicions about Richard's movements on January 14.]

---

**ASHFORD POLICE DEPARTMENT**
Witness Statement

**Case Number:** 87-0114
**Date:** January 23, 1987
**Time:** 9:30 AM
**Interviewing Officer:** Det. Frank Callahan
**Witness:** Donna Marie Walsh
**DOB:** 05/03/1955
**Address:** 77 Maple Court, Ashford, OR
**Occupation:** Executive Secretary, Hargrove Development Corporation

---

**STATEMENT:**

I've worked for Mr. Hargrove for four years. I'm at the office from 8:30 to 5:30 every weekday, and sometimes Saturday mornings if we have a project deadline. I handle his schedule, his correspondence, his filing, and his phones.

On January 14, I was at the office as usual. Mr. Hargrove arrived around 9:00 AM. He seemed distracted — not his usual self. He's usually very focused, very organized, but that day he kept looking at the phone, like he was waiting for a call. He asked me twice if Eleanor had called. She hadn't.

He made a few calls during the morning — I heard him on the phone with the gallery and with the county permits office. Normal business. After lunch, he was in his office with the door closed. I don't know who he called in the afternoon — I was at my desk in the reception area and his office door muffles the sound.

I left at 5:30 PM as usual. Mr. Hargrove was still in his office. He said, "Donna, you can go. I'll be here late." I said goodnight. I didn't see anything unusual — his briefcase was on the desk, his coat was on the hook behind the door.

Here's what I want to tell you, Detective, and I've been going back and forth about whether to say it. The next morning — January 15 — I came in at 8:30 as usual. The office was unlocked, which was odd because Mr. Hargrove always locks up when he leaves. I assumed he'd come in early, but he wasn't there. His briefcase was exactly where it had been the night before. His coat was still on the hook. The coffee maker was off — if he'd been there until midnight, he would have made coffee. He always makes coffee when he works late. I've seen the cups on his desk many mornings after his late nights.

There were no coffee cups. There was no sign that anyone had been in that office after I left at 5:30 PM.

I'm not saying Mr. Hargrove is lying. I'm saying the office didn't look like someone had worked there for six and a half hours. It looked like someone left shortly after I did and didn't come back.

[Signed] Donna Walsh
[Date] 01/23/87

---

**DET. CALLAHAN NOTE:**

*Walsh is credible, careful, and clearly nervous about going against her employer. But she's telling me the truth — no coffee, no moved papers, briefcase in the same spot, door unlocked. Richard wasn't at that office until midnight. He left shortly after 5:30 — maybe right after Walsh drove away.*

*That gives him six and a half hours. Plenty of time to get to the theatre parking lot by 11:42 PM, intercept Eleanor, drive to the lake house, and do what he did.*

*Walsh gave me this statement on her own — she called the station and asked to come in. She's risking her job. I told her I'd keep it confidential, and I will. But it's in the notes.*

*If this case ever reopens, find Donna Walsh. She knows.*

*— FC*

**DET. CHEN NOTE (2024):**

*The Route 26 Motor Lodge closed in 2003. The property was purchased by a storage company, which found boxes of old registration cards in the office. When we canvassed Route 26 businesses and properties during the reopened investigation, a storage unit employee mentioned the cards.*

*This registration card matches Victor Hargrove's movements perfectly. "V. Howard" — a thin alias. Silver Chevy pickup. Checked in at 5:00 PM on January 14 — consistent with leaving the lake house at 4:30 PM (as Brewer observed) and driving 15 minutes east toward Ashford.*

*Victor didn't drive all the way back to Portland after visiting the lake house. He stopped at this motel — 15 minutes from the lake house, 25 minutes from Ashford. He waited. He used the payphone (calling Richard's office at 8:30 PM? The timing lines up). Then, after midnight, he drove back to the lake house for the second time that night.*

*This explains the timeline gap. Victor checked out of the Heathman at 2:00 PM, drove to the lake house (arriving 3:45), left at 4:30, stopped at the motor lodge at 5:00, waited until after midnight, returned to the lake house. Meanwhile, he made his hotel calls from the Heathman later that evening — meaning he drove BACK to Portland after the motor lodge, checked into the Heathman at 6:45, had dinner with Drummond, returned to his room, made the 8:30 and 11:55 PM calls, then drove BACK to the lake house a third time.*

*Or — more likely — someone else was in the Heathman room making those calls. Did Victor give his room key to an associate? Did he arrange for the calls to be made from his room to create an alibi? We can't confirm this 37 years later.*

*What we can confirm: Victor was on Route 26 at 5:00 PM on January 14. He was within striking distance of the lake house all evening. His Portland alibi was a construction.*

*— SC*

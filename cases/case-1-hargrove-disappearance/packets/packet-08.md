## Evidence Item 1: Witness Statement — Thomas Brewer

[PRODUCTION NOTES: Official APD witness statement form. Brewer's voice is simple, hesitant, careful with words. He speaks like a man who has learned that saying the wrong thing has consequences. His statement is shorter than others and less articulate — he's not educated and he's intimidated. Print on aged cream paper.]

---

**ASHFORD POLICE DEPARTMENT**
Witness Statement

**Case Number:** 87-0114
**Date:** January 17, 1987
**Time:** 9:00 AM
**Interviewing Officer:** Det. Frank Callahan
**Location:** Hargrove Lake Property, Route 26, Mile Marker 14
**Witness:** Thomas Earl Brewer
**DOB:** 12/08/1934
**Address:** Groundskeeper's Cottage, Hargrove Lake Property, Ashford, OR
**Occupation:** Groundskeeper/Caretaker, Hargrove Properties (employed since 1980)

---

**STATEMENT:**

I'll tell you what I heard. I can't tell you what it means. That's your job.

I live in the cottage on the Hargrove lake property. Been living there seven years. Mr. Hargrove — Richard — he hired me in 1980 to keep up the grounds, the lake house, the fencing, the dock. It's a full-time job in summer and about half-time in winter. I don't go into town much. I keep to myself. That's how I like it and that's how Mr. Hargrove likes it.

The night of January 14, I went to bed around 10 PM. I don't have a television. I read for a while — I was reading a Louis L'Amour — and I turned the light off around 10:30.

Something woke me up. I checked the clock by my bed — it was 1:15 AM. January 15.

I heard voices from the main house. The lake house is about 200 yards from my cottage, across the clearing. Sound carries out here, especially at night, especially in winter when the air is still and cold. I couldn't hear words. But I could hear the tone. It was an argument. Loud. Two voices, maybe three. One of them was high — a woman's voice, I'm pretty sure. The others were lower. Men's voices.

It went on for maybe fifteen or twenty minutes. Then it stopped. Not gradually — it just stopped. Like someone turned off a radio.

After that, I heard a car engine. Maybe two. Vehicles moving on the gravel drive. I heard them pull away, heading toward Route 26. I looked out my window but it was dark — no moon visible from my angle, too many trees. I couldn't see the vehicles.

I thought about going over to the house to check, but it wasn't my place. Mr. Hargrove doesn't like me coming to the main house uninvited. He's made that clear. So I went back to bed.

The next day, January 15, I walked over to the lake house around 8 AM to check on a pipe I'd been worried about in the cold snap. The house was empty. No vehicles. Everything looked normal except — I noticed the rug in the front room had been moved. It's a big oriental rug that Mrs. Hargrove picked out. It's usually centered under the coffee table. That morning it was shifted about two feet toward the kitchen. Like it had been pulled or bunched up and then straightened out but not put back right.

I didn't think much of it at the time. Later that day I heard about Mrs. Hargrove from the radio. Then I started thinking.

I told Mr. Hargrove what I'd heard. He came to the lake house on January 16 — he said to "check on things." I told him about the arguing at 1:15 AM. He looked at me for a long time and then he said, very calmly, "Tom, I called Eleanor from the house phone that night to check on her. We argued on the phone. That's what you heard. My side of a phone call."

I said, "It sounded like more than one person in the room, Mr. Hargrove."

He said, "You were two hundred yards away and half asleep, Tom. It was a phone call. Let's leave it at that."

I understood what he was saying. He was telling me what happened, and he was telling me to agree with it. He didn't threaten me. He didn't have to. This is his property. This is my home. I've got nowhere else to go and we both know it.

But I'm telling you what I heard, Detective. It wasn't a phone call. There were people in that house. More than one. And one of them was a woman, and she was not happy.

That's all I know. I'm sorry I can't tell you more. I wish I'd gone over there. I wish I had.

[Signed] Thomas E. Brewer
[Date] 01/17/87

---

**DET. CALLAHAN NOTE (handwritten at bottom):**

*Brewer is scared. He's a decent man in an impossible position — living on the property of the man he may have heard commit a murder. I believe every word he said. The question is whether anyone else will.*

*1:15 AM. Eleanor was supposedly on the bridge by midnight at the latest (based on the 11:42 PM sighting and the 12-minute walk). If Brewer heard arguing at 1:15 AM — and one of the voices was a woman — then Eleanor was alive more than an hour after she allegedly jumped. And she was at the lake house, 40 minutes outside town.*

*This blows the suicide timeline apart. But Brewer's background is going to be a problem. — FC*

**DET. CALLAHAN NOTE:**

*Key entries:*

*1. Victor Hargrove at the lake house at 3:45 PM on Jan 14 — the day of Eleanor's disappearance. Brewer says he was on the phone, pacing. Preparing? Coordinating?*

*2. Victor left at 4:30 PM — but did he come back later? The arguing Brewer heard at 1:15 AM involved multiple voices.*

*3. Richard came to the lake house on Jan 16 — after the police interview — and removed a box. What was in that box? I can't subpoena it without probable cause, and I don't have probable cause without a body.*

*4. The rug. The unlocked back door. The dripping faucet. Small things. Signs that something happened in that house that wasn't normal.*

*— FC*

## Evidence Item 4: Richard Hargrove's Counter-Statement

[PRODUCTION NOTES: Official APD statement, but this one is more polished — clearly influenced by his attorney. The tone is smooth, reasonable, and subtly condescending toward Brewer.]

---

**ASHFORD POLICE DEPARTMENT**
Supplemental Statement

**Case Number:** 87-0114
**Date:** January 20, 1987
**Time:** 2:00 PM
**Interviewing Officer:** Det. Frank Callahan
**Subject:** Richard A. Hargrove
**Attorney Present:** William Crane, Esq.

---

**STATEMENT (re: Thomas Brewer's report of January 15, 1:15 AM):**

Detective Callahan informed me that my groundskeeper, Thomas Brewer, reported hearing what he described as an "argument" at the lake house in the early morning hours of January 15. I'd like to address this directly.

On the evening of January 14, I was working at my office until approximately midnight, as I've previously stated. Before leaving the office, I called our lake house landline — I sometimes do this to check the answering machine for messages from contractors. On this occasion, I decided to call Eleanor's cell phone as well. She didn't answer, which concerned me. I called the lake house again and left a message.

At some point during these calls, I became frustrated. I admit I may have raised my voice while leaving a message. I was worried about my wife — I hadn't heard from her all evening — and I expressed that frustration vocally. The calls were made from my office phone, and the records will confirm this.

Mr. Brewer's cottage is approximately two hundred yards from the lake house. At that distance, at night, with the windows closed, distinguishing between a person speaking loudly on the telephone and multiple people arguing in person would be extremely difficult — especially for someone who was, by his own account, half asleep.

I want to be clear: I have nothing but respect for Thomas. He's a loyal employee and a hard worker. But he has acknowledged that he could not make out specific words, could not see the house clearly, and was woken from sleep. I believe what he heard was me, on the phone, talking to myself in frustration when Eleanor didn't answer. I've done this before — asked anyone who knows me. I'm not a quiet person when I'm upset.

I also want to note, respectfully, that Mr. Brewer has a history that may be relevant to assessing the reliability of his account. I don't say this to impugn his character — he has been a dependable employee — but it's a matter of record and I believe the investigation should be aware of it.

[Signed] Richard A. Hargrove
[Date] 01/20/87
[Witnessed] William Crane, Esq.

---

**DET. CALLAHAN NOTE:**

*"I believe the investigation should be aware of it." That's Richard telling me to look at Brewer's criminal record. He's trying to discredit his own employee — the one person who might have heard what happened that night.*

*Let's fact-check his story. He says he called the lake house and Eleanor's cell phone from his office. Two problems:*

*1. Eleanor didn't have a cell phone. Car phones existed in 1987 but Eleanor's Volvo didn't have one. I confirmed this with the vehicle search. Richard KNOWS this. He's either lying or making things up on the fly.*

*2. He says "the records will confirm" his calls. Fine. I've requested the phone records. If there are calls from his office to the lake house at midnight, that puts him at the office. If there aren't...*

*UPDATE (01/22): Phone records received. Richard's office phone shows ONE outgoing call on January 14: to his brother Victor at 4:12 PM. No calls after that. No calls to the lake house. No calls to any phone number associated with Eleanor.*

*He lied. And his lawyer let him. — FC*

## Evidence Item 5: Detective Callahan's Notes — The Groundskeeper

[PRODUCTION NOTES: Yellow legal pad, handwritten.]

---

**Personal Case Notes — Det. F. Callahan**
**Case #87-0114 — Hargrove**
**January 22, 1987**

Thomas Brewer heard an argument at 1:15 AM at the lake house on January 15. At least two voices, possibly three. One was a woman's.

If that woman was Eleanor, she was alive at 1:15 AM. The suicide theory says she walked to the bridge after 11:42 PM — a 12-minute walk puts her at the bridge by midnight at the latest. If she jumped, she was in the water before 12:15 AM.

But Brewer heard a woman arguing an hour later, 40 minutes outside town.

These two things cannot both be true.

Either Brewer is lying — and I don't believe he is — or Eleanor did not go to that bridge. She went to the lake house. Or she was taken there.

Victor's truck was at the lake house at 3:45 PM that same day. He left at 4:30. Richard claims he was at his office until midnight. Phone records show one call from the office at 4:12 PM to Victor — and then nothing. No calls from the office after 4:12 PM.

So either Richard sat in silence in his office for eight hours without making a single call — no calls to his wife, no calls to colleagues, no business calls, nothing — or he left the office long before midnight and lied about it.

Here's what I think happened:

4:12 PM — Richard calls Victor from the office. They coordinate.
3:45 PM — Victor arrives at the lake house. Prepares.
Evening — Eleanor attends the play, goes to the tavern, returns to the theatre for her coat.
11:42 PM — Eleanor at the theatre, caught on camera.
~12:15 AM — Richard intercepts Eleanor. Takes her to the lake house (40-minute drive).
~12:45-1:15 AM — Confrontation at the lake house. Eleanor is killed.
~2:00-4:00 AM — Richard stages the suicide. Plants the navy coat on the bridge. Drives Eleanor's Volvo to the theatre lot. Walks home.

The timeline works. Everything fits. But I have no body, no forensic evidence, and my key witness is a man with an assault conviction from 1971 who lives on the suspect's property.

Briggs won't extend the investigation. The Hargrove family's attorney has started making noises about "harassment." The case closes in ten days.

I feel like I'm watching someone get away with murder in slow motion.

— FC
